## SECTION 1: EPIDEMIOLOGY

### 1.1 Incidence & Demographics

- **TBI is the leading cause of death and acquired disability in children and adolescents** in developed countries
- **US CDC estimates**: ~475,000 TBI-related emergency department (ED) visits annually in children aged 0-14 years; ~35,000 hospitalizations; ~2,200 deaths
- **Age distribution**: Bimodal peaks — infants/toddlers (0-4 years, highest rate) and adolescents (15-19 years)
- **Sex**: Males 1.5-2x more likely than females across all age groups
- **Mechanisms by age**:
  - Infants (<1 year): Falls most common mechanism; abusive head trauma (AHT) a significant cause of severe TBI in this age group
  - Toddlers/preschool (1-4 years): Falls most common
  - School-age (5-14 years): Falls, sports/recreation, bicycle injuries, motor vehicle accidents
  - Adolescents (15-19 years): Motor vehicle accidents (driver/passenger), sports injuries, assaults
- **Sports concussion**: ~1.1-1.9 million sport/recreation-related concussions per year in youth <18 years in the US
- **Severity distribution**: ~80% mild TBI (mTBI/concussion), ~10% moderate, ~10% severe

### 1.2 Mortality & Outcomes

- **Mortality**: Overall pediatric TBI mortality ~2-5 per 100,000; severe TBI mortality ~15-25%
- **Children have better recovery potential** than adults for equivalent injury severity due to neuroplasticity, but this is not unlimited — the concept of "children always do better" is an oversimplification
- **Young age at injury is NOT always protective**: Injuries before age 2-3 may have WORSE long-term cognitive outcomes due to disruption of critical developmental windows ("early vulnerability" hypothesis)
- **Long-term outcomes**: Many children with moderate-severe TBI have persistent cognitive, behavioral, social, academic, and emotional difficulties years after injury

#### Severity Classification by GCS

| Severity | GCS Score | Key Features |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Mild (mTBI/concussion) | 13-15 | May have brief LOC (<30 min), PTA <24 hr |
| Moderate | 9-12 | LOC 30 min-24 hr, PTA 1-7 days |
| Severe | 3-8 | LOC >24 hr, PTA >7 days |

### 2.2 Additional Severity Markers

- **Post-traumatic amnesia (PTA)**: Duration is one of the best predictors of outcome
  - <1 hour: mild
  - 1-24 hours: moderate
  - 1-7 days: severe
  - >7 days: very severe
- **Loss of consciousness (LOC)**: Duration correlates with severity
- **Imaging findings**: Presence of intracranial hemorrhage, contusion, diffuse axonal injury on CT/MRI
- **Ranchos Los Amigos Scale**: Cognitive function levels I-X used for tracking recovery in moderate-severe TBI (same scale used in adults; applicable to older children/adolescents)

## SECTION 3: PECARN CLINICAL DECISION RULES

### 3.1 Overview

- **PECARN** = Pediatric Emergency Care Applied Research Network
- **Purpose**: Clinical decision rules to identify children at very low risk for clinically important traumatic brain injuries (ciTBI) who do NOT need CT scanning, thereby reducing unnecessary radiation exposure
- **Landmark study**: Kuppermann N, Holmes JF, Dayan PS, et al. Identification of children at very low risk of clinically-important brain injuries after head trauma: a prospective cohort study. Lancet. 2009;374(9696):1160-1170.
- **Study design**: Prospective multicenter cohort study; 42,412 children <18 years with head trauma; 25 PECARN-affiliated EDs
- **Clinically important TBI (ciTBI) defined as**: Death from TBI, neurosurgical intervention, intubation >24 hours for TBI, or hospital admission ≥2 nights for TBI with positive CT findings
- **Two separate algorithms**: One for children <2 years, one for children ≥2 years

### 3.2 PECARN Algorithm — Children <2 Years

**Step 1: High-risk criteria (CT recommended)**
- GCS ≤14
- Altered mental status (agitation, somnolence, repetitive questioning, slow response)
- Palpable skull fracture

→ If ANY present: CT recommended (ciTBI risk ~4.4%)

**Step 2: Intermediate-risk criteria (CT vs. observation)**
- Non-frontal scalp hematoma (occipital, parietal, temporal)
- LOC ≥5 seconds
- Severe mechanism of injury (MVC with patient ejection/rollover/death of another passenger, pedestrian/cyclist without helmet struck by motor vehicle, fall >3 feet, head struck by high-impact object)
- Not acting normally per parent assessment

→ If ANY present but no Step 1 criteria: Observation vs. CT based on clinical judgment, physician experience, age <3 months, multiple findings, worsening symptoms, parental preference (ciTBI risk ~0.9%)
→ If NONE present: CT NOT recommended (ciTBI risk <0.02%; negative predictive value 100%)

### 3.3 PECARN Algorithm — Children ≥2 Years

**Step 1: High-risk criteria (CT recommended)**
- GCS ≤14
- Altered mental status
- Signs of basilar skull fracture (hemotympanum, raccoon eyes, Battle sign, CSF otorrhea/rhinorrhea)

→ If ANY present: CT recommended (ciTBI risk ~4.3%)

**Step 2: Intermediate-risk criteria (CT vs. observation)**
- LOC (any duration)
- History of vomiting
- Severe mechanism of injury (same definitions as <2 years but fall threshold >5 feet for ≥2 years)
- Severe headache

→ If ANY present but no Step 1 criteria: Observation vs. CT based on clinical judgment (ciTBI risk ~0.9%)
→ If NONE present: CT NOT recommended (ciTBI risk <0.05%; negative predictive value 99.95%)

### 3.4 Board-Relevant PECARN Pearls

- **Fall height thresholds differ by age**: >3 feet for <2 years; >5 feet for ≥2 years
- **Scalp hematoma location matters in <2 years**: Non-frontal (occipital/parietal/temporal) hematomas are more concerning than frontal
- **Palpable skull fracture** is a high-risk criterion in <2 years (not in ≥2 years where basilar skull fracture signs are used instead)
- **"Acting normally" per parent** is used as a criterion in <2 years
- **Observation period**: 4-6 hours in the ED is an acceptable alternative to CT for intermediate-risk patients
- **PECARN rules have been validated** in multiple external cohorts and are the most widely used pediatric head trauma clinical decision rules globally
- **Sensitivity for ciTBI**: >99% for both age groups

## SECTION 4: CONCUSSION / MILD TBI

### 4.1 Definitions

- **Concussion/mTBI**: Biomechanically induced alteration of brain function; GCS 13-15; may include brief LOC (<30 min), amnesia, confusion, focal neurological deficits that resolve; NO structural abnormality on standard neuroimaging
- **Key concept**: Concussion is a FUNCTIONAL (metabolic/physiological) rather than STRUCTURAL injury — normal CT/MRI
- **Pathophysiology**: Ionic flux (K+ efflux, Ca2+ influx), glutamate release, mitochondrial dysfunction, metabolic crisis (increased glucose demand with decreased supply), axonal stretch/dysfunction; recovery typically 7-10 days at cellular level in adults, may be longer in children

### 4.2 Berlin/Amsterdam 2022 Concussion in Sport Consensus (6th International Conference)

**Major update to the concussion in sport consensus statements — replaces Berlin 2016 (5th Conference).**

- **Reference**: Patricios JS, Schneider KJ, Dvorak J, et al. Consensus statement on concussion in sport: the 6th International Conference on Concussion in Sport — Amsterdam, October 2022. British Journal of Sports Medicine. 2023;57(11):695-711.

**Key Changes and Recommendations (2022 vs. previous):**

1. **Sport-Related Concussion (SRC) definition updated**: Recognition that definition is evolving; emphasis on clinical diagnosis; imaging is normal by definition
2. **No same-day return to sport**: Minimum 24-hour rest from sport following ANY suspected concussion; athlete should not return to contact/collision sport on the day of injury regardless of symptom resolution. **This is stricter than some previous interpretations.**
3. **Symptom-limited activity rather than strict rest**: Previous consensus recommended cognitive and physical rest; 2022 consensus emphasizes that strict/prolonged rest (beyond 24-48 hours) is NOT beneficial and may be harmful; encourages symptom-limited physical and cognitive activity starting 24-48 hours after injury. **Key shift from older "cocoon" approach.**
4. **Return to Learn (RTL) BEFORE Return to Sport (RTS)**: Emphasized more strongly; children/adolescents should achieve return to school/learning before progressing through sport return protocol
5. **Graduated Return to Sport Protocol**: Updated 6-step protocol (see Section 4.3)
6. **Children/adolescents**: More conservative approach recommended than for adults; longer recovery expected (2-4 weeks typical for children vs. 10-14 days for adults); no return to contact sport until fully symptom-free AND completed return-to-sport protocol
7. **Multidomain assessment**: SCAT6 (Sport Concussion Assessment Tool, 6th edition) and child-SCAT6 introduced as updated assessment tools
8. **Neck and vestibular assessment**: Greater emphasis on cervical spine assessment and vestibulo-ocular screening as part of concussion evaluation
9. **Persistent symptoms**: Now termed "persistent post-concussion symptoms" rather than "post-concussion syndrome"; defined as symptoms lasting >4 weeks in children (>2 weeks in adults)
10. **Retirement/prolonged absence from sport**: Considered after multiple concussions or prolonged recovery; individualized decision-making

### 4.3 Return-to-Sport (RTS) Protocol — 6-Step Graduated Protocol

**Board-critical protocol — know the steps and principles.**

| Step | Activity | Objective | Minimum Duration |
|------|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited activity | Daily activities that do not provoke symptoms; gradual reintroduction of cognitive/physical activity | 24-48 hours |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise | Walking, swimming, stationary cycling; HR <70% max; no resistance training | 24 hours minimum |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise | Running drills, skating drills; no head impact activities | 24 hours minimum |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills | Progressively harder training drills; may begin resistance training; no body contact | 24 hours minimum |
| 5 | Full contact practice | Full contact practice with medical clearance; restore confidence; functional assessment by coaching staff | 24 hours minimum |
| 6 | Return to competition | Full game play | — |

**Key Principles:**
- Minimum 24 hours at each step before advancing
- **If symptoms return at any step**: Drop back to the previous asymptomatic step and retry after 24 hours
- Total minimum time from injury to return to full contact: At least 1 week (6 steps x 24 hours minimum each, plus initial rest)
- **Children/adolescents**: May require more than 24 hours per step; more conservative timeline recommended
- **Medical clearance**: Required before Step 5 (full contact) — physician or licensed healthcare provider familiar with concussion
- **No same-day return to play**: Regardless of symptom resolution on sideline

### 4.4 Return-to-Learn (RTL) Protocol

**Key concept: Return to Learn should be prioritized BEFORE Return to Sport in children/adolescents.**

- **Academic accommodations**: May include reduced workload, extra time for tests, rest breaks, avoiding standardized testing during recovery, modified schedule, preferential seating, reduced screen time
- **Graduated return**:
  1. **Stage 1**: Daily activities at home that do not provoke symptoms
  2. **Stage 2**: Homework, reading, light cognitive work (symptom-limited)
  3. **Stage 3**: Part-time school attendance (half-days, modified schedule)
  4. **Stage 4**: Full school attendance with accommodations as needed
  5. **Stage 5**: Full school activities without accommodations
- **Then proceed with Return-to-Sport protocol**
- **Reference**: DeMatteo C, Stazyk K, Singh SK, et al. Development of a conservative protocol to return children and youth to activity following concussive injury. Clinical Pediatrics. 2015;54(2):152-163.
- **Key point**: Return to school does NOT require being fully asymptomatic — symptom-limited return is recommended; prolonged absence from school may worsen outcomes (social isolation, academic stress, anxiety/depression)

### 4.5 CDC Guideline on Pediatric mTBI (2018)

- **Reference**: Lumba-Brown A, Yeates KO, Sarmiento K, et al. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention guideline on the diagnosis and management of mild traumatic brain injury among children. JAMA Pediatrics. 2018;172(11):e182853.
- **19 recommendations** covering diagnosis, prognosis, and management

**Key recommendations include:**
1. **Imaging**: Do not routinely use CT for children with GCS 14-15; use validated clinical decision rules (PECARN) to guide imaging decisions; do not use MRI routinely in acute management of mTBI
2. **Assessment**: Use validated symptom rating scales for diagnosis and monitoring; perform comprehensive physical examination including neurological and cognitive assessment
3. **Prognosis**: Risk factors for prolonged recovery include: prior concussion history, symptom severity at presentation, personal/family psychiatric history, learning disabilities; use validated tools to assess risk
4. **Acute management**: Brief period (24-48 hours) of rest followed by gradual return to non-contact activity; do NOT prescribe strict rest beyond 2-3 days
5. **Activity**: Encourage return to gradual physical and cognitive activity as tolerated; prolonged rest/inactivity NOT recommended
6. **Patient/family education**: Provide anticipatory guidance about expected recovery, warning signs, activity modification
7. **Follow-up**: Recommended for all children with mTBI; assess recovery trajectory; refer for persistent symptoms (>4 weeks)
8. **Post-injury interventions**: Consider targeted therapies for persistent symptoms (vestibular rehab, vision therapy, cognitive-behavioral therapy, graded exercise therapy)

### 4.6 Post-Concussion Syndrome / Persistent Post-Concussion Symptoms

- **Definition**: Symptoms persisting beyond expected recovery period; >4 weeks in children (Amsterdam 2022), >10-14 days historically for adults
- **Prevalence**: ~10-30% of pediatric concussions
- **Symptoms**: Headache (most common), dizziness, fatigue, cognitive difficulties (attention, memory, processing speed), irritability, sleep disturbance, emotional lability, light/noise sensitivity, balance difficulties
- **Risk factors for prolonged recovery**: Female sex (adolescents), prior concussion history, premorbid mood/anxiety disorders, greater initial symptom burden, family history of psychiatric illness, high-symptom score at presentation
- **Management**: Active rehabilitation approaches — graded aerobic exercise (Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test protocol), vestibular therapy, vision therapy, cervical spine physiotherapy, cognitive-behavioral therapy for mood/anxiety symptoms, academic accommodations
- **Board pearl**: Most concussions resolve within 2-4 weeks in children; prolonged strict rest is HARMFUL; active rehabilitation is preferred for persistent symptoms

### 4.7 Concussion Assessment Tools

- **SCAT6 / Child-SCAT6**: Sport Concussion Assessment Tool, 6th edition (2023) — standardized sideline and office-based assessment; includes symptom evaluation, cognitive screening (orientation, immediate memory, concentration), neurological screening, balance examination (BESS — Balance Error Scoring System)
- **PCSS (Post-Concussion Symptom Scale)**: 22-item self-report symptom checklist; rated on 0-6 severity scale
- **ImPACT (Immediate Post-Concussion Assessment and Cognitive Testing)**: Computerized neurocognitive test battery; measures verbal/visual memory, processing speed, reaction time; used pre-season baseline and post-injury comparison; widely used in organized sports
- **King-Devick Test**: Rapid number naming test measuring saccadic eye movement; sensitive sideline screening tool; takes ~2 minutes
- **VOMS (Vestibular/Ocular-Motor Screening)**: Assesses vestibular and ocular-motor function after concussion; includes smooth pursuit, saccades, convergence, VOR, visual motion sensitivity
- **Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT)**: Graded aerobic exercise test to determine exercise tolerance and symptom threshold; used to guide exercise prescription in recovery

## SECTION 5: ABUSIVE HEAD TRAUMA (AHT)

### 5.1 Terminology & Definition

- **Current preferred term**: Abusive head trauma (AHT) — replaces "shaken baby syndrome" per AAP recommendation (2009)
- **Reference**: Christian CW, Block R, Committee on Child Abuse and Neglect, American Academy of Pediatrics. Abusive head trauma in infants and children. Pediatrics. 2009;123(5):1409-1411.
- **Definition**: Head injury resulting from inflicted trauma (shaking, impact, or both) in infants and young children
- **Most common cause of traumatic death in children <2 years**

### 5.2 The "Triad" (Historical/Board-Relevant)

The classic triad associated with abusive head trauma:
1. **Subdural hemorrhage(s)**: Often bilateral, interhemispheric, posterior fossa; thin film subdural hematomas; may be of different ages (acute + chronic)
2. **Retinal hemorrhages**: Multiple layers (preretinal, intraretinal, subretinal), bilateral, extending to periphery; more extensive/multilayer retinal hemorrhages are more specific for AHT; too numerous to count (TNTC) pattern is highly suspicious
3. **Encephalopathy/cerebral edema**: Diffuse cerebral edema, hypoxic-ischemic injury, diffuse axonal injury

**Board caveats:**
- Not ALL three elements need to be present for diagnosis
- The triad is NOT pathognomonic — must consider differential diagnosis (accidental trauma, medical mimics such as coagulopathy, metabolic diseases, glutaric aciduria type I, Menkes disease, osteogenesis imperfecta)
- Court/medicolegal controversies exist but should not prevent appropriate evaluation and reporting
- The ABSENCE of a credible mechanism (e.g., "rolled off the couch" doesn't explain severe SDH + retinal hemorrhages + encephalopathy) is a key red flag

### 5.3 Clinical Features

- **Peak age**: 2-4 months (highest risk period)
- **Presenting symptoms**: Irritability, lethargy, poor feeding, vomiting, seizures, apnea, bulging fontanelle, altered consciousness; may be nonspecific, leading to delayed diagnosis
- **Physical examination findings**: Bruising (especially in pre-mobile infants — "those who don't cruise rarely bruise"), bulging fontanelle, increased head circumference, retinal hemorrhages on dilated fundoscopy
- **Skeletal survey findings**: Metaphyseal corner fractures ("bucket handle" or "chip" fractures — highly specific for abuse), rib fractures (especially posterior, bilateral, or multiple), long bone fractures in non-ambulatory infants
- **Imaging**: Non-contrast CT head (acute evaluation); MRI brain (better characterization of injury patterns, timing, DAI, parenchymal injury); skeletal survey; repeat skeletal survey in 2 weeks (may reveal healing fractures not visible initially)

### 5.4 AAP Guidelines & Evaluation

- **Mandatory reporting**: Healthcare providers are mandatory reporters of suspected child abuse in all US states
- **Evaluation protocol for suspected AHT**:
  1. Non-contrast CT head
  2. MRI brain (within 24-72 hours if possible)
  3. Dilated ophthalmologic examination (by ophthalmologist)
  4. Skeletal survey (entire skeleton AP views)
  5. Laboratory: CBC, coagulation studies (PT, PTT, INR, fibrinogen), liver function tests (hepatic injury screening), lipase (abdominal injury), urinalysis (renal injury)
  6. Repeat skeletal survey in 2 weeks
  7. Consider metabolic workup if differential diagnosis includes metabolic disease
- **Reference**: Jenny C, Hymel KP, Ritzen A, et al. Analysis of missed cases of abusive head trauma. JAMA. 1999;281(7):621-626. — Demonstrated high rate of missed AHT diagnoses initially, emphasizing need for high clinical suspicion.

### 5.5 Outcomes of AHT

- **Mortality**: ~15-25% (highest for severe TBI from abuse)
- **Morbidity**: High — up to 60-80% of survivors have significant long-term neurological disabilities (cognitive impairment, motor deficits, seizure disorders, visual impairment, behavioral problems)
- **Worse outcomes** than accidental TBI of equivalent severity — likely due to diffuse injury pattern, hypoxic-ischemic component, repeat injuries, delay in seeking medical attention

## SECTION 6: SECOND IMPACT SYNDROME

### 6.1 Definition & Pathophysiology

- **Definition**: Rare, potentially fatal condition in which an individual (typically adolescent athlete) sustains a second concussion before fully recovering from a first concussion, leading to rapid, catastrophic cerebral edema and herniation
- **Pathophysiology**: Hypothesized dysregulation of cerebral vascular autoregulation — second impact leads to massive cerebral vasodilation, increased cerebral blood volume, malignant cerebral edema, transtentorial herniation
- **Controversial**: Some experts debate whether this is a distinct entity vs. diffuse cerebral swelling from a single severe impact; case reports are limited; difficult to study prospectively

### 6.2 Clinical Features

- **Typically adolescent male athletes** (football, boxing, hockey, soccer)
- **Timeline**: Days to weeks after initial concussion (still symptomatic from first injury)
- **Presentation**: After second (often seemingly minor) impact — rapid neurological deterioration within minutes; pupillary dilation, loss of consciousness, respiratory failure, herniation
- **Mortality**: ~50%; significant morbidity in survivors (~100% have residual deficits)
- **Prevention**: This is the primary justification for NOT allowing return to play while still symptomatic from concussion; strict adherence to RTS protocols

### 6.3 Key References

- Cantu RC. Second-impact syndrome. Clinics in Sports Medicine. 1998;17(1):37-44.
- McCrory P, Davis G, Makdissi M. Second impact syndrome or cerebral swelling after sporting head injury. Current Sports Medicine Reports. 2012;11(1):21-23.

## SECTION 7: NEUROENDOCRINE DYSFUNCTION AFTER PEDIATRIC TBI

### 7.1 Overview

- **Increasingly recognized** complication of moderate-severe pediatric TBI
- **Hypothalamic-pituitary axis vulnerability**: Hypothalamus and pituitary gland are susceptible to TBI due to anatomic position (near base of skull, proximity to bony structures, vulnerable vascular supply)
- **Prevalence**: Estimated 10-30% of children with moderate-severe TBI develop some degree of pituitary dysfunction; may be underdiagnosed

### 7.2 Specific Endocrine Disorders

- **Growth hormone deficiency (GHD)**: Most common long-term pituitary deficit post-TBI; may manifest as growth deceleration months-years after injury; screening recommended
- **Central precocious puberty**: Accelerated onset of puberty post-TBI; more common in children injured before normal pubertal age
- **Diabetes insipidus (DI)**: Central DI from posterior pituitary/hypothalamic injury; may be acute (transient — common) or chronic; presents with polyuria, polydipsia, hypernatremia
- **SIADH**: Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion; hyponatremia in acute phase; more common acutely than DI
- **Adrenal insufficiency**: Central (secondary) adrenal insufficiency; potentially life-threatening if unrecognized
- **Hypothyroidism**: Central hypothyroidism; less common
- **Hyperprolactinemia**: From stalk disruption; usually asymptomatic
- **Hypogonadism**: Central hypogonadotropic hypogonadism; may manifest as delayed puberty

### 7.3 Screening Recommendations

- **All children with moderate-severe TBI** should be screened for endocrine dysfunction
- **Timing**: Baseline assessment during acute hospitalization; repeat screening at 3-6 months, 12 months, and annually for at least 5 years post-injury
- **Tests**: Growth velocity monitoring, GH stimulation testing if growth deceleration, thyroid function (TSH, free T4), morning cortisol, electrolytes/osmolality, pubertal staging
- **Reference**: Acerini CL, Tasker RC. Neuroendocrine consequences of traumatic brain injury. Journal of Pediatric Endocrinology and Metabolism. 2008;21(7):611-619.

## SECTION 8: POST-TRAUMATIC EPILEPSY (PTE)

### 8.1 Definitions & Classification

- **Early post-traumatic seizures**: Seizures occurring within 7 days of injury (sometimes called "provoked" or "acute symptomatic" seizures)
- **Late post-traumatic seizures / Post-traumatic epilepsy**: Unprovoked seizures occurring >7 days after injury; represents true epilepsy (increased risk of recurrence)
- **Impact seizures**: Seizures occurring at the time of or immediately after impact; generally benign if isolated

### 8.2 Epidemiology in Children

- **Risk correlates with TBI severity**:
  - Mild TBI: ~1-2% risk of PTE (some studies suggest ~1.6% for moderate TBI)
  - Moderate TBI: ~1.6% (Annegers et al. data)
  - Severe TBI: ~7.4-10% risk of PTE
- **Reference**: Annegers JF, Hauser WA, Coan SP, et al. A population-based study of seizures after traumatic brain injuries. New England Journal of Medicine. 1998;338(1):20-24.
  - Population-based cohort study; 4,541 persons with TBI; followed for seizure development
  - Standardized incidence ratios (SIR) for unprovoked seizures compared to general population:
    - Mild TBI: SIR 1.5 (modest increase)
    - Moderate TBI: SIR 2.9
    - Severe TBI: SIR 17.0
  - Risk remained elevated for >10 years after severe TBI
- **Children**: Risk of early seizures higher in young children (especially <2 years) than adults for equivalent injury; impact seizures more common in children; risk of late PTE may be lower in children than adults for equivalent severity

### 8.3 Risk Factors for PTE

- **Severe TBI** (GCS ≤8)
- **Penetrating injury** (highest risk — up to 50%)
- **Intracranial hemorrhage** (subdural, epidural, intraparenchymal)
- **Depressed skull fracture** (especially if depressed >1 cm or dural penetration)
- **Cortical contusion**
- **Prolonged LOC/PTA**
- **Early post-traumatic seizures** (within 7 days) — increase risk of later PTE
- **Young age** (<2 years) for early seizures; however, age effect on late PTE is less clear

### 8.4 Prophylaxis

- **Acute seizure prophylaxis**: Phenytoin (or levetiracetam increasingly used) may be given in first 7 days after severe TBI to prevent early seizures; evidence supports efficacy for early seizures only
- **No evidence** that antiepileptic medications prevent late PTE/epileptogenesis — prophylaxis beyond 7 days NOT recommended
- **Reference**: Temkin NR, Dikmen SS, Wilensky AJ, et al. A randomized, double-blind study of phenytoin for the prevention of post-traumatic seizures. New England Journal of Medicine. 1990;323(8):497-502.

## SECTION 9: SCIWORA (SPINAL CORD INJURY WITHOUT RADIOGRAPHIC ABNORMALITY)

*Note: Covered in more detail in Chapter 7 — brief overview here for completeness in neurotrauma context*

- **Definition**: Spinal cord injury with neurological deficits but NO fracture, dislocation, or ligamentous instability on plain radiographs and CT
- **MRI findings**: May show cord edema, hemorrhage, transection, disc herniation; some cases have normal MRI (true SCIWORA)
- **More common in children** than adults due to pediatric spinal column characteristics (ligamentous laxity, horizontal facet orientation, relatively large head, underdeveloped paraspinal muscles)
- **Age predilection**: Typically <8 years
- **Delayed deficit**: Neurological deterioration may occur up to 48 hours after initial injury; external immobilization recommended even with normal imaging if neurological symptoms reported
- **See Chapter 7 for complete discussion**

## SECTION 10: COGNITIVE REHABILITATION AFTER PEDIATRIC TBI

### 10.1 Evidence Base

- **Growing body of evidence** supporting cognitive rehabilitation in pediatric TBI; however, fewer RCTs than in adult TBI
- **Key domains addressed**: Attention, memory, executive function, processing speed, social cognition, behavioral self-regulation
- **Approaches**: Direct intervention (strategy training, attention process training), compensatory strategies (external aids, organizational systems), metacognitive approaches, technology-assisted interventions, family-centered approaches

### 10.2 Specific Interventions with Evidence

- **Attention training**: Structured attention process training may improve sustained and divided attention; evidence is moderate
- **Memory strategies**: External memory aids (smartphone apps, notebooks, checklists), internal strategies (visual imagery, verbal mediation); evidence supports external aids
- **Executive function training**: Goal management training, problem-solving therapy; online executive function programs (e.g., Amsterdam Memory and Attention Training [Amat-c])
- **Family-based interventions**: Counselor-Assisted Problem-Solving (CAPS) program — online family problem-solving intervention; showed improvements in child behavior and family functioning. Wade SL et al. A randomized trial of teen online problem solving: efficacy in preventing post-concussion syndrome. Pediatric Rehabilitation Medicine. Various publications 2006-2015.
- **Social skills training**: Group-based interventions for social cognitive deficits; evidence limited but promising
- **School-based support**: 504 plans, IEPs (Individualized Education Programs), classroom accommodations

### 10.3 Key Principles

- **Family-centered**: Parents/caregivers are essential partners in rehabilitation
- **Ecologically valid**: Training in real-world settings/tasks preferred over decontextualized drill
- **Developmentally appropriate**: Must account for the child's developmental stage and ongoing brain maturation
- **Long-term follow-up**: Children may "grow into" deficits as demands increase (e.g., executive function demands increase in middle school/high school)
- **Reference**: Laatsch L, Harrington D, Hotz G, et al. An evidence-based review of cognitive and behavioral rehabilitation treatment studies in children with acquired brain injury. Journal of Head Trauma Rehabilitation. 2007;22(4):248-256.
