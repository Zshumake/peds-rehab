## 13.1 Ankle-Foot Orthosis Types and Biomechanics

`[BOARD]`

The ankle-foot orthosis (AFO) is the most commonly prescribed orthosis in pediatric rehabilitation. Approximately 50-80% of children with cerebral palsy use some form of lower extremity orthosis, with AFO usage varying by GMFCS level: roughly 30% at GMFCS I, 60-70% at GMFCS II, and over 80% at GMFCS III and above. Orthotic prescription requires a clear understanding of the biomechanical rationale for each device and the specific gait deviation being addressed.

### 13.1.1 Supramalleolar Orthosis (SMO)

**Design:** The SMO extends just above the malleoli, with trim lines below the level of the malleolar tips. It provides a rigid footplate with medial and lateral walls that encompass the hindfoot and midfoot.

**Biomechanics:** The SMO controls hindfoot valgus and varus by providing mediolateral stability at the subtalar joint. It does not control sagittal plane motion at the ankle and therefore permits dorsiflexion and plantarflexion.

**Indications:**
- Pes planovalgus (pronated foot) --- the most common indication
- Mild hypotonia with foot and ankle instability (commonly seen in Down syndrome)
- GMFCS I-II with isolated foot and ankle alignment issues

> **Clinical Pearl:** The SMO does not control ankle dorsiflexion or plantarflexion. It functions exclusively in the coronal plane at the subtalar joint. Therefore, it is inappropriate for children with equinus, crouch gait, or any sagittal plane gait deviation. When a child requires sagittal plane control, an AFO extending above the ankle is necessary.

### 13.1.2 Posterior Leaf Spring AFO (PLS)

**Design:** The PLS consists of a thin, narrow posterior upright with inherent flexibility at the ankle level and a rigid footplate. The narrow trim lines behind the malleoli allow the posterior strut to flex during push-off.

**Biomechanics:** The PLS provides dorsiflexion assistance during swing phase, preventing foot drop. Its flexibility allows some degree of plantarflexion during terminal stance (third rocker), permitting a more natural push-off pattern. However, it provides minimal control of knee kinematics during stance and offers limited mediolateral stability.

**Indications:**
- Foot drop (drop-foot gait) due to weak dorsiflexors
- Hemiplegic CP with isolated dorsiflexor weakness and adequate quadriceps strength
- GMFCS I-II hemiplegic pattern

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PLS AFO is an excellent orthosis for isolated foot drop but is NOT appropriate for crouch gait. Because the PLS allows ankle dorsiflexion, it cannot generate a knee extension moment and will not correct excessive knee flexion in stance. A child with crouch gait who is placed in a PLS will continue to collapse into the crouch pattern.

### 13.1.3 Solid (Rigid) AFO

`[BOARD]`

**Design:** The solid AFO has a rigid construction with trim lines anterior to the malleoli, blocking all ankle motion. It can be fabricated in a neutral ankle position or with the ankle set in slight dorsiflexion (typically 5 degrees) for biomechanical tuning.

**Biomechanics:** The solid AFO eliminates all ankle motion in the sagittal plane, creating a rigid lever arm during stance. When set in slight dorsiflexion, the solid AFO shifts the ground reaction force (GRF) vector anteriorly relative to the knee, promoting knee extension during stance. This rigidity comes at the cost of eliminating third rocker (heel rise), which may slow gait velocity.

**Indications:**
- Moderate to severe spasticity with equinus
- Poor selective motor control
- Crouch gait (set in slight dorsiflexion)
- Recurvatum (knee hyperextension) gait (set in slight dorsiflexion to prevent excessive anterior tibial progression)
- GMFCS II-IV

**Limitations:** Elimination of third rocker reduces push-off power and may slow gait velocity. The rigidity may also cause increased pressure at the anterior tibial contact point.

### 13.1.4 Hinged AFO

**Design:** The hinged AFO features a rigid frame with a mechanical hinge at the ankle joint. Plantarflexion stops (PF stops) block plantarflexion beyond neutral (90 degrees), while permitting dorsiflexion in stance.

**Biomechanics:** The plantarflexion stop prevents equinus positioning throughout the gait cycle while allowing controlled tibial advancement over the foot in stance (dorsiflexion). By permitting third rocker (heel rise), the hinged AFO allows a more normal gait pattern than the solid AFO. However, allowing dorsiflexion means that the quadriceps must actively control tibial advancement during stance.

**Indications:**
- Equinus gait with adequate quadriceps strength
- Post-botulinum toxin injection (to maintain range while allowing functional motion)
- GMFCS I-II with dynamic equinus but preserved proximal strength

> **Clinical Pearl:** The hinged AFO requires adequate quadriceps strength to control the rate of tibial advancement during stance. If the quadriceps are weak, the child will collapse into excessive dorsiflexion and knee flexion, worsening a crouch pattern. Therefore, the hinged AFO is contraindicated in crouch gait. This is one of the most frequently tested orthotic principles on board examinations.

### 13.1.5 Ground Reaction AFO (GRAFO)

`[BOARD]`

**Design:** The GRAFO (also called floor reaction AFO) is essentially a solid AFO set in approximately 5 degrees of dorsiflexion with the addition of an anterior tibial shell (clamshell design or anterior strap). This anterior component prevents the tibia from advancing forward over the foot.

**Biomechanics:** The GRAFO directs the ground reaction force vector anterior to the knee joint center during stance, creating a knee extension moment. This external extension force compensates for weak quadriceps and counteracts the tendency toward excessive knee flexion.

**Indications:**
- **Crouch gait** --- this is the primary and most important indication
- Weak quadriceps with excessive dorsiflexion during stance
- GMFCS II-III

**Contraindications:**
- Knee flexion contracture exceeding 10-15 degrees (the orthosis cannot generate an extension moment if the knee cannot extend)
- Equinus gait (wrong biomechanical problem)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The GRAFO is THE orthosis for crouch gait. Its mechanism relies on two elements working together: (1) the ankle set in dorsiflexion, which tips the tibia forward, and (2) the anterior tibial shell, which prevents the tibia from collapsing forward. Together, these components redirect the GRF anterior to the knee, generating a passive knee extension moment. When a board question describes a child with spastic diplegia in crouch gait, the answer is almost always GRAFO.

### 13.1.6 Summary Table: AFO Selection

| AFO Type | Controls | Allows | Key Indication | Contraindication |
|----------|---------|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| SMO | Hindfoot valgus/varus | Full ankle ROM | Pronation, mild hypotonia | Any sagittal plane deviation |
| PLS | Foot drop (swing) | PF in stance | Isolated dorsiflexor weakness | Crouch gait |
| Solid AFO | All ankle motion | Nothing | Moderate-severe equinus; crouch | Need for third rocker |
| Hinged AFO | PF (with PF stop) | DF in stance | Dynamic equinus with strong quads | Crouch gait; weak quads |
| GRAFO | DF + PF (anterior shell) | Neither | Crouch gait | Knee flexion contracture >10-15 deg |

## 13.2 AFO Prescription by CP Gait Type

`[BOARD]`

### 13.2.1 Winters-Gage-Hicks Classification (Hemiplegic Gait)

The Winters-Gage-Hicks classification (1987) categorizes hemiplegic gait patterns in children with CP by the extent of sagittal plane involvement. Each type has specific orthotic implications.

| Type | Gait Pattern | Affected Joints | Recommended Orthosis |
|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| I | Drop foot in swing only; normal stance | Ankle (swing phase) | PLS AFO |
| II | True equinus throughout gait cycle | Ankle (stance and swing) | Hinged AFO or Solid AFO |
| III | Equinus + knee recurvatum or stiff knee | Ankle + knee | Solid AFO |
| IV | Equinus + knee + hip + pelvis involvement | Ankle + knee + hip + pelvis | Solid AFO + consider multilevel surgery |

### 13.2.2 Diplegic Gait Patterns

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

| Gait Pattern | Description | Recommended AFO |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| True equinus (Group 1) | Toe-walking without significant knee involvement | PLS or Hinged AFO (with PF stop) |
| Jump gait (Group 2) | Equinus combined with knee flexion and hip flexion; "bouncing" quality | Solid AFO or Hinged AFO |
| Apparent equinus (Group 3) | Knee flexion causes apparent equinus (ankle may be in neutral or DF) | Solid AFO or GRAFO |
| Crouch gait (Group 4) | Excessive dorsiflexion with persistent knee flexion | GRAFO |
| Stiff knee gait | Reduced knee flexion during swing phase | AFO alone insufficient; consider BoNT-A to rectus femoris or distal rectus femoris transfer |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The concept of "AFO tuning" --- optimizing the AFO-footwear combination to achieve an ideal shank-to-vertical angle during stance --- has gained prominence. Owen (2010) demonstrated that the alignment of the shank during stance profoundly affects knee kinematics. A shank inclined too far forward promotes knee flexion (crouch); a shank too vertical promotes knee hyperextension (recurvatum). Heel height in the shoe and AFO ankle angle must be calibrated together.

## 13.3 KAFO and HKAFO Prescription by Spina Bifida Level

`[BOARD]`

Orthotic prescription for children with myelomeningocele is determined by the neurological motor level, which dictates which muscle groups are innervated and what ambulatory potential exists. A critical concept is that the functional level is often one to two levels below the anatomic level due to weakness, orthopedic deformities, obesity, and the high energy cost of walking with limited motor function.

| Neurological Level | Key Innervated Muscles | Orthosis | Expected Ambulatory Status |
|-------------------|----------------------|---------|---------------------------|
| Thoracic | No lower extremity motor function | HKAFO, parapodium, or standing frame | Standing/therapeutic only; wheelchair primary |
| L1-L2 (High lumbar) | Iliopsoas (hip flexion) | KAFO + forearm crutches | Household/exercise ambulation; wheelchair for community |
| L3 | Quadriceps (knee extension) | KAFO + forearm crutches | Household to limited community ambulation |
| L4 | Tibialis anterior (ankle dorsiflexion) | AFO (hinged or solid) + crutches or canes | Community ambulation with devices |
| L5 | Peroneals, partial gluteus medius and hip extensors | AFO (PLS or SMO) | Community ambulation; Trendelenburg gait common |
| S1-S2 | Gastrocnemius-soleus, gluteals | SMO or shoe insert, or none | Community ambulation; mild gait deviations |
| S2-S3 | Foot intrinsics, bladder/bowel sphincters | None or SMO | Normal or near-normal gait; bladder issues persist |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Ambulatory status in spina bifida commonly declines with age. Many children with L3-L4 level lesions who walk with KAFOs during childhood transition to wheelchair use as adolescents and adults because of increasing body mass, declining energy reserves, and joint deterioration. The energy cost of walking with KAFOs and crutches is three to five times normal, which becomes increasingly unsustainable as the child grows. The rehabilitation team should proactively plan for this transition rather than viewing it as a failure.

### 13.3.1 Reciprocating Gait Orthosis (RGO)

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The RGO consists of bilateral HKAFOs linked by a cable mechanism at the hips. Hip flexion on one side mechanically drives hip extension on the contralateral side through the reciprocal cable coupling, enabling a reciprocal walking pattern in patients with no voluntary hip or knee control.

**Indication:** High-level spina bifida (thoracic, L1-L2) or thoracic spinal cord injury in children desiring upright ambulation.

**Limitation:** Despite enabling reciprocal gait, the energy expenditure remains three to five times normal, and most children ultimately transition to wheelchair use for community mobility. The RGO is therefore best understood as a tool for therapeutic standing and exercise ambulation rather than as a primary means of community mobility.

## 13.4 Spinal Orthoses

`[BOARD]`

### 13.4.1 Scoliosis Bracing Fundamentals

**Indication for bracing:** Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) with Cobb angle 25-45 degrees in skeletally immature patients (Risser 0-2). The goal of bracing is to prevent curve progression to the surgical threshold of 50 degrees.

**BrAIST Trial (Weinstein et al., 2013):** This landmark study, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, combined randomized (n=116) and preference (n=126) cohorts for a total of 242 patients. The trial was stopped early for efficacy. Key findings:
- Bracing success rate: 72% (combined cohort) versus 48% with observation alone
- Clear dose-response relationship: wearing the brace more than 12-13 hours per day achieved up to 90% success rates, while fewer than 6 hours per day produced outcomes no better than observation
- This provided the first rigorous RCT-level evidence confirming the efficacy of bracing for AIS

### 13.4.2 Types of Scoliosis Braces

| Orthosis | Type | Design | Indication | Wear Time |
|----------|------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Boston Brace (TLSO) | Prefabricated, modified with pads | Symmetric module with corrective pads | Lumbar/thoracolumbar curves (apex at or below T8) | 16-23 hours/day |
| Wilmington Brace (TLSO) | Custom-molded total contact | CAD/CAM or plaster mold | Similar to Boston; improved fit | 16-23 hours/day |
| Milwaukee Brace (CTLSO) | Pelvic girdle + neck ring + uprights | 1 anterior + 2 posterior uprights with lateral pressure pads | Historically for high thoracic curves (apex above T8) | 23 hours/day |
| Charleston Bending Brace | Nighttime overcorrection | Hypercorrection principle | Single lumbar/TL curves <35 degrees | Nighttime only (8-10 hours) |
| Providence Brace | Nighttime overcorrection | CAD/CAM hypercorrection | Similar to Charleston | Nighttime only |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Milwaukee brace, with its conspicuous neck ring, has been largely replaced by TLSO designs due to poor cosmetic acceptance and compliance. The Boston brace is the most commonly prescribed scoliosis orthosis in North America. Nighttime-only braces (Charleston, Providence) offer an attractive compliance advantage for single moderate curves but are not appropriate for double curves or curves exceeding 35 degrees.

### 13.4.3 Key Bracing Rules

`[BOARD]`

- Curves 25-45 degrees with Risser 0-2: brace
- Curves below 25 degrees: observation only
- Curves above 45-50 degrees: bracing is ineffective; refer for surgical evaluation
- Neuromuscular scoliosis: bracing is less effective long-term and curves often progress despite bracing; earlier surgical fusion may be required
- Neuromuscular scoliosis prevalence: 25-50% in CP (increasing with GMFCS level: 2% at GMFCS I to 75-100% at GMFCS V), over 90% in DMD after loss of ambulation, 60-90% in SMA, 60-90% in thoracic-level spina bifida

## 13.5 Early Power Mobility

`[BOARD]`

### 13.5.1 Rationale and Evidence

The paradigm surrounding power mobility for young children has shifted dramatically over the past two decades. Current evidence supports introducing power mobility as early as 12-18 months of age for children with significant mobility limitations (Butler et al., 1983; Lynch et al., 2009; Kenyon et al., 2021).

**Developmental rationale:** Self-initiated movement drives cognitive, social, and language development through experience-dependent neural mechanisms. When a child cannot move independently, they miss critical opportunities for spatial exploration, cause-and-effect learning, social interaction initiation, and environmental mastery. Restriction of mobility leads to learned helplessness and secondary developmental delays that compound the primary motor disability.

**Candidacy:** Power mobility should be considered for any child with mobility limitations who could benefit from independent exploration. This includes children with CP at GMFCS III-V, spina bifida with thoracic or high lumbar lesions, SMA, and other neuromuscular conditions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Two persistent myths about early power mobility have been definitively refuted by research. First, there is no minimum cognitive level required to learn power mobility. Children with significant intellectual disability can learn to operate a joystick or alternative access switch. Second, early power mobility does not reduce motivation to walk. Studies have consistently shown that power mobility complements rather than replaces walking goals. Mobility should be understood as a means for exploration and participation, not merely a form of transportation.

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 13.5.2 Devices for Young Children

Purpose-built power mobility devices for very young children include the Permobil Explorer Mini (designed for ages 12 months and older) and the Wizzybug (a charity-loaned powered wheelchair in the UK for children 14 months to 5 years). Modified ride-on cars (the Go Baby Go program) provide a low-cost alternative for early mobility exploration, though they lack the adjustability and positioning features of purpose-built devices.

## 13.6 Augmentative and Alternative Communication

`[BOARD]`

### 13.6.1 Overview

Approximately 25-30% of children with cerebral palsy have communication impairments that require augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) support. An estimated two million Americans use some form of AAC. Communication difficulties arise most commonly from dysarthria (in spastic and dyskinetic CP subtypes) but may also reflect language processing deficits associated with intellectual disability.

### 13.6.2 AAC Device Continuum

| Category | Examples | Access Method | Typical Users |
|----------|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Low-tech | Picture boards, communication books, eye-gaze boards | Direct selection, partner-assisted scanning | All levels; backup systems |
| Mid-tech | Single-message devices (BigMack), simple multi-message devices (GoTalk) | Direct selection, switch activation | Young children; those with limited language |
| High-tech | Speech-generating devices (Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life), dedicated devices (Tobii Dynavox) | Touch, eye-gaze, switch scanning, head pointing | Children with complex communication needs |

### 13.6.3 Access Methods

- **Direct selection:** Touch screen, eye gaze, head pointing
- **Switch scanning:** Single or dual switch with visual or auditory scanning; appropriate for children with limited motor access
- **Eye-tracking/eye-gaze:** Rapidly expanding technology for children with severe motor impairment (GMFCS V); enables direct selection through eye movement

> **Clinical Pearl:** AAC is not a "last resort" to be considered only when speech therapy has failed. Current best practice calls for early introduction of AAC alongside speech therapy as part of a multimodal communication approach. Research consistently demonstrates that AAC does not inhibit speech development; rather, it can facilitate verbal language acquisition by reducing communication frustration and providing a scaffold for language learning.

## 13.7 Seating and Positioning

`[BOARD]`

### 13.7.1 The Seating Hierarchy

The fundamental principle of wheelchair seating is that proximal stability enables distal function. The seating assessment and intervention sequence follows a strict bottom-up hierarchy:

1. **PELVIS (Foundation):** The pelvis must be stabilized first. Correct pelvic obliquity, rotation, and posterior tilt. Provide a firm, contoured seat base with ASIS and PSIS alignment.

2. **TRUNK (Second):** Once the pelvis is stabilized, address trunk support. Add lateral trunk supports for trunk lean, anterior trunk support for forward collapse, and posterior support as needed.

3. **HEAD (Last):** Head positioning is addressed only after the pelvis and trunk are stabilized. Options include headrests, suboccipital supports, forehead straps, and circumferential head supports depending on the degree of head control impairment.

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Fix the pelvis, free the hands." This axiom captures the essence of the seating hierarchy. A child whose pelvis is unstable in the wheelchair will use their upper extremities for postural support rather than functional activities. By providing appropriate pelvic stabilization, the hands are liberated for communication, self-care, and environmental interaction.

### 13.7.2 Key Seating Principles

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

- **90-90-90 Rule:** The starting position for seating assessment is 90 degrees at hips, knees, and ankles, modified based on individual needs.
- **Tilt-in-space:** Maintains the seated angle (hip-to-trunk relationship) while tilting the entire system posteriorly. This redistributes pressure without changing the postural alignment and is particularly useful for children who fatigue or for pressure management.
- **Recline:** Extends the hip angle, opening the hip-to-trunk relationship. Differs from tilt-in-space because it changes the postural alignment.
- **Pressure mapping:** Essential for children at risk of pressure injury, particularly those with insensate skin (spina bifida, spinal cord injury).
- **Standing programs:** Integrated into wheelchair systems via standers or standing wheelchairs; promotes weight-bearing, bone mineral density maintenance, bowel function, and psychosocial well-being.

## 13.8 The Gross Motor Function Classification System (GMFCS)

`[BOARD]`

### 13.8.1 Overview and Purpose

The GMFCS, developed by Palisano, Rosenbaum, and colleagues (1997), is a five-level classification system for children with cerebral palsy based on self-initiated movement, with emphasis on sitting, walking, and wheeled mobility. The Expanded and Revised version (GMFCS-E&R, 2008) includes age bands from birth through 18 years.

**Critical distinction:** The GMFCS is a CLASSIFICATION system --- it describes and predicts a child's functional mobility level. It is NOT an evaluative outcome measure and cannot be used to detect change over time in response to intervention.

| Level | Description (Ages 6-12 Years) | Ambulatory Status |
|-------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| I | Walks without limitations; limitations in advanced motor skills | Community ambulator; no gait aids |
| II | Walks with limitations; difficulty with uneven surfaces and stairs without railing | Community ambulator; may use handheld device for long distances |
| III | Walks using handheld mobility device indoors and outdoors | Community ambulator with devices; may use wheelchair for long distances |
| IV | Self-mobility with limitations; may use powered mobility | Primarily wheelchair user; may walk short distances with walker and assistance |
| V | Transported in manual wheelchair | Dependent for all mobility and transfers |

### 13.8.2 Key Board Pearls

- GMFCS level is **stable over time** and does not change with intervention. A child classified as GMFCS III at age 2 will remain GMFCS III throughout life (Hanna et al., 2009).
- GMFCS level by age 2 years is predictive of adult mobility.
- GMFCS I-III: likely community ambulators.
- GMFCS IV-V: primarily wheelchair users.
- GMFCS level predicts orthotic needs, surgical timing, hip surveillance intervals, and scoliosis risk.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The stability of the GMFCS is its defining feature and its primary source of clinical utility. Because GMFCS level does not change, it serves as a powerful prognostic tool for counseling families about long-term mobility expectations. The temptation to reclassify a child to a lower (better) GMFCS level after a successful intervention reflects a misunderstanding of the system. The GMFM-66, not the GMFCS, is the appropriate tool for detecting functional change.

## 13.9 Gross Motor Function Measure (GMFM-66 and GMFM-88)

`[BOARD]`

### 13.9.1 GMFM-88

The GMFM-88 is a standardized observational measure containing 88 items across five dimensions:

| Dimension | Content | Number of Items |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| A | Lying and Rolling | 17 |
| B | Sitting | 20 |
| C | Crawling and Kneeling | 14 |
| D | Standing | 13 |
| E | Walking, Running, and Jumping | 24 |

Each item is scored on a four-point ordinal scale: 0 (does not initiate), 1 (initiates), 2 (partially completes), and 3 (completes). Dimension scores are expressed as a percentage of the maximum possible score.

### 13.9.2 GMFM-66

The GMFM-66 was derived from the GMFM-88 through Rasch analysis (Russell et al., 2000), selecting 66 items that form a unidimensional, interval-level scale. This transformation is clinically significant because it enables meaningful comparison of change scores --- a two-point improvement on the GMFM-66 represents the same magnitude of change regardless of where on the scale it occurs.

**Key features:**
- Interval-level measurement (unlike the ordinal GMFM-88)
- Score range: 0-100
- Administered via the Gross Motor Ability Estimator (GMAE) computer software
- Can be computed from a subset of items
- Preferred for research because of its superior psychometric properties

### 13.9.3 Motor Growth Curves

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Hanna et al. (2009) published motor development trajectory curves by GMFCS level, providing essential prognostic information:

| GMFCS Level | Plateau GMFM-66 Score | Age at Plateau |
|-------------|----------------------|----------------|
| I | ~90-100 | 5-6 years |
| II | ~70-80 | 5-7 years |
| III | ~50-60 | 5-7 years |
| IV | ~30-40 | 4-6 years |
| V | ~15-25 | 3-5 years |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Children at GMFCS levels IV and V may show a DECLINE in gross motor function after ages 7-8 as their body outgrows their motor capacity. This decline is an expected trajectory, not a treatment failure, and should be anticipated in goal-setting and family counseling. The motor growth curves allow clinicians to set realistic expectations: a child at GMFCS III who has reached a GMFM-66 score of 58 at age 7 is at or near their developmental ceiling for gross motor function.

## 13.10 WeeFIM (Functional Independence Measure for Children)

`[BOARD]`

The WeeFIM (Msall et al., 1994) measures the burden of care and functional independence in children. It was derived from the adult FIM and is validated for children with a developmental age of 6 months to 7 years, though it can be applied to older children and adolescents with disabilities.

**Structure: 18 items across 3 domains:**

| Domain | Items | Number |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Self-care | Eating, grooming, bathing, dressing upper body, dressing lower body, toileting, bladder management, bowel management | 8 |
| Mobility | Transfers (chair/wheelchair, toilet, tub/shower), locomotion (walk/wheelchair), stairs | 5 |
| Cognition | Comprehension, expression, social interaction, problem solving, memory | 5 |

**Scoring: 7-point ordinal scale per item:**

| Score | Level of Independence |
|-------|----------------------|
| 7 | Complete independence |
| 6 | Modified independence (device, extra time) |
| 5 | Supervision or setup |
| 4 | Minimal assistance (patient performs 75%+) |
| 3 | Moderate assistance (patient performs 50%+) |
| 2 | Maximal assistance (patient performs 25%+) |
| 1 | Total assistance (patient performs <25%) |

**Score range:** 18 (total dependence) to 126 (complete independence).

> **Clinical Pearl:** The WeeFIM is fundamentally a measure of caregiver burden rather than capability. A child may be capable of performing a task (as measured by the PEDI Functional Skills scale) but still require significant caregiver assistance to actually perform it in daily life (as measured by the WeeFIM). This distinction between capability and performance is clinically important and frequently tested.

## 13.11 PEDI and PEDI-CAT

`[BOARD]`

### 13.11.1 PEDI (Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory)

The original PEDI (Haley et al., 1992) was designed for children with a developmental age of 6 months to 7.5 years.

**Three domains:**
- Self-care (73 items)
- Mobility (59 items)
- Social function (65 items)

**Two scales per domain:**
1. **Functional Skills (Capability):** What the child CAN do. Items scored dichotomously (0 = unable, 1 = capable).
2. **Caregiver Assistance (Performance):** How much help the child actually needs. Scored on a 6-point scale from 5 (independent) to 0 (total assistance).

A third scale measures the amount of equipment or environmental modification required.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PEDI uniquely measures both capability and performance within the same instrument. A child may demonstrate capability on the Functional Skills scale (they can perform the task in a structured assessment) while still requiring substantial caregiver assistance on the Caregiver Assistance scale (they do not consistently perform the task at home). This dual measurement aligns with the ICF distinction between capacity and performance.

### 13.11.2 PEDI-CAT

The PEDI-CAT (Haley et al., 2012) is a computer adaptive test version with several important advances over the original PEDI:

| Feature | PEDI | PEDI-CAT |
|---------|------|----------|
| Age range | 6 months - 7.5 years | Birth - 20 years |
| Administration | 197 items (lengthy) | ~15-20 items per domain (via CAT algorithm) |
| Domains | 3 (Self-care, Mobility, Social function) | 4 (Daily Activities, Mobility, Social/Cognitive, **Responsibility**) |
| Scoring | Normative and scaled scores | Item response theory; scaled scores 20-80 |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PEDI-CAT's Responsibility domain is a unique addition that measures the child's role in managing their own life tasks --- a concept aligned with self-determination and transition readiness. This domain assesses not just whether the child can perform a task, but the extent to which the child takes responsibility for initiating, organizing, and managing the task independently.

## 13.12 Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development, 4th Edition (Bayley-4)

`[BOARD]`

The Bayley-4 (Bayley and Aylward, 2019) is the gold standard diagnostic assessment for infant and toddler development, validated for ages 1-42 months.

**Five scales:**

| Scale | Administration | Scoring |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| Cognitive | Direct testing (play-based) | Composite: mean 100, SD 15 |
| Language (Receptive + Expressive) | Direct testing | Composite: mean 100, SD 15 |
| Motor (Fine + Gross) | Direct testing | Composite: mean 100, SD 15 |
| Social-Emotional | Caregiver questionnaire | Composite: mean 100, SD 15 |
| Adaptive Behavior | Caregiver questionnaire | Composite: mean 100, SD 15 |

**Interpretation:**
- Composite score below 70 (more than 2 SD below mean): significant developmental delay
- Composite score 70-84 (1-2 SD below mean): mild delay or at risk
- Updated from Bayley-III (2006) with improved normative data

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Bayley-4 is a DIAGNOSTIC measure, not a screening tool. It provides norm-referenced scores that identify the presence and severity of developmental delay. It is used extensively in high-risk infant follow-up programs (NICU graduates, premature infants) and as the outcome measure of choice in early intervention research. The Denver II and ASQ-3, by contrast, are screening tools that identify children who need further diagnostic evaluation.

---

## 13.13 Denver Developmental Screening Test II (Denver II)

`[BOARD]`

The Denver II (Frankenburg et al., 1992) is a clinician-administered screening tool for children from birth to 6 years.

**Four domains:**
1. Personal-Social
2. Fine Motor-Adaptive
3. Language
4. Gross Motor

The test contains 125 items displayed on a single-page form with age-based norms. Each item is rated as Pass, Fail, No Opportunity, or Refusal.

**Interpretation:**
- **Normal:** No delays and at most one caution
- **Suspect:** Two or more cautions and/or one or more delays
- **Untestable:** Excessive refusals prevent interpretation

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Denver II has high sensitivity but limited specificity, resulting in a significant false-positive rate. A "suspect" Denver II result should always be followed by a diagnostic evaluation (such as the Bayley-4), not used as the sole basis for a developmental delay diagnosis. The four domains of the Denver II are a classic board examination question.

---

## 13.14 Ages and Stages Questionnaire, 3rd Edition (ASQ-3)

`[BOARD]`

The ASQ-3 (Squires and Bricker, 2009) is a parent-completed screening tool covering ages 1-66 months through 21 age-specific questionnaires.

**Five domains:**
- Communication
- Gross Motor
- Fine Motor
- Problem Solving
- Personal-Social

Each questionnaire contains 30 items (6 per domain), scored as Yes (10 points), Sometimes (5 points), or Not Yet (0 points). Domain scores are compared to established cutoff values; scores below cutoff trigger referral for further evaluation.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ASQ-3 is parent-completed, making it far more practical for population-level screening than the Denver II, which requires clinician administration. The ASQ-3 is widely used in primary care practices and early intervention programs. The companion ASQ:SE-2 screens specifically for social-emotional development and behavioral concerns.

---

## 13.15 Modified Ashworth Scale and Tardieu Scale

`[BOARD]`

These scales are discussed in detail in Chapter 12 (Sections 12.8.1 and 12.8.2). Key distinctions for the outcome measures chapter:

| Feature | Modified Ashworth Scale | Modified Tardieu Scale |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Measures | Resistance to passive stretch | Angle of catch (R1) and passive ROM (R2) |
| Velocity-dependent | No (as typically performed) | Yes |
| Distinguishes spasticity from contracture | No | Yes (R2-R1 dynamic component) |
| Reliability | Moderate; limited interrater reliability | Superior for distinguishing tone types |
| Clinical utility | Most commonly used; quick | Better for treatment planning |

## 13.16 Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS)

`[BOARD]`

Goal Attainment Scaling (Kiresuk and Sherman, 1968) is an individualized outcome measure that allows clinicians and families to define specific, measurable goals with five possible outcome levels.

**Scale structure:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| -2 | Much less than expected outcome |
| -1 | Less than expected outcome |
| 0 | Expected outcome (goal achieved) |
| +1 | More than expected outcome |
| +2 | Much more than expected outcome |

The overall GAS T-score has a mean of 50 and standard deviation of 10, enabling comparison of goal achievement across different patients with different goals.

> **Clinical Pearl:** GAS is particularly valuable in botulinum toxin outcome studies because it captures individualized, functional goals (e.g., improved shoe clearance during swing, easier hand hygiene for a caregiver) that standardized measures may not detect. Its strength lies in its sensitivity to clinically meaningful change that matters to the family. Its weakness is that goal-setting quality varies with clinical expertise, potentially introducing bias.

---

## 13.17 Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM)

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The COPM (Law et al., 1990) is a client-centered, semi-structured interview that identifies the child's and family's priorities across three performance areas: self-care, productivity, and leisure. The client rates the importance of each identified problem (1-10), then rates their current performance (1-10) and satisfaction (1-10) for the most important problems.

**Clinically significant change:** A change of 2 or more points on performance or satisfaction scores is considered clinically meaningful.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The COPM operationalizes the ICF concept of participation by centering the assessment on activities that are meaningful to the child and family. It does not impose predetermined goals; rather, it elicits goals from the family's perspective. This alignment with family-centered care principles makes the COPM particularly valuable in pediatric rehabilitation, where the family's priorities should drive the intervention plan.

---

## 13.18 Comparison of Outcome Measures

`[BOARD]`

| Measure | Type | Age Range | What It Measures | Key Feature |
|---------|------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| GMFCS | Classification | 0-18 years | Gross motor level | Stable; predictive; NOT evaluative |
| GMFM-66 | Evaluative | CP (any age) | Gross motor function change | Interval-level; detects change over time |
| WeeFIM | Evaluative | 6 months-7 years (dev age) | Functional independence / burden of care | 18 items, 7-point scale, 3 domains |
| PEDI | Evaluative | 6 months-7.5 years (dev age) | Capability AND performance | Dual scales per domain |
| PEDI-CAT | Evaluative | Birth-20 years | Capability, performance, responsibility | CAT algorithm; 4 domains |
| Bayley-4 | Diagnostic | 1-42 months | Developmental level | Norm-referenced; 5 scales; NOT a screener |
| Denver II | Screening | Birth-6 years | Developmental delay risk | Clinician-administered; 4 domains |
| ASQ-3 | Screening | 1-66 months | Developmental delay risk | Parent-completed; 5 domains |
| MAS | Evaluative | Any age | Resistance to passive stretch | Does NOT distinguish spasticity from contracture |
| Tardieu | Evaluative | Any age | Spastic component vs contracture | R2-R1 guides treatment decisions |
| GAS | Evaluative | Any age | Individualized goal achievement | T-score: mean 50, SD 10 |
| COPM | Evaluative | Any age | Client-centered performance & satisfaction | Family identifies priorities |
