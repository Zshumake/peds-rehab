## [BOARD] Key Concepts

### Pediatric Burn Epidemiology

Burns are a leading cause of injury-related morbidity in children. Approximately 100,000 children seek burn treatment annually in the United States. Mortality has declined to less than 3% for hospitalized pediatric burns owing to advances in critical care, though inhalation injury remains associated with 40% to 60% of burn deaths.

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Peak age | 1 to 5 years (toddlers and preschoolers) |
| Leading mechanism (under 5 years) | Scald burns (60% to 80% of pediatric burns) |
| Leading mechanism (over 5 years) | Flame burns |
| Contact burns | Third most common; hot irons, stoves, curling irons |
| Non-accidental trauma (NAT) | 10% to 20% of pediatric burns |

Scald burns from hot liquids (bathwater, spilled beverages, cooking) overwhelmingly predominate in young children due to their exploratory behavior, short stature, and limited ability to escape a heat source. In older children and adolescents, flame burns become the leading mechanism (Smolle et al., 2017).

### Burn Surface Area Estimation

#### Modified Rule of Nines

The Wallace Rule of Nines, designed for adult body proportions, must be modified for children because the pediatric head is proportionally larger and the legs are proportionally smaller.

| Body Region | Adult | Child | Infant |
|-------------|-------|-------|--------|
| Head and neck | 9% | 18% | 18% |
| Each upper extremity | 9% | 9% | 9% |
| Anterior trunk | 18% | 18% | 18% |
| Posterior trunk | 18% | 18% | 18% |
| Each lower extremity | 18% | 14% | 14% |
| Perineum/genitalia | 1% | 1% | 1% |

> **Clinical Pearl:** In children, the head accounts for 18% TBSA (versus 9% in adults), and each leg accounts for 14% (versus 18% in adults). The head loses approximately 1% TBSA per year of age from 18% at birth toward 7% in adults, with the legs gaining proportionally. The palmar method (the patient's palm including fingers equals approximately 1% TBSA) is useful for estimating scattered or small burns.

#### Lund-Browder Chart

The Lund-Browder chart is the gold standard for pediatric TBSA estimation because it provides age-specific percentages that account for the changing body proportions as children grow (Lund and Browder, 1944).

| Body Area | 0-1 yr | 1-4 yr | 5-9 yr | 10-14 yr | 15+ yr / Adult |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|----------|----------------|
| Head (total) | 19% | 17% | 13% | 11% | 7% |
| Neck | 2% | 2% | 2% | 2% | 2% |
| Anterior trunk | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% |
| Posterior trunk | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% |
| Each buttock | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% |
| Genitalia | 1% | 1% | 1% | 1% | 1% |
| Each upper arm | 4% | 4% | 4% | 4% | 4% |
| Each forearm | 3% | 3% | 3% | 3% | 3% |
| Each hand | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% |
| Each thigh | 5.5% | 6.5% | 8% | 8.5% | 9.5% |
| Each lower leg | 5% | 5% | 5.5% | 6% | 7% |
| Each foot | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Superficial (first-degree) burns are NOT counted in TBSA calculations for fluid resuscitation. Only partial-thickness and full-thickness burns are included.

### Burn Depth Classification

| Classification | Former Term | Depth | Appearance | Sensation | Healing Time | Scarring |
|---------------|-------------|-------|------------|-----------|--------------|----------|
| Superficial | First degree | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters (sunburn-like) | Painful | 3-7 days | No scarring |
| Superficial partial-thickness | Superficial second degree | Epidermis + papillary dermis | Pink, moist, blisters, blanches with pressure | Very painful | 7-14 days | Minimal |
| Deep partial-thickness | Deep second degree | Epidermis + reticular dermis | Pale, mottled, less moisture, may blister | Decreased | 14-35 days | Significant; may need grafting |
| Full-thickness | Third degree | Entire epidermis and dermis into subcutaneous fat | White, waxy, leathery, dry eschar | Insensate | Will NOT heal spontaneously | Requires excision and grafting |
| Fourth degree (subdermal) | Fourth degree | Through skin into muscle, bone, or tendon | Charred, exposed deep structures | Insensate | Requires excision; amputation may be needed | N/A |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Pain level inversely correlates with burn depth. Superficial partial-thickness burns are the most painful because intact nerve endings in the papillary dermis are exposed and stimulated. Full-thickness burns are insensate because nerve endings have been destroyed. This inverse relationship is a frequently tested board concept.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Burns that take longer than 14 to 21 days to heal have significantly higher risk of hypertrophic scarring. Deep partial-thickness and full-thickness burns therefore carry the greatest scarring risk.

### Anti-Deformity Positioning: COMFORT = CONTRACTURE

The central principle of burn rehabilitation positioning is that the position of comfort is the position of contracture. Burned skin and underlying tissue undergo contraction during healing, and the body naturally assumes flexed, adducted positions that minimize tension on healing wounds. If left in these comfort positions, permanent contractures develop. Anti-deformity positioning places the burned area in the opposite direction of the expected contracture to maintain tissue length (Schneider et al., 2006; Serghiou et al., 2009).

| Body Area | Expected Contracture | Anti-Deformity Position |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Neck (anterior burn)** | Flexion | Extension — no pillow; towel roll under shoulders |
| **Neck (posterior burn)** | Extension | Slight flexion |
| **Neck (lateral burn)** | Lateral flexion toward burn | Lateral flexion AWAY from burn |
| **Axilla/Shoulder** | Adduction | Abduction to 90 degrees; airplane splint |
| **Elbow (anterior burn)** | Flexion | Extension |
| **Elbow (posterior burn)** | Extension | Flexion 30 to 40 degrees |
| **Wrist (volar burn)** | Flexion | Extension 20 to 30 degrees |
| **Hand (dorsal burn)** | MCP hyperextension, IP flexion, thumb adduction ("claw hand") | "Safe position" / "Intrinsic plus": MCPs flexed 70 to 90 degrees, IPs extended, thumb abducted and opposed |
| **Hip (anterior burn)** | Flexion | Extension — prone positioning encouraged; neutral rotation |
| **Knee (posterior burn)** | Flexion | Extension |
| **Ankle (anterior burn)** | Dorsiflexion | Neutral 90 degrees |
| **Ankle (posterior/plantar burn)** | Plantarflexion | Neutral 90 degrees; posterior splint or AFO |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The dorsal hand burn positioning deserves special attention. The dorsal hand burn leads to the "claw hand" deformity (MCP hyperextension, IP flexion, thumb adduction) because the thin dorsal skin contracts and the MCP collateral ligaments shorten in extension. The anti-deformity "safe position" or "intrinsic plus" position maintains MCPs in 70 to 90 degrees of flexion (keeping collateral ligaments at maximum length), IPs in extension, and the thumb in abduction and opposition.

### Burn Rehabilitation: Comprehensive Approach

**Early Mobilization:**
Active range of motion and stretching should begin within 24 to 48 hours of admission and continue throughout recovery. Early mobilization prevents the deconditioning, contracture, and joint stiffness that develop rapidly in immobilized burn patients.

**Splinting:**
Splints maintain anti-deformity positions when the patient is resting or sleeping. They are fabricated from thermoplastic materials and adjusted as wound healing progresses. Splinting is removed several times daily for active exercise.

**Pressure Garment Therapy:**
Custom pressure garments delivering 23 to 25 mmHg of pressure are worn 23 hours per day for 12 to 18 months or until scar maturation. Evidence supports their effectiveness in reducing scar thickness, though effects on color and pliability are less clear (Engrav et al., 2010). Scar maturation is indicated by a scar that has become flat, soft, and pale.

**Silicone Therapy:**
Silicone sheeting or gel applied to healing scars improves pliability and thickness. The mechanism is debated but likely involves sustained hydration of the stratum corneum, which modulates collagen synthesis and cytokine expression.

**Exercise and Conditioning:**
Structured exercise programs during burn recovery improve lean body mass and muscle strength. Benefits are maintained only with ongoing participation; they are lost when programs are discontinued (Suman and Herndon, 2020).

**Scar Assessment:**
The Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS) is the most widely used tool, scoring pigmentation (0-3), vascularity (0-3), pliability (0-5), and height (0-3) for a total of 0 to 14 (higher = worse scar). The Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale (POSAS) is more comprehensive, incorporating the patient's perspective on pain, pruritus, color, stiffness, thickness, and irregularity.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hypertrophic scars differ from keloid scars. Hypertrophic scars remain within the boundaries of the original wound and tend to regress over time. Keloid scars extend beyond wound boundaries and do not regress. Hypertrophic scarring is more common after deep partial-thickness and full-thickness burns, particularly when healing takes longer than 14 to 21 days. Z-plasty is the surgical technique used to release linear contracture bands by redirecting tension and increasing length along the line of contracture.

### Non-Accidental Trauma (NAT) Burns

Approximately 10% to 20% of pediatric burns result from child abuse. The clinician must maintain a high index of suspicion and recognize patterns inconsistent with accidental injury.

| Indicator | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Stocking/glove distribution** | Symmetric, circumferential burns of hands or feet with clear demarcation lines, indicating forced immersion |
| **Sparing of flexion creases** | Child reflexively curls body during forced immersion; palms, soles, antecubital, and popliteal fossae are spared |
| **Uniform burn depth** | Suggests prolonged, forced contact with a single heat source |
| **Doughnut sign** | Central sparing of the buttocks or perineum in forced immersion (skin contacts the cool bottom of the tub) |
| **Dorsal hand burns** | Children reflexively withdraw the hand in a fisting pattern; dorsal burns suggest the hand was forced onto a hot surface |
| **Cigarette burns** | Circular, deep, uniform burns, often multiple |
| **Delay in seeking care** | More than 2 hours suggests NAT |
| **Inconsistent history** | Stated mechanism does not match burn pattern or child's developmental capability |
| **Burns at different stages of healing** | Indicates repeated injury over time |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The doughnut sign is pathognomonic for forced immersion burns. When a child is forcibly held in hot water in a seated position, the buttocks press against the cooler bottom of the tub, creating a central zone of sparing surrounded by deep burns. This pattern cannot be explained by accidental immersion.

### ABA Burn Center Referral Criteria

The American Burn Association (2018 revision) criteria for burn center referral include:

1. Partial-thickness burns greater than 10% TBSA
2. Burns involving the face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints
3. Full-thickness burns of any size
4. Electrical burns (including lightning)
5. Chemical burns
6. Inhalation injury
7. Burns in patients with complicating preexisting conditions
8. Burns with concomitant trauma (when the burn represents the greater risk)
9. Burns in children at facilities without pediatric capabilities
10. Patients requiring special rehabilitation or psychosocial intervention, including suspected NAT

### Fluid Resuscitation

While detailed critical care management is beyond the scope of rehabilitation medicine, the Parkland formula is frequently tested:

- **Parkland formula:** 4 mL/kg/% TBSA of lactated Ringer's for the first 24 hours (half administered in the first 8 hours from the time of burn, the remainder over the next 16 hours)
- **Pediatric modification:** Children under 20 kg require maintenance fluids with dextrose (D5 with appropriate electrolytes) added to the Parkland formula because of their limited glycogen stores and risk of hypoglycemia
- **Goal urine output:** 1 to 2 mL/kg/hr for children under 30 kg; 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr for children over 30 kg

## Pediatric Cancer Epidemiology

Approximately 16,000 new pediatric cancer cases are diagnosed annually in the United States (ages 0 to 19). The overall 5-year survival rate has improved dramatically from less than 60% in the 1970s to approximately 85% today, making survivorship and late effects increasingly important considerations.

| Cancer Type | Key Epidemiologic Facts |
|-------------|------------------------|
| **Leukemia** | Most common pediatric malignancy overall (~30% of childhood cancers) |
| **ALL** | ~80% of pediatric leukemias, ~25% of all childhood cancers; peak age 2-5 yr; 5-yr survival ~90% |
| **AML** | ~20% of pediatric leukemias; 5-yr survival ~65-70% |
| **CNS tumors** | Most common solid tumor (~25% of pediatric cancers) |
| **Neuroblastoma** | Most common extracranial solid tumor |
| **Osteosarcoma** | Most common primary bone tumor |
| **Ewing sarcoma** | Second most common bone tumor |
| **Wilms tumor** | Most common renal tumor in children; peak age 3-4 yr; 5-yr survival ~90% |
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma** | Most common soft tissue sarcoma in children |

---

### Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

ALL is the most curable childhood cancer, with 5-year survival rates approaching 90%. Treatment spans 2 to 3 years and consists of induction, consolidation, and maintenance phases, with intrathecal chemotherapy for CNS prophylaxis (Dixon et al., 2020).

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Cell type | B-cell precursor (85%); T-cell (15%) |
| Favorable prognosis | Age 1-9 years, WBC less than 50,000, B-cell, hyperdiploidy (>50 chromosomes), t(12;21)/TEL-AML1 |
| Unfavorable prognosis | Age less than 1 or greater than 10, WBC greater than 50,000, T-cell, MLL rearrangements, t(9;22)/Philadelphia chromosome, hypodiploidy |

**Rehabilitation-Relevant Treatment Effects:**

| Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Steroid myopathy | Dexamethasone/prednisone cause proximal weakness and functional decline during treatment |
| AVN (osteonecrosis) | Corticosteroid-related; affects hips and knees; a late effect |
| Vincristine neuropathy | Peripheral sensorimotor neuropathy; foot drop; dose-limiting toxicity |
| Methotrexate neurotoxicity | Leukoencephalopathy (especially with intrathecal or high-dose IV); executive function deficits |
| Obesity | Common late effect of ALL treatment |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Vincristine-induced peripheral neuropathy is the most important rehabilitation-relevant side effect during ALL treatment. It presents as distal sensorimotor neuropathy with weakness (foot drop is characteristic), numbness, and pain. It is typically dose-limiting, meaning the chemotherapy dose must be reduced or held if neuropathy becomes severe.

---

### Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML)

AML accounts for approximately 20% of pediatric leukemias and has a 5-year survival of 65% to 70%.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Auer rods — needle-shaped crystalline inclusions visible in blast cell cytoplasm — are pathognomonic for AML. They are especially prominent in acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL/M3), which carries a unique treatment approach [ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid) plus arsenic trioxide] and a unique risk of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).

Children with Down syndrome have a 10- to 20-fold increased risk of AML, particularly acute megakaryoblastic leukemia (AMKL), and generally respond well to treatment.

### Bone Tumors: Osteosarcoma Versus Ewing Sarcoma

The distinction between osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma is one of the most frequently tested bone tumor concepts in board examinations.

| Feature | Osteosarcoma | Ewing Sarcoma |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Location in bone** | Metaphysis | Diaphysis |
| **Bone preference** | Long bones around the knee (distal femur #1, proximal tibia #2, proximal humerus #3) | Long bones + flat bones (pelvis, ribs, scapula) |
| **Peak age** | 10-25 years (adolescent growth spurt) | 5-25 years (peak 10-15) |
| **Radiographic pattern** | "Sunburst" pattern; Codman triangle (periosteal elevation) | "Onion skin" periosteal reaction; permeative/moth-eaten |
| **Histology** | Malignant osteoid production by tumor cells | Small round blue cell tumor |
| **Molecular** | Complex karyotype; RB1 and TP53 mutations | t(11;22)(q24;q12) EWSR1-FLI1 fusion in ~85% |
| **Metastasis** | Lung (#1 site); 15-20% metastatic at diagnosis | Lung and bone; 20-25% metastatic at diagnosis |
| **Radiosensitivity** | No — radiation has limited role | Yes — radiation used for local control |
| **Treatment** | Neoadjuvant chemo, wide surgical resection, adjuvant chemo | Chemo + surgery and/or radiation |
| **5-year survival (localized)** | ~70% | ~70% |
| **5-year survival (metastatic)** | ~20-30% | ~20-30% |
| **Race** | All races | Rare in African Americans |
| **Risk factors** | Prior radiation, hereditary retinoblastoma (RB1), Li-Fraumeni (TP53), Paget disease | None well-established |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Osteosarcoma = METAPHYSIS; Ewing sarcoma = DIAPHYSIS. Both tumors commonly involve the femur, but in different anatomic regions of the bone. This is the single most important distinguishing feature for board purposes.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Ewing sarcoma is radiosensitive, while osteosarcoma is not. This fundamental difference affects local control strategies: Ewing sarcoma can be treated with radiation when surgical resection is not feasible (such as sacral or pelvic locations), while osteosarcoma requires wide surgical resection for local control.

**Bone Tumor Location Map:**

| Tumor | Location Within Bone | Typical Sites |
|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| Osteosarcoma | Metaphysis | Distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus |
| Ewing sarcoma | Diaphysis (and flat bones) | Femur > pelvis > ribs > tibia |
| Osteochondroma | Metaphysis (growing away from joint) | Distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus |
| Giant cell tumor | Epiphysis (extends to subchondral bone) | Distal femur > proximal tibia > distal radius |
| Chondroblastoma | Epiphysis | Proximal humerus > distal femur > proximal tibia |
| Aneurysmal bone cyst | Metaphysis (eccentric) | Posterior elements of spine; long bones |
| Unicameral bone cyst | Metaphysis (central) | Proximal humerus > proximal femur |
| Eosinophilic granuloma (LCH) | Diaphysis | Skull > femur > spine |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Giant cell tumor and chondroblastoma are the epiphyseal bone tumors. This is frequently tested as a "what tumor occurs in the epiphysis?" question. Both are benign but locally aggressive.

### Van Ness Rotationplasty

Van Ness rotationplasty is a limb-salvage procedure used for malignant bone tumors around the knee in skeletally immature patients. The technique involves resecting the distal femur and proximal tibia with the tumor, rotating the remaining distal limb 180 degrees, and reattaching it so that the ankle joint functions as a biological knee joint (Hillmann et al., 2000).

**Key Functional Principles:**
- The ankle plantarflexors (gastrocnemius-soleus) function as "knee extensors" because the limb has been rotated 180 degrees
- The result is a functional below-knee amputation level, which is biomechanically superior to an above-knee amputation
- A prosthesis attaches to the rotated foot
- Advantages include preservation of a biological joint, growth potential in skeletally immature patients, and better prosthetic function and energy expenditure compared with above-knee amputation with endoprosthesis

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Van Ness rotationplasty converts what would be an above-knee amputation into a functional below-knee amputation by using the ankle as a knee joint. The key rehabilitation concept is that ankle plantarflexors become knee extensors. Gait analysis studies demonstrate functional outcomes comparable to above-knee amputation with endoprosthesis, with the advantage of a biological joint and preserved growth.

### Cancer-Related Fatigue

Cancer-related fatigue is the most common symptom reported by 50% to 90% of pediatric cancer patients during treatment. It is multifactorial, arising from the disease process itself, chemotherapy and radiation effects, anemia, physical deconditioning, sleep disruption, and psychosocial distress.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Exercise is the most effective intervention for cancer-related fatigue in both adults and children. Supervised aerobic and resistance training programs are safe during active cancer treatment and improve fitness, reduce fatigue, and enhance quality of life. Energy conservation techniques (pacing, prioritization, sleep hygiene) complement exercise. Fatigue persists into survivorship in 20% to 30% of survivors years after treatment completion.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### Burn Pathophysiology and Wound Healing

A burn wound consists of three concentric zones described by Jackson (1953):

1. **Zone of coagulation:** The center of the wound, where tissue has been irreversibly destroyed by thermal injury. Protein denaturation and cell death are complete.
2. **Zone of stasis:** Surrounds the zone of coagulation. Tissue is injured but potentially viable. Impaired blood flow and edema threaten tissue survival. This is the zone where clinical interventions (adequate fluid resuscitation, prevention of infection, avoidance of hypotension) can make the greatest difference in final wound outcome.
3. **Zone of hyperemia:** The outermost zone, with increased blood flow and inflammation. This tissue typically recovers fully.

The clinical importance of Jackson's zones lies in the concept of wound conversion: inadequate management of the zone of stasis (through poor perfusion, infection, or desiccation) can convert potentially viable tissue into full-thickness necrosis, deepening the wound.

### Burn Rehabilitation Across Phases of Care

**Acute Phase (ICU/Inpatient):**
Rehabilitation begins from the moment of admission. Positioning, splinting, and gentle active ROM are initiated within 24 to 48 hours. Pain management is essential to enable participation in therapy. Interdisciplinary communication with the surgical burn team ensures that exercise and mobilization do not compromise grafts.

After skin grafting, the grafted area is typically immobilized for 3 to 5 days to allow graft adherence, after which gentle ROM is resumed. Donor sites heal as superficial partial-thickness wounds and may be more painful than the graft sites.

**Subacute/Outpatient Phase:**
Once wounds are closed, the focus shifts to scar management and functional restoration. Pressure garments are fitted when wounds are sufficiently healed to tolerate them. Silicone sheeting is applied under pressure garments. Progressive strengthening and aerobic conditioning address the substantial deconditioning that occurs during prolonged hospitalization.

**Long-Term/Reconstructive Phase:**
Scar maturation continues for 12 to 18 months (and sometimes up to 2 years). Surgical revision of contractures, including Z-plasty, W-plasty, tissue expansion, and free flap reconstruction, may be needed once scars have matured. Ongoing psychological support addresses body image concerns, post-traumatic stress, and social reintegration, all of which are particularly significant in children and adolescents.

### Pediatric-Specific Burn Considerations

Children present unique challenges in burn rehabilitation:
- **Growth-related contracture recurrence:** Even after successful release, contractures may recur as the child grows because scar tissue does not grow proportionally with the surrounding skin. Serial release procedures may be necessary through the growth period.
- **Compliance challenges:** Young children cannot understand the rationale for uncomfortable splints and pressure garments. Family education and creative strategies to improve adherence are essential.
- **Developmental impact:** Burns during critical developmental periods can delay motor milestones, cause school absenteeism, and create lasting psychosocial sequelae.
- **Thermoregulation:** Large burns impair thermoregulatory capacity due to loss of sweat glands in grafted and scarred skin. Exercise and activity counseling must account for this.

### Cancer Rehabilitation: The Impairment-Driven Model

Cancer rehabilitation uses an impairment-driven model, addressing specific functional deficits caused by the disease or its treatment. Common impairments in pediatric cancer include:

**Musculoskeletal:**
- Steroid myopathy (proximal weakness, Cushingoid habitus)
- Avascular necrosis (hips, knees — especially after ALL treatment)
- Osteoporosis and fracture risk (corticosteroids, methotrexate, radiation, inactivity)
- Gait abnormalities after amputation or limb-salvage surgery

**Neurologic:**
- Vincristine-induced peripheral neuropathy (sensorimotor; foot drop is characteristic)
- Methotrexate-related leukoencephalopathy (cognitive and executive function deficits)
- Cranial radiation effects (neurocognitive impairment affecting attention, processing speed, and memory)
- Posterior fossa syndrome after cerebellar tumor resection (mutism, ataxia, emotional lability)

**Cardiovascular:**
- Anthracycline-related cardiomyopathy (dose-dependent; may be acute or delayed)
- Deconditioning from prolonged treatment and inactivity

**Pulmonary:**
- Bleomycin-related pulmonary fibrosis
- Radiation-related pneumonitis

**Endocrine:**
- Growth hormone deficiency (cranial radiation)
- Hypothyroidism (neck or craniospinal radiation)
- Gonadal dysfunction and infertility (alkylating agents, gonadal radiation)
- Obesity (especially after ALL treatment and cranial radiation)

### Exercise Guidelines for Pediatric Cancer

Current evidence strongly supports that supervised exercise is safe during active pediatric cancer treatment and does not increase adverse events (Wurz et al., 2021; Stout et al., 2017).

**Benefits during treatment:**
- Maintenance of cardiovascular fitness and strength
- Reduced cancer-related fatigue
- Improved quality of life
- Maintenance of bone mineral density
- Improved body composition

**Precautions and individualization:**
- Avoid high-intensity exercise if platelet count is below 50,000/uL (risk of bleeding)
- Avoid high-intensity exercise if absolute neutrophil count (ANC) is below 500/uL (infection risk)
- Central line precautions: avoid activities that stress the catheter site (contact sports, swimming in some institutions)
- Avoid weight-bearing exercise in the setting of bone metastases
- Adjust intensity and type of exercise based on treatment phase and current medical status

**Physical therapy interventions during ALL treatment** have been shown to improve ankle ROM, knee extension strength, and functional mobility (Marchese et al., 2004).

### COG Survivorship Guidelines and Late Effects

The Children's Oncology Group Long-Term Follow-Up Guidelines (Version 6.0, 2023) provide risk-based screening recommendations for late effects of cancer therapy. Given that approximately 85% of children with cancer now survive, an estimated 500,000-plus childhood cancer survivors live in the United States, and approximately two-thirds will develop at least one chronic health condition.

Childhood cancer survivors experience accelerated aging and premature frailty. A landmark study found that adult survivors of childhood cancer demonstrated physiologic frailty at rates comparable to adults 20 to 30 years older (Ness et al., 2013).

**Major Late Effects by Treatment Exposure:**

| Treatment | Late Effect | Screening Recommendation |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Anthracyclines (doxorubicin, daunorubicin) | Dose-dependent cardiomyopathy | Echocardiogram starting 2 years after therapy; frequency based on cumulative dose |
| Cranial radiation | Neurocognitive deficits, GH deficiency, hypothyroidism, secondary CNS tumors | Neuropsychological testing; endocrine monitoring; brain MRI surveillance |
| Alkylating agents (cyclophosphamide, ifosfamide) | Gonadal dysfunction, infertility, secondary leukemia (MDS/AML) | Gonadal function testing; CBC monitoring |
| Bleomycin | Pulmonary fibrosis | PFTs at baseline and with respiratory symptoms |
| Cisplatin | Ototoxicity (high-frequency SNHL), nephrotoxicity | Audiometry; renal function monitoring |
| Vincristine | Peripheral neuropathy (motor and sensory) | Clinical neurologic examination |
| Corticosteroids | AVN, osteoporosis, obesity, metabolic syndrome | DEXA screening; metabolic panels |
| Radiation (any field) | Secondary malignancies (3-6x increased risk overall); field-specific organ damage | Risk-based cancer screening per COG guidelines |

**Exercise Recommendations for Survivors:**
The COG guidelines recommend 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity for all childhood cancer survivors, consistent with general population exercise guidelines but with special emphasis on the documented cardiovascular, metabolic, and psychosocial benefits in this population.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Bone health is a critical concern in childhood cancer survivors. Multiple treatment exposures compromise bone density: corticosteroids, methotrexate, cranial radiation (via growth hormone deficiency), and prolonged inactivity during treatment. DEXA scanning is recommended for at-risk survivors, and weight-bearing exercise, calcium, vitamin D supplementation, and endocrine optimization are the cornerstones of prevention.

### Phantom Limb Pain in Children

Phantom limb sensation and phantom limb pain occur in children after amputation for bone tumors, though traditionally they were believed to be less common than in adults. Current evidence suggests that phantom phenomena are common even in young children. They occur more frequently after amputation than after limb-salvage surgery.

Management approaches include mirror therapy, desensitization techniques, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), medications (gabapentin, amitriptyline), and psychological support. Early prosthetic fitting and active prosthetic use are associated with reduced phantom pain intensity.

### Psychosocial Considerations in Pediatric Cancer

The psychosocial impact of childhood cancer extends beyond the patient to the entire family. During treatment, children face disruptions to schooling, peer relationships, self-image, and normal developmental tasks. Siblings may experience emotional distress from altered family dynamics and parental attention distribution.

Survivors face ongoing challenges including fear of cancer recurrence, body image concerns (particularly after amputation or visible treatment effects), neurocognitive limitations affecting academic performance, and difficulties with social integration. Transition to adult-oriented follow-up care is a critical juncture, as many young adult survivors disengage from recommended surveillance.
