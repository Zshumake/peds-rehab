## CEREBRAL PALSY — Classification & Clinical Features

### Classification Systems Documented
- **Motor patterns**: Spastic (85%), Dyskinetic (15%), Ataxic (4-13%), Hypotonic (rare/debated)
- **Spastic subtypes**: Diplegia, Hemiplegia, Quadriplegia
- **Dyskinetic subtypes**: Dystonic, Choreoathetotic
- **Hypertonia Assessment Tool (HAT)**: Differentiates spasticity vs rigidity vs dystonia

### Functional Classification Systems (all Level I-V)
- GMFCS (gross motor), MACS (manual ability), CFCS (communication), VFCS (visual function), EDACS (eating/drinking), FMS (functional mobility)
- GMFCS Level descriptions for ages 6-12 documented

### Associated Conditions (frequencies from systematic reviews)
- Chronic pain: 50-75%
- Intellectual disability: 50%
- Speech-language disorders: 40-60%
- Bladder control problems: 30-60%
- Visual impairment: 30-50%
- Epilepsy: 25-45%
- Behavior disorder: 25-40%
- Hip displacement: 30%
- Sleep disorder: 20%
- Drooling: 20%
- Hearing impairment: 10-20%
- Gastrostomy dependence: 7%

### Early Signs
- Developmental reflex persistence beyond expected age
- Absent/reduced fidgety movements (birth to 5 months) correlates with CP diagnosis
- Motor milestone delays at/below 75th percentile warrant evaluation
- Key red flags: hand preference before 12 months, sustained fisting after 4 months, head lag beyond 4 months, inability to sit without support by 9 months

### Spasticity Details
- Velocity-dependent increased resistance to passive stretch
- Positive signs: spasticity, hyperreflexia, extensor plantar responses, clonus
- Negative signs: weakness, loss of dexterity, fatigability, axial hypotonia

### Orthopedic Complications
- Contractures, hip dysplasia/subluxation/dislocation, foot/hand deformities, neuromuscular scoliosis
- Osteopenia: 77% prevalence of reduced BMD in moderate-severe CP
- Fracture lifetime prevalence: 9-16%, 75% lower limb
- Scoliosis: 25-50% of CP children, increases with GMFCS level (2% GMFCS I, 30% GMFCS IV/V)

## CEREBRAL PALSY — Treatment of Spasticity & Orthopedic Conditions

### Spasticity Management Ladder
1. PT/OT + positioning + orthotics (all patients)
2. Oral medications (baclofen or benzodiazepine first-line)
3. BoNT injections (focal/segmental spasticity)
4. Neurolysis (phenol/ethanol blocks)
5. Intrathecal baclofen pump
6. Selective dorsal rhizotomy

### Oral Medications — Dosing Details
- **Baclofen**: Start 0.5 mg/kg/day divided BID-TID, titrate weekly by 0.5 mg/kg/day, max 2 mg/kg/day. Wean slowly (seizures/hallucinations if abrupt stop)
- **Diazepam**: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg/day divided BID-QID, max 2 mg/dose. Wean slowly
- **Dantrolene**: Start 0.5 mg/kg once daily, titrate to max 2 mg/kg QID (max single dose 100 mg). Hepatotoxicity monitoring required (28% case-fatality for dantrolene liver disease)
- **Tizanidine**: Start 1-2 mg at bedtime, increase by 2-4 mg/day every 3-7 days, max 24 mg/day

### Botulinum Toxin
- Blocks presynaptic ACh release at NMJ
- Onset within 1 week, peak at 1 month, lasts 3-8 months
- Reevaluate 6-8 weeks post-injection
- Systemic adverse events in 3.6% of injections (single-center study, n=2219 injections)
- Severe systemic events requiring hospitalization: 0.7%
- Risk factors for adverse events: GMFCS IV-V, underlying dysphagia, history of aspiration pneumonia
- Neutralizing antibodies: 3-10% after repeated injections
- Does NOT prevent contractures long-term
- Does NOT reduce need for orthopedic hip surgery

### Intrathecal Baclofen
- Surgically implanted pump delivering baclofen to intrathecal space
- Adverse effects in up to 50%: lethargy, confusion, hypotonia
- Catheter complications: infection, seroma, CSF leak
- PUMP MALFUNCTION = LIFE-THREATENING (withdrawal or overdose)

### Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy
- Creates surgical lesion in dorsal spinal nerve roots
- Meta-analysis of 3 RCTs (90 children, spastic diplegia): SDR + PT improved spasticity (mean Ashworth -1.2 points) and modestly improved motor function (GMFM-66 +2.6 points) vs PT alone
- England study (137 children, GMFCS II-III): GMFM-66 improved 3.2/year vs expected 1.9/year
- Greater improvements in GMFCS II vs III
- ~10% adverse events (wound infection, foot dysesthesias)
- Long-term: durable spasticity reduction, but ~40% needed additional BoNT or orthopedic surgery

### Dystonia Management
- First-line: baclofen (same dosing as spasticity)
- Second-line: gabapentin (5-15 mg/kg/day TID, max 2400 mg/day), clonidine (start 0.025-0.05 mg/day BID, max 0.6 mg/day), or benzodiazepines
- Deep brain stimulation: for refractory dystonia, GPi target, age ≥7 years

### Hip Disorders
- Develop in ~1/3 of spastic CP children
- Nearly 50% of non-ambulatory quadriplegic children develop subluxation by age 5
- Screening: AP pelvis radiograph, interval based on GMFCS level and migration percentage
- Migration percentage >40% → reconstructive surgery considered
- Screening programs reduce dislocation rates when paired with timely intervention

### Scoliosis Surgery
- Reasonable candidates: >40° progressive curve interfering with sitting, age >10 years
- Complication rates: 25-50% (higher than idiopathic scoliosis)
- Most common complications: surgical site infection, pneumonia, blood loss
- Mortality may be reduced by surgery (8.7 vs 5.3 per 1000 follow-up years nonsurgical vs surgical)

## MYELOMENINGOCELE — Management and Outcome

### MOMS Trial Key Data
- RCT, N=183, 3 institutions, 2003-2010, stopped early for efficacy
- Fetal surgery (18-25 weeks) vs postnatal repair
- VP shunt needed: 44% fetal vs 84% postnatal (RR 0.53)
- Independent ambulation at 30 months: 45% fetal vs 24% postnatal
- At age 6-10 years: 49% fetal vs 85% postnatal needed shunt; walking independently 29% vs 11%
- Cognitive outcomes: SIMILAR in both groups (mental development index ~89 vs ~86)
- Fetal surgery risks: preterm birth 46% vs 5%, maternal complications

### Hydrocephalus
- ~60% require VP shunt (with conservative shunting protocol)
- Shunt placement increases survival through childhood but may reduce adult survival
- 96% of shunted patients need at least one revision; 86% need two or more

### Tethered Cord
- Nearly all post-repair patients have radiographic tethering
- 10-30% develop symptomatic deterioration requiring surgical release
- Symptoms: progressive LE weakness, changing urologic function, progressive scoliosis, pain

### Arnold-Chiari II
- Present on MRI in almost all myelomeningocele patients
- Only 5-10% require surgical decompression
- Symptoms: swallowing difficulties, vocal cord paresis, stridor, apneic episodes
- Must exclude shunt malfunction FIRST before considering decompression

### Long-Term Outcomes (25-year follow-up, single institution, n=118)
- Overall mortality: 24%, continued increasing into young adulthood
- 73% had stable motor level compared with infancy
- Ambulatory status declined: ~75% in early childhood → ~50% in young adulthood
- 85% on CIC for bladder management
- 37% required special education
- 50% attended/graduated college; 45% employed
- 18% living independently; none had children

### Bowel Management
- Fecal incontinence: 60-70%
- ACE (Malone) procedure achieves continence in ~85%

## DMD/BMD — Management

### Birnkrant 2018 Guidelines Key Points
- Multidisciplinary care essential (table of specialists)
- Passive stretching 4-6x/week at ankles, knees, hips
- Scoliosis monitoring: visual inspection annually (ambulatory), every 6 months (non-ambulatory)
- Spine radiography if curve detected; refer orthopedics if >20°
- Exercise: regular submaximum aerobic exercise, avoid eccentric/high-resistance
- Swimming and cycling encouraged through non-ambulatory phase

### Cardiac Management
- Baseline cardiac evaluation at diagnosis (ECG + echo or cardiac MRI)
- Annual repeat minimum
- ACE inhibitor or ARB recommended by age 10 years
- Perindopril trial: 10-year survival 93% (perindopril) vs 66% (placebo)
- Eplerenone/spironolactone as add-on therapy supported
- Female carriers: cardiac evaluation in late adolescence/early adulthood, repeat every 3-5 years

### Respiratory Management
- Serial FVC monitoring from age 5-6, yearly during ambulatory stage
- Non-ambulatory: every 6 months with full panel (FVC, MIP, MEP, peak cough flow, SpO2, pCO2)
- Lung volume recruitment when FVC <60% predicted
- Assisted cough when FVC <50% or peak cough flow <270 L/min
- Nocturnal ventilation when FVC <50% or MIP <60 cmH2O or SpO2 <95%
- Succinylcholine ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED (hyperkalemia, rhabdomyolysis)
- Potent inhaled halogenated anesthetics also risky
- Total IV anesthesia required

### Prognosis
- Meta-analysis: median life expectancy ~28 years (born after 1990)
- With ventilatory support: median ~30 years
- BMD: ambulatory past age 16, mean age of death mid-40s, cardiac cause most common

## SMA — Clinical Features and Treatment

### Clinical Classification
- Type 0: Prenatal onset, death by 6 months (usually 1 month), 1 SMN2 copy
- Type 1: Birth to 6 months, never sit, death before 2 years, 2-3 SMN2 copies
- Type 2: 3-15 months, sit but never stand/walk, ~2/3 alive at 25 years, 3 SMN2 copies
- Type 3: >18 months, walk independently then lose, normal lifespan, 3-4 SMN2 copies
- Type 4: Adult onset, mildest, normal lifespan, 4-8 SMN2 copies

### Gene Therapy Outcomes
- **Nusinersen (ENDEAR)**: 51% improved motor milestones vs 0% sham; 39% vs 68% died/permanent ventilation
- **Nusinersen (CHERISH)**: HFMSE +3.9 vs -1.0 (difference 4.9 points, clinically meaningful ≥3)
- **Onasemnogene (START)**: All 15 alive at 20 months without permanent ventilation (vs 8% historical); 11/12 high-dose sat independently
- **Risdiplam (FIREFISH)**: 29-41% sitting without support at 12 months; 85-90% alive without permanent ventilation
- **Risdiplam (SUNFISH)**: MFM36 +1.36 vs -0.19 (difference 1.55)

### Costs
- Nusinersen: $125,000 per dose (4 loading + maintenance every 4 months)
- Onasemnogene: ~$2,125,000 (one-time)
- Risdiplam: daily oral

### Newborn Screening
- Added to US RUSP in 2018
- Detects ~95% via exon 7 deletion (misses point mutations)

## INTELLECTUAL DISABILITY

### Key Facts for Board
- Prevalence: 1-2% of population
- Diagnosis requires BOTH intellectual AND adaptive impairment, onset before 18 years
- GDD used for children <5 years
- Severity classified by adaptive functioning level, NOT IQ score (DSM-5)
- Historical IQ cutoffs: Mild 50-70, Moderate 35-50, Severe 20-35, Profound <20
- Comorbidities: ASD (28% of ID), ADHD (25-40%), epilepsy, feeding disorders, sleep disorders
- Vision disorders: 13-50%; hearing impairment: 15-20%
- Sexual abuse: 15-30% of children with ID
- Screening tools: Bayley-4, Denver II, ASQ-3, Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scale

## JIA — Epidemiology and Pathogenesis

### Key Board Facts
- Incidence: ~14 per 100,000/year
- Prevalence: ~70,000-100,000 in US
- Female:male ratio 2-3:1 overall
- Peak incidence: 1-3 years (girls), 8-10 years (boys)
- Oligoarticular JIA: most distinctive HLA associations (DR8, DR11, DR13, DPw2)
- HLA-A2 associated with early-onset oligoarticular JIA in females
- RF-positive polyarticular JIA has same HLA-DR4 associations as adult RA
- sJIA associated with HLA-DRB1*11
- Environmental factors: infection, antibiotics (dose-dependent association), possibly seasonal for sJIA
- Pannus formation is pathologic hallmark (similar to adult RA)
- TNF-alpha: pivotal role in trafficking inflammatory cells into synovium
- sJIA may be better classified as autoinflammatory (innate immunity driven)
- MAS: uncontrolled proliferation of activated CD8+ T cells, IFN-gamma pivotal cytokine

## PSORIATIC JIA

### Key Board Facts
- ~7% of all JIA (0-11% depending on criteria)
- Bimodal onset: preschool (like oligoarticular JIA) and middle/late childhood (like adult PsA)
- Younger patients: female predominance, ANA+, dactylitis, small joint involvement
- Older patients: ~1:1 sex ratio, enthesitis, axial disease, HLA-B27+
- Frank psoriasis absent at presentation in ~50%
- Dactylitis in 20-40%; reflects enthesitis within digit
- Nail changes in 50-80%
- Uveitis: 6-15% (chronic anterior, same screening as other JIA)
- Extension to ≥5 joints in 60-80% without treatment

## NEONATAL BRACHIAL PLEXUS PALSY

### Key Board Facts
- Incidence: 0.04-0.3% of live births (~0.1-0.15%)
- Erb palsy (C5-C6): ~50%, upper arm adducted/internally rotated, forearm extended
- Erb plus (C5-C6-C7): ~35%, "waiter's tip" posture
- Total plexus (C5-T1): flail arm ± Horner syndrome
- True Klumpke (C8-T1 only): may not exist in neonates
- Can occur WITHOUT shoulder dystocia or excessive traction
- Can occur after cesarean section
- Spontaneous recovery in many (1-3 months), persistent impairment 18-50%
- Favorable prognostic indicators: early recovery within 2-4 weeks, elbow flexion at 3 months
- Cookie test: inability to bring cookie to mouth at 9 months → consider surgery
- Towel test: inability to remove towel from face at 6 months
- Toronto Test Score: threshold for surgical referral
- Active Movement Scale: evaluative tool
- Narakas classification: 4 groups
- Surgery timing debate: 3 vs 6 vs 9 months
- Nerve transfers: Oberlin (ulnar fascicle to musculocutaneous), intercostal transfers
- Phrenic nerve involvement → diaphragmatic paralysis (minority of Erb cases)
- Higher incidence of torticollis and early speech delay
