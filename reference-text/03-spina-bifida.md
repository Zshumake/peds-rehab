## Section 1: Epidemiology and Prevention

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**NTD Prevalence:**
- United States (post-fortification): approximately 0.5-0.7 per 1,000 live births (decreased from approximately 1.0-1.5 per 1,000 pre-fortification)
- Worldwide: approximately 0.5-2.0 per 1,000 births (varies by region and population)
- Myelomeningocele accounts for approximately 90% of live-born NTDs requiring treatment

**Embryology:**
- The neural tube closes by day 28 post-conception (4 weeks gestational age)
- Anterior neuropore closes day 25; posterior neuropore closes day 27-28
- Myelomeningocele results from failure of posterior neuropore closure
- Because closure occurs before most women know they are pregnant, prevention must begin preconceptionally

**Risk Factors:**

| Risk Factor | Details |
|------------|---------|
| Prior NTD-affected pregnancy | Recurrence risk 2-5% |
| Family history | Sibling: 2-5%; two affected siblings: ~10% |
| Maternal folate deficiency | Primary modifiable risk factor |
| Maternal diabetes | 2-10x increased risk |
| Maternal obesity | 1.5-3.5x increased risk |
| Maternal hyperthermia | First trimester exposure |
| Valproic acid | 1-2% NTD risk (~10-20x baseline); most teratogenic common AED |
| Carbamazepine | 0.5-1% NTD risk |
| Folic acid antagonists | Methotrexate, trimethoprim, aminopterin |
| Hispanic ethnicity | Higher risk population |
| Low socioeconomic status | Multifactorial including nutrition |

**Folic Acid Prevention:**

The MRC Vitamin Study (Lancet, 1991) demonstrated a 72% reduction in NTD recurrence with 4 mg folic acid daily. Czeizel and Dudas (NEJM, 1992) demonstrated that folic acid also prevents first occurrence of NTDs.

| Population | Recommended Dose | Timing |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| All women of childbearing age | 0.4 mg (400 mcg) daily | Ongoing; preconceptional |
| Women with prior NTD pregnancy | 4 mg (4,000 mcg) daily | Start 1-3 months before conception through first trimester |

- US mandatory folic acid fortification of enriched grain products since 1998 resulted in approximately 26-28% reduction in NTD prevalence

> **Clinical Pearl:** Valproic acid is the most teratogenic commonly used antiepileptic drug for NTDs, with a 1-2% risk of NTDs (10-20x baseline). Its mechanism involves inhibition of folate metabolism and histone deacetylase activity. Women of childbearing potential on valproic acid should receive high-dose folic acid supplementation and counseling about alternative medications.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Folic acid must be started BEFORE conception because the neural tube closes by day 28 post-conception -- before most women know they are pregnant. This is why all women of childbearing age should take 0.4 mg daily, regardless of pregnancy plans.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The epidemiology of NTDs has been dramatically shaped by public health interventions. The introduction of mandatory folic acid fortification in the United States in 1998 was one of the most successful nutritional public health measures in modern history, resulting in a sustained reduction in NTD prevalence. However, NTDs have not been eliminated, suggesting that folate deficiency is not the sole etiological factor.

The etiology of NTDs is multifactorial, involving complex gene-environment interactions. More than 300 genes have been identified in mouse models that, when disrupted, produce NTDs. The folate pathway genes (MTHFR, MTR, MTHFD1) have been studied most extensively in human populations. The MTHFR C677T variant, which reduces enzyme activity by approximately 30% in homozygotes, is associated with modestly increased NTD risk, particularly in populations with low folate intake.

Geographic and ethnic variation in NTD prevalence is well documented. Populations of Irish, Welsh, and Sikh descent historically have had among the highest NTD rates, while populations of African descent have lower rates. Hispanic populations in the United States have consistently higher NTD prevalence than non-Hispanic populations, likely reflecting both genetic susceptibility and dietary factors.

Maternal diabetes (both pre-gestational type 1 and type 2) carries a substantially elevated risk, with the degree of risk correlating with the degree of glycemic control during the critical period of neural tube closure. Optimal preconceptional glycemic control can reduce but not eliminate this excess risk.

## Section 2: Types of Spina Bifida

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

| Type | Description | Neural Tissue Involved? | MSAFP Elevated? | Prognosis |
|------|-------------|------------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Anencephaly | Failure of anterior neural tube closure | Yes | Yes | Incompatible with life; ~50% of NTDs |
| Myelomeningocele | Herniation of meninges AND neural tissue through posterior vertebral defect | Yes | Yes | Most clinically significant NTD; variable by level |
| Meningocele | Herniation of meninges only; no neural tissue | No | Yes (usually) | Better prognosis; ~5% of NTDs |
| Encephalocele | Herniation of brain tissue through skull defect | Yes | Yes | Usually occipital; variable prognosis |
| Lipomyelomeningocele | Closed dysraphism with lipoma attached to spinal cord | No exposed tissue | No | Associated with tethered cord; better than MMC |
| Spina bifida occulta | Posterior vertebral arch defect only | No | No | Usually asymptomatic; 5-10% of general population |

**Key Distinctions:**
- Open NTDs (myelomeningocele, anencephaly): Exposed neural tissue; elevated MSAFP; require surgical closure
- Closed NTDs (lipomyelomeningocele, spina bifida occulta): No exposed neural tissue; MSAFP NOT elevated; may be asymptomatic or present with tethered cord

**Cutaneous Markers of Closed Spinal Dysraphism:**
- Hairy patch
- Sacral dimple ABOVE the gluteal cleft (dimples within the cleft are usually benign)
- Hemangioma or vascular malformation
- Dermal sinus tract
- Subcutaneous lipoma
- Skin appendage or tail

> **Clinical Pearl:** MSAFP is elevated in OPEN NTDs only. Closed spinal dysraphisms (lipomyelomeningocele, spina bifida occulta) do NOT elevate MSAFP. This is a frequently tested distinction.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The spectrum of spinal dysraphism ranges from clinically insignificant posterior arch defects (spina bifida occulta, present in 5-10% of the general population) to devastating open defects with extensive neurological impairment. Understanding this spectrum is essential for counseling families and for appropriate clinical management.

Spina bifida occulta is an incidental radiographic finding in most cases and requires no treatment. However, a subset of patients with occulta have underlying closed spinal dysraphisms (tethered cord, split cord malformation, dermoid cyst) that may become symptomatic with growth. The presence of cutaneous stigmata overlying the lumbosacral spine should prompt imaging evaluation even in the absence of neurological symptoms.

Lipomyelomeningocele is the most common closed spinal dysraphism requiring surgical management. Unlike myelomeningocele, the neural tissue is covered by skin at birth and there is no open defect. However, the lipoma is adherent to the spinal cord and produces tethering, which can cause progressive neurological deterioration during periods of growth. The timing of surgical intervention for asymptomatic lipomyelomeningocele remains debated, with some surgeons favoring prophylactic untethering and others advocating observation with surgery only for progressive symptoms.

## Section 3: Prenatal Diagnosis

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Screening Algorithm:**

1. Maternal Serum Alpha-Fetoprotein (MSAFP): Screening at 15-20 weeks gestation
   - Sensitivity: approximately 80% for open spina bifida at cutoff of 2.5 MoM (multiples of the median)
   - False positives: Multiple gestation, incorrect dating, abdominal wall defects, fetal demise
   - NOT elevated in closed spinal dysraphisms

2. Amniocentesis (if MSAFP elevated): AFP + Acetylcholinesterase (AChE)
   - AChE is more specific for open NTDs than AFP alone
   - AFP elevated + AChE positive = high specificity for open NTD

3. Ultrasonographic Findings:
   - Direct visualization of spinal defect
   - Lemon sign: Frontal bone scalloping (lemon-shaped skull) -- seen in approximately 99% of NTDs before 24 weeks
   - Banana sign: Cerebellar compression (banana-shaped cerebellum) -- associated with Chiari II malformation
   - Ventriculomegaly
   - Sensitivity: approximately 90-95% in experienced hands

4. Fetal MRI: Increasingly used for detailed evaluation after ultrasound detection; better delineation of defect level and associated anomalies

> **Clinical Pearl:** The lemon sign (scalloped frontal bones) and banana sign (compressed cerebellum) are classic ultrasonographic findings in fetal myelomeningocele. The lemon sign reflects abnormal skull shape from reduced intracranial pressure due to CSF leaking through the open defect. The banana sign reflects cerebellar herniation from the developing Chiari II malformation.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The prenatal diagnostic algorithm has evolved substantially with advances in ultrasound technology. High-resolution ultrasonography in experienced hands can now detect myelomeningocele with sensitivity exceeding 90%, often identifying the defect at the routine 18-20 week anatomic survey. This early detection enables timely counseling, referral for fetal surgery evaluation, and preparation for postnatal management.

Fetal MRI has become an important adjunct to ultrasonography, providing superior soft tissue contrast and more precise delineation of the anatomic level of the defect, associated brain anomalies, and the degree of hindbrain herniation. Fetal MRI is particularly valuable in the evaluation of candidates for prenatal repair, where accurate assessment of inclusion and exclusion criteria is essential.

Cell-free fetal DNA screening, while primarily used for chromosomal aneuploidy screening, does not currently detect structural anomalies such as NTDs. MSAFP remains the primary serum screening tool for NTDs and is typically offered as part of the quad screen or sequential screening protocol.

## Section 4: MOMS Trial

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Trial Design:**
- Management of Myelomeningocele Study (MOMS)
- Published: NEJM, 2011
- Multicenter RCT across 3 centers (Vanderbilt, CHOP, UCSF)
- N = 183 (planned 200, stopped early for efficacy at interim analysis)
- Prenatal repair (n=91) at 19-25 weeks gestation vs. Postnatal repair (n=92) within 12 hours of birth

**Primary Outcomes:**
1. Composite of fetal/neonatal death or need for VP shunt by 12 months
2. Bayley MDI and motor function at 30 months

**Key Results:**

| Outcome | Prenatal Repair | Postnatal Repair | Significance |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| VP shunt by 12 months | 40% | 82% | RR 0.48, p<0.001 |
| Chiari II improvement | 36% reversal | 4% reversal | Significant |
| Walking independently (30 months) | 42% | 21% | Significant |
| Improvement >=2 functional levels above predicted | 32% | 12% | Significant |
| Bayley MDI (30 months) | Similar | Similar | No significant difference in cognition |

**MOMS2 Follow-Up (School Age, approximately 6-10 years):**
- VP shunt: 44% prenatal vs. 84% postnatal (sustained benefit)
- Community walking: 45% prenatal vs. 24% postnatal (sustained benefit)
- Cognitive and adaptive behavior scores: Similar between groups
- Independent walking: 29% prenatal vs. 11% postnatal

**Risks of Prenatal Repair:**

| Complication | Prenatal | Postnatal |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Preterm birth (mean GA) | 34.1 weeks | 37.3 weeks |
| Uterine dehiscence at delivery | 35% | N/A |
| Chorioamniotic membrane separation | 26% | 0% |
| Oligohydramnios | 21% | 4% |
| All future deliveries must be C-section | Yes (classical hysterotomy) | No |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The MOMS trial is THE landmark spina bifida trial. Know these three key results: (1) VP shunt rate cut in half (40% vs. 82%), (2) motor function significantly better with prenatal repair (walking 42% vs. 21%), and (3) cognitive outcomes were NOT different. The trade-off is increased preterm birth and maternal morbidity.

> **Clinical Pearl:** MOMS trial results should NOT be generalized to less experienced centers or patients who do not meet eligibility criteria. Maternal BMI >35 was an exclusion criterion. Prenatal repair requires a specialized fetal surgery center.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The MOMS trial fundamentally changed the landscape of myelomeningocele management by establishing prenatal repair as a viable option that reduces the need for VP shunting and improves motor outcomes. The mechanism of benefit is thought to involve both protection of neural tissue from ongoing amniotic fluid exposure and cerebrospinal fluid dynamics -- closure of the defect restores the "pressure seal" that promotes normal hindbrain development and reduces hydrocephalus progression.

The improvement in Chiari II malformation with prenatal repair is particularly notable. Reversal of hindbrain herniation occurred in 36% of the prenatal group versus only 4% in the postnatal group, suggesting that fetal repair can alter the natural history of this otherwise progressive structural anomaly when performed during the critical window of brain development.

Fetoscopic (minimally invasive) prenatal repair approaches are under investigation at multiple centers. Potential advantages include smaller uterine incisions (potentially reducing dehiscence risk), the possibility of vaginal delivery in subsequent pregnancies, and reduced maternal morbidity. Randomized trials comparing fetoscopic to open fetal repair are in progress. Early results suggest comparable neurological outcomes, though the technique remains investigational and demands highly specialized surgical expertise.

It is important to emphasize that the cognitive outcomes (Bayley MDI) were similar between groups at both 30-month and school-age follow-up, indicating that prenatal repair improves motor but not cognitive development. The cognitive profile in spina bifida is influenced primarily by hydrocephalus severity, shunt complications, and the intrinsic brain malformation associated with Chiari II, rather than by the timing of defect closure.

## Section 5: Arnold-Chiari II Malformation

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Anatomy:**
- Present in virtually all patients (approximately 95-100%) with myelomeningocele
- Downward displacement of cerebellar tonsils AND vermis through the foramen magnum into the upper cervical canal
- Also involves displacement of the fourth ventricle and brainstem (medulla)
- Distinguished from Chiari I: Chiari I = tonsillar herniation only (>5mm below foramen magnum); Chiari II = tonsils + vermis + brainstem + 4th ventricle, AND associated with myelomeningocele

> **Clinical Pearl:** Chiari II is associated with myelomeningocele. Chiari I is NOT associated with myelomeningocele -- Chiari I is associated with syringomyelia and may be idiopathic or related to connective tissue disorders. This is a frequently tested distinction.

**Symptoms by Age:**

| Age Group | Symptoms | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| Infants (0-2 years) -- MOST DANGEROUS | Stridor (bilateral vocal cord paralysis), dysphagia/aspiration, apneic spells/central apnea, weak/absent cry, opisthotonus, nystagmus, upper extremity weakness | 15-35% mortality if symptomatic in infancy; requires urgent posterior fossa decompression |
| Children (2-12 years) | Headaches (with Valsalva), progressive scoliosis, upper extremity weakness, worsening gait, spasticity, sleep apnea | May indicate syringomyelia |
| Adolescents/Adults | Headaches, neck pain, progressive scoliosis, upper extremity changes, pain syndromes | Consider syringomyelia |

**Management:**
- Only 5-10% of all myelomeningocele patients with Chiari II require surgical decompression
- Surgery: Posterior fossa decompression (suboccipital craniectomy + C1 laminectomy +/- duraplasty)
- CRITICAL RULE: ALWAYS check for VP shunt malfunction FIRST before attributing symptoms to Chiari II. Shunt malfunction can mimic or exacerbate Chiari II symptoms.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In any spina bifida patient presenting with new neurological symptoms, ALWAYS evaluate VP shunt function first. VP shunt malfunction is the most common cause of acute neurological change in spina bifida and can present identically to symptomatic Chiari II or tethered cord.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The pathogenesis of Chiari II malformation is intimately linked to the open spinal defect. The prevailing theory holds that CSF leakage through the myelomeningocele prevents normal distension of the developing ventricular system, which in turn fails to induce normal posterior fossa expansion. The resulting small posterior fossa cannot accommodate the cerebellum and brainstem, forcing their herniation through the foramen magnum. This theory is supported by the observation that prenatal closure of the defect (MOMS trial) can partially reverse hindbrain herniation.

The clinical presentation of symptomatic Chiari II differs markedly by age. In infants, brainstem compression produces life-threatening symptoms including bilateral vocal cord paralysis (causing stridor), central apnea, and swallowing dysfunction. The infant with myelomeningocele who develops new-onset stridor or feeding difficulties requires urgent evaluation -- after confirming adequate shunt function, posterior fossa decompression should be considered emergently.

Sleep-disordered breathing is prevalent in the spina bifida population due to brainstem dysfunction from Chiari II. Central apnea, obstructive apnea, and mixed patterns all occur. Polysomnography should be performed in symptomatic patients. Treatment depends on the pattern and severity and may include CPAP, BiPAP, or posterior fossa decompression for central apnea refractory to positive pressure support.

## Section 6: Hydrocephalus

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Prevalence and Mechanism:**
- Approximately 80-90% of children with myelomeningocele develop hydrocephalus requiring treatment
- Mechanism: Chiari II malformation obstructs CSF flow at the posterior fossa level (obstruction of 4th ventricle outlets and/or aqueduct)
- VP shunt placement rate: approximately 80-85% historically (MOMS postnatal group: 82%; prenatal group: 40%)

**VP Shunt Complications:**

| Complication | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Shunt malfunction (obstruction) | Most common complication; 40-50% malfunction within first 2 years |
| Shunt infection | 5-10% per procedure; most common organism: Staphylococcus epidermidis (coagulase-negative staph) |
| Over-drainage | Slit ventricle syndrome, subdural hematomas/hygromas, craniosynostosis |
| Abdominal complications | Pseudocyst, peritonitis, catheter migration, viscus perforation |

**Symptoms of Shunt Malfunction:**
- Headache, vomiting, lethargy, irritability
- Bulging fontanelle (if still open)
- Sunsetting eyes (downward gaze deviation)
- Increased head circumference
- Change in mentation, new seizures
- Declining school performance or behavior change

**Shunt Revision Data:**
- 96% of shunted patients need at least one revision
- 86% need two or more revisions

**ETV (Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy):**
- Alternative to VP shunt; creates opening in floor of 3rd ventricle for CSF bypass
- No hardware (eliminates shunt malfunction/infection risk)
- Success rate in spina bifida: approximately 50-60% (lower than other hydrocephalus etiologies)
- ETV + CPC (choroid plexus cauterization): approximately 60-70% success in some series
- Less successful in infants <6 months and those with prior shunt infections

> **Clinical Pearl:** ANY acute neurological change in a patient with a VP shunt should be treated as shunt malfunction until proven otherwise. This includes new seizures, behavior changes, declining school performance, headaches, and worsening motor function. Do not attribute symptoms to other causes until shunt function has been confirmed.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The management of hydrocephalus remains the most significant source of morbidity in the spina bifida population. VP shunts, while life-saving, carry a lifelong burden of complications. The high revision rate (96% needing at least one revision) means that most individuals with spina bifida will undergo multiple neurosurgical procedures over their lifetime, each carrying risks of infection, anesthesia, and recovery time.

Shunt infection management typically requires shunt externalization, intravenous antibiotics, and eventual shunt replacement. The most common organism is Staphylococcus epidermidis, reflecting the hardware-associated nature of these infections. Gram-negative infections and polymicrobial infections also occur, particularly with abdominal complications.

Long-term data suggest a complex relationship between shunting and survival. While shunt placement increases survival through childhood, some studies suggest that shunted patients may have reduced adult survival compared to non-shunted patients, likely reflecting the cumulative burden of shunt-related complications and the associated conditions that necessitated shunting.

ETV has emerged as an important alternative, particularly for patients with shunt failure who may be candidates for shunt independence. The lower success rate in the spina bifida population compared to other hydrocephalus etiologies reflects the different pathophysiology (communicating hydrocephalus with impaired CSF absorption in many cases) and the structural changes associated with Chiari II malformation.

## Section 7: Tethered Cord

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Definition:** Abnormal attachment of the spinal cord to surrounding structures (scar tissue, lipoma, tight filum terminale) that limits normal ascent during growth, producing traction injury to the spinal cord.

**Prevalence:**
- Radiographic tethering is present in virtually ALL post-repair myelomeningocele patients
- Symptomatic tethered cord requiring surgical release: 10-30% (some sources cite 20-50%)

**Clinical Presentation:**

Symptoms are often subtle and progressive. Any change in neurological, urological, or orthopedic status should prompt evaluation:
- Progressive scoliosis (rapidly progressive or new onset)
- Change in gait pattern or loss of ambulatory ability
- Increasing spasticity in lower extremities
- New or worsening foot deformity (especially unilateral)
- Back pain (often positional, worse with flexion)
- Progressive neurogenic bladder changes (new incontinence, increased post-void residual, change in urodynamic pattern)
- Upper extremity symptoms if associated with syringomyelia

**Diagnosis:**
- MRI of spine: Low-lying conus medullaris (below L2 in adults, below L3 in infants)
- CRITICAL: In myelomeningocele, the conus is ALWAYS low -- so position alone is not diagnostic
- Tethered cord in MMC is a CLINICAL diagnosis based on PROGRESSIVE SYMPTOMS, not purely radiographic
- Urodynamic changes may be the earliest detectable sign

**Treatment:**
- Surgical untethering (laminectomy and release of adhesions)
- Goal: Halt progression of deficits; recovery of lost function is variable
- Risk of re-tethering: approximately 10-20%

> **Clinical Pearl:** Tethered cord in myelomeningocele is a CLINICAL diagnosis, not a radiographic one. MRI shows tethering in virtually all MMC patients, so the decision to operate is driven by SYMPTOMS (progressive motor loss, scoliosis, bladder changes, foot deformity) -- not by imaging alone.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The "rule of progressive change" applies: New or rapidly progressive scoliosis, changing bladder function, or worsening foot deformity in a patient with spina bifida should prompt evaluation for tethered cord AND shunt malfunction. Always evaluate shunt function first.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The pathophysiology of symptomatic tethered cord involves chronic traction on the spinal cord during periods of growth. As the child grows in height, the spinal column elongates, but the tethered cord cannot ascend within the spinal canal as it normally would. This creates progressive stretch and ischemic injury to the caudal spinal cord, producing neurological deterioration.

Serial urodynamic monitoring plays an important role in tethered cord surveillance. Changes in detrusor compliance, new detrusor overactivity, increasing post-void residuals, or new detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia may precede clinically apparent motor changes. Some centers perform annual urodynamics in all MMC patients as part of tethered cord surveillance, using changes from baseline as triggers for MRI evaluation and surgical consideration.

Re-tethering after initial untethering surgery remains a significant challenge, occurring in approximately 10-20% of cases. Each subsequent untethering carries higher surgical risk due to scar tissue and distorted anatomy. Research into tethered cord prevention, including barrier materials placed during the initial untethering procedure, is ongoing.

## Section 8: Functional Motor Levels

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

This is one of the MOST TESTED topics in spina bifida rehabilitation. Understanding the key muscle at each level, the expected orthopedic deformity pattern, orthotic needs, and ambulatory prognosis is essential.

| Level | Key Muscle(s) | Orthotic | Ambulatory Outcome |
|-------|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| Thoracic | No LE function | Parapodium/RGO (therapeutic) | Wheelchair; therapeutic standing only |
| L1-L2 | Iliopsoas (hip flexion) | HKAFO/RGO | Household/therapeutic as child; wheelchair as adult |
| L3 | Quadriceps (knee extension) | KAFO | Household/limited community; wheelchair for community as adult |
| L4 | Tibialis anterior (ankle DF) | AFO | Community ambulation with AFO |
| L5 | Lateral hamstrings, peroneals, partial gluteus medius | AFO | Community ambulation; Trendelenburg gait |
| S1 | Gastrocnemius/soleus (plantarflexion) | AFO or shoe insert | Near-normal ambulation |
| S2-S3 | Foot intrinsics, sphincters | None needed | Normal or near-normal ambulation; bowel/bladder issues persist |

**Orthopedic Deformity Patterns by Level:**

| Level | Hip | Knee | Foot |
|-------|-----|------|------|
| Thoracic | Flexion contracture, dislocation | Flexion contracture | Equinovarus (positioning) |
| L1-L2 | Flexion contracture, dislocation (unopposed flexors) | Flexion or extension contracture | Variable |
| L3 | Flexion contracture, dislocation (flexors + quads, no extensors/abductors) | Recurvatum (unopposed quads) | Variable |
| L4 | Subluxation less common | Flexion contracture (hamstrings present) | CALCANEOVALGUS (TA without gastroc) |
| L5 | Subluxation rare | Usually normal | CALCANEUS (dorsiflexors and evertors without plantarflexors) |
| S1 | Usually normal | Usually normal | CAVOVARUS (intrinsic minus foot) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** L3 = quadriceps level. L4 = tibialis anterior level. L5 = peroneal/lateral hamstring level. S1 = gastrocnemius level. These level-muscle associations are MUST-KNOW for boards.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The classic foot deformity pattern: L4-L5 levels develop CALCANEUS deformity (dorsiflexors present without plantarflexors). S1 develops CAVOVARUS (intrinsic minus foot). This deformity pattern reflects muscle imbalance from differential innervation.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Quadriceps function (L3 level) is the key determinant of ambulation potential. Community ambulation typically requires at least L4 level (tibialis anterior for ankle dorsiflexion control).

**Critical Board Facts:**
- Energy cost of ambulation increases dramatically with higher lesion levels (oxygen consumption 2-4x normal at L1-L2 level)
- Most individuals with thoracic through L2 levels become full-time wheelchair users by adolescence/adulthood
- L4 is the classic "AFO level" -- has quads AND tibialis anterior, making community ambulation with AFOs realistic
- L5 produces Trendelenburg gait due to gluteus medius weakness
- Factors predicting loss of ambulation: higher lesion level, obesity, musculoskeletal deformities, hydrocephalus with shunt complications, lower cognitive function

**Ambulation Prognosis Rules:**
- Ambulation status tends to decline from childhood to adulthood: approximately 75% ambulatory in early childhood decreases to approximately 50% in young adulthood
- Obesity is a major contributor to loss of ambulatory function

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The functional motor level classification system, while clinically useful, represents a simplification of a complex clinical picture. Individual patients may have mixed levels (different levels on each side), skip lesions (preserved function below a non-functional segment), or sacral sparing that modifies expected functional outcomes. Additionally, the relationship between anatomic level on MRI and functional motor level is not always straightforward -- the functional level may be better or worse than predicted by the anatomic defect.

The Hoffer ambulatory classification system (community, household, exercise, non-functional) provides a standardized vocabulary for describing ambulatory status. Community ambulators walk for most daily activities, potentially using orthoses. Household ambulators walk within the home but use a wheelchair for community mobility. Exercise ambulators walk only during therapy sessions. Non-functional ambulators (non-ambulatory) do not walk.

The energy cost of ambulation is a critical factor in long-term ambulatory prognosis. Studies using oxygen consumption analysis have demonstrated that walking energy cost for individuals with thoracic-level lesions using HKAFOs exceeds 2-4 times normal walking energy cost, which is physiologically unsustainable for daily mobility. This is why virtually all thoracic and L1-L2 level patients eventually transition to wheelchair use despite successful orthotic ambulation programs in childhood.

## Section 9: Neurogenic Bladder

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Prevalence:** Nearly all patients (approximately 90-95%) with myelomeningocele have neurogenic bladder dysfunction.

**Bladder Types:**
- Upper motor neuron (reflexic/spastic): Detrusor overactivity, detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD), high intravesical pressures, risk of upper tract damage
- Lower motor neuron (areflexic/flaccid): Large capacity, low pressure, overflow incontinence, incompetent sphincter
- Most dangerous pattern: High-pressure bladder (detrusor overactivity with DSD or poor compliance) leads to vesicoureteral reflux, hydronephrosis, and renal damage

**Critical Pressure Threshold:**
- Detrusor leak point pressure >40 cm H2O = HIGH RISK for upper tract (renal) deterioration (McGuire et al., 1981)
- Requires aggressive management

**Evaluation:**
- Baseline renal ultrasound at birth/diagnosis
- Baseline urodynamic study in newborn period or within first few months
- Serial renal ultrasound and urodynamics (typically annually)
- VCUG if vesicoureteral reflux suspected
- Serum creatinine monitoring

> **Clinical Pearl:** Urodynamics is the GOLD STANDARD for evaluating neurogenic bladder in spina bifida. Key parameters: detrusor pressure, compliance, capacity, leak point pressure, and presence of DSD. A detrusor leak point pressure >40 cm H2O is the critical threshold that demands aggressive intervention to protect the kidneys.

**Management Ladder:**

**Step 1: Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC)**
- CORNERSTONE of neurogenic bladder management
- Frequency: Every 3-4 hours (4-6 times daily)
- Start: Newborn period (proactive approach) or when indicated by urodynamics
- Self-catheterization training: Begin teaching at age 5-7 years; independence goal by 8-10 years

**Step 2: Anticholinergic Medications**
- Oxybutynin: Most commonly used; 0.1-0.2 mg/kg/dose BID-TID; also available as intravesical instillation
- Purpose: Reduce detrusor overactivity, increase capacity, improve compliance
- Side effects: Dry mouth, constipation, cognitive effects (especially oxybutynin -- crosses blood-brain barrier)

**Step 3: Botulinum Toxin (Detrusor Injection)**
- OnabotulinumtoxinA injected into detrusor via cystoscopy
- Dose: 10-12 U/kg (typical 100-300 U total in children)
- Duration: approximately 6-9 months; repeat injections needed

**Step 4: Augmentation Cystoplasty**
- Surgical bladder enlargement using bowel segment (ileocystoplasty most common)
- Indications: Refractory high-pressure bladder, small capacity despite medical management
- Complications: Mucus production, metabolic acidosis, bladder stones, perforation risk, small long-term malignancy risk
- Still requires CIC after augmentation

**Step 5: Continent Urinary Diversion**
- Mitrofanoff (appendicovesicostomy): Continent catheterizable channel using appendix from umbilicus/navel to bladder
- Monti channel: Using ileal segment if appendix unavailable
- Enables self-catheterization through abdominal stoma (useful for wheelchair users, females with positioning challenges)

**Continence Outcomes:**
- Social continence (dry between catheterizations) achievable in approximately 80-90% with CIC + anticholinergics
- 85% of adults with spina bifida are on CIC for bladder management

> **Clinical Pearl:** CIC (Lapides et al., 1972) revolutionized neurogenic bladder management and remains the cornerstone of treatment. The key principle is that regular, complete bladder emptying at low pressure is far more important than sterility -- "clean" rather than "sterile" catheterization is sufficient.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Historically, renal failure was the leading cause of death in spina bifida. The introduction of CIC and urodynamic surveillance has dramatically reduced renal morbidity, shifting the leading causes of mortality to shunt-related complications and Chiari II complications.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The proactive approach to neurogenic bladder management -- starting CIC and anticholinergics in the newborn period based on urodynamic assessment rather than waiting for clinical deterioration -- has gained increasing support. Early studies demonstrated that proactive management results in better preservation of upper urinary tract function, lower rates of vesicoureteral reflux, and improved continence outcomes compared to expectant management.

The Mitrofanoff procedure, named after the French urologist Paul Mitrofanoff, uses the appendix as a conduit from the bladder to an abdominal stoma, creating a catheterizable channel that enables continence and independence in self-catheterization. This is particularly valuable for wheelchair users who cannot easily access the urethra for self-catheterization and for females who find urethral catheterization technically challenging. When the appendix is unavailable (previously removed or used for a MACE procedure), a Monti channel created from a reconfigured segment of ileum serves as an alternative.

## Section 10: Neurogenic Bowel

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Prevalence:** Virtually ALL patients with myelomeningocele have neurogenic bowel dysfunction.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Upper motor neuron bowel: Reflex bowel activity, constipation, difficulty with voluntary evacuation
- Lower motor neuron bowel: Reduced rectal tone, reduced sensation, fecal incontinence
- Most common presentation: Constipation with overflow incontinence (fecal soiling)

**Fecal incontinence:** 60-70% of patients

**Bowel Management Ladder:**

**Step 1: Dietary and Behavioral**
- High-fiber diet, adequate fluid intake
- Timed toileting (use gastrocolic reflex, post-meal timing)
- Activity and exercise

**Step 2: Oral Medications**
- Osmotic laxatives: Polyethylene glycol (MiraLAX), lactulose
- Stimulant laxatives: Senna, bisacodyl (scheduled, not PRN)
- Stool softeners: Docusate (limited evidence alone)

**Step 3: Rectal Interventions**
- Glycerin or bisacodyl suppositories
- Digital stimulation
- Mini-enemas

**Step 4: Antegrade Continence Enema (ACE/Malone Procedure)**
- Surgically created channel (appendicostomy or cecostomy) from skin to cecum
- Enema solution instilled via stoma into cecum produces predictable bowel evacuation
- Goal: Pseudo-continence (complete evacuation at scheduled time, dry between flushes)
- Success rate: approximately 70-90% achieve social continence
- Alternative: Cecostomy button (Chait tube) for percutaneous access

**Step 5: Colostomy (rare, last resort)**

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Malone Antegrade Continence Enema (MACE) procedure is a game-changer for bowel management in spina bifida. It achieves social continence in approximately 85% of patients by allowing predictable, scheduled bowel evacuations through an antegrade flush. It is one of the most commonly referenced surgical procedures in spina bifida board questions.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Fecal incontinence is often cited by patients and families as the most socially limiting aspect of spina bifida, exceeding even urinary incontinence and mobility limitations in its impact on quality of life, social participation, and self-esteem. The development of structured bowel management programs and surgical options such as the MACE procedure has dramatically improved continence outcomes and quality of life.

The ACE/Malone procedure can be performed simultaneously with the Mitrofanoff procedure (using the appendix for bladder, with a cecostomy button for bowel), allowing comprehensive continence management in a single surgical session. When both procedures are needed and only one appendix is available, the appendix is typically used for the Mitrofanoff (urinary continence), with a cecostomy tube used for antegrade enemas.

Transition to self-management for bowel programs typically begins in early childhood, with increasing independence through the school years. By adolescence, the goal is for the individual to independently manage their bowel program, including enema administration if applicable. This self-management skill is critical for adult independence and should be a focus of rehabilitation throughout childhood.

## Section 11: Latex Allergy

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Prevalence:**
- Latex allergy in spina bifida: 30-65% (compared to approximately 1% in the general population)
- Due to repeated early exposure to latex through multiple surgeries, catheterizations, and examinations
- IgE-mediated (Type I) hypersensitivity to natural rubber latex proteins

**Cross-Reactive Foods (Highest Risk):**
- Banana
- Avocado
- Chestnut
- Kiwi
- Mnemonic: "BACK" (Banana, Avocado, Chestnut, Kiwi)

Other cross-reactive foods (moderate risk): Potato, tomato, papaya, fig, passion fruit, pineapple, mango, peach, celery

**Prevention and Management:**
- Latex-free environment from birth is standard of care for ALL patients with spina bifida
- Non-latex gloves (nitrile, vinyl), non-latex catheters, non-latex balloons
- Latex allergy alert bracelet/documentation
- Patients with known allergy: Carry epinephrine auto-injector
- Surgical precautions: First case of the day, latex-free operating room
- Implementation of latex-free protocols has significantly reduced sensitization rates in newer cohorts

> **Clinical Pearl:** The BACK mnemonic (Banana, Avocado, Chestnut, Kiwi) for latex cross-reactive foods is a perennial board favorite. Any spina bifida patient reporting food allergies to these items should be evaluated for latex allergy.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Latex-free precautions from birth are now standard of care for ALL spina bifida patients, not just those with known allergy. This proactive approach has dramatically reduced latex sensitization rates in recent cohorts.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The mechanism of latex-fruit cross-reactivity involves structural homology between latex proteins (particularly hevein) and plant defense proteins called class I chitinases found in certain fruits. These shared epitopes enable IgE antibodies directed against latex proteins to cross-react with fruit proteins, producing allergic symptoms that can range from oral allergy syndrome (tingling, itching of the mouth) to systemic anaphylaxis.

The dramatic reduction in latex sensitization rates in more recent cohorts of spina bifida patients represents a triumph of preventive medicine. Earlier cohorts, exposed to latex during multiple surgeries and catheterizations before latex-free protocols were implemented, showed sensitization rates as high as 65%. Current cohorts, managed with strict latex-free protocols from birth, show substantially lower rates, though the exact prevalence continues to be tracked.

## Section 12: Orthopedic Management

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**General Principles:**
- Orthopedic management goals vary by functional level:
  - Ambulators (L4-S2): Maintain and improve ambulation; correct deformities that interfere with bracing or walking
  - Non-ambulators (thoracic-L2): Maintain sitting balance; prevent pain; facilitate hygiene and positioning
  - L3: Variable -- may be ambulatory as child, wheelchair as adult

**Hip Management:**
- Hip dislocation most common at L1-L3 levels (unopposed hip flexors without extensors/abductors)
- Unlike CP, hip reduction surgery in spina bifida does NOT improve ambulation outcomes
- Ambulation is determined by neurological level, not hip location
- Surgery for hip dislocation reserved for cases causing pain or skin problems

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between hip management in CP vs. spina bifida is a CLASSIC board question. In CP, hip surveillance and timely surgical intervention are critical because hip displacement causes pain and functional decline. In spina bifida, hip reduction does NOT improve ambulation -- ambulation depends on the neurological level. This is a fundamental conceptual difference.

**Foot Deformity Management:**
- Clubfoot (equinovarus): Most common foot deformity at BIRTH in spina bifida; serial casting (Ponseti may be used but less predictable than in idiopathic clubfoot); surgical correction if needed
- Calcaneovalgus: Most common ACQUIRED deformity, especially L4-L5; can cause calcaneal pressure ulcers in insensate skin
- Foot deformities MUST be corrected to allow brace fitting and prevent pressure ulcers on insensate skin

**Scoliosis:**
- Prevalence: approximately 50-80% (increases with higher level and VP shunt)
- Congenital type: Vertebral anomalies (hemivertebra, bar); present at birth; may be rapidly progressive
- Developmental/paralytic type: Due to muscle imbalance and pelvic obliquity; appears after age 5-10; most common type
- ALWAYS evaluate for tethered cord, syringomyelia, or shunt malfunction when scoliosis progresses rapidly
- Bracing delays progression but does not prevent surgery
- Surgical fusion when curve >40-50 degrees and approaching skeletal maturity

> **Clinical Pearl:** New or rapidly progressive scoliosis in spina bifida = ALWAYS evaluate for tethered cord and shunt malfunction. This is a critical management rule.

**Fractures:**
- Common below the level of paralysis due to osteopenia and insensate limbs
- Classic board scenario: Child with spina bifida presents with warm, swollen, erythematous leg -- think FRACTURE first (not cellulitis), especially below the neurological level
- Physeal fractures common in children
- Treatment: Minimal immobilization (splinting/lightweight casting) to avoid further osteopenia from prolonged immobilization

> **Clinical Pearl:** Fractures in spina bifida present as PAINLESS swelling, warmth, and erythema of a limb below the neurological level. This presentation is frequently mistaken for cellulitis or DVT. The key diagnostic clue is the location below the level of sensation. Radiographs confirm the diagnosis.

**Skin Integrity:**
- Insensate skin below the neurological level is at high risk for pressure injury
- Common locations: Ischial tuberosities (wheelchair users), sacrum, feet (in orthoses)
- Prevention: Pressure relief, skin inspection, properly fitting orthotics, wheelchair cushion
- Patients should learn weight shifts every 15-30 minutes and daily skin checks using a mirror

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The orthopedic management of spina bifida requires a fundamentally different philosophy from that used in cerebral palsy. In CP, orthopedic interventions aim to improve motor function (reduce spasticity, correct lever arm dysfunction, optimize gait). In spina bifida, the flaccid paralysis means that orthopedic interventions cannot restore function that was never present -- instead, the goals are to correct deformities that interfere with bracing, prevent pressure injuries, maintain sitting posture, and manage pain.

The decision-making around hip management illustrates this difference clearly. In CP, progressive hip subluxation causes pain and functional decline, making surveillance and early intervention critical. In spina bifida, hip dislocation at L1-L3 levels is expected from the muscle imbalance (strong hip flexors without extensors or abductors), and studies have consistently demonstrated that hip reduction surgery does not improve ambulatory outcomes. The hip will re-dislocate because the underlying muscle imbalance is permanent. Therefore, hip surgery in spina bifida is reserved for symptomatic (painful) dislocations in older patients.

## Section 13: Outcomes and Prognosis

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Survival:**
- With modern multidisciplinary care: >85% survive to adulthood
- Life expectancy: Most individuals now live into their 40s-60s+ with appropriate care
- Leading causes of death: VP shunt malfunction/infection, Chiari II complications, renal failure (historically leading cause, now improved with CIC)
- Overall mortality approximately 24% over 25-year follow-up; continues to increase into young adulthood

**Long-Term Functional Outcomes (25-year follow-up data):**
- 73% had stable motor level compared with infancy
- Ambulatory status declined: approximately 75% ambulatory in early childhood decreased to approximately 50% in young adulthood
- 85% on CIC for bladder management
- 37% required special education
- 50% attended or graduated college; 45% employed
- 18% living independently; none had biological children in the reported cohort

**Cognitive Profile:**
- Average IQ: approximately 80 (range 60-110)
- Generally preserved verbal skills with deficits in:
  - Visuospatial processing
  - Executive function
  - Attention
  - Mathematics
- Hydrocephalus and shunt complications lower IQ scores
- "Cocktail party syndrome": Verbal fluency without depth of understanding (described but not universal)

**Secondary Health Conditions:**
- Obesity: 35-50% prevalence; contributes to loss of ambulation, cardiovascular risk, skin breakdown
- Sleep-disordered breathing: Prevalent due to brainstem dysfunction from Chiari II
- Reproductive health: Males often have erectile dysfunction and fertility issues; females can become pregnant (genetic counseling recommended)
- Depression and anxiety: Common in adolescents and adults

> **Clinical Pearl:** The cognitive profile in spina bifida is characterized by a discrepancy between relatively preserved verbal abilities and deficits in visuospatial processing and executive function. This means that verbal IQ may overestimate overall cognitive capacity, and academic accommodations should address nonverbal learning deficits.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Obesity is one of the most impactful secondary conditions in spina bifida. It contributes to loss of ambulation (increasing energy cost of walking), cardiovascular disease, skin breakdown (increased pressure on insensate skin), and metabolic syndrome. Weight management should be a priority starting in early childhood.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The transition from pediatric to adult care represents a critical vulnerability for individuals with spina bifida. Many adults with spina bifida report difficulty finding adult providers knowledgeable about their condition, and the loss of coordinated multidisciplinary care that characterizes pediatric spina bifida clinics can lead to gaps in surveillance and management.

Structured transition programs, beginning in early adolescence, focus on developing self-management skills (CIC, bowel program, skin checks, medication management), building health literacy, and establishing connections with adult providers. The Spina Bifida Association guidelines emphasize that transition planning should begin by age 12-14 and that self-management competencies should be explicitly taught and tracked.

Employment and independent living outcomes remain below population norms, reflecting the combined impact of motor disability, cognitive challenges, and medical complexity. However, outcomes have improved over recent decades with better educational inclusion, assistive technology, and vocational support. Programs that emphasize self-determination, community participation, and vocational preparation during the school years are associated with better adult outcomes.

Reproductive counseling is important for both males and females with spina bifida. Males frequently have erectile dysfunction and reduced fertility due to neurological involvement of sacral nerve roots. Females can become pregnant, but pregnancy is considered high-risk due to spinal deformity, VP shunt presence, and the increased recurrence risk for NTDs (2-5% in offspring). Preconceptional counseling should include high-dose folic acid supplementation and discussion of genetic testing options.
