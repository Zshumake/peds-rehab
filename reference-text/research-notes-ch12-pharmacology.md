## 1. GUIDELINES FOUND

### 1.1 Spasticity Management Guidelines

- **American Academy of Neurology (AAN) / Child Neurology Society Practice Parameter: Pharmacologic Treatment of Spasticity in Children and Adolescents with Cerebral Palsy (2010).** Delgado MR, Hirtz D, Aisen M, et al. *Neurology*. 2010;74(4):336-343.
  - Level A recommendation: Diazepam for reducing spasticity (short-term use)
  - Level B recommendation: Botulinum toxin A (BoNT-A) for localized spasticity in upper and lower extremity
  - Level B recommendation: Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) for reducing spasticity and improving comfort
  - Level C recommendation: Dantrolene, oral baclofen, tizanidine
  - Insufficient evidence for oral tizanidine in children (extrapolated from adult data)

- **European Consensus Table on the Use of Botulinum Toxin for Children with Cerebral Palsy (Love et al., 2010; Heinen et al., 2010).** *European Journal of Paediatric Neurology*.
  - Recommended maximum doses per session and per muscle
  - Integration into multimodal rehabilitation programs

- **NICE Guideline: Spasticity in Under 19s: Management (CG145, 2012, updated 2016).**
  - Stepwise approach to spasticity management
  - Physical management as foundation
  - Oral medications as adjunct
  - Focal treatments (BoNT-A, phenol) for localized spasticity
  - ITB for generalized spasticity not responding to oral agents

- **American Academy for Cerebral Palsy and Developmental Medicine (AACPDM) Care Pathways:**
  - Spasticity management pathway (ongoing updates)
  - Emphasis on goal-directed functional outcomes

### 1.2 DMD Corticosteroid Guidelines

- **DMD Care Considerations Working Group (Bushby K, Finkel R, Birnkrant DJ, et al., 2010).** "Diagnosis and management of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Part 1 and Part 2." *Lancet Neurology*. 9(1):77-93 and 9(2):177-189.
  - Corticosteroids recommended as standard of care
  - Initiation at age 4-6 years (plateau/decline phase of motor function)
  - Prednisone 0.75 mg/kg/day OR deflazacort 0.9 mg/kg/day
  - Weekend-only dosing regimen as alternative

- **TREAT-NMD Guidelines on Corticosteroid Use in DMD (2016 update).**

### 1.3 SMA Gene Therapy Guidelines

- **SMA Standard of Care Guidelines (Mercuri E, Finkel RS, et al., 2018).** *Neuromuscular Disorders*. 28(7):548-564.
  - Updated to include disease-modifying therapies
  - Nusinersen, onasemnogene abeparvovec, risdiplam recommendations

### 1.4 Pain Management Guidelines

- **WHO Analgesic Ladder — Adapted for Pediatrics.**
- **AAP Policy Statement on Recognition and Management of Pain in Children (2001, reaffirmed).**
- **SPR (Society for Pediatric Radiology) and IASP Guidelines on Pediatric Pain Assessment.**

## 2. LANDMARK TRIALS & PIVOTAL STUDIES

### 2.1 Baclofen / Intrathecal Baclofen

- **Albright AL, Cervi A, Singletary J (1991).** "Intrathecal baclofen for spasticity in cerebral palsy." *JAMA*. 265(11):1418-1422.
  - First major publication establishing ITB efficacy in pediatric CP
  - Demonstrated significant spasticity reduction (Ashworth scale)

- **Butler C, Campbell S (2000).** "Evidence of the effects of intrathecal baclofen for spastic and dystonic cerebral palsy." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 42(9):634-645.
  - Systematic review: ITB effective for reducing spasticity and improving comfort
  - Level of evidence: moderate for spasticity reduction, limited for functional improvement

- **Hoving MA, van Raak EP, Spincemaille GH, et al. (2009).** "Efficacy of intrathecal baclofen therapy in children with intractable spastic cerebral palsy: a randomized controlled trial." *European Journal of Paediatric Neurology*. 13(3):240-246.
  - Dutch RCT: ITB vs placebo in children with severe spastic CP
  - Significant reduction in spasticity and pain
  - Improved ease of care

- **Ward AB (2008).** "Intrathecal baclofen for spasticity in cerebral palsy." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 50(Suppl 4):16.

### 2.2 Botulinum Toxin

- **Koman LA, Mooney JF III, Smith BP, et al. (2000).** "Botulinum toxin type A neuromuscular blockade in the treatment of lower extremity spasticity in cerebral palsy: a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial." *Journal of Pediatric Orthopedics*. 20(1):108-115.
  - Landmark RCT establishing BoNT-A efficacy for equinus gait in CP
  - Significant improvement in Physician Rating Scale and gait

- **Molenaers G, Desloovere K, Fabry G, De Cock P (2006).** "The effects of quantitative gait assessment and botulinum toxin A on musculoskeletal surgery in cerebral palsy patients." *J Bone Joint Surg Am*. 88(1):161-170.
  - Integrated BoNT-A with gait analysis-guided intervention
  - Reduced need for surgical intervention when BoNT-A used early

- **Love SC, Novak I, Kentish M, et al. (2010).** "Botulinum toxin assessment, intervention and after-care for lower limb spasticity in children with cerebral palsy: international consensus statement." *Eur J Neurol*. 17(Suppl 2):9-37.
  - Consensus on dosing, muscle selection, injection technique
  - Integration with therapy programs

- **Heinen F, Desloovere K, Schroeder AS, et al. (2010).** "The updated European Consensus 2009 on the use of Botulinum toxin for children with cerebral palsy." *Eur J Paediatr Neurol*. 14(1):45-66.

### 2.3 Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR)

- **McLaughlin J, Bjornson K, Temkin N, et al. (2002).** "Selective dorsal rhizotomy: meta-analysis of three randomized controlled trials." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 44(1):17-25.
  - Meta-analysis of 3 RCTs (Steinbok 1997, Wright 1998, McLaughlin 1998)
  - SDR + PT superior to PT alone for spasticity reduction
  - Modest functional improvement (GMFM) at 1 year

- **Steinbok P, Reiner AM, Beauchamp R, et al. (1997).** "A randomized clinical trial to compare selective posterior rhizotomy plus physiotherapy with physiotherapy alone in children with spastic diplegic cerebral palsy." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 39(3):178-184.
  - Seminal RCT: SDR + PT vs PT alone
  - Significant spasticity reduction and GMFM improvement in SDR group

- **Park TS, Johnston JM (2006).** "Surgical techniques of selective dorsal rhizotomy for spastic cerebral palsy." *Neurosurg Focus*. 21(2):e7.

### 2.4 DMD Corticosteroid Trials

- **Griggs RC, Moxley RT III, Mendell JR, et al. (1991).** "Prednisone in Duchenne dystrophy: a randomized, controlled trial defining the time course and dose response." *Arch Neurol*. 48(4):383-388.
  - Established 0.75 mg/kg/day as optimal dose
  - Prolonged ambulation by 2-5 years

- **Biggar WD, Harris VA, Eliasoph L, Alman B (2006).** "Long-term benefits of deflazacort treatment for boys with Duchenne muscular dystrophy in their second decade." *Neuromuscul Disord*. 16(4):249-255.
  - Deflazacort associated with less weight gain vs prednisone
  - Similar efficacy in prolonging ambulation
  - Increased cataract risk with deflazacort

- **FOR-DMD Trial (Finding the Optimum Regimen for DMD): McDonald CM, et al. (2022).** *Neurology*.
  - Compared daily prednisone, daily deflazacort, intermittent prednisone
  - All regimens showed benefit; daily deflazacort associated with less weight gain but more cataracts

### 2.5 SMA Gene Therapy Pivotal Trials

- **ENDEAR Trial (Finkel RS, Mercuri E, Darras BT, et al., 2017).** "Nusinersen versus sham control in infantile-onset spinal muscular atrophy." *NEJM*. 377(18):1723-1732.
  - Phase 3 RCT for nusinersen (Spinraza) in SMA Type 1
  - Significant motor milestone achievement vs sham
  - FDA approval December 2016

- **CHERISH Trial (Mercuri E, Darras BT, Chiriboga CA, et al., 2018).** "Nusinersen versus sham control in later-onset spinal muscular atrophy." *NEJM*. 378(7):625-635.
  - Phase 3 RCT for nusinersen in SMA Types 2/3
  - Significant improvement in HFMSE scores

- **STR1VE Trial (Day JW, Finkel RS, Chiriboga CA, et al., 2021).** "Onasemnogene abeparvovec gene therapy for symptomatic infantile-onset spinal muscular atrophy in patients with two copies of SMN2 (STR1VE): an open-label, single-arm, multicentre, phase 3 trial." *Lancet Neurology*. 20(4):284-293.
  - Onasemnogene (Zolgensma) in SMA Type 1
  - Single IV infusion, AAV9 vector
  - FDA approval May 2019

- **FIREFISH Trial (Darras BT, Masson R, Mazurkiewicz-Beldzinska M, et al., 2021).** "Risdiplam-treated infants with type 1 spinal muscular atrophy versus historical controls." *NEJM*. 385(5):427-435.
  - Risdiplam (Evrysdi) oral SMN2 splicing modifier
  - FDA approval August 2020

- **SUNFISH Trial (Mercuri E, et al., 2022).** Risdiplam in SMA Types 2/3.

- **Eteplirsen (Exondys 51):** Mendell JR, et al. (2013, 2016). *Ann Neurol*. FDA accelerated approval September 2016 for DMD with exon 51 amenable mutations.

## 4. EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### 4.1 Spasticity in Cerebral Palsy

- **Prevalence of spasticity in CP:** 70-80% of all CP cases are spastic type
  - Spastic diplegia: ~30-35% of spastic CP
  - Spastic hemiplegia: ~25-30%
  - Spastic quadriplegia: ~25-30%
- **Prevalence of CP overall:** 2.0-3.5 per 1,000 live births (historically); declining to ~1.4-2.0 per 1,000 in some registries (ACPR, 2023)
- **ITB pump implantation rates:** ~3,000-4,000 implants/year in US (adult + pediatric)
- **BoNT-A usage:** Most common focal spasticity intervention in pediatric CP worldwide

### 4.2 Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy

- **Incidence:** 1 in 3,500-5,000 live male births (X-linked recessive)
- **Prevalence:** ~15.9 per 100,000 live male births (CDC Muscular Dystrophy Surveillance, 2019)
- **Natural history without treatment:**
  - Loss of ambulation: ~10-12 years
  - Scoliosis: 90%+ after loss of ambulation
  - Cardiomyopathy: universal by age 18
  - Death: late teens to mid-20s (historically); now 30s with ventilatory support
- **With corticosteroids:** Ambulation prolonged to ~13-15 years

### 4.3 Spinal Muscular Atrophy

- **Incidence:** 1 in 6,000-10,000 live births
- **Carrier frequency:** ~1 in 40-60 individuals
- **SMA Type 1 (Werdnig-Hoffmann):** ~60% of all SMA; onset <6 months; death by age 2 without treatment
- **SMA Type 2:** ~27%; onset 6-18 months; sit independently, never walk
- **SMA Type 3 (Kugelberg-Welander):** ~12%; onset >18 months; walk independently, progressive weakness
- **SMA Type 4:** ~1%; adult onset

## 5. CLINICAL SCALES

### 5.1 Pain Assessment Scales

#### FLACC Scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability)
- **Citation:** Merkel SI, Voepel-Lewis T, Shevchenko JR, Malviya S (1997). "The FLACC: a behavioral scale for scoring postoperative pain in young children." *Pediatric Nursing*. 23(3):293-297.
- **Age range:** 2 months to 7 years (also validated for nonverbal/cognitively impaired patients)
- **Scoring:** 5 categories, each scored 0-2; **total score 0-10**
  - **Face:** 0 = no expression/smile; 1 = occasional grimace/frown; 2 = frequent-constant quivering chin, clenched jaw
  - **Legs:** 0 = normal/relaxed; 1 = uneasy, restless, tense; 2 = kicking/drawn up
  - **Activity:** 0 = lying quietly, normal position; 1 = squirming, shifting, tense; 2 = arched, rigid, jerking
  - **Cry:** 0 = no cry; 1 = moans, whimpers; 2 = crying steadily, screaming
  - **Consolability:** 0 = content, relaxed; 1 = reassured by touch, distractible; 2 = difficult to console
- **Interpretation:**
  - 0 = relaxed/comfortable
  - 1-3 = mild discomfort
  - 4-6 = moderate pain
  - 7-10 = severe pain/discomfort
- **Board pearl:** Revised FLACC (r-FLACC) validated for children with cognitive impairment — individualized descriptors added

#### Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale
- **Citation:** Wong DL, Baker CM (1988). "Pain in children: comparison of assessment scales." *Pediatric Nursing*. 14(1):9-17.
- **Age range:** 3+ years (self-report)
- **Scoring:** 6 faces rated 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 (or 0-5 in original)
- **Board pearl:** Self-report scale; child selects the face that best represents their pain; validated across cultures

#### Numeric Rating Scale (NRS)
- Ages 8+ years; 0-10 verbal/written scale

#### NIPS (Neonatal Infant Pain Scale)
- Neonates; 6 categories; score 0-7

### 5.2 Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS)
- **Citation:** Bohannon RW, Smith MB (1987). "Interrater reliability of a modified Ashworth scale of muscle spasticity." *Physical Therapy*. 67(2):206-207.
- **Scoring:**
  - 0 = no increase in muscle tone
  - 1 = slight increase; catch and release or minimal resistance at end-ROM
  - 1+ = slight increase; catch followed by minimal resistance through remainder (<1/2 ROM)
  - 2 = more marked increase through most of ROM, but part easily moved
  - 3 = considerable increase; passive movement difficult
  - 4 = rigid in flexion or extension
- **Board pearl:** MAS is an ordinal scale measuring resistance to passive stretch; it does NOT distinguish spasticity from contracture or dystonia. It is velocity-independent as typically administered.

### 5.3 Tardieu Scale / Modified Tardieu Scale
- **Citation:** Boyd RN, Graham HK (1999). "Objective measurement of clinical findings in the use of botulinum toxin type A for the management of children with cerebral palsy." *Eur J Neurol*. 6(Suppl 4):S23-S35.
- **Key concept:**
  - **R1** = angle of catch at fast velocity (spastic component)
  - **R2** = passive ROM at slow velocity (muscle length/contracture)
  - **Dynamic component = R2 - R1**
    - Large R2-R1 difference = predominantly spastic (responds to spasticity treatment)
    - Small R2-R1 difference = fixed contracture (needs surgical/mechanical intervention)
- **Board pearl:** Tardieu is velocity-dependent and SUPERIOR to MAS for distinguishing spasticity from contracture. The dynamic component (R2 - R1) guides treatment selection.

### 5.4 Spasticity Quality Scores
- **Modified Ashworth (MAS):** Most commonly used clinically; limited reliability
- **Tardieu:** Better at differentiating spasticity from contracture
- **Barry-Albright Dystonia Scale:** For dystonia in CP (5-point scale, 8 body regions)

## 6. BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD

### 6.1 Spasticity Management Ladder (Board Classic)

**Step-wise approach (MUST KNOW):**
1. **Stretching / Positioning / Serial casting / Therapy** — Always the foundation
2. **Oral medications** — For generalized spasticity
3. **Focal interventions (BoNT-A, phenol/alcohol)** — For localized spasticity
4. **Intrathecal baclofen (ITB)** — For severe generalized spasticity
5. **Selective dorsal rhizotomy (SDR)** — For selected spastic diplegia patients
6. **Orthopedic surgery** — For fixed contractures and bony deformities

### 6.2 Baclofen — Oral vs Intrathecal

#### Oral Baclofen
- **Mechanism:** GABA-B receptor agonist (presynaptic inhibition at spinal cord level)
- **Acts on:** Presynaptic terminals in the dorsal horn; inhibits release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, aspartate)
- **Dosing:** Start 5 mg BID-TID; titrate by 5 mg q3 days; max 80 mg/day (adults); pediatric max ~60 mg/day
- **Side effects:** Sedation, drowsiness, fatigue, hypotonia, dizziness, nausea, constipation
- **Withdrawal:** MUST taper slowly; abrupt withdrawal can cause seizures, hallucinations, rebound spasticity
- **Board pearl:** Poor CNS penetration orally (only ~5% crosses BBB); this is why ITB is more effective

#### Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB)
- **Mechanism:** Same GABA-B agonist, delivered directly to intrathecal space
- **Advantage:** 100-1000x lower dose needed compared to oral; direct CSF delivery
- **Typical dose:** 50-1500 mcg/day (average ~200-400 mcg/day in children)
- **Pump:** Programmable (SynchroMed II by Medtronic); surgically implanted in abdomen; catheter threaded to thoracic intrathecal space
- **Pump refill:** Every 1-6 months depending on dose/concentration; **pump battery life ~5-7 years**
- **Catheter tip level:** T10-T12 for lower extremity spasticity; higher (C5-T2) for upper extremity involvement

#### ITB Trial Protocol (Board Favorite)
- **Purpose:** Screen for response before permanent pump implantation
- **Method:** Lumbar puncture with bolus injection of baclofen
- **Starting dose:** 25-50 mcg (children); 50 mcg (adolescents/adults)
- **Escalation:** If no response, repeat at 75 mcg, then 100 mcg (24 hours apart)
- **Positive trial:** >1 point decrease in Ashworth score in target muscles, lasting 4-8 hours
- **Onset of bolus effect:** 30-60 minutes
- **Peak effect:** 4 hours after bolus
- **Duration:** 4-8 hours
- **Board pearl:** If no response to 100 mcg trial dose, patient is NOT a candidate for ITB pump

#### ITB Withdrawal Syndrome (CRITICAL Board Topic)
- **Cause:** Abrupt cessation (catheter kink/fracture/migration, pump malfunction, missed refill, empty reservoir)
- **Symptoms (early):** Increased spasticity, pruritus, diaphoresis, anxiety
- **Symptoms (progressive):** HIGH FEVER (hyperthermia >40C/104F), tachycardia, labile BP
- **Symptoms (severe/fatal):**
  - **Rhabdomyolysis** (CK elevation, myoglobinuria, renal failure)
  - **DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)**
  - **Multi-organ failure**
  - **DEATH** (case fatality rate up to 10-22% in reported series)
- **Mimics:** Malignant hyperthermia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, serotonin syndrome, autonomic dysreflexia, sepsis
- **Board pearl:** ITB withdrawal is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY and can be FATAL
- **Treatment:**
  - Restore intrathecal baclofen delivery ASAP (evaluate pump/catheter)
  - High-dose oral baclofen (poor substitute but temporizing)
  - IV benzodiazepines (diazepam)
  - Cyproheptadine (case reports)
  - ICU admission, aggressive cooling, IV fluids
  - Dantrolene may be considered for rigidity

#### ITB Overdose
- **Symptoms:** Excessive hypotonia, drowsiness, respiratory depression, coma, bradycardia, hypotension, seizures (rare)
- **Treatment:** Supportive (airway management, ventilation), lumbar puncture to remove CSF (removes drug), physostigmine (reports of benefit, not standard)
- **Board pearl:** ITB overdose causes RESPIRATORY DEPRESSION (can be fatal) but is more readily managed than withdrawal

### 6.3 Botulinum Toxin

#### Mechanism of Action
- Cleaves SNARE proteins (specifically SNAP-25 for BoNT-A) at the neuromuscular junction
- Prevents acetylcholine vesicle fusion and release
- Causes chemical denervation and muscle relaxation
- **Does NOT affect sensory nerves** (Board pearl: no sensory side effects)
- Collateral sprouting occurs; original NMJ recovers in 3-6 months

#### OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) vs AbobotulinumtoxinA (Dysport)

| Feature | OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) | AbobotulinumtoxinA (Dysport) |
|---------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Units** | NOT interchangeable | NOT interchangeable |
| **Conversion ratio** | 1 unit Botox | ~2.5-3 units Dysport (approx.) |
| **Max dose/session** | 12-16 U/kg (up to 400 U total) | 30 U/kg (up to 1000 U total) |
| **Max dose/injection site** | 50 U per site | 125 U per site |
| **Max dose/muscle** | Varies by muscle size | Varies by muscle size |
| **Dilution** | 100 U in 1-2 mL saline typical | 300-500 U in 1.5-2.5 mL |
| **Onset** | 24-72 hours | 24-72 hours |
| **Peak effect** | **2 weeks** | **2 weeks** |
| **Duration** | **3-4 months (avg 3 months)** | **3-4 months** |
| **Reinjection interval** | Minimum 3 months | Minimum 3 months |

#### Dosing Guidelines per Muscle (OnabotulinumtoxinA — approximate)

| Muscle | Dose (U) | Indication |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Gastrocnemius (medial head) | 3-6 U/kg (total both heads) | Equinus |
| Gastrocnemius (lateral head) | (included above) | Equinus |
| Soleus | 2-3 U/kg | Equinus |
| Hamstrings (medial) | 3-6 U/kg (each) | Crouch, scissoring |
| Hamstrings (lateral) | 3-6 U/kg (each) | Crouch |
| Hip adductors | 2-6 U/kg (per side) | Scissoring |
| Tibialis posterior | 1-2 U/kg | Varus foot |
| Biceps brachii | 1-3 U/kg | Elbow flexion |
| Pronator teres | 0.5-1.5 U/kg | Forearm pronation |
| Flexor carpi ulnaris | 0.5-1.5 U/kg | Wrist flexion |

- **Board pearl:** Total maximum dose per session = 12-16 U/kg body weight OR 400 U (whichever is less) for OnabotulinumtoxinA
- **Board pearl:** Minimum interval between sessions = 3 months (to reduce antibody formation)
- **Board pearl:** Antibody formation (neutralizing antibodies) can cause secondary non-response; risk increases with higher doses and shorter intervals

#### Key BoNT-A Side Effects
- Local: Pain at injection site, bruising, excessive weakness of injected muscle
- Systemic (rare): Generalized weakness, dysphagia, respiratory compromise, flu-like symptoms
- **Board pearl:** FDA black box warning (2009) — potential for distant spread of toxin effect; dysphagia and respiratory compromise reported, especially in children with underlying neuromuscular disease
- **NO sensory side effects** (Board classic)

### 6.4 Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR) — Selection Criteria

**Ideal Candidate (Board Favorite):**
- **Age:** 3-8 years (some centers extend to 2-10)
- **Diagnosis:** Spastic DIPLEGIA (bilateral spastic CP)
- **GMFCS Level:** II or III (ambulatory with/without devices)
- **Spasticity type:** Pure spasticity (NO dystonia, NO athetosis, NO ataxia)
- **Preserved underlying strength:** Must have adequate voluntary motor control "underneath" the spasticity
- **Adequate trunk/head control**
- **Cognitive ability:** Able to cooperate with intensive post-op PT
- **No significant fixed contractures** (or contractures correctable at time of SDR)
- **Born premature:** Historically, best responders were ex-premature children with periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) pattern
- **MRI:** PVL pattern favorable; cortical/subcortical injury less favorable

**Contraindications:**
- Dystonia or mixed tone
- Severe weakness underlying spasticity
- Fixed contractures >15-20 degrees
- GMFCS IV-V (non-ambulatory) — relative contraindication; some centers perform for comfort
- Significant ataxia
- Severe cognitive impairment limiting PT participation

**Procedure:**
- Laminectomy (L1-S1 or single-level L1)
- Dorsal rootlets divided based on intraoperative EMG response
- Typically 50-70% of selected dorsal rootlets sectioned
- **Irreversible procedure** — spasticity reduction is permanent

**Outcomes:**
- Significant, lasting spasticity reduction (20+ year follow-up data)
- GMFM improvement (modest, 5-10 point increase)
- Risk: Transient sensory changes, bladder dysfunction (usually temporary), weakness, spinal deformity (kyphosis/lordosis)

### 6.5 Dantrolene Sodium

- **Mechanism:** PERIPHERAL action — acts directly on skeletal muscle
  - Inhibits calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum via ryanodine receptor
  - Reduces excitation-contraction coupling
  - **Board pearl:** ONLY peripherally-acting antispasticity agent (all others act centrally)
- **Dosing:** Start 0.5 mg/kg/day divided BID; titrate to max 12 mg/kg/day (max 400 mg/day)
- **Side effects:**
  - **HEPATOTOXICITY** (Board pearl — most important side effect)
    - Idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity; can be fatal
    - Monitor LFTs at baseline, monthly for first 6 months
    - Risk increases with doses >400 mg/day and duration >60 days
  - Drowsiness, dizziness, diarrhea, weakness, photosensitivity
- **Other use:** Treatment of malignant hyperthermia (IV dantrolene is first-line)
- **Board pearl:** Avoid in patients with active liver disease

### 6.6 Tizanidine

- **Mechanism:** Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist (acts centrally in spinal cord)
  - Reduces presynaptic release of excitatory amino acids
  - Similar mechanism to clonidine
- **Dosing:** Start 1-2 mg at bedtime; titrate to max 36 mg/day (adults); pediatric dosing not well established (off-label use)
- **Side effects:** Sedation (most common), dry mouth, hypotension, dizziness
- **Hepatotoxicity:** LFT monitoring recommended (less hepatotoxic than dantrolene)
- **Board pearl:** Limited pediatric evidence; AAN rated Level C for pediatric spasticity (2010)
- **Drug interactions:** CYP1A2 substrate; contraindicated with fluvoxamine, ciprofloxacin (potent CYP1A2 inhibitors)

### 6.7 Diazepam

- **Mechanism:** GABA-A receptor agonist (enhances GABA-A inhibition at spinal and supraspinal levels)
- **Board pearl:** Different from baclofen which is GABA-B
- **Side effects:** Sedation, cognitive impairment, tolerance, dependence, respiratory depression
- **Use in children:** Short-term; AAN Level A for spasticity reduction but significant side effect profile
- **Withdrawal:** Taper to avoid seizures

### 6.8 Phenol & Alcohol Neurolysis

#### Phenol (5-7% aqueous solution)
- **Mechanism:** Chemical neurolysis — protein denaturation causing Wallerian degeneration
- **Onset:** Immediate (within minutes)
- **Duration:** 3-12 months (variable, often 6 months)
- **Target nerves:** Obturator nerve (hip adductor spasticity), musculocutaneous nerve (elbow flexor spasticity), tibial nerve branches
- **Side effects:**
  - **Dysesthesia/pain** (if mixed sensorimotor nerve is injected) — Board pearl
  - Tissue necrosis at injection site
  - Vascular damage
- **Board pearl:** Phenol should ideally be used on PURE MOTOR nerves (e.g., motor branches of obturator nerve) to avoid painful dysesthesias
- **Advantage over BoNT-A:** Immediate onset, longer duration, lower cost, no antibody formation
- **Disadvantage:** Painful dysesthesia risk, less precise, requires nerve stimulator/US guidance

#### Alcohol (45-100% ethanol)
- Similar mechanism to phenol
- Used less frequently in pediatrics

### 6.9 NSAIDs for Children

- **Ibuprofen:** 5-10 mg/kg/dose q6-8h; max 40 mg/kg/day
  - FDA approved for age >6 months
  - Most commonly used pediatric NSAID
- **Naproxen:** 5-7 mg/kg/dose q12h; max 20 mg/kg/day
  - Age >2 years
  - Longer half-life, BID dosing
- **Ketorolac:** 0.5 mg/kg IV/IM q6h (max 30 mg/dose); max 5 days
  - Short-term acute pain only
- **Acetaminophen:** Not an NSAID but commonly used; 10-15 mg/kg/dose q4-6h; max 75 mg/kg/day
- **Board pearls:**
  - Avoid aspirin in children <19 years (Reye syndrome risk)
  - NSAIDs: GI bleeding, renal toxicity, platelet dysfunction
  - Celecoxib approved for JIA (age >2 years)

### 6.10 AED (Antiepileptic Drug) Side Effects (Board High-Yield)

| AED | Key Side Effects |
|-----|-----------------|
| **Valproic acid** | Hepatotoxicity (<2yr highest risk), pancreatitis, teratogenic (NTDs), weight gain, thrombocytopenia, tremor, hair loss |
| **Carbamazepine** | Hyponatremia (SIADH), aplastic anemia, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (HLA-B*1502 in Asian populations), diplopia, ataxia |
| **Phenytoin** | Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, osteoporosis, cerebellar atrophy, coarsened facies, folate deficiency, teratogenic |
| **Phenobarbital** | Sedation, cognitive impairment, behavioral problems, vitamin D deficiency, osteomalacia |
| **Levetiracetam** | Behavioral/psychiatric (irritability, aggression, depression); relatively benign otherwise |
| **Topiramate** | Cognitive slowing ("dopamax"), metabolic acidosis, nephrolithiasis, weight loss, glaucoma |
| **Lamotrigine** | Stevens-Johnson syndrome (especially with rapid titration or concurrent valproic acid), rash |
| **Vigabatrin** | Irreversible visual field constriction (bilateral concentric); first-line for infantile spasms (tuberous sclerosis) |
| **Gabapentin** | Sedation, dizziness, weight gain; used off-label for neuropathic pain and spasticity |
| **Oxcarbazepine** | Hyponatremia (more than carbamazepine), rash, dizziness |
| **Zonisamide** | Nephrolithiasis, oligohydrosis/hyperthermia, metabolic acidosis |

- **Board pearl:** Valproic acid is CONTRAINDICATED in children <2 years with metabolic disease (highest risk of fatal hepatotoxicity)
- **Board pearl:** Phenytoin — zero-order kinetics; small dose changes cause large serum level changes

### 6.11 DMD Corticosteroid Management

#### Prednisone vs Deflazacort

| Feature | Prednisone | Deflazacort |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Dose** | 0.75 mg/kg/day | 0.9 mg/kg/day |
| **Start age** | 4-6 years (motor plateau/early decline) | 4-6 years |
| **Mechanism** | Glucocorticoid — anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, membrane stabilization | Oxazoline derivative of prednisone |
| **Weight gain** | More significant | Less weight gain (major advantage) |
| **Bone density** | Both reduce BMD; fracture risk | Similar |
| **Cataracts** | Lower risk | HIGHER risk of posterior subcapsular cataracts |
| **Growth suppression** | Both cause short stature | Similar |
| **Behavioral** | Both: mood changes, irritability | Similar |
| **Cushingoid features** | Common | Less pronounced |
| **Benefits** | Prolongs ambulation 2-5 years; preserves respiratory/cardiac function; reduces scoliosis risk | Similar efficacy |
| **FDA approval** | Generic, widely available | FDA approved for DMD (2017) |

- **Board pearl:** Start corticosteroids when motor function plateaus or begins to decline (typically age 4-6, or when no longer gaining motor milestones)
- **Board pearl:** Side effect monitoring: growth, weight, BMD (DEXA), ophthalmology (cataracts), BP, glucose, behavioral screening, vertebral fracture surveillance
- **Alternative regimens:** Weekend-only dosing (10 mg/kg/weekend), intermittent 10-days-on/10-days-off — may reduce side effects with some preserved benefit

### 6.12 Gene Therapies for Neuromuscular Disease (CRITICAL Board Content)

#### Nusinersen (Spinraza) — SMA
- **Mechanism:** Antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) that modifies SMN2 pre-mRNA splicing; promotes inclusion of exon 7, producing functional full-length SMN protein
- **Route:** INTRATHECAL injection
- **Dosing schedule:** Loading doses at Days 0, 14, 28, 63; then maintenance every 4 months
- **FDA approval:** December 23, 2016 (first FDA-approved therapy for SMA)
- **All types:** Approved for all SMA types (infantile and later onset)
- **Board pearl:** Requires repeated lumbar punctures; challenging in patients with severe scoliosis or spinal fusion (may need fluoroscopy or CT-guided injection)

#### Onasemnogene Abeparvovec (Zolgensma) — SMA
- **Mechanism:** AAV9 viral vector delivering functional SMN1 gene; gene REPLACEMENT therapy
- **Route:** Single IV infusion (ONE-TIME dose)
- **FDA approval:** May 24, 2019
- **Age:** Approved for <2 years of age with SMA (any type, bi-allelic SMN1 mutation)
- **Dose:** 1.1 x 10^14 vg/kg (based on body weight)
- **Monitoring:** Liver function (prednisolone pre-treatment and monitoring for hepatotoxicity), platelet count (thrombocytopenia/TMA risk)
- **Board pearl:** MOST EXPENSIVE drug at launch (~$2.1 million per dose); single administration
- **Board pearl:** AAV9 crosses blood-brain barrier; systemic IV delivery reaches motor neurons

#### Risdiplam (Evrysdi) — SMA
- **Mechanism:** Small molecule SMN2 splicing modifier (same target as nusinersen but different mechanism — not an ASO)
- **Route:** ORAL (liquid, daily)
- **FDA approval:** August 7, 2020
- **Age:** Approved for age 2 months and older
- **Board pearl:** Only ORAL SMA therapy; administered at home; does not require intrathecal injection or IV infusion
- **Advantage:** Systemic distribution (CNS + peripheral); no injection required

#### Eteplirsen (Exondys 51) — DMD
- **Mechanism:** Phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligomer (PMO); exon 51 SKIPPING antisense oligonucleotide
  - Causes exon 51 to be skipped during mRNA processing
  - Produces truncated but partially functional dystrophin (Becker-like)
- **Route:** IV infusion weekly
- **FDA approval:** September 19, 2016 (accelerated approval; controversial — limited efficacy data)
- **Applicable to:** ~13% of DMD patients with mutations amenable to exon 51 skipping
- **Board pearl:** Modest dystrophin production (<1% of normal in studies); clinical benefit debated

### 6.13 Growth Plate Medication Effects

- **Corticosteroids (chronic):**
  - Growth suppression — inhibit GH axis, reduce IGF-1
  - Premature closure of growth plates is NOT typical; rather, growth velocity slows
  - Osteopenia → increased fracture risk (vertebral compression fractures common in DMD on steroids)
- **Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin):**
  - Tendon damage and potential effect on growing cartilage
  - FDA black box warning; generally avoided in <18 years (except specific indications)
  - Board pearl: Theoretical risk to growth plate cartilage
- **Retinoids (isotretinoin):**
  - Premature epiphyseal closure (case reports)
  - Hyperostosis
- **Chemotherapy agents:**
  - Methotrexate: can affect growth plates
  - Radiation: premature closure of irradiated growth plates; asymmetric growth
- **Excessive Vitamin A:** Premature closure of growth plates
- **GH (Growth Hormone):**
  - Does NOT cause premature closure; accelerates growth velocity
  - Used therapeutically in GH deficiency, Turner syndrome, Prader-Willi, SGA
- **Sex steroids (testosterone, estrogen):**
  - Accelerate skeletal maturation and eventual epiphyseal fusion
  - Precocious puberty → early fusion → short adult stature
- **Bisphosphonates (pamidronate, zoledronic acid):**
  - Used in OI, DMD-related osteoporosis
  - No direct growth plate effect but increase BMD
