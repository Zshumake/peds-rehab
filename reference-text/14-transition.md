## 14.1 IDEA Part C: Early Intervention (Birth to 3 Years)

`[BOARD]`

### 14.1.1 Legislative Framework

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), most recently reauthorized in 2004 (PL 108-446), is the cornerstone federal law governing special education and early intervention services in the United States. Part C of IDEA establishes the federal framework for early intervention services for infants and toddlers from birth through age 2 (up to the third birthday).

IDEA traces its origins to the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 (PL 94-142), which first guaranteed a free appropriate public education to children with disabilities. The law was renamed IDEA in 1990 (PL 101-476) and has undergone subsequent reauthorizations that expanded protections and services.

### 14.1.2 Key Features of Part C

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Age range | Birth to 3 years (0-36 months) |
| Service plan | IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan) |
| Focus | Family-centered; the family is the unit of service |
| Setting | Natural environments (home, daycare, community) |
| Eligibility | Developmental delay (state-defined criteria), established condition with high probability of delay, or at-risk status (some states) |
| Services available | PT, OT, speech therapy, special instruction, audiology, vision services, nutrition, social work, service coordination |
| Service coordinator | Required; single point of contact for the family |
| Cost | Free evaluation and assessment; states may charge sliding-scale fees for services |
| Lead agency | Varies by state (may be health department, education department, or developmental disabilities agency) |
| Timeline | Evaluation must be completed within 45 days of referral; IFSP developed within 45 days |
| Review schedule | Every 6 months (interim review); annually (full review) |
| Transition planning | Must begin at age 2.5 (at least 90 days before the third birthday) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The defining feature of Part C is its family-centered orientation. The IFSP addresses family outcomes and priorities, not just child-focused goals. Services are delivered in natural environments --- the settings where the child and family naturally spend their time --- rather than in clinical or school-based settings. This philosophical distinction from Part B (which is child-centered and school-based) is a core board examination concept.

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 14.1.3 Epidemiology and Referral

Approximately 400,000-430,000 infants and toddlers receive Part C services annually in the United States, representing roughly 3.4% of the birth-to-three population. Common referral sources include primary care providers (the most frequent source), neonatal intensive care units, parents, and childcare providers.

Qualifying conditions frequently encountered in rehabilitation practice include prematurity and low birth weight, motor developmental delay (often the presenting concern in cerebral palsy), communication delay, cognitive delay, and established conditions such as Down syndrome, cerebral palsy, hearing loss, and genetic syndromes. Each state defines its own eligibility criteria for developmental delay, leading to significant interstate variability in the percentage of children who qualify.

Evidence consistently supports the efficacy of early intervention. The Cochrane review by Spittle et al. (2015) demonstrated that early developmental intervention programs improve cognitive outcomes at infant and preschool ages, though effects on motor outcomes are less clear. Guralnick (2011) articulated a developmental systems model explaining that early intervention works primarily by optimizing family patterns of interaction, which serve as the mediating variable between the intervention and child developmental outcomes.

## 14.2 IDEA Part B: Special Education (Ages 3-21)

`[BOARD]`

### 14.2.1 Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Age range | 3-21 years (3 through the 21st birthday) |
| Service plan | IEP (Individualized Education Program) |
| Focus | Child/student-centered; education-focused |
| Setting | Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) |
| Eligibility | Must meet criteria for one of 13 disability categories AND require specially designed instruction |
| Services | Special education plus related services (PT, OT, SLP, counseling, transportation, assistive technology) |
| IEP team | Parent, regular education teacher, special education teacher, LEA representative, evaluation specialist, others as needed |
| Transition planning | Must begin by age 16 (some states require age 14) |
| Review | Annual IEP review; re-evaluation every 3 years |
| Cost | Free --- FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education) is guaranteed |

### 14.2.2 The 13 IDEA Disability Categories

Approximately 7.5 million students ages 3-21 (roughly 15% of all public school students) receive special education services under IDEA Part B. The distribution across disability categories reflects the breadth of conditions served:

| Category | Approximate Percentage |
|----------|----------------------|
| Specific Learning Disability | 33% |
| Speech or Language Impairment | 19% |
| Other Health Impairment (includes ADHD) | 15% |
| Autism | 12% (rapidly increasing) |
| Developmental Delay (ages 3-9 only) | 7% |
| Intellectual Disability | 6% |
| Emotional Disturbance | 5% |
| Multiple Disabilities | 2% |
| Orthopedic Impairment | 0.7% |
| Traumatic Brain Injury | 0.4% |

### 14.2.3 Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)

`[BOARD]`

FAPE is the legal standard that defines the educational entitlement under IDEA. Two Supreme Court decisions frame the interpretation of what constitutes an "appropriate" education:

**Board of Education v. Rowley (1982):** Established that FAPE requires provision of educational services sufficient to provide "some educational benefit." The Court held that IDEA does not guarantee the "best" possible education.

**Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District (2017):** Raised the FAPE standard significantly. The Court ruled that an IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." This rejected the prior "merely more than de minimis" standard and required meaningful, individualized progress.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Endrew F. decision is particularly important for children with severe disabilities. Under the older Rowley standard, a child making any measurable progress --- no matter how trivial --- was arguably receiving FAPE. Under Endrew F., progress must be "appropriate in light of the child's circumstances," requiring the IEP team to develop challenging, individualized objectives even for children with the most significant disabilities.

### 14.2.4 Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

IDEA mandates that children with disabilities be educated alongside non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Removal from the general education setting is justified only when supplementary aids and services cannot achieve satisfactory results in the general classroom.

The LRE continuum ranges from least to most restrictive:
1. General education classroom with supports
2. General education with resource room services
3. Self-contained special education classroom
4. Separate school for students with disabilities
5. Homebound instruction
6. Hospital or institutional setting

### 14.2.5 Related Services

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Related services under IDEA are defined as supportive services required for a child to benefit from special education. They include physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language pathology, audiology, psychological services, social work, school nursing services, counseling, recreation, transportation, orientation and mobility services, and assistive technology services and devices.

**Key legal distinctions:**
- Related services must be educationally necessary, not medically necessary. A child who needs physical therapy to access the school environment qualifies; a child who needs PT solely for community walking may not.
- Medical services under IDEA are limited to diagnostic purposes only. Ongoing medical treatment is not a related service.
- **Cedar Rapids v. Garret F. (1999):** If a health service can be provided by a qualified nurse (rather than a physician), it is a related service under IDEA. This case expanded the scope of school health services to include ventilator management, catheterization, and suctioning for medically fragile students.
- **Irving ISD v. Tatro (1984):** Clean intermittent catheterization is a related service under IDEA.

### 14.2.6 Assistive Technology Under IDEA

Both assistive technology devices and services must be considered for every child with an IEP. If AT is determined necessary for the child to receive FAPE, the school district must provide it at no cost to the family. An AT device is broadly defined as any item that increases, maintains, or improves the functional capability of a child with a disability.

## 14.3 IDEA Part C versus Part B: Key Comparisons

`[BOARD]`

| Feature | Part C (Birth to 3) | Part B (Ages 3-21) |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Service plan | IFSP | IEP |
| Focus | Family-centered | Student/child-centered |
| Setting | Natural environments | Least Restrictive Environment |
| Unit of service | Family | Child |
| Review | Every 6 months / annually | Annually / 3-year re-evaluation |
| Lead agency | Varies by state | State education agency |
| Transition | To Part B at age 3 | To adult services at age 21 (or graduation) |
| Cost | May have family cost sharing | Free (FAPE guaranteed) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The transition from Part C to Part B at age 3 is one of the most significant service transitions in early childhood. It involves a shift from family-centered to child-centered services, from natural environments to school-based settings, and from the IFSP to the IEP. Transition planning must begin at least 90 days before the child's third birthday. Families often experience this transition as a loss of personalized, home-based services, and rehabilitation professionals play an important role in preparing families for this shift.

## 14.4 Section 504 Plans

`[BOARD]`

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 U.S.C. Section 794) is a civil rights statute that prohibits disability discrimination in programs receiving federal funding. In the school context, Section 504 provides protections and accommodations for students who have a disability but may not qualify for special education services under IDEA.

### 14.4.1 Section 504 versus IEP Comparison

| Feature | Section 504 Plan | IEP (IDEA Part B) |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Governing law | Rehabilitation Act (1973) | IDEA (2004) |
| Type of law | Civil rights / anti-discrimination | Education entitlement |
| Eligibility | Physical or mental impairment substantially limiting one or more major life activities | Must meet 1 of 13 disability categories AND need specially designed instruction |
| Breadth | Broader (more students qualify) | Narrower (more specific criteria) |
| Services | Accommodations and modifications | Specially designed instruction + related services |
| Written plan | 504 plan (less formally structured) | IEP (detailed, formal document) |
| Procedural safeguards | Less extensive | Extensive (due process, mediation) |
| Federal funding | No additional funding | Federal IDEA funding |

**Common 504 plan scenarios:** Diabetes (blood glucose monitoring, snack accommodations), epilepsy (seizure action plan, medication administration), ADHD without need for specially designed instruction (preferential seating, extended test time), food allergies (allergen-free environment), asthma (inhaler access, activity modifications).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Section 504 eligibility is broader than IDEA eligibility. A child who does not qualify for an IEP (because they do not need specially designed instruction) may still qualify for a 504 plan (because their disability substantially limits a major life activity such as learning, breathing, or walking). Conversely, every child with an IEP is automatically protected under Section 504, though the reverse is not true.

## 14.5 Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

`[BOARD]`

The ADA (PL 101-336, 1990; amended by the ADA Amendments Act, 2008) extends disability protections beyond federally funded programs to virtually all public and private settings.

| Title | Scope | Key Provisions |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| I | Employment | Employers with 15+ employees must provide reasonable accommodations; prohibition of discrimination in hiring, firing, and advancement |
| II | State and local government | Accessible public services, transportation, and public schools |
| III | Public accommodations | Private entities (restaurants, hotels, medical offices, stores) must be architecturally accessible |
| IV | Telecommunications | Relay services for individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing |
| V | Miscellaneous | Anti-retaliation, attorney fees, other provisions |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ADA is broader than IDEA in scope --- it applies to employment, public services, private businesses, and telecommunications, not just education. For youth transitioning to adulthood, the ADA becomes the primary disability rights law governing workplace accommodations, community access, and healthcare access. Understanding this shift from IDEA (which ends at age 21 or graduation) to ADA (which provides lifelong protections) is essential for effective transition planning.

## 14.6 Medical Home Model

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The AAP Medical Home model (AAP, 2002) defines an approach to primary care delivery that is accessible, continuous, comprehensive, family-centered, coordinated, compassionate, and culturally effective. For children with disabilities, the medical home serves as the hub for coordinating care across medical specialists, rehabilitation services, educational programs, and community resources.

**Key principles:**
- Physician-directed, team-based care
- Whole-child approach addressing medical, developmental, behavioral, and psychosocial needs
- Care coordination across settings and providers
- Enhanced access (same-day appointments, after-hours availability)
- Quality improvement and data-driven care

> **Clinical Pearl:** The medical home is not a physical location --- it is a model of delivering primary care. The concept emphasizes that a physician or practice partners with the family to ensure that all of the child's medical and nonmedical needs are addressed. For children receiving rehabilitation services, the medical home provides the longitudinal relationship and care coordination that ensures interventions are aligned across therapy, school, and specialty care settings.

## 14.7 Got Transition: 6 Core Elements of Health Care Transition

`[BOARD]`

The Got Transition framework, developed by the National Center for Health Care Transition Improvement (funded by the Maternal and Child Health Bureau), provides a structured approach to healthcare transition endorsed jointly by the AAP, ACP, and AAFP (White et al., 2018).

| Core Element | Target Age | Description |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| 1. Transition Policy | 12-14 years | Establish an office-wide transition policy; introduce the concept of transition to youth and families |
| 2. Transition Tracking and Monitoring | 14-18 years | Implement a tracking system to monitor progress; identify youth approaching transition age |
| 3. Transition Readiness | 14-18 years | Assess readiness using validated tools; educate youth in self-management and self-advocacy |
| 4. Transition Planning | 14-18 years | Develop a written transition plan; identify adult providers; create a portable medical summary |
| 5. Transfer of Care | 18-21 years | Execute the transfer to adult healthcare; send records; establish communication between pediatric and adult providers |
| 6. Transfer Completion | 18-26 years | Confirm integration into adult care system; obtain feedback from young adult and adult provider |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Transition planning should begin at age 12-14 --- not at age 18 when transfer is imminent. This early start allows time to build self-management skills, foster self-advocacy, and establish a relationship with the concept of adult healthcare. Research consistently shows that structured transition programs improve disease knowledge, self-management skills, and patient satisfaction, though evidence for impact on hard health outcomes remains limited (Schmidt et al., 2020).

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

National survey data from Lotstein et al. (2005) found that only approximately 50% of youth with special health care needs received any form of transition planning services, with significant disparities by race, income, and condition severity. These data underscore the gap between recommended practice and actual implementation, though the Got Transition framework has provided increasingly practical tools for clinical implementation.

## 14.8 Vocational Rehabilitation and the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act

`[BOARD]`

### 14.8.1 WIOA Overview

The Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA, PL 113-128, 2014) replaced the Workforce Investment Act and significantly expanded vocational rehabilitation (VR) services for youth and young adults with disabilities. VR is a state-federal partnership, with services federally funded and state-administered.

**Eligibility:** An individual with a disability that constitutes or results in a substantial impediment to employment and who requires VR services to prepare for, secure, retain, or regain employment.

**Order of selection:** When VR resources are limited (which is common), individuals with the most significant disabilities are served first.

### 14.8.2 Five Required Pre-Employment Transition Services

WIOA mandates that state VR agencies set aside 15% of their federal funding allocation for pre-employment transition services (Pre-ETS) targeting students with disabilities ages 14-21. The five required services are:

1. **Job exploration counseling:** Career interest inventories, labor market information, workplace tours
2. **Work-based learning experiences:** Internships, apprenticeships, community-based work experiences, on-the-job training
3. **Counseling on post-secondary enrollment opportunities:** College exploration, vocational training program information, application assistance
4. **Workplace readiness training:** Social skills, independent living skills, financial literacy, workplace norms
5. **Instruction in self-advocacy:** Understanding rights, communicating needs, requesting accommodations

> **Clinical Pearl:** The WIOA Pre-ETS mandate represents a fundamental shift toward earlier, proactive vocational preparation. Before WIOA, VR services were largely reactive --- individuals applied after leaving school. WIOA requires VR agencies to engage students while they are still in secondary school, creating a more seamless pathway from education to employment.

## 14.9 SSI and SSDI Eligibility

`[BOARD]`

### 14.9.1 Supplemental Security Income (SSI) for Children

SSI provides cash benefits to children with disabilities in families with limited income and resources.

**Requirements:**
- **Disability standard:** Marked and severe functional limitations expected to last more than 12 months (or result in death)
- **Means-tested:** Family income and resources must fall below established thresholds
- **Functional assessment domains:** Acquiring and using information; attending and completing tasks; interacting and relating with others; moving about and manipulating objects; caring for yourself; health and physical well-being

**Age-18 redetermination:** When a child receiving SSI turns 18, eligibility is redetermined using adult disability criteria. Critically, at age 18, only the individual's own income and resources are counted (not the family's). Many children who qualified for SSI based on family income lose eligibility under adult criteria because their disability may not meet the adult standard, while others who were previously ineligible due to family income may now qualify based on their own limited resources.

### 14.9.2 SSDI: Disabled Adult Child (DAC) Benefits

SSDI Disabled Adult Child benefits are available to adults who became disabled before age 22. These benefits are drawn from a parent's Social Security earnings record (the parent must be retired, disabled, or deceased). Unlike SSI, DAC benefits are not means-tested.

> **Clinical Pearl:** DAC benefits represent a critical safety net for adults with childhood-onset disabilities who cannot work enough to establish their own SSDI eligibility. Because DAC benefits are based on the parent's work history rather than the individual's, they are often substantially higher than SSI payments. Rehabilitation physicians should be aware of this benefit pathway and ensure it is discussed during transition planning, as documentation of disability onset before age 22 must be established.

## 14.10 Guardianship and Supported Decision-Making

`[BOARD]`

### 14.10.1 The Decision-Making Continuum

Decision-making supports for adults with disabilities exist along a continuum from least to most restrictive:

| Level | Support Type | Key Features |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Informal supports | Natural family assistance; no legal designation |
| 2 | Supported Decision-Making (SDM) | Formal agreement; individual retains ALL legal rights |
| 3 | Power of Attorney | Individual grants authority voluntarily; revocable |
| 4 | Representative Payee | Manages Social Security benefits only |
| 5 | Health Care Proxy / Advance Directive | Medical decisions only |
| 6 | Conservatorship | Financial decisions only (in some states) |
| 7 | Limited Guardianship | Authority in specified areas only |
| 8 | Full (Plenary) Guardianship | Guardian makes ALL decisions; most restrictive |

### 14.10.2 Full Guardianship

Full guardianship involves a court proceeding in which an individual is determined to lack capacity, and a guardian is appointed to make all decisions on their behalf --- medical, financial, legal, and residential. The individual loses their legal right to make independent decisions.

### 14.10.3 Supported Decision-Making

Supported Decision-Making (SDM) represents a paradigm shift from substituted judgment to supported autonomy. Under SDM, the individual retains all legal decision-making authority but designates trusted supporters who help them understand information, weigh options, and communicate decisions. Supporters may not make decisions for the person or override their choices.

Texas became the first state to pass SDM legislation in 2015, and more than 15 states have since enacted similar laws. The National Council on Disability has advocated strongly for SDM as a less restrictive alternative to guardianship (2018 report).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Guardianship is the most restrictive decision-making option and should be a last resort. The trend in disability rights and law is toward the least restrictive alternative that enables the individual to exercise as much autonomy as possible. Rehabilitation physicians should understand the full continuum and advocate for the least restrictive option appropriate to each patient's circumstances. Many young adults with disabilities who would have been placed under full guardianship in the past can be effectively supported through SDM agreements.

## 14.11 Post-Secondary Education for Students with Intellectual Disability

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 14.11.1 Think College Programs

The Think College National Coordinating Center tracks more than 300 post-secondary education programs for students with intellectual disability across the United States. These programs were made possible by the Higher Education Opportunity Act of 2008 (HEOA, PL 110-315), which created Comprehensive Transition and Postsecondary (CTP) programs and extended federal financial aid eligibility (Pell Grants, Federal Work-Study) to students with intellectual disability enrolled in approved CTP programs.

**Program models:**
- **Inclusive individual support:** Students are fully included in regular college courses with individualized supports (academic coaches, tutors, peer mentors)
- **Mixed/hybrid:** Combination of inclusive regular courses and specialized courses designed for students with intellectual disability
- **Substantially separate:** Primarily separate courses and programming on a college campus

Research demonstrates that participation in inclusive post-secondary education is associated with improved employment outcomes and greater independent living achievement compared with non-participants, supporting the value of these programs beyond their educational content.

## 14.12 Transition Planning Under IDEA

`[BOARD]`

IDEA Part B mandates that beginning no later than age 16 (some states require age 14), the IEP must include:

1. **Appropriate measurable postsecondary goals** in education/training, employment, and (where appropriate) independent living
2. **Transition services** needed to reach those goals
3. **Course of study** aligned with postsecondary goals
4. **Interagency involvement** (VR, community agencies, adult service providers)

The student must be invited to attend the IEP meeting when transition goals are being discussed. At the age of majority (18 in most states), educational rights transfer from the parent to the student, unless guardianship has been established.

When a student exits IDEA eligibility (through graduation or aging out at age 21), the school must provide a Summary of Performance (SOP) documenting the student's academic achievement, functional performance, and recommendations to assist in meeting postsecondary goals.

## 14.13 Key Legal Cases Affecting Special Education

`[BOARD]`

| Case | Year | Key Ruling |
|------|------|-----------|
| Board of Education v. Rowley | 1982 | FAPE requires "some educational benefit" |
| Endrew F. v. Douglas County | 2017 | FAPE must enable progress "appropriate in light of child's circumstances" |
| Cedar Rapids v. Garret F. | 1999 | Services performable by a nurse (not physician) are related services under IDEA |
| Irving ISD v. Tatro | 1984 | Clean intermittent catheterization is a related service |
| Honig v. Doe | 1988 | Cannot expel or suspend a student for more than 10 days if behavior is a manifestation of the disability |
| Timothy W. v. Rochester | 1989 | Zero-reject principle: ALL children are entitled to education regardless of disability severity |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Timothy W. v. Rochester decision established the zero-reject principle, ruling that even a child in a persistent vegetative state is entitled to public education under IDEA. This case eliminated any "severity threshold" for educational eligibility. Rehabilitation physicians may be asked to provide documentation supporting the educational needs of children with the most severe disabilities, and this ruling ensures those children cannot be excluded from services.

## 14.14 Aging with Childhood-Onset Disability

`[BOARD]`

### 14.14.1 Cerebral Palsy

Adults with cerebral palsy face a constellation of aging-related health concerns that differ from the general population, largely because of the cumulative effects of abnormal biomechanics, chronic muscle imbalance, and decades of compensatory movement patterns.

**Life expectancy:** GMFCS I-III: near-normal. GMFCS IV-V: significantly reduced, with 50-60% surviving to ages 30-40.

**Key aging-related issues:**

| Concern | Prevalence | Details |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Neck pain | 60-80% | Most common musculoskeletal complaint in adults with CP; caused by abnormal posturing, cervical degenerative disease, prolonged wheelchair use; cervical myelopathy may develop, particularly in dyskinetic CP |
| Chronic pain | 50-75% | Back, hip (from subluxation or osteoarthritis), and knee pain; multifactorial |
| Fatigue | >50% | Major contributor to functional decline; related to excessive energy expenditure during movement |
| Functional decline | 25-40% | Ambulatory adults report declining walking ability by ages 30-40; many transition from community ambulation to wheelchair use |
| Depression | 2-3x general rate | Under-recognized and under-treated |
| Osteoporosis | Increased prevalence | Especially in non-ambulatory individuals; increased fracture risk |
| Dysphagia | May worsen | Increased aspiration pneumonia risk with aging |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Neck pain is the most common musculoskeletal complaint in adults with CP, not back or hip pain. This finding from the landmark Murphy et al. (1995) study is a frequently tested board fact. The mechanism involves chronic abnormal head and neck posturing, accelerated cervical degenerative changes, and the effects of prolonged wheelchair use on cervical spine alignment. In patients with dyskinetic CP and cervical dystonia, cervical myelopathy may develop and requires monitoring.

### 14.14.2 Spina Bifida

`[BOARD]`

Adults with spina bifida face unique challenges related to the multisystem nature of their condition, with urological complications representing the single greatest threat to longevity after the childhood period.

**Life expectancy:** Approximately 70-80% survive to adulthood with current care.

**Key aging-related issues:**

| Concern | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Urologic mortality | ~40% of deaths between ages 5-30 are urologic/renal in origin (chronic renal failure from reflux, hydronephrosis, recurrent pyelonephritis); lifelong urologic monitoring essential |
| Shunt complications | Second major cause of mortality; infection, malfunction requiring revision (96% need at least one revision; 86% need two or more) |
| Latex allergy | 40-70% prevalence; due to repeated surgical and catheterization exposures; requires latex-free precautions for all procedures |
| Obesity | >50% prevalence; reduced mobility, lower metabolic rate, dietary factors |
| Pressure injuries | Insensate skin below lesion level creates lifelong risk; requires education and surveillance |
| Tethered cord recurrence | 15-30% after initial repair; presents with pain, progressive weakness, scoliosis progression, changing bladder function |
| Chiari II malformation | Present in >95% with myelomeningocele; 5-10% become symptomatic (brainstem compression, syringomyelia) |
| Sexual health | Males: ~80% erectile dysfunction, most infertile. Females: fertility preserved but high-risk pregnancies |
| Ambulatory decline | Many children who walk with orthotics transition to wheelchair in adolescence/adulthood due to energy cost |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Approximately 40% of deaths in individuals with spina bifida between ages 5 and 30 are attributable to urologic and renal complications. This makes lifelong urologic surveillance --- including renal ultrasound, urodynamic studies, and monitoring of clean intermittent catheterization compliance --- among the most important elements of spina bifida care. Shunt malfunction is the second major cause of mortality and must be suspected whenever a patient presents with headache, altered mental status, or neurological deterioration.

### 14.14.3 Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy

`[BOARD]`

The natural history of DMD has been transformed by advances in cardiac, respiratory, and pharmacologic management, with median life expectancy now extending into the late twenties to early thirties for individuals born after 1990.

**Key aging-related issues:**

| Concern | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Cardiomyopathy | Universal by age 18; leading cause of death; dilated cardiomyopathy progresses insidiously; ACE inhibitors/ARBs started at diagnosis of dysfunction or prophylactically; cardiac transplant increasingly considered |
| Respiratory decline | Progressive FVC decline; FVC <50% triggers nocturnal ventilation (BiPAP); FVC <30% or daytime hypercapnia necessitates continuous ventilation; cough assist when peak cough flow <270 L/min |
| Loss of ambulation | ~12-15 years with corticosteroid therapy (vs 10-12 without) |
| Scoliosis | >90% after loss of ambulation; posterior spinal fusion when curve >20-25 degrees if still growing; must be performed before FVC declines excessively |
| Bone health | Osteoporosis from non-ambulation + chronic corticosteroid use; vertebral and long bone fractures common |
| GI complications | Constipation, gastroparesis, nutritional decline; gastrostomy may be needed |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The anesthetic implications of DMD are critically important. Succinylcholine is absolutely contraindicated because it can trigger fatal hyperkalemia and rhabdomyolysis in patients with dystrophinopathies. Potent inhaled halogenated anesthetics also carry significant risk. Total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA) is the recommended approach. Any DMD patient --- even those not yet diagnosed --- who receives succinylcholine may present with cardiac arrest as the initial presentation. This principle extends to female carriers of DMD mutations as well.

## 14.15 Employment Outcomes in Disability

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 14.15.1 General Employment Data

Bureau of Labor Statistics data (2023) document a persistent employment gap: approximately 22.5% of persons with disabilities are employed, compared with approximately 65.8% of the non-disabled population. For adults with intellectual and developmental disabilities, the National Core Indicators report an integrated employment rate of approximately 19%.

### 14.15.2 Vocational Predictors in Cerebral Palsy

`[BOARD]`

Research has identified four key predictors of vocational and employment outcomes in adults with cerebral palsy (Michelsen et al., 2006; Huang et al., 2013):

1. **Cognitive ability / IQ:** The single strongest predictor of employment
2. **Ambulation status:** Community ambulation correlates with higher employment rates
3. **Speech / Communication ability:** Intelligible speech predicts better vocational outcomes
4. **Hand function / Manual ability:** MACS level and fine motor skill for workplace tasks

Employment rates in CP range from approximately 30-50% depending on the population studied and the country, with higher rates in those with milder functional limitations.

> **Clinical Pearl:** When counseling families about long-term vocational prospects for a child with cerebral palsy, cognitive function is the single most important predictor of eventual employment. This finding has practical implications: ensuring that cognitive development is maximized through appropriate educational programming, communication supports, and management of seizures and other conditions that may impair cognition is, in a sense, vocational preparation.

### 14.15.3 Employment for Youth with Disabilities

Approximately 65-70% of youth with disabilities graduate from high school (compared with approximately 85% in the general population). Within four years of leaving high school, approximately 34% of young adults with disabilities are employed, and approximately 34% attend some form of post-secondary education. Independent living rates vary widely by disability type, with approximately 15-25% of adults with intellectual disability living independently.

## 14.16 Summary: The Transition Landscape

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The transition from pediatric to adult services involves navigating multiple simultaneous shifts across healthcare, education, employment, independent living, and legal decision-making. The table below summarizes the key service systems and their transition points.

| Domain | Pediatric System | Transition | Adult System |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|--------------|
| Healthcare | Pediatric medical home | Got Transition 6 Core Elements (ages 12-26) | Adult primary care + specialists |
| Education | IDEA Part B (ages 3-21) | IEP transition planning (age 16+) | ADA Title II/III; post-secondary supports |
| Employment | Pre-ETS under WIOA (ages 14-21) | VR transition services | VR + ADA Title I workplace accommodations |
| Income support | SSI (family means-tested) | Age-18 redetermination | SSI (individual) or SSDI/DAC benefits |
| Decision-making | Parent authority | Guardianship evaluation vs SDM | SDM agreement, POA, limited/full guardianship |
| Insurance | Parents' plan or Medicaid | ACA coverage to age 26 on parents' plan | Medicaid, SSDI Medicare, employer insurance |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Transition is not a single event but a process that unfolds over a decade or more. The most effective transition programs begin early (age 12-14), are systematic (using frameworks like the 6 Core Elements), are youth-directed (building self-advocacy and self-management skills), and are coordinated across healthcare, education, and community service systems. The rehabilitation physician's role is to serve as a navigator across these systems, ensuring that the medical, functional, and participatory needs of the young adult are addressed holistically.
