## 8.1 Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics and Epidemiology

Down syndrome is the most common chromosomal cause of intellectual disability, occurring in approximately 1 in 700 live births. The overwhelming majority (approximately 95 percent) result from nondisjunction producing full trisomy 21, while approximately 3 to 4 percent involve Robertsonian translocation (most commonly between chromosomes 14 and 21), and 1 to 2 percent are mosaic. The risk increases exponentially with maternal age: approximately 1 in 1,500 at age 20, 1 in 350 at age 35, 1 in 100 at age 40, and 1 in 25 at age 45. Life expectancy has improved dramatically, from approximately 25 years in 1983 to approximately 60 years currently (Roizen and Patterson, 2003).

### Cardinal Clinical Features

Newborns with Down syndrome demonstrate a characteristic constellation of findings:

- **Hypotonia:** Present in nearly 100 percent of neonates; central hypotonia
- **Facial features:** Upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, flat nasal bridge, small ears, protruding tongue
- **Brushfield spots:** White or light-colored speckles on the iris periphery, present in approximately 38 to 60 percent
- **Single palmar crease (simian crease):** Approximately 45 percent
- **Sandal gap:** Wide space between first and second toes
- **Intellectual disability:** Average IQ 40 to 55 (moderate range); DSM-5 emphasizes adaptive functioning over IQ scores

### Cardiac Disease

Congenital heart disease occurs in 40 to 50 percent of individuals with Down syndrome (Freeman et al., 2008). The distribution of cardiac defects is:

| Cardiac Defect | Prevalence Among DS CHD |
|---|---|
| Atrioventricular septal defect (AVSD/endocardial cushion defect) | 40-45% (most common) |
| Ventricular septal defect (VSD) | ~35% |
| Atrial septal defect (ASD) | ~8% |
| Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) | ~7% |
| Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) | ~4% |

Echocardiography is recommended in all newborns with Down syndrome regardless of the presence of a murmur.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The atrioventricular septal defect (endocardial cushion defect) is the most common cardiac lesion in Down syndrome and is the classic board association. AVSD results from incomplete fusion of the endocardial cushions, producing a common atrioventricular valve with both atrial and ventricular communications.

### AAP Health Supervision Guidelines (Bull et al., 2022)

The 2022 updated AAP guidelines provide a comprehensive screening schedule:

| Screening | Schedule |
|---|---|
| **Cardiac** | Echocardiogram in all newborns |
| **Thyroid** | TSH at birth, 6 months, 12 months, then annually |
| **Hearing** | ABR at birth or by 1 month; audiologic evaluation every 6 months until age 3, then annually |
| **Vision** | Ophthalmologic evaluation by 6 months, then annually (refractive errors in 50-70%) |
| **Cervical spine** | Lateral cervical radiographs (flexion/extension + neutral) between ages 3-5 years |
| **Hematology** | CBC at birth (polycythemia, TMD in ~10%) |
| **Celiac disease** | Tissue transglutaminase IgA antibody starting at age 2 |
| **Sleep** | Polysomnography by age 4 (OSA in 50-75%) |
| **Growth** | DS-specific growth charts (Zemel et al., 2015) |
| **Mental health** | Screening in adolescents and adults (2022 emphasis) |
| **Bone density** | DEXA screening for osteoporosis (2022 addition) |

### Associated Medical Conditions

| Condition | Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Hearing loss (conductive, sensorineural, or mixed) | 75% |
| Obstructive sleep apnea | 50-75% |
| Hypothyroidism | 15-20% |
| Atlantoaxial instability (radiographic) | 10-30% (symptomatic: 1-2%) |
| Leukemia risk (ALL and AMKL) | 10-30x increased |
| Transient myeloproliferative disorder (neonatal) | ~10% |
| Celiac disease | 5-16% |
| Duodenal atresia | ~5% (30% of all duodenal atresia has DS) |
| Hirschsprung disease | 2-15x increased risk |
| Alzheimer disease (by age 60) | 50-70% |

### Atlantoaxial Instability in Down Syndrome

(Covered in detail in Chapter 7, Section 7.5)

The ADI greater than 4.5 mm on flexion-extension radiographs defines radiographic AAI. Special Olympics requires screening cervical spine films before participation in designated high-risk sports. Clinical monitoring for myelopathic signs (hyperreflexia, clonus, gait changes, neck pain, torticollis, bowel/bladder dysfunction) is more important than radiographic measurements.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The duodenal atresia association produces the classic "double bubble sign" on abdominal radiograph. Approximately 30 percent of infants with duodenal atresia have Down syndrome — making this a frequently tested association.

## 8.2 Trisomy 18 (Edwards Syndrome)

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology and Prognosis

Trisomy 18 occurs in approximately 1 in 5,000 to 8,000 live births with a female predominance of 3:1. Prognosis is grave: 50 percent die within the first week of life, 90 percent die by age 1 year, and only 5 to 10 percent survive beyond 1 year.

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Overlapping fingers** | Index finger overlaps 3rd, 5th overlaps 4th — considered pathognomonic |
| **Clenched fists** | Persistent |
| **Rocker-bottom feet** | Convex sole with prominent calcaneus |
| **Prominent occiput** | Dolichocephaly |
| **Micrognathia** | Small jaw |
| **Low-set ears** | Posteriorly rotated |
| **Cardiac defects** | >90% (VSD, PDA most common) |
| **Growth restriction** | Severe prenatal and postnatal |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The combination of overlapping fingers (index over third, fifth over fourth) with clenched fists and rocker-bottom feet in a growth-restricted neonate should immediately raise suspicion for trisomy 18.

## 8.3 Trisomy 13 (Patau Syndrome)

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology and Prognosis

Trisomy 13 occurs in approximately 1 in 10,000 to 16,000 live births. Median survival is 7 to 10 days, with 90 percent dying within the first year.

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Holoprosencephaly** | Failure of prosencephalon to divide; spectrum from alobar (most severe) to lobar |
| **Postaxial polydactyly** | Extra digits on the ulnar (hand) or fibular (foot) side |
| **Cutis aplasia** | Midline scalp defect |
| **Microphthalmia/cyclopia** | In severe cases of holoprosencephaly |
| **Cleft lip and palate** | Midline or bilateral |
| **Cardiac defects** | 80% |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Trisomy 13 is distinguished from trisomy 18 by midline defects — holoprosencephaly, midline cleft lip, and scalp defects (cutis aplasia) — combined with postaxial polydactyly. Trisomy 18 features hand and foot deformities (overlapping fingers, rocker-bottom feet) without midline brain defects.

## 8.4 Turner Syndrome (45,X)

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology

Turner syndrome affects approximately 1 in 2,000 to 2,500 live female births. It is the most common sex chromosome abnormality in females. Remarkably, 99 percent of 45,X conceptions result in spontaneous abortion.

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Short stature** | Near-universal; mean adult height 147 cm (4'10") without treatment; SHOX gene haploinsufficiency |
| **Webbed neck (pterygium colli)** | Classic feature |
| **Shield chest** | Broad chest with widely spaced, inverted nipples |
| **Neonatal lymphedema** | Puffy hands and feet at birth |
| **Streak ovaries** | Primary amenorrhea, infertility in ~90%; estrogen replacement at puberty |
| **Renal anomalies (including horseshoe kidney)** | ~30% (horseshoe kidney specifically ~10%) |
| **Cystic hygroma** | Detected prenatally on ultrasound |

### Cardiovascular Associations

| Cardiac Finding | Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Bicuspid aortic valve | 15-30% (most common cardiac defect) |
| Coarctation of the aorta | 7-18% (classic board association) |
| Overall cardiac defects | 25-45% |

### Cognitive Profile

Intelligence is generally normal, but nonverbal learning disability is common, with characteristic visuospatial processing deficits. Verbal skills are typically stronger than performance (spatial) skills.

### Management

- **Growth hormone therapy:** FDA-approved for Turner syndrome; improves adult height by approximately 5 to 8 cm; based on SHOX gene haploinsufficiency
- **Estrogen replacement:** Initiated at pubertal age (typically 12 to 13 years) to induce secondary sexual characteristics; progesterone added later for menstrual cycling

> **Clinical Pearl:** Turner syndrome is classically associated with coarctation of the aorta on board examinations, but bicuspid aortic valve is actually more prevalent. Intelligence is typically normal, but visuospatial deficits are characteristic and distinguish the cognitive profile from Klinefelter syndrome.

## 8.5 Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology

Klinefelter syndrome occurs in approximately 1 in 500 to 1,000 live male births, making it one of the most common chromosomal abnormalities. Despite this prevalence, approximately 75 percent of affected individuals remain undiagnosed, often until puberty or during infertility evaluation.

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Tall stature** | Long limbs, eunuchoid body habitus |
| **Gynecomastia** | 40-75%; breast cancer risk 20x the general male population |
| **Small, firm testes** | Testicular failure; azoospermia and infertility |
| **Hypogonadism** | Low testosterone; replacement typically needed from adolescence |
| **Cognitive profile** | Average IQ 85-90; verbal IQ typically lower than performance IQ (opposite of Turner) |

### Management

Testosterone replacement is initiated at puberty to promote virilization, maintain bone density, support muscle development, and address hypogonadism. Fertility options are limited but may include testicular sperm extraction with intracytoplasmic sperm injection in select cases.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The cognitive profiles of Turner syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome are opposite: Turner has stronger verbal than visuospatial skills, while Klinefelter has stronger performance (spatial) than verbal skills. This contrast is a classic board question.

## 8.6 Prader-Willi Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics and the Imprinting Paradigm

Prader-Willi syndrome (PWS) results from loss of function of paternally expressed genes at chromosome 15q11.2-q13. The condition arises through several mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Paternal deletion of 15q11-q13 | 65-75% |
| Maternal uniparental disomy (UPD) | 20-30% |
| Imprinting center defect | 1-3% |

PWS and Angelman syndrome represent the paradigmatic example of genomic imprinting: the same chromosomal region (15q11-q13) produces entirely different phenotypes depending on the parent of origin. Paternal loss produces PWS; maternal loss produces Angelman syndrome (Cassidy et al., 2012).

**Diagnostic test:** DNA methylation analysis is the gold standard, detecting more than 99 percent of cases regardless of mechanism.

### Clinical Features: The Two-Phase Pattern

The hallmark of PWS is a dramatic phenotypic shift from neonatal hypotonia with feeding difficulty to childhood hyperphagia and obesity.

**Phase 1 (Infancy):** Severe central hypotonia with poor feeding, weak cry, failure to thrive; may require gavage feeding

**Phase 2 (Childhood, typically ages 2-8):** Onset of insatiable appetite (hyperphagia) driven by hypothalamic dysfunction; progressive obesity without intervention; food-seeking behavior

### Associated Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Hypothalamic dysfunction** | Hyperphagia, temperature instability, high pain threshold |
| **Hypogonadism** | Cryptorchidism in males, delayed/incomplete puberty |
| **Short stature** | GH deficiency in 40-100% |
| **Small hands and feet (acromicria)** | Characteristic |
| **Behavioral** | Skin picking, temper tantrums, perseveration, obsessive-compulsive features |
| **Intellectual disability** | Mild to moderate (mean IQ ~65-70) |

### Growth Hormone Therapy

GH therapy is FDA-approved for PWS (since 2000) based on the landmark study by Carrel and colleagues (2002) demonstrating improvements in body composition, linear growth, muscle mass, and physical function over 4 years. GH therapy has become a cornerstone of management, typically initiated in infancy.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The two-phase clinical pattern of PWS — neonatal hypotonia with poor feeding followed by childhood hyperphagia and obesity — is one of the most tested genetic syndrome features on rehabilitation board examinations. DNA methylation analysis is the gold standard diagnostic test because it detects all genetic mechanisms.

## 8.7 Angelman Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics

Angelman syndrome results from loss of function of the maternally expressed UBE3A gene at chromosome 15q11.2-q13 — the same region as PWS but with the opposite parent of origin (genomic imprinting).

| Mechanism | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Maternal deletion of 15q11-q13 | 68-75% |
| UBE3A mutation | 11% |
| Paternal uniparental disomy | 3-7% |
| Imprinting center defect | 3% |

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **"Happy puppet" phenotype** | Frequent inappropriate laughter, hand flapping, excitable personality |
| **Severe intellectual disability** | Near absence of expressive speech |
| **Ataxic, puppet-like gait** | Wide-based, stiff-legged |
| **Seizures** | 80-90%; onset typically 1-3 years; characteristic EEG: high-amplitude 2-3 Hz delta activity |
| **Microcephaly** | Acquired; head circumference decelerates postnatally |
| **Fascination with water** | Classic behavioral feature |

Incidence is approximately 1 in 12,000 to 20,000 live births (Williams et al., 2010).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Prader-Willi and Angelman syndrome are the textbook example of genomic imprinting: deletion of the same chromosomal region (15q11-q13) produces entirely different phenotypes depending on the parent of origin. Paternal deletion causes PWS (hypotonia, obesity, hypogonadism); maternal deletion causes Angelman (happy affect, severe ID, seizures, ataxia).

## 8.8 Rett Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics

Rett syndrome is caused by mutations in the MECP2 gene located at Xq28. It is inherited in an X-linked dominant pattern and occurs almost exclusively in females, as males with MECP2 mutations typically present with severe neonatal encephalopathy and die in infancy. De novo mutations account for more than 99 percent of cases (Amir et al., 1999). Incidence is approximately 1 in 10,000 to 15,000 live female births.

### The Four Clinical Stages

The defining feature of Rett syndrome is a period of apparently normal development followed by regression. Neul and colleagues (2010) codified the revised diagnostic criteria and clinical staging.

| Stage | Age of Onset | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage I (Stagnation)** | 6-18 months | Developmental deceleration; subtle loss of interest; hypotonia; head growth deceleration |
| **Stage II (Regression)** | 1-4 years | Rapid loss of acquired hand skills and language; onset of hand stereotypies; seizures; breathing irregularities; social withdrawal; may mimic autism |
| **Stage III (Plateau/Pseudostationary)** | Preschool to school age | Some improvement in social interaction; hand stereotypies and seizures persist; motor deterioration continues; scoliosis develops |
| **Stage IV (Late Motor Deterioration)** | Variable | Severe motor impairment; wheelchair dependence; reduced mobility; scoliosis progression; may lose ambulation |

### Hallmark Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Hand stereotypies** | Wringing, washing, squeezing, clapping — the hallmark; loss of purposeful hand use |
| **Breathing irregularities** | Hyperventilation while awake, breath holding, air swallowing |
| **Seizures** | 60-80% |
| **Scoliosis** | Progressive; common indication for surgical intervention |
| **Acquired microcephaly** | Head circumference is normal at birth but decelerates postnatally |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The triad of (1) regression after apparently normal early development, (2) loss of purposeful hand movements replaced by stereotypic hand-wringing, and (3) acquired microcephaly should immediately suggest Rett syndrome. It is almost exclusively a diagnosis in females.

## 8.9 Williams Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics

Williams syndrome results from a microdeletion at chromosome 7q11.23, which includes the elastin gene (ELN) and approximately 26 to 28 other genes. Incidence is approximately 1 in 7,500 to 10,000 live births (Morris, GeneReviews, 1999/2023).

### Cardinal Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **"Cocktail party personality"** | Overly friendly, excessive sociability, lack of stranger anxiety, strong empathy |
| **Supravalvular aortic stenosis (SVAS)** | 75% — the classic cardiovascular association; due to elastin gene deletion |
| **Peripheral pulmonary stenosis** | Common in infancy; may resolve with age |
| **Elfin facies** | Broad forehead, periorbital fullness, stellate iris pattern, long philtrum, full lips, small chin |
| **Infantile hypercalcemia** | 15-50%; may cause irritability, vomiting, constipation |
| **Cognitive profile** | Mean IQ 50-60; remarkable verbal and auditory skills; musical ability; severe visuospatial deficits |
| **Renal anomalies** | Renal artery stenosis, bladder diverticula |
| **Endocrine** | Hypothyroidism, early puberty |

### The Cognitive Paradox

The cognitive profile of Williams syndrome is distinctively uneven. Verbal abilities, auditory memory, and social-emotional processing are relative strengths, often described as remarkably articulate speech with sophisticated vocabulary. In stark contrast, visuospatial construction, mathematical reasoning, and visuomotor integration are profoundly impaired. This dissociation creates a misleading impression of higher cognitive ability based on conversational interactions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Williams syndrome produces one of the most distinctive cognitive profiles in genetics: strong verbal and social skills combined with severe visuospatial deficits. SVAS (75 percent prevalence) is the classic cardiac association and results directly from haploinsufficiency of the elastin gene within the 7q11.23 deletion.

## 8.10 Fragile X Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

### Genetics: The Trinucleotide Repeat Paradigm

Fragile X syndrome is caused by expansion of a CGG trinucleotide repeat in the 5' untranslated region of the FMR1 gene at Xq27.3 (Hagerman et al., 2017). It is the most common inherited cause of intellectual disability and the second most common genetic cause overall (after Down syndrome).

| Category | CGG Repeat Number | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | 5-44 | No clinical effect |
| Intermediate (gray zone) | 45-54 | Stable in most transmissions |
| Premutation | 55-200 | Carrier state; associated with FXTAS and FXPOI |
| Full mutation | >200 | Fragile X syndrome; FMR1 silencing by methylation |

### Anticipation

A key genetic concept tested on boards is anticipation: premutation alleles are unstable during maternal transmission and tend to expand in successive generations. A mother with a premutation may have a child with a full mutation. Paternal premutation alleles are generally stable — a father with a premutation will transmit a premutation (not a full mutation) to his daughters.

### Clinical Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Intellectual disability** | Moderate in males; approximately 50% of females have some cognitive impairment |
| **Macro-orchidism** | Post-pubertal; present in approximately 80% of affected males — classic finding |
| **Facial features (post-pubertal)** | Long face, prominent jaw, large ears |
| **Behavioral** | ADHD, anxiety, autism features (~30%), hand flapping, gaze aversion, tactile defensiveness |
| **Connective tissue** | Joint hypermobility, flat feet, mitral valve prolapse (~50%) |

### Premutation Carrier Phenotypes

Premutation carriers (55-200 repeats) are not simply silent carriers; they may develop distinct clinical conditions:

- **FXTAS (Fragile X-associated tremor/ataxia syndrome):** Progressive cerebellar ataxia and intention tremor in premutation carrier males, typically after age 50
- **FXPOI (Fragile X-associated primary ovarian insufficiency):** Premature ovarian failure in approximately 20 percent of female premutation carriers

> **Clinical Pearl:** Fragile X is the most common inherited cause of intellectual disability, while Down syndrome is the most common genetic cause overall. The hallmark post-pubertal finding is macro-orchidism (large testes) in approximately 80 percent of affected males. Anticipation through maternal transmission explains why the repeat expands across generations.

## 8.11 TORCH Infections

`[BOARD]`

TORCH infections represent a group of congenital infections that share overlapping clinical features but can be distinguished by characteristic findings.

### Comparative Features

| Infection | Pathognomonic/Classic Features | Cardiac | CNS Calcifications | Other |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Toxoplasmosis** | Chorioretinitis + hydrocephalus + intracranial calcifications (classic triad) | — | Scattered/diffuse | Acquired from cat feces, undercooked meat |
| **Other (Syphilis)** | Hutchinson triad: Hutchinson teeth + interstitial keratitis + CN VIII deafness | — | — | Saddle nose, saber shins, snuffles |
| **Rubella** | Sensorineural hearing loss (#1 feature) | PDA (classic) | — | Cataracts; "blueberry muffin" rash |
| **CMV** | Most common congenital infection; periventricular calcifications | — | Periventricular | Hepatosplenomegaly; progressive SNHL; leading non-genetic cause of SNHL |
| **Herpes (HSV)** | Skin vesicles, encephalitis, keratoconjunctivitis | — | — | Acquired during delivery |
| **Zika** | Severe microcephaly, arthrogryposis | — | Cortical/subcortical junction | Macular scarring |

### The CMV Versus Toxoplasmosis Calcification Distinction

This is one of the most frequently tested board distinctions in pediatric genetics and infectious disease:

- **CMV:** Periventricular calcifications (around the ventricles)
- **Toxoplasmosis:** Scattered or diffuse intracranial calcifications (distributed throughout the parenchyma)

> **Clinical Pearl:** CMV is the most common congenital infection and the leading non-genetic cause of sensorineural hearing loss in children. The periventricular pattern of intracranial calcifications distinguishes CMV from toxoplasmosis, which produces scattered, diffuse calcifications. This distinction is among the most commonly tested board facts in this domain.

## 8.12 VACTERL Association

`[BOARD]`

VACTERL is a non-random association of congenital anomalies — importantly, it is an association, not a syndrome, because no single unifying genetic cause has been identified. Diagnosis requires three or more of the following features:

| Letter | Anomaly | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **V** | Vertebral anomalies | Hemivertebrae, butterfly vertebrae |
| **A** | Anal atresia | Imperforate anus |
| **C** | Cardiac defects | VSD is the most common |
| **T** | Tracheoesophageal fistula | TE fistula, often with esophageal atresia |
| **E** | Esophageal atresia | Frequently co-occurs with TE fistula |
| **R** | Renal anomalies | Renal agenesis, horseshoe kidney |
| **L** | Limb anomalies | Radial ray defects (absent or hypoplastic thumb or radius) |

Incidence is approximately 1 in 10,000 to 40,000 live births (Solomon, 2011).

> **Clinical Pearl:** VACTERL is an association (not a syndrome) requiring three or more component features. The key distinguishing concept is that no single gene mutation explains VACTERL, unlike syndromes such as Down or Fragile X where a specific genetic etiology is defined. Radial ray defects (absent thumb, absent radius) are the characteristic limb anomalies.

## 8.13 Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology

Fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASD) represent the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability. The full fetal alcohol syndrome (FAS) occurs in 0.5 to 3 per 1,000 live births, while the broader FASD spectrum is estimated to affect 2 to 5 percent of school-age children in the United States and Western Europe. The AAP, ACOG, and the Surgeon General all advise that there is no safe amount of alcohol consumption during pregnancy (Hoyme et al., 2016).

### Diagnostic Features of Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

FAS requires findings in three domains:

1. **Characteristic facial features (all three required):**
   - Smooth philtrum
   - Thin upper lip (thin vermillion border)
   - Short palpebral fissures

2. **Growth deficiency:**
   - Prenatal and/or postnatal growth restriction (height or weight at or below the 10th percentile)

3. **Central nervous system dysfunction:**
   - Structural: microcephaly
   - Functional: intellectual disability (mean IQ approximately 70), behavioral problems (ADHD-like symptoms), executive function deficits, learning disabilities
   - Cardiac defects: VSD, ASD

### The FASD Spectrum

The broader spectrum includes partial FAS (some but not all facial features), alcohol-related neurodevelopmental disorder (ARND, CNS dysfunction without facial features), and alcohol-related birth defects (ARBD, structural anomalies without facial features). Neurobehavioral impairment may be the most functionally significant consequence, as many individuals with prenatal alcohol exposure lack the characteristic facial features but still demonstrate substantial cognitive and behavioral difficulties.

> **Clinical Pearl:** All three facial features — smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, and short palpebral fissures — must be present for the diagnosis of full fetal alcohol syndrome. However, many children with significant prenatal alcohol-related neurodevelopmental impairment lack the classic facial phenotype. FASD remains the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability.

## 8.14 Genetic Testing Approaches

`[BOARD]`

### The Diagnostic Testing Hierarchy

The approach to genetic evaluation in a child with unexplained developmental delay, intellectual disability, or congenital anomalies has evolved significantly with advances in genomic technology. The ACMG consensus statement (Miller et al., 2010) established chromosomal microarray as the first-tier test for these indications, replacing the traditional karyotype.

| Test | Resolution | What It Detects | Diagnostic Yield | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Karyotype** | 5-10 Mb | Aneuploidies, large structural changes, balanced translocations | ~3% for unexplained DD/ID | Cannot detect submicroscopic changes |
| **FISH** | Targeted probe | Known microdeletion/duplication syndromes | Targeted only | Requires clinical suspicion for specific syndrome |
| **Chromosomal Microarray (CMA)** | 50-100 kb | Copy number variants (deletions, duplications) | 15-20% for unexplained DD/ID | Cannot detect balanced translocations, point mutations |
| **Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)** | Single nucleotide | Point mutations in coding regions (~1.5% of genome) | 25-40% | Misses non-coding variants, structural variants, repeat expansions |
| **Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)** | Single nucleotide + structural | All variant types including non-coding | 30-50% | Cost, interpretation of variants of uncertain significance |
| **DNA Methylation Analysis** | Epigenetic | Imprinting disorders (PWS, Angelman) | Gold standard for PWS/AS | Specific to methylation defects |
| **Trinucleotide Repeat Analysis** | Repeat expansion | Fragile X, myotonic dystrophy, Huntington | Targeted | Requires clinical suspicion |

### Testing Strategy

**First-tier testing for unexplained DD/ID, ASD, or multiple congenital anomalies:**
- Chromosomal microarray (CMA) — recommended by ACMG consensus (Miller et al., 2010)
- Fragile X testing (if male with ID, or if clinically suspected)

**Second-tier testing (when CMA is non-diagnostic):**
- Whole exome sequencing (WES) — diagnostic yield 25-40% (Manickam et al., 2021)

**Emerging approach:**
- Whole genome sequencing (WGS) as potential first-line test — supported by evidence of 30-50% diagnostic yield in some studies (Clark et al., 2018; Buxbaum et al., 2020)

**Targeted testing when a specific syndrome is suspected:**
- Karyotype: when trisomy or balanced translocation is suspected
- FISH: when a known microdeletion syndrome is clinically evident (e.g., Williams, DiGeorge)
- DNA methylation: when PWS or Angelman syndrome is suspected
- Trinucleotide repeat analysis: when Fragile X or myotonic dystrophy is suspected

> **Clinical Pearl:** Chromosomal microarray has replaced karyotype as the recommended first-tier genetic test for unexplained developmental delay, intellectual disability, and ASD, with a diagnostic yield of 15-20 percent compared to only 3 percent for traditional karyotype. However, karyotype remains necessary when a balanced translocation is suspected (CMA cannot detect balanced rearrangements), and methylation analysis is the gold standard for suspected Prader-Willi or Angelman syndrome.

## 8.15 Intellectual Disability Classification

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The DSM-5 and the American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities (AAIDD) both emphasize that the diagnosis of intellectual disability requires deficits in both intellectual function AND adaptive behavior, with onset during the developmental period (before age 18). The DSM-5 specifically emphasizes adaptive functioning rather than IQ scores as the basis for severity classification.

| Severity | Historical IQ Range | Adaptive Support Level |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 50-70 | Intermittent support |
| Moderate | 35-50 | Limited support |
| Severe | 20-35 | Extensive support |
| Profound | <20 | Pervasive support |

Common comorbidities include ASD (28 percent of individuals with ID), ADHD (25 to 40 percent), epilepsy, feeding disorders, sleep disorders, visual impairment (13 to 50 percent), hearing impairment (15 to 20 percent), and an increased rate of sexual abuse (15 to 30 percent). Assessment tools include the Bayley Scales of Infant Development (Bayley-4), Denver Developmental Screening Test (Denver II), Ages and Stages Questionnaire (ASQ-3), and Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales.

For Down syndrome specifically, DS-specific developmental milestone ranges have been established, and DS-specific growth charts should be used rather than standard population charts.

## 8.16 Comparative Summary: High-Yield Syndrome Features

`[BOARD]`
