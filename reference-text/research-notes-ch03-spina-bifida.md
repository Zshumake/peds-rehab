## 2. LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### MOMS Trial (Management of Myelomeningocele Study)
- Adzick NS, Thom EA, Spong CY, et al. A Randomized Trial of Prenatal versus Postnatal Repair of Myelomeningocele. N Engl J Med. 2011;364(11):993-1004.
- **[BOARD]** THE landmark trial for spina bifida. NEJM 2011.

**Trial Design:**
- Multicenter RCT (3 centers: Vanderbilt, CHOP, UCSF)
- **N = 183** (planned 200, stopped early for efficacy at planned interim analysis)
- Prenatal repair (n=91) at 19-25 weeks gestation vs. Postnatal repair (n=92) within 12 hours of birth
- Primary outcomes: Composite of fetal/neonatal death or need for VP shunt by 12 months; and Bayley MDI + motor function at 30 months

**[BOARD]** Key Findings:
1. **VP shunt placement by 12 months**: Prenatal 40% vs. Postnatal 82% (RR 0.48, p<0.001)
   - Actual shunting rates at 12 months: 40% vs 82%
2. **Hindbrain herniation (Chiari II)**: Reversal/improvement in 36% of prenatal group vs 4% postnatal at 12 months
3. **Motor function at 30 months**: Prenatal group had significantly better motor function
   - Walking independently: 42% prenatal vs. 21% postnatal
   - Improvement of >=2 functional levels above predicted by anatomic level: 32% prenatal vs. 12% postnatal
4. **Bayley MDI at 30 months**: No significant difference (both groups similar cognitive scores)

**[BOARD]** Risks of Prenatal Repair:
- **Preterm birth**: Prenatal group delivered earlier (34.1 weeks vs. 37.3 weeks)
- **Uterine dehiscence at delivery**: 35% in prenatal group (at time of C-section)
- Chorioamniotic membrane separation: 26% prenatal vs. 0% postnatal
- Oligohydramnios: 21% prenatal vs. 4% postnatal
- Maternal pulmonary edema, blood transfusion
- **All future deliveries must be by C-section** (classical hysterotomy)
- **[BOARD]** Prenatal repair reduces VP shunt rate and improves motor function BUT increases preterm birth and maternal morbidity.

### MOMS2 Follow-Up Study
- Farmer DL, Thom EA, Brock JW, et al. The Management of Myelomeningocele Study: full cohort 30-month outcomes. Am J Obstet Gynecol. 2018;218(2):256.e1-256.e13.
- Houtrow AJ, Thom EA, Fletcher JM, et al. Prenatal Repair of Myelomeningocele and School-age Functional Outcomes. Pediatrics. 2020;145(2):e20191544.
- **MOMS2 (School-age follow-up, ~5-10 years)**:
  - Prenatal repair group continued to show benefits in motor function at school age
  - Community walking: 45% prenatal vs. 24% postnatal (at ~10 years)
  - VP shunt placement: 44% prenatal vs. 84% postnatal (by school age)
  - Adaptive behavior and executive function: similar between groups
  - Tethered cord requiring surgery: 10-15% in prenatal group (lower scar adhesion)

### MRC Vitamin Study (Folic Acid Prevention)
- MRC Vitamin Study Research Group. Prevention of neural tube defects: results of the Medical Research Council Vitamin Study. Lancet. 1991;338(8760):131-137.
- **[BOARD]** Landmark trial demonstrating 72% reduction in NTD recurrence with 4 mg folic acid daily.
- RCT of women with prior NTD pregnancy: folic acid 4 mg vs. other vitamins vs. both vs. placebo.
- Result: 72% risk reduction (1% recurrence with folic acid vs 3.5% without).

### CDC/USPHS Recommendation (1992)
- Based on MRC Vitamin Study and other evidence:
  - **[BOARD]** All women of childbearing age: 0.4 mg (400 mcg) folic acid daily
  - **[BOARD]** Women with prior NTD pregnancy: 4 mg (4,000 mcg) daily, starting 1-3 months before conception through first trimester
  - US mandatory folic acid fortification of enriched grain products since 1998 -- resulted in ~26-28% reduction in NTD prevalence.

### Czeizel and Dudas (1992) -- Primary Prevention
- Czeizel AE, Dudas I. Prevention of the first occurrence of neural-tube defects by periconceptional vitamin supplementation. N Engl J Med. 1992;327(26):1832-1835.
- Demonstrated that periconceptional folic acid supplementation prevents FIRST occurrence (not just recurrence) of NTDs.

## 3. RECENT EVIDENCE (2020-2025)

### Fetoscopic Repair
- Pedreira DA, Zanon N, Nishikuni K, et al. Endoscopic surgery for the antenatal treatment of myelomeningocele: the CECAM trial. Am J Obstet Gynecol. 2016;214(1):111.e1-111.e11.
- Fetoscopic (minimally invasive) prenatal repair approaches under investigation at multiple centers.
- Potential advantages: Lower maternal morbidity, possibility of vaginal delivery, smaller uterine incision.
- Current status (as of 2024): Several centers performing fetoscopic repair; outcomes appear comparable to open fetal surgery in experienced hands, but still considered investigational. Randomized trials in progress.

### MOMS2 School-Age Outcomes (2020)
- Houtrow AJ, Thom EA, Fletcher JM, et al. Prenatal Repair of Myelomeningocele and School-age Functional Outcomes. Pediatrics. 2020;145(2):e20191544.
- Sustained motor benefits of prenatal repair at school age.
- No significant differences in cognitive or adaptive behavior scores.
- Quality of life measures similar between groups.

### Neurogenic Bladder Management Updates
- Emerging evidence for early clean intermittent catheterization (CIC) starting in newborn period.
- Bauer SB, Austin PF, Rawashdeh YF, et al. International Children's Continence Society's recommendations for initial diagnostic evaluation and follow-up in congenital neuropathic bladder and bowel dysfunction in children. Neurourol Urodyn. 2012;31(5):610-614.
- Updated antibiotic prophylaxis recommendations (more selective approach, though still commonly used with vesicoureteral reflux).
- Botulinum toxin injection into detrusor for neurogenic detrusor overactivity increasingly used in pediatric population.
- Appendicovesicostomy (Mitrofanoff procedure) and augmentation cystoplasty remain standard for refractory cases.

### Transition of Care
- Growing emphasis on structured transition programs (pediatric to adult care).
- SBA guidelines emphasize self-management skills development starting in early childhood.

## 4. EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### Neural Tube Defect Prevalence
- **[BOARD]** NTD prevalence (US, post-fortification): ~0.5-0.7 per 1,000 live births (decreased from ~1.0-1.5 per 1,000 pre-fortification).
- Worldwide: ~0.5-2.0 per 1,000 births (varies by region; higher in certain populations -- Irish/Welsh descent, Hispanic, lower socioeconomic status).
- **[BOARD]** Myelomeningocele (open spina bifida) is the most common type of spina bifida requiring treatment (~90% of live-born NTDs that require intervention).
- Spina bifida occulta (posterior arch defect without neural tissue involvement): Present in ~5-10% of the general population. Usually incidental finding. No clinical significance in most cases.

### Types of Neural Tube Defects
**[BOARD]**:
- **Anencephaly**: Failure of anterior neural tube closure. Incompatible with life. ~50% of NTDs.
- **Myelomeningocele (open spina bifida)**: Herniation of meninges AND neural tissue through posterior vertebral defect. Most clinically significant NTD.
- **Meningocele**: Herniation of meninges only (no neural tissue). Better prognosis than myelomeningocele. ~5% of NTDs.
- **Encephalocele**: Herniation of brain tissue through skull defect. Usually occipital.
- **Lipomyelomeningocele**: Closed spinal dysraphism with lipoma attached to spinal cord. No exposed neural tissue. Associated with tethered cord.
- **Spina bifida occulta**: Posterior vertebral arch defect only. Usually asymptomatic. May have overlying cutaneous markers (hairy patch, dimple above gluteal cleft, hemangioma, dermal sinus tract).

### Survival and Life Expectancy
- With modern multidisciplinary care: >85% survive to adulthood.
- Life expectancy: Most individuals with spina bifida now live into their 40s-60s+ with appropriate care.
- Leading causes of death: Renal failure (historically #1, now improved with CIC and surveillance), VP shunt malfunction/infection, Chiari II complications.

### Risk Factors for NTDs
**[BOARD]**:
- Prior NTD-affected pregnancy (recurrence risk ~2-5%)
- Maternal folate deficiency
- Maternal diabetes mellitus (2-10x increased risk)
- Maternal obesity (1.5-3.5x increased risk)
- Maternal hyperthermia in first trimester
- **[BOARD]** Antiepileptic drugs: Valproic acid (1-2% risk of NTD, ~10-20x baseline), carbamazepine (0.5-1% risk)
- Hispanic ethnicity (higher risk)
- Low socioeconomic status
- Family history (sibling: 2-5%; two affected siblings: 10%)
- Maternal use of folic acid antagonists (methotrexate, trimethoprim, aminopterin)

## 5. CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATIONS

### Functional Motor Levels (Key Muscles and Ambulatory Outcomes)
**[BOARD]** This is one of the MOST TESTED topics in spina bifida rehabilitation:

| Level | Key Muscle(s) | Hip Flexion | Hip Extension | Knee Extension | Knee Flexion | Ankle | Ambulatory Outcome |
|-------|--------------|-------------|---------------|----------------|--------------|-------|-------------------|
| **Thoracic** | No LE function | - | - | - | - | - | Wheelchair; may use parapodium/RGO for therapeutic standing in childhood |
| **L1-L2** | Iliopsoas (hip flexion) | + | - | - | - | - | Household/therapeutic ambulation with HKAFO/RGO as child; wheelchair as adult |
| **L3** | Quadriceps (knee extension) | + | - | + | - | - | Household/limited community ambulation with KAFO; wheelchair for community as adult |
| **L4** | Tibialis anterior (ankle dorsiflexion), Medial hamstrings (partial) | + | - | + | +/- | DF+ | Community ambulation with AFO; may need wheelchair for long distances |
| **L5** | Lateral hamstrings, Peroneus longus/brevis (eversion), Gluteus medius (partial hip abduction) | + | +/- | + | + | DF+, EV+ | Community ambulation with AFO; Trendelenburg gait (weak hip abductors) |
| **S1** | Gastrocnemius/Soleus (plantarflexion), Gluteus maximus (partial), Peroneals | + | +/- | + | + | DF+, PF+ | Community ambulation; may need AFO or shoe insert; near-normal gait |
| **S2-S3** | Foot intrinsics, Bladder/bowel sphincters | + | + | + | + | + | Normal or near-normal ambulation; bowel/bladder issues persist |

**[BOARD]** Critical Board Facts by Level:

- **Thoracic**: No voluntary LE function. Wheelchair dependent. May use standing frame or parapodium for therapeutic standing/exercise (bone health, psychosocial). RGO (reciprocating gait orthosis) for exercise ambulation in young children. Almost all become full-time wheelchair users by adolescence.

- **L1-L2**: Hip flexion present (iliopsoas). No knee extension. May achieve therapeutic/household ambulation as young child with HKAFO or RGO. Energy cost of ambulation is very high (oxygen consumption 2-4x normal walking). Most become wheelchair users by adolescence/adulthood.

- **L3**: **[BOARD]** Key muscle = QUADRICEPS. Can extend knee against gravity. Household ambulator with KAFO. Energy cost still high. Many transition to wheelchair for community mobility by adulthood. Classic presentation: Can lock knees with quads but has no ankle control -- needs KAFO to control ankle.

- **L4**: **[BOARD]** Key muscle = TIBIALIS ANTERIOR (ankle dorsiflexion). Community ambulator with AFO. Has quads AND tibialis anterior. The CLASSIC AFO-user level. Medial hamstrings partially innervated. Still has significant hip abductor weakness. Most retain community ambulation into adulthood.

- **L5**: **[BOARD]** Key addition = LATERAL HAMSTRINGS and PERONEUS LONGUS/BREVIS (eversion) and partial gluteus medius. Community ambulator, usually with AFO. Trendelenburg gait due to gluteus medius weakness. Foot deformity tendency: CALCANEUS (calcaneovalgus) because dorsiflexors and evertors are stronger than absent/weak plantarflexors.

- **S1**: **[BOARD]** Key addition = GASTROCNEMIUS/SOLEUS (plantarflexion). Near-normal ambulation. May use AFO or just shoe insert. Has most muscle groups. Still has bowel/bladder issues and some foot intrinsic weakness.

- **S2-S3**: Near-normal motor function. Foot intrinsics present. Main issues are bowel/bladder dysfunction and possible subtle foot deformities.

**[BOARD]** KEY ORTHOPEDIC DEFORMITY PATTERNS BY LEVEL:

| Level | Hip | Knee | Foot |
|-------|-----|------|------|
| **Thoracic** | Flexion contracture, dislocation (no muscular forces) | Flexion contracture | Equinovarus (from positioning) |
| **L1-L2** | Flexion contracture, dislocation (unopposed hip flexors, no extensors/abductors) | Flexion or extension contracture | Variable |
| **L3** | Flexion contracture, dislocation (flexors + quads but no extensors/abductors) | Recurvatum (unopposed quads) | Variable |
| **L4** | Subluxation less common (some hamstring balance) | Flexion contracture (hamstrings present) | **Calcaneovalgus** (tibialis anterior without gastroc-soleus) |
| **L5** | Subluxation rare | Usually normal alignment | **Calcaneus** (dorsiflexors and evertors without plantarflexors) |
| **S1** | Usually normal | Usually normal | **Cavovarus** (intrinsic minus foot) or mild pes planus |

**[BOARD]** Hip dislocation in spina bifida:
- Most common at L1-L3 levels (unopposed hip flexors without extensors/abductors)
- Unlike CP, hip reduction surgery in spina bifida has NOT been shown to improve ambulation outcomes
- Sharrard: Hip dislocation in spina bifida is generally NOT treated surgically unless causing pain or skin problems in older patients
- This is a CLASSIC board distinction from CP (where hip surveillance and intervention are critical)

### Ambulation Prognosis Rules
**[BOARD]**:
- **Quadriceps function (L3) is the key determinant of ambulation potential**
- Functional community ambulation requires at least L4 level (tibialis anterior)
- Energy cost of walking increases dramatically with higher levels of paralysis
- Most individuals with thoracic-L2 levels become wheelchair users by adulthood
- Factors predicting loss of ambulation: higher lesion level, obesity, musculoskeletal deformities, hydrocephalus with shunt complications, lower IQ scores

## 6. ARNOLD-CHIARI II MALFORMATION

### Definition and Anatomy
**[BOARD]**:
- Chiari II (Arnold-Chiari) malformation is present in virtually ALL patients with myelomeningocele (~95-100%).
- Anatomy: Downward displacement of cerebellar tonsils AND vermis through foramen magnum into upper cervical spinal canal. Also involves displacement of the fourth ventricle and brainstem (medulla).
- Distinguished from Chiari I: Chiari I = tonsillar herniation only (>5mm below foramen magnum); Chiari II = tonsils + vermis + brainstem + 4th ventricle herniation, AND associated with myelomeningocele.
- **[BOARD]** Chiari II is associated with myelomeningocele; Chiari I is NOT (Chiari I is associated with syringomyelia and may be idiopathic or associated with connective tissue disorders).

### Symptoms by Age
**[BOARD]**:

**Infants (0-2 years)** -- Most dangerous period, highest mortality:
- **Stridor** (vocal cord paralysis -- bilateral abductor paralysis is most concerning)
- Swallowing difficulty / dysphagia / aspiration
- Apneic spells / central apnea
- Weak or absent cry
- Opisthotonus (arching)
- Nystagmus
- Upper extremity weakness
- **[BOARD]** Symptomatic Chiari II in infancy carries 15-35% mortality. Requires urgent posterior fossa decompression.

**Children (2-12 years)**:
- Headaches (especially with Valsalva/coughing)
- Progressive scoliosis (may indicate syringomyelia)
- Upper extremity weakness / change in hand function
- Worsening gait
- Spasticity in upper extremities
- Sleep apnea

**Adolescents/Adults**:
- Headaches and neck pain
- Progressive scoliosis
- Change in upper extremity function
- Pain syndromes
- Syringomyelia symptoms

### Treatment
- **[BOARD]** Symptomatic Chiari II: Posterior fossa decompression (suboccipital craniectomy + C1 laminectomy +/- duraplasty).
- ALWAYS check for hydrocephalus/shunt malfunction FIRST -- symptoms of Chiari II may be caused by increased ICP from shunt malfunction. Treat shunt malfunction before considering posterior fossa decompression.
- **[BOARD]** Rule: In spina bifida patient with new neurological symptoms -- ALWAYS evaluate shunt function first.

## 7. HYDROCEPHALUS AND VP SHUNTS

### Hydrocephalus in Spina Bifida
**[BOARD]**:
- ~80-90% of children with myelomeningocele develop hydrocephalus requiring treatment.
- Mechanism: Chiari II malformation obstructs CSF flow at posterior fossa level (obstruction of 4th ventricle outlets/aqueduct).
- **VP shunt rate**: ~80-85% historically (MOMS trial postnatal group: 82%). With prenatal repair: ~40%.

### VP Shunt Complications
**[BOARD]**:
- **Shunt malfunction** (obstruction): Most common complication. ~40-50% malfunction within first 2 years. Symptoms: Headache, vomiting, lethargy, irritability, bulging fontanelle (if open), sunsetting eyes, increased head circumference, change in mentation, new seizures.
- **Shunt infection**: ~5-10% per surgical procedure. Most common organism: **Staphylococcus epidermidis** (coagulase-negative staph). Also S. aureus, gram-negatives.
- **Over-drainage**: Slit ventricle syndrome, subdural hematomas/hygromas, craniosynostosis.
- **Abdominal complications**: Pseudocyst, peritonitis, migration, perforation of viscus.
- **[BOARD]** ANY acute neurological change in a patient with VP shunt = shunt malfunction until proven otherwise. This includes new-onset seizures, behavior change, declining school performance, headaches, and worsening motor function.

### ETV (Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy)
- Alternative to VP shunt; creates opening in floor of third ventricle to allow CSF bypass.
- **[BOARD]** ETV success rate: ~50-60% in spina bifida population (lower than in other causes of hydrocephalus).
- Advantage: No hardware (no shunt to malfunction/infect).
- ETV+CPC (choroid plexus cauterization): Increasingly used, especially in younger infants. Success rate ~60-70% in some series.
- **[BOARD]** ETV is less successful in infants <6 months and in patients with prior shunt infections.

## 8. TETHERED CORD

### Definition
**[BOARD]**:
- Tethered spinal cord: Abnormal attachment of the spinal cord to surrounding structures (scar tissue, lipoma, tight filum terminale), limiting normal ascent during growth and resulting in traction injury.
- Occurs in virtually all patients with myelomeningocele (post-surgical scarring at repair site).
- Symptomatic tethered cord: ~20-50% of patients with myelomeningocele will become symptomatic and require surgical release.

### Clinical Presentation (Symptoms of Tethered Cord)
**[BOARD]**:
- Progressive scoliosis (rapidly progressive or new onset)
- Change in gait pattern or loss of ambulatory ability
- Increasing spasticity in lower extremities
- New or worsening foot deformity (especially unilateral)
- Back pain (often positional, worse with flexion)
- Progressive neurogenic bladder changes (change in urodynamic pattern, increased post-void residual, new incontinence)
- Upper extremity symptoms if associated with syringomyelia
- **[BOARD]** Tethered cord symptoms are often SUBTLE and PROGRESSIVE. Any change in neurological, urological, or orthopedic status should prompt MRI evaluation for tethered cord.

### Diagnosis
- MRI of spine: Low-lying conus medullaris (below L2 in adults, below L3 in infants). However, in myelomeningocele, the conus is ALWAYS low -- so position alone is not diagnostic.
- **[BOARD]** In myelomeningocele, tethered cord is a CLINICAL diagnosis (based on progressive symptoms), NOT purely radiographic. MRI shows tethering in virtually all MMC patients -- so surgery is indicated by SYMPTOMS, not just MRI findings.
- Urodynamics: Baseline and serial monitoring. Change in pattern (new detrusor overactivity, decreased compliance, increased residuals) may indicate tethering.

### Treatment
- Surgical untethering (laminectomy and release of adhesions/tethering elements).
- **[BOARD]** Goal: Halt progression of deficits. Recovery of lost function is variable and less predictable.
- Risk of re-tethering: ~10-20% (scar reformation).

## 9. LATEX ALLERGY

### Prevalence and Pathophysiology
**[BOARD]**:
- **Latex allergy prevalence in spina bifida: 30-65%** (compared to ~1% in general population).
- Due to: Repeated early exposure to latex through multiple surgeries, catheterizations, and examinations.
- IgE-mediated (Type I) hypersensitivity to natural rubber latex proteins.
- Cross-reactivity between latex proteins and certain food proteins.

### Cross-Reactive Foods
**[BOARD]** Foods with HIGHEST cross-reactivity to latex:
- **Banana**
- **Avocado**
- **Chestnut**
- **Kiwi**
- Mnemonic: "BACK" -- Banana, Avocado, Chestnut, Kiwi

Other cross-reactive foods (moderate risk):
- Potato, tomato, papaya, fig, passion fruit, pineapple, mango, peach, celery

### Prevention and Management
**[BOARD]**:
- **Latex-free environment from birth** -- standard of care for ALL patients with spina bifida.
- Use non-latex gloves (nitrile, vinyl), non-latex catheters, non-latex balloons.
- Latex allergy alert bracelet/documentation.
- Patients with known latex allergy: Carry epinephrine auto-injector.
- Surgical precautions: First case of the day (minimize airborne latex particles), latex-free operating room.
- **[BOARD]** Implementation of latex-free protocols has significantly reduced latex sensitization rates in newer cohorts of spina bifida patients.

## 10. NEUROGENIC BLADDER MANAGEMENT

### Bladder Dysfunction in Spina Bifida
**[BOARD]**:
- Nearly ALL patients with myelomeningocele have neurogenic bladder (~90-95%).
- Upper motor neuron bladder (reflexic/spastic): Detrusor overactivity, detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD). High intravesical pressures. Risk of upper tract damage.
- Lower motor neuron bladder (areflexic/flaccid): Large capacity, low pressure, overflow incontinence. Incompetent sphincter.
- **[BOARD]** Most dangerous pattern: HIGH-PRESSURE BLADDER (detrusor overactivity with DSD or poor compliance). Leads to vesicoureteral reflux, hydronephrosis, renal damage, renal failure.
- **[BOARD]** Detrusor leak point pressure >40 cm H2O = HIGH RISK for upper tract deterioration (McGuire et al., J Urol, 1981).

### Evaluation
**[BOARD]**:
- Baseline renal ultrasound at birth/diagnosis
- Baseline urodynamic study (recommended in newborn period or within first few months)
- Serial renal ultrasound and urodynamics (typically annually or more frequently if abnormal)
- VCUG (voiding cystourethrogram) if vesicoureteral reflux suspected
- Serum creatinine and BUN monitoring
- **[BOARD]** Urodynamics is the GOLD STANDARD for evaluating neurogenic bladder in spina bifida. Key parameters: Detrusor pressure, compliance, capacity, leak point pressure, presence of DSD.

### Management Algorithm
**[BOARD]**:

**Step 1: Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC)**
- **[BOARD]** CIC is the CORNERSTONE of neurogenic bladder management in spina bifida.
- Lapides J, Diokno AC, Silber SJ, Lowe BS. Clean, intermittent self-catheterization in the treatment of urinary tract disease. J Urol. 1972;107(3):458-461.
- Frequency: Every 3-4 hours (typically 4-6 times daily)
- Start: Newborn period (proactive approach) or when indicated by urodynamics
- Goal: Low-pressure bladder drainage, continence, prevention of upper tract damage
- Self-catheterization training: Begin teaching at age 5-7 years; independence goal by 8-10 years
- Mitrofanoff (appendicovesicostomy): Continent catheterizable channel using appendix from umbilicus to bladder. Used when urethral catheterization is difficult (wheelchair users, females with positioning challenges). Named after Paul Mitrofanoff.

**Step 2: Anticholinergic Medications**
- Oxybutynin (Ditropan): Most commonly used. Dose: 0.1-0.2 mg/kg/dose BID-TID. Also available as intravesical instillation.
- Tolterodine (Detrol), solifenacin, others.
- **[BOARD]** Purpose: Reduce detrusor overactivity, increase bladder capacity, improve compliance, reduce intravesical pressures.
- Side effects: Dry mouth, constipation (problematic in patients already prone to constipation), flushing, cognitive effects (especially oxybutynin -- crosses blood-brain barrier).

**Step 3: Botulinum Toxin Injection (Detrusor)**
- OnabotulinumtoxinA injected into detrusor muscle via cystoscopy.
- Dose: 10-12 U/kg (typical range 100-300 U total in children).
- Duration: ~6-9 months. Repeat injections needed.
- For refractory detrusor overactivity despite anticholinergics.

**Step 4: Augmentation Cystoplasty**
- Surgical enlargement of bladder using bowel segment (ileum most common = ileocystoplasty).
- **[BOARD]** Indications: Refractory high-pressure bladder, small capacity despite medical management.
- Complications: Mucus production, metabolic acidosis (bowel segment absorbs chloride and ammonium), bladder stones, bowel-related issues, risk of perforation, small risk of malignancy (long-term).
- Still requires CIC after augmentation.

**Step 5: Continent Urinary Diversion**
- Mitrofanoff (appendicovesicostomy) -- see above
- Monti channel (using ileal segment if appendix unavailable)
- For self-catheterization through abdominal stoma

### Continence Outcomes
- Social continence (dry between catheterizations): Achievable in ~80-90% with CIC + anticholinergics.
- Most children achieve reliable continence by school age with appropriate management.

## 11. BOWEL MANAGEMENT

### Neurogenic Bowel in Spina Bifida
**[BOARD]**:
- Virtually ALL patients with myelomeningocele have neurogenic bowel dysfunction.
- Upper motor neuron bowel: Reflex bowel activity, constipation, difficulty with voluntary evacuation. Intact or hyperactive anorectal reflex.
- Lower motor neuron bowel: Reduced rectal tone, reduced sensation, fecal incontinence, constipation.
- **[BOARD]** Most common presentation: CONSTIPATION with overflow incontinence (fecal soiling).

### Bowel Management Program
**[BOARD]**:

**Step 1: Dietary and Behavioral**
- High-fiber diet
- Adequate fluid intake
- Timed toileting (post-meal, gastrocolic reflex)
- Activity/exercise

**Step 2: Oral Medications**
- Osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol/MiraLAX, lactulose)
- Stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) -- scheduled, not PRN
- Stool softeners (docusate) -- limited evidence alone

**Step 3: Rectal Interventions**
- Glycerin or bisacodyl suppositories
- Digital stimulation
- Mini-enemas (Enemeez, bisacodyl-based)

**Step 4: Antegrade Continence Enema (ACE/Malone Procedure)**
- **[BOARD]** Malone Antegrade Continence Enema (MACE): Surgically created channel (appendicostomy or cecostomy) from skin to cecum. Allows administration of antegrade enema (flush) through the channel.
- Malone PS, Ransley PG, Kiely EM. Preliminary report: the antegrade continence enema. Lancet. 1990;336(8725):1217-1218.
- Enema solution (saline, GoLYTELY, or soapsuds) instilled via stoma into cecum -> produces predictable bowel evacuation.
- Goal: Pseudo-continence (evacuation at scheduled time, dry between flushes).
- Success rate: ~70-90% achieve social continence.
- Alternative: Cecostomy button (Chait tube) -- percutaneous access to cecum for antegrade flushes.

**Step 5: Colostomy (rare)**
- Last resort for intractable fecal incontinence.

## 12. PRENATAL SCREENING

### Maternal Serum Screening
**[BOARD]**:
- **Maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (MSAFP)**: Elevated in open NTDs (myelomeningocele, anencephaly). Screening at 15-20 weeks gestation.
  - Sensitivity: ~80% for open spina bifida at cutoff of 2.5 MoM.
  - False positives: Multiple gestation, incorrect dating, abdominal wall defects, fetal demise.
  - **[BOARD]** MSAFP is NOT elevated in CLOSED spinal dysraphisms (lipomyelomeningocele, spina bifida occulta).
- **Amniotic fluid AFP + Acetylcholinesterase (AChE)**: If MSAFP elevated. AChE is more specific for open NTDs.
  - Amniocentesis: AFP elevated + AChE positive = high specificity for open NTD.

### Ultrasound
**[BOARD]**:
- Anatomic survey at 18-20 weeks can detect most myelomeningoceles.
- **Lemon sign**: Frontal bone scalloping (lemon-shaped skull) -- seen in ~99% of NTDs <24 weeks.
- **Banana sign**: Cerebellar compression (banana-shaped cerebellum) -- associated with Chiari II malformation.
- Direct visualization of spinal defect.
- Ventriculomegaly (may be present).
- **[BOARD]** Ultrasound sensitivity for detecting myelomeningocele: ~90-95% in experienced hands.

### Fetal MRI
- Increasingly used for detailed evaluation after ultrasound detection.
- Better delineation of level of defect, associated anomalies, brain findings.
- Used in MOMS trial eligibility assessment.

## 13. ORTHOPEDIC MANAGEMENT BY LEVEL

### General Principles
**[BOARD]**:
- Goal of orthopedic management varies by functional level:
  - Ambulators (L4-S2): Maintain/improve ambulation; correct deformities that interfere with brace use or walking
  - Non-ambulators (thoracic-L2): Maintain sitting balance, prevent pain, facilitate hygiene and positioning
  - L3: Variable -- may be ambulatory as child, wheelchair as adult
- **[BOARD]** Unlike in CP, hip reduction surgery in spina bifida does NOT improve ambulation. Ambulation depends on neurological level, not hip location.

### Foot Deformities
**[BOARD]**:
- **Clubfoot (equinovarus)**: Most common foot deformity at birth in spina bifida. Often rigid. Treatment: Serial casting (Ponseti technique may be used but results less predictable than idiopathic clubfoot), surgical correction if needed.
- **Calcaneovalgus**: Most common ACQUIRED deformity, especially L4-L5 levels (dorsiflexors without plantarflexors). Can lead to calcaneal pressure ulcers in braced patients.
- **Cavovarus**: S1 level and below (intrinsic minus foot).
- **Vertical talus (rocker-bottom foot)**: Can occur at any level.
- **[BOARD]** Foot deformities in spina bifida must be corrected to allow brace fitting and prevent pressure ulcers (insensate skin).

### Scoliosis in Spina Bifida
**[BOARD]**:
- Prevalence: ~50-80% (increases with higher level and with VP shunt).
- Types:
  - Congenital: Vertebral anomalies (hemivertebra, bar, mixed). Present at birth. May be rapidly progressive.
  - Developmental/Paralytic: Due to muscle imbalance and pelvic obliquity. Usually appears after age 5-10. Most common type in myelomeningocele.
- Always evaluate for tethered cord, syringomyelia, or shunt malfunction when scoliosis progresses rapidly.
- Treatment: Bracing (delays progression, does not prevent surgery), posterior spinal fusion when curve >40-50 degrees and skeletal maturity approaches. In congenital scoliosis: may need early fusion or growing rod constructs.
- **[BOARD]** Rule: New or rapidly progressive scoliosis in spina bifida = evaluate for tethered cord and shunt malfunction.

### Fractures
**[BOARD]**:
- Pathologic fractures are common in spina bifida, especially below the level of paralysis.
- Due to: Osteopenia from disuse/non-weight bearing, insensate limbs.
- Often present as painless swelling, warmth, and erythema of a limb (may be mistaken for infection or DVT).
- **[BOARD]** Classic board scenario: Child with spina bifida presents with warm, swollen, erythematous leg -- think FRACTURE first (not infection), especially if below the neurological level. Physeal fractures common.
- Treatment: Splinting/casting (minimal immobilization to avoid further osteopenia). Avoid prolonged immobilization.

### Skin Integrity
**[BOARD]**:
- Insensate skin below the neurological level is at high risk for pressure injury.
- Common locations: Ischial tuberosities (wheelchair users), sacrum, feet (in orthoses/shoes).
- Prevention: Pressure relief, skin inspection (patient/family education), properly fitting orthotics, wheelchair cushion.
- **[BOARD]** Patients with spina bifida should be taught to perform weight shifts (every 15-30 minutes) and daily skin checks using a mirror.

## 14. ADDITIONAL BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS (TAGGED)

**[BOARD]** Neural tube closes by day 28 post-conception (4 weeks gestational age). Anterior neuropore closes day 25; posterior neuropore closes day 27-28. Myelomeningocele results from failure of POSTERIOR neuropore closure.

**[BOARD]** Folic acid 0.4 mg for ALL women of childbearing age; 4 mg for women with prior NTD pregnancy. Must start BEFORE conception (NTD occurs before most women know they are pregnant).

**[BOARD]** Valproic acid is the most teratogenic common AED for NTDs (~1-2% NTD risk, 10-20x baseline). Mechanism: Inhibits folate metabolism and histone deacetylase.

**[BOARD]** MOMS trial: N=183, prenatal repair at 19-25 weeks reduced VP shunt rate (40% vs 82%) and improved motor function (walking 42% vs 21% at 30 months) but increased preterm birth and maternal morbidity.

**[BOARD]** Chiari II = myelomeningocele + hindbrain herniation (tonsils, vermis, brainstem, 4th ventricle). Present in ~100% of myelomeningocele. Chiari I = tonsillar herniation only, NOT associated with myelomeningocele.

**[BOARD]** Symptomatic Chiari II in infancy: Stridor (vocal cord paralysis), swallowing difficulty, apnea, weak cry. 15-35% mortality if symptomatic in infancy. Always rule out shunt malfunction first.

**[BOARD]** VP shunt malfunction is the MOST COMMON CAUSE of acute neurological change in spina bifida. Always evaluate shunt before attributing symptoms to tethered cord or Chiari II.

**[BOARD]** Detrusor leak point pressure >40 cm H2O = HIGH RISK for upper tract (renal) damage. Requires aggressive management (CIC + anticholinergics).

**[BOARD]** CIC (clean intermittent catheterization) is the CORNERSTONE of neurogenic bladder management. Lapides et al., 1972.

**[BOARD]** Latex allergy in spina bifida: 30-65%. Cross-reactive foods: Banana, Avocado, Chestnut, Kiwi (BACK). Latex-free precautions from birth.

**[BOARD]** L3 = quadriceps level. L4 = tibialis anterior level. L5 = peroneus/lateral hamstring level. S1 = gastrocnemius level. These are the MUST-KNOW motor level associations.

**[BOARD]** L4-L5 foot deformity = CALCANEUS (dorsiflexors without plantarflexors). S1 foot deformity = CAVOVARUS (intrinsic minus).

**[BOARD]** Hip reduction surgery in spina bifida does NOT improve ambulation outcomes (unlike CP). Ambulation is determined by neurological level.

**[BOARD]** Fractures in spina bifida present as painless swelling and warmth of a limb (below level of sensation). Often mistaken for cellulitis or DVT. X-ray confirms.

**[BOARD]** Tethered cord in myelomeningocele: Clinical diagnosis based on progressive symptoms (motor loss, scoliosis, bladder changes, foot deformity). MRI shows tethering in virtually all MMC patients, so imaging alone is not sufficient -- symptoms drive surgical decision.

**[BOARD]** Malone (MACE) procedure: Antegrade continence enema via appendicostomy/cecostomy for neurogenic bowel management. ~70-90% achieve social continence.

**[BOARD]** Prenatal screening: MSAFP elevated at 15-20 weeks -> amniocentesis for AFP + AChE -> confirmatory ultrasound. Lemon sign (skull) and banana sign (cerebellum) on ultrasound.

**[BOARD]** MSAFP is elevated in OPEN NTDs only, NOT in closed defects (lipomyelomeningocele, spina bifida occulta).

**[BOARD]** Obesity is a major secondary condition in spina bifida (prevalence ~35-50%). Contributes to loss of ambulation, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, skin breakdown, and reduced functional mobility. Weight management is critical.

**[BOARD]** Cognitive profile in spina bifida: Generally preserved verbal skills with deficits in visuospatial processing, executive function, attention, and math. Average IQ ~80 (range 60-110). Hydrocephalus and shunt complications lower IQ scores. "Cocktail party syndrome" (verbal fluency without depth of understanding) -- described but not universal.

**[BOARD]** Sleep-disordered breathing: Prevalent in spina bifida (Chiari II, central apnea, brainstem dysfunction). Polysomnography recommended for symptomatic patients.

**[BOARD]** Reproductive health: Males with spina bifida often have erectile dysfunction and fertility issues. Females can become pregnant (genetic counseling recommended -- recurrence risk + high-risk pregnancy with spinal deformity/VP shunt).
