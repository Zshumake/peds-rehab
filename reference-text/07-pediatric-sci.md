## 7.1 Epidemiology

`[BOARD]`

Pediatric spinal cord injury is uncommon relative to adult SCI, comprising approximately 3 to 5 percent of all spinal cord injuries. In the United States, an estimated 1,200 to 1,400 new pediatric SCIs occur annually. The National Spinal Cord Injury Statistical Center (NSCISC) data demonstrate a bimodal age distribution, with peaks in children aged 0 to 4 years and again in adolescence from 15 to 18 years. Males predominate overall (approximately 60 to 70 percent), with the male preponderance increasing in the adolescent group (exceeding 80 percent). In-hospital mortality is approximately 10 to 15 percent, with higher rates in very young children and those with complete injuries (Parent et al., 2011).

### Mechanisms by Age Group

| Age Group | Primary Mechanisms |
|---|---|
| Children <8 years | Motor vehicle collisions (passenger, pedestrian), falls, non-accidental trauma (important cause in infants) |
| Children 8-14 years | Motor vehicle collisions, sports (diving, football, gymnastics, trampoline), bicycle accidents |
| Adolescents 15-18 years | Motor vehicle collisions (driver), sports, violence (gunshot wounds), diving |
| Birth injuries | Rare; difficult deliveries (forceps, breech); may cause upper cervical cord injury |

Motor vehicle collisions are the most common mechanism across all age groups, though the proportion attributable to sports and violence increases with age.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Non-accidental trauma (child abuse) is an important and sometimes overlooked cause of SCI in infants. As with abusive head trauma, a high index of suspicion is necessary when the reported mechanism does not match the severity or pattern of spinal cord injury.

## 7.2 Anatomical Differences in the Pediatric Spine

`[BOARD]`

The pediatric spinal column possesses several characteristics that distinguish it fundamentally from the adult spine, influencing both injury patterns and the occurrence of SCIWORA.

### Key Anatomical Features

| Feature | Pediatric Characteristic | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Head-to-body ratio** | Head constitutes ~25% of body weight at birth vs. ~6% in adults | Higher fulcrum point; greater leverage forces on upper cervical spine |
| **Ligamentous laxity** | Greater elasticity of spinal ligaments | Allows spinal column displacement without fracture; predisposes to SCIWORA |
| **Facet joint orientation** | More horizontal, especially C1-C3 | Permits greater translational sliding movement |
| **Uncinate processes** | Underdeveloped | Less bony restraint on lateral flexion |
| **Vertebral body shape** | Anterior wedging | Promotes forward sliding |
| **Paraspinal musculature** | Immature, less developed | Less muscular stabilization and protection |
| **Ossification centers** | Multiple, incompletely fused | Can mimic fractures on imaging; create potential planes of weakness |

### Fulcrum of Cervical Motion

The fulcrum of cervical spine motion in young children is located at C2-C3, in contrast to C5-C6 in adults. This higher fulcrum, combined with the disproportionately large head, explains the marked predilection for upper cervical (C1-C4) injuries in children younger than 8 years.

The transition from pediatric to adult-type spinal biomechanics occurs at approximately 8 to 10 years of age, as head-to-body proportions normalize, facet joints become more vertically oriented, ossification progresses, and paraspinal musculature strengthens.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key biomechanical concept for board examinations is the discrepancy between spinal column and spinal cord elasticity: the pediatric spinal column can stretch up to 5 cm (2 inches) without structural disruption, while the spinal cord can tolerate only approximately 6 mm (0.25 inches) of distraction before sustaining injury. This discrepancy is the fundamental basis for SCIWORA.

## 7.3 SCIWORA: Spinal Cord Injury Without Radiographic Abnormality

`[BOARD]`

### Definition and Historical Context

SCIWORA was first described by Pang and Wilberger in their landmark 1982 paper as objective signs of myelopathy following trauma with no evidence of fracture, dislocation, or ligamentous instability on plain radiographs and CT scan. The condition is far more prevalent in children than in adults, accounting for 6 to 19 percent of all pediatric SCIs and up to 30 to 60 percent of SCIs in young children.

### Pathophysiology

The mechanism centers on elastic deformation of the pediatric spinal column. During hyperflexion, hyperextension, or longitudinal distraction, the spinal column deforms beyond the tolerance threshold of the spinal cord, producing cord injury, and then recoils to its normal anatomic alignment. The resulting radiographic and CT imaging is normal because no permanent column disruption occurred. Additional contributing mechanisms include vascular injury (ischemia from transient compression or stretching of cord vasculature) and disc herniation that may only be visible on MRI.

### Pang Classification of SCIWORA (MRI-Based)

Pang refined the SCIWORA classification in 2004, stratifying patients by MRI findings, which represent the strongest prognostic indicator.

| Type | MRI Finding | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Type I** | Major intramedullary hemorrhage or cord disruption | Poor: typically complete SCI with minimal recovery |
| **Type II** | Minor, focal hemorrhage | Intermediate: variable recovery |
| **Type III** | Cord edema only (no hemorrhage) | Good: significant recovery expected |
| **Type IV** | Normal MRI (true SCIWORA) | Best: generally good recovery; may represent cord concussion/neuropraxia |

### Delayed Neurological Deficits

A critical and unique feature of SCIWORA is the potential for delayed neurological deterioration occurring up to 48 hours after the initial injury. This likely results from secondary injury processes including progressive edema, ischemia, and reperfusion injury. Some children report transient warning symptoms — paresthesias, brief weakness, or numbness — that resolve before frank myelopathy develops hours later (Pang and Pollack, 1989).

**Clinical implication:** Any child who reports transient neurological symptoms following spinal trauma must be immobilized, observed for at least 48 hours, and undergo MRI even if the initial symptoms have resolved and CT is normal.

### Evaluation

1. **Plain radiographs:** Normal by definition; obtained to exclude fracture or dislocation
2. **CT spine:** Normal for bone; rules out subtle fractures but does not evaluate the cord
3. **MRI spine:** Essential and the gold standard; evaluates cord parenchyma for edema, hemorrhage, transection, disc herniation, epidural hematoma, and ligamentous injury
4. **Flexion-extension radiographs:** May be obtained after the acute phase (not acutely) to assess ligamentous instability
5. **Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs):** May show abnormalities even when MRI is normal; useful for prognostication

### Management

- **External cervical immobilization:** 8 to 12 weeks per the Pang protocol; rigid cervical collar
- **Activity restriction:** Contact sports and high-risk activities restricted for 6 to 12 months
- **Surgical intervention:** Generally not indicated unless MRI demonstrates compressive pathology (disc herniation, epidural hematoma) requiring decompression
- **Recurrent SCIWORA:** Reported in children who resume activity prematurely, supporting the rationale for the immobilization protocol

> **Clinical Pearl:** A normal MRI does not exclude SCIWORA. True SCIWORA with an entirely normal cord MRI can occur and may represent transient cord concussion. Nevertheless, these patients typically have the best prognosis among the SCIWORA subtypes.

## 7.4 Age-Specific Injury Patterns

`[BOARD]`

### Children Younger Than 8 Years: Upper Cervical Predominance

In young children, C1 through C4 injuries are disproportionately common. The anatomical factors responsible for this pattern — large head-to-body ratio, ligamentous laxity, horizontal facet orientation, underdeveloped uncinate processes, immature musculature, and the high fulcrum of cervical motion at C2-C3 — collectively direct injuring forces to the upper cervical spine.

Complete injuries (ASIA A) may be proportionally more common in young children with upper cervical injuries, though the higher rate of SCIWORA (which may carry a more favorable prognosis) partially offsets this observation.

### Children 8 Years and Older: Adult-Pattern Injuries

As the child grows, the cervical spine transitions to adult-like biomechanics: head-to-body ratio normalizes, facets assume a more vertical orientation, ossification progresses, and musculature develops. Consequently, C5 through C7 injuries become predominant, mirroring the adult pattern of lower cervical predominance. Thoracolumbar injuries also become more common in this age group, particularly with motor vehicle collisions and sports.

## 7.5 Atlantoaxial Instability

`[BOARD]`

### Definition and Measurement

Atlantoaxial instability (AAI) refers to excessive motion at the C1-C2 articulation. It is assessed by measuring the atlantodental interval (ADI) — the distance between the posterior aspect of the anterior arch of C1 and the anterior surface of the odontoid process of C2 — on lateral flexion-extension cervical radiographs. In children, an ADI greater than 4.5 mm (some sources use 5 mm) is considered abnormal.

### Down Syndrome

AAI occurs in approximately 10 to 30 percent of individuals with Down syndrome on radiographic assessment, but only 1 to 2 percent develop symptomatic cord compression requiring intervention (Pueschel and Scola, 1987).

**Pathophysiology:** Generalized ligamentous laxity (affecting the transverse ligament of the atlas that stabilizes the dens) combined with potential os odontoideum (hypoplastic or unfused dens) or hypoplastic posterior arch of C1.

**Screening and clearance:**
- The AAP (2011, updated 2022) recommends lateral cervical spine radiographs (flexion, extension, and neutral views) between ages 3 and 5 years, or earlier if symptomatic
- Special Olympics requires cervical spine radiographs before participation in high-risk sports (gymnastics, diving, butterfly stroke, diving starts in swimming, high jump, soccer, alpine skiing, equestrian sports)
- Athletes with ADI greater than 4.5 mm or signs of myelopathy are restricted from these activities
- The AAP has noted that screening radiographs have limited predictive value for identifying those at risk for symptomatic AAI

**Signs of symptomatic AAI (myelopathy):** New-onset neck pain, torticollis, gait deterioration, spasticity, hyperreflexia, clonus, extensor plantar responses, deterioration in hand function, bowel or bladder changes.

**Management:** Symptomatic AAI requires posterior C1-C2 fusion. Activity restriction for asymptomatic radiographic AAI remains debated.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Radiographic atlantoaxial instability is common in Down syndrome (10 to 30 percent), but symptomatic cord compression is rare (1 to 2 percent). Clinical signs of myelopathy — not radiographic measurements alone — should drive management decisions. However, Special Olympics still requires screening radiographs for clearance, making this a frequent board topic.

### Morquio Syndrome (Mucopolysaccharidosis Type IV)

Morquio syndrome, an autosomal recessive lysosomal storage disorder, carries an exceptionally high risk of craniocervical instability. Odontoid hypoplasia is nearly universal, and combined with ligamentous laxity, produces AAI that is a major source of morbidity and mortality.

- Cervical myelopathy is common and often insidious in onset, presenting as progressive weakness, spasticity, and respiratory compromise
- Prophylactic posterior cervical fusion is frequently recommended before symptomatic cord compression develops
- Anesthetic management requires extreme caution: high risk of cervical cord injury during intubation; fiberoptic intubation with manual in-line stabilization is recommended (Solanki et al., 2013)

### Other Conditions Associated with AAI

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Juvenile idiopathic arthritis | Cervical spine involvement in 30-60% of polyarticular JIA; pannus erosion |
| Os odontoideum | Congenital or post-fracture unfused dens |
| Klippel-Feil syndrome | Congenital cervical fusion; instability at adjacent unfused segments |
| Skeletal dysplasias | Various forms (spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, diastrophic dysplasia) |
| Ehlers-Danlos syndrome | Generalized ligamentous laxity |
| Grisel syndrome | Non-traumatic AAI following upper respiratory infection or pharyngeal surgery; inflammation-related ligamentous laxity |

## 7.6 Acute Management of Pediatric SCI

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Prehospital and Emergency Department Principles

Acute management follows the same fundamental principles as adult SCI with important pediatric modifications:

1. **Spinal immobilization:** Full spinal precautions during transport. In young children, the relatively large occiput causes flexion of the cervical spine when supine on a flat board; either a recess for the occiput or elevation of the torso is needed to maintain neutral cervical alignment.
2. **Airway management:** Cervical injuries may compromise respiratory function. Manual in-line stabilization during intubation is mandatory.
3. **Hemodynamic support:** Neurogenic shock (hypotension with bradycardia from loss of sympathetic tone) must be distinguished from hypovolemic shock (hypotension with tachycardia). Vasopressors may be required for neurogenic shock.
4. **Imaging:** Plain radiographs and CT for bony evaluation; MRI for cord assessment, particularly when neurological deficits are present without radiographic abnormality (suspected SCIWORA).
5. **Methylprednisolone:** The role of high-dose methylprednisolone remains controversial and is not standard of care in pediatric SCI. The NASCIS trials had significant methodological limitations, and current guidelines do not recommend routine steroid use.

### Spinal Shock

Spinal shock — the transient loss of all spinal cord function (motor, sensory, reflex, autonomic) below the level of injury — occurs immediately after acute SCI. It typically resolves over days to weeks, with return of the bulbocavernosus reflex signaling the end of spinal shock. Only after spinal shock resolves can the true extent of the neurological injury be assessed using the ASIA/ISNCSCI classification.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In young children, the relatively large occiput causes cervical flexion when the child is placed supine on a standard backboard. To maintain neutral alignment, either a recess must be cut in the board for the occiput or the torso must be elevated. This is a frequently tested board concept.

## 7.7 Neurogenic Bladder

`[BOARD]`

### Pathophysiology

Neurogenic bladder management is among the most critical aspects of lifelong care in pediatric SCI. The bladder pattern depends on the level of spinal cord injury.

| Lesion Level | Bladder Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Suprasacral** (above S2-S4 conus) | Upper motor neuron bladder | Detrusor hyperreflexia (spastic bladder); detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD); high intravesical pressures; risk of upper tract damage |
| **Sacral/Conus/Cauda equina** | Lower motor neuron bladder | Areflexic bladder; large capacity, low pressure; stress incontinence; denervated sphincter with decreased outlet resistance |
| **Mixed patterns** | Common, especially incomplete injuries | Variable features of both UMN and LMN bladder |

The primary goal of neurogenic bladder management is renal preservation — preventing hydronephrosis, vesicoureteral reflux, and renal failure from chronically elevated intravesical pressures.

### Evaluation

- **Urodynamic studies:** Gold standard for assessing detrusor pressure, capacity, compliance, sphincter function, and DSD; performed after spinal shock resolves and repeated periodically (annually or semi-annually)
- **Renal ultrasound:** Annual screening for hydronephrosis and bladder wall changes
- **Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG):** Evaluates vesicoureteral reflux
- **Serum creatinine/BUN:** Renal function monitoring

### Management

**Clean intermittent catheterization (CIC):** The mainstay of management. Performed every 4 to 6 hours; children typically learn self-catheterization by age 6 to 8, with family or caregivers performing the procedure in younger children.

**Pharmacologic adjuncts:**
- **Anticholinergics (oxybutynin):** Reduce detrusor overactivity, increase bladder capacity, lower intravesical pressure. Side effects include dry mouth, constipation, cognitive effects, and heat intolerance.
- **Onabotulinum toxin A (intradetrusor injection):** For refractory detrusor overactivity
- **Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin, terazosin):** May help with bladder outlet obstruction or DSD; less commonly used in young children

**Surgical options:**
- **Augmentation cystoplasty:** Bladder augmentation using an intestinal segment (typically ileum) for refractory low-compliance or high-pressure bladder; increases capacity and reduces pressure; complications include mucus production, metabolic acidosis, bladder stones, and perforation risk
- **Continent catheterizable channel (Mitrofanoff procedure):** Appendicovesicostomy creating a catheterizable channel from bladder to umbilicus; important for wheelchair users who have difficulty with urethral catheterization

### Urinary Tract Infections

UTIs are extremely common with neurogenic bladder and represent a major source of morbidity. Asymptomatic bacteriuria is prevalent in patients performing CIC and is generally not treated. Symptomatic UTIs present with cloudy or malodorous urine, increased spasticity, autonomic dysreflexia, new incontinence, fever, or malaise. Prophylactic antibiotics are generally not recommended for routine use due to resistance concerns.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The primary goal of neurogenic bladder management in pediatric SCI is renal preservation through maintenance of low intravesical pressures, not urinary continence. However, achieving social continence is an important secondary goal that significantly impacts quality of life and social participation.

## 7.8 Neurogenic Bowel

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Classification

| Type | Lesion Level | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **UMN bowel** | Supraconal | Increased rectal tone; preserved reflex activity; spastic external anal sphincter; constipation predominates; digital stimulation triggers reflex evacuation |
| **LMN bowel** | Conus/Cauda equina | Decreased rectal tone; areflexic; lax external sphincter; both constipation and incontinence are problematic |

### Management

A structured timed bowel program is fundamental, with regularity being the most important factor. The program should be performed at the same time daily or every other day, ideally after a meal to leverage the gastrocolic reflex.

**Approaches include:**
- Dietary management: adequate fiber and fluid intake
- Stool softeners (docusate) and osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol)
- Stimulant laxatives (bisacodyl, senna) and suppositories
- Digital stimulation for UMN bowel (triggers rectocolic reflex)
- Transanal irrigation (Peristeen system): retrograde colonic washout; growing evidence in pediatric SCI
- Antegrade continence enema (ACE/Malone procedure): appendicostomy providing antegrade access to the cecum for flush enemas; highly effective for achieving social continence in children

## 7.9 Autonomic Dysreflexia

`[BOARD]`

### Pathophysiology

Autonomic dysreflexia (AD) is a potentially life-threatening condition occurring in patients with SCI at T6 and above (above the splanchnic sympathetic outflow). A noxious stimulus below the injury level generates afferent signals that enter the spinal cord and trigger a massive, uninhibited sympathetic discharge causing vasoconstriction and hypertension below the lesion. Baroreceptor-mediated parasympathetic activation produces compensatory bradycardia and vasodilation above the lesion, but descending inhibitory signals cannot cross the injury to modulate the sympathetic output below. The result is a sustained hypertensive crisis that persists until the offending stimulus is removed.

### Clinical Presentation

| Above the Lesion | Below the Lesion |
|---|---|
| Headache (often pounding and severe) | Pallor |
| Facial flushing | Piloerection ("goose bumps") |
| Nasal congestion | Cool skin |
| Diaphoresis | |
| Blurred vision | |

Bradycardia is typical (baroreceptor-mediated), though tachycardia can also occur. If untreated, complications include seizures, intracranial hemorrhage, retinal hemorrhage, pulmonary edema, cardiac arrhythmia, and death.

### Common Triggers

| Trigger Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Bladder** (most common, ~75-85%) | Distension, blocked catheter, UTI, bladder spasm, urodynamic testing |
| **Bowel** (second most common) | Constipation, fecal impaction, rectal distension during bowel program |
| **Skin** | Pressure ulcers, ingrown toenails, tight clothing, burns |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Occult fractures, heterotopic ossification |
| **Other** | Menstruation, labor/delivery, DVT, surgical procedures, temperature extremes |

### Acute Management Protocol

1. **Sit the patient upright** and dangle legs to lower blood pressure via orthostatic pooling
2. **Loosen all clothing, straps, and abdominal binders**
3. **Identify and remove the trigger:**
   - Check bladder first: assess catheter for kinks/blockage; irrigate or replace; if no catheter, perform straight catheterization (use lidocaine jelly)
   - Check bowel: perform digital rectal examination with lidocaine jelly; gently disimpact if indicated
   - Inspect skin for irritants; relieve pressure
4. **Monitor blood pressure every 2 to 5 minutes**
5. **If blood pressure remains elevated after trigger management:**
   - Nifedipine (sublingual/oral): calcium channel blocker with rapid onset
   - Nitroglycerin paste (topical): can be removed when pressure normalizes
   - Hydralazine or prazosin
   - Avoid beta-blockers as first-line (risk of unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction)
6. **If refractory:** treat as hypertensive emergency; ICU admission

### Pediatric Considerations

AD can occur in children of any age with high-level SCI, including infants. Recognition may be challenging because young children cannot articulate symptoms; irritability, crying, and facial flushing may be the only observable signs. Additionally, baseline blood pressures in children with SCI are typically low, so readings that appear "normal" by standard pediatric charts may represent significantly elevated pressures for a given patient.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In autonomic dysreflexia, always check the bladder first — it is the most common trigger, accounting for 75 to 85 percent of episodes. A blocked catheter is the single most frequent precipitant and is rapidly reversible. The first non-pharmacologic intervention is to sit the patient upright to exploit gravity-mediated blood pressure reduction.

## 7.10 Scoliosis in Pediatric SCI

`[BOARD]`

### Epidemiology and Risk

Neuromuscular scoliosis develops in nearly 100 percent of children who sustain SCI before age 10 years — a critical board fact. The risk is inversely proportional to age at injury:

| Age at SCI | Scoliosis Risk |
|---|---|
| Before age 10 | 97-100% |
| Ages 10-15 | 50-80% |
| After skeletal maturity | 5-10% (similar to adult SCI) |

The typical curve pattern is a long, collapsing C-curve (paralytic neuromuscular scoliosis), fundamentally different from the structural curves of idiopathic scoliosis. Contributing factors include muscle paralysis and asymmetric trunk support, growth asymmetry, and prolonged sitting posture (Dearolf et al., 1990).

### Monitoring and Management

- **Monitoring:** Clinical examination and radiographs (standing or sitting) at least annually during growth
- **Bracing (TLSO):** Limited effectiveness for neuromuscular scoliosis; may slow progression and improve sitting posture but does not prevent the need for surgery in most cases
- **Surgical indications:** Progressive curve exceeding 40 to 50 degrees; loss of sitting balance; respiratory compromise from thoracic deformity; skin breakdown from pelvic obliquity
- **Surgical approach:** Posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation; typically requires fusion from upper thoracic spine to pelvis (longer constructs than idiopathic scoliosis)
- **Growing rods:** Used in very young children to allow continued spinal growth before definitive fusion; magnetically controlled growing rods (MAGEC) allow office-based lengthening, avoiding repetitive surgical procedures
- **Complications:** High rates in SCI patients — pressure ulcers from hardware, delayed wound healing, osteoporotic bone quality, respiratory compromise, perioperative autonomic instability

### Pelvic Obliquity

Pelvic obliquity commonly accompanies scoliosis, causing uneven weight distribution in the wheelchair that increases pressure ulcer risk, contributes to sitting imbalance, and may lead to hip subluxation. Management includes wheelchair seating modifications and surgical correction with fusion extended to the pelvis.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nearly all children who sustain SCI before age 10 will develop scoliosis. This mandates lifelong spinal surveillance and early engagement with orthopedic specialists. Bracing can slow progression and improve sitting function but is generally unable to prevent the eventual need for surgical fusion.

## 7.11 Growth and Bone Health

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Growth Considerations

SCI in childhood affects linear growth through multiple mechanisms: paralyzed limbs may be shorter due to disuse effects on growth plates, and paralytic scoliosis reduces trunk height. Nutritional status — potentially compromised by dysphagia (in cervical injuries), depression, medication effects, or alternatively predisposed to obesity (reduced energy expenditure) — must be carefully monitored.

Puberty timing is generally normal, though central precocious puberty may occur if the SCI is accompanied by associated traumatic brain injury. Sexual health education, including discussion of altered sensation, erectile function (reflexogenic erections preserved in UMN lesions above S2-S4; psychogenic erections in lower lesions), fertility considerations, and safe practices, should begin in adolescence.

### Bone Health and Fracture Risk

Rapid bone mineral density loss occurs below the level of injury, beginning within weeks and affecting primarily the sublesional skeleton — particularly the distal femur and proximal tibia. Fractures in insensate limbs may occur with minimal or no trauma during routine activities such as transfers or range of motion exercises.

**Fracture presentation in SCI:** Because the limb is insensate, pain is absent. The presenting features are limb swelling, erythema, and warmth — a constellation that closely mimics deep vein thrombosis and cellulitis. New-onset autonomic dysreflexia or increased spasticity may also signal an occult fracture.

**Management strategies:**
- Weight-bearing activities: standing frame programs, gait training when possible
- Calcium and vitamin D supplementation
- Bisphosphonates: limited pediatric SCI data; used selectively
- Fracture management: typically conservative with splinting and soft immobilization; rigid casts are avoided because they risk causing pressure ulcers on insensate skin

### Heterotopic Ossification

Heterotopic ossification occurs in approximately 3 to 10 percent of children with SCI (lower than the 15 to 35 percent rate in adults). It affects large joints below the injury level, most commonly the hips and knees. Presentation includes swelling, warmth, and decreased range of motion. Elevated alkaline phosphatase and a positive triple-phase bone scan are characteristic. Management includes range of motion exercises, NSAIDs (indomethacin) for prevention, and surgical excision if the HO is mature and functionally limiting (typically delayed 12 to 18 months).

> **Clinical Pearl:** In a child with SCI who presents with a warm, swollen, erythematous lower extremity, the differential diagnosis must include fracture (even with trivial or no reported trauma), deep vein thrombosis, cellulitis, and heterotopic ossification. A fracture is particularly likely given the severe osteoporosis that develops below the level of injury.

## 7.12 Wheelchair Prescription for Growing Children

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Principles

Wheelchair prescription in pediatric SCI must balance current functional needs with anticipated growth. Pediatric wheelchairs require adjustable components (seat width, depth, back height, footrest height) and may need replacement every 2 to 4 years during growth periods.

Developmentally, the wheelchair should facilitate exploration, play, socialization, and education. Early mobility is critical for cognitive and psychosocial development — evidence supports introducing power mobility as early as 12 to 18 months of age for children who cannot achieve independent mobility otherwise (Butler, 1986).

### Manual Versus Power Wheelchairs

| Type | Indications | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Manual** | Adequate upper extremity strength and endurance | Lightweight rigid or folding frame; proper fit essential for propulsion efficiency and shoulder preservation |
| **Power** | Insufficient UE strength; high cervical injuries; multiple limb involvement | Joystick, head array, sip-and-puff, or eye-tracking drive controls depending on functional level |

### Essential Features

- **Tilt-in-space:** 30 to 45+ degrees for pressure relief, postural management, blood pressure support (orthostatic hypotension), and comfort
- **Recline:** Pressure redistribution, catheterization access, rest
- **Pressure-distributing cushion:** Air cell (ROHO), foam/gel (Jay), or hybrid designs; pressure mapping to guide selection
- **Back support:** Contoured or custom-molded for scoliosis management; thoracic lateral supports as needed
- **Headrest:** Required for cervical injuries

### Standing Programs

Standing frames are recommended for children with SCI to promote bone health (weight-bearing), maintain joint range of motion, support bowel and bladder function, and provide cardiovascular and psychosocial benefits. Options include supine, prone, and sit-to-stand standers, as well as power wheelchairs with integrated standing features. A typical prescription is 45 to 60 minutes daily, which can be incorporated into the school day.

## 7.13 Additional Pediatric SCI Topics

### Thermoregulation

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Children with high-level SCI are at risk for poikilothermia — the inability to regulate body temperature due to loss of sympathetic control of vasomotor tone and sweating below the lesion. Body temperature fluctuates with environmental conditions. Children are particularly vulnerable, and families and schools must be educated about appropriate clothing, environmental temperature control, monitoring, and adequate hydration.

### Spasticity Management

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Spasticity management in pediatric SCI follows the same pharmacologic ladder as in cerebral palsy:

1. Physical therapy, positioning, orthotics, and serial casting
2. Oral medications: baclofen (most commonly used), tizanidine, diazepam, dantrolene
3. Botulinum toxin injections for focal spasticity
4. Intrathecal baclofen pump for severe, generalized spasticity

Intrathecal baclofen pump malfunction represents a medical emergency. Withdrawal syndrome — fever, rebound severe spasticity, rhabdomyolysis, seizures — can be fatal and requires emergent evaluation and treatment.

### Pressure Injuries

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Risk factors in pediatric SCI include immobility, insensate skin, incontinence, malnutrition, spasticity, and bony prominences. Common sites differ somewhat from adults: the occiput is a common site in young children (owing to the proportionally larger head), along with sacrum, ischial tuberosities, greater trochanters, and heels. Prevention centers on scheduled weight shifts every 15 to 30 minutes, appropriate cushion and mattress selection, twice-daily skin inspection, nutrition optimization, and moisture management. Age-appropriate skin care education — including self-inspection using a mirror for areas the child cannot see — should be introduced and gradually transferred to the child as maturity allows.

### Latex Allergy

Children with SCI are at increased risk for latex allergy due to frequent exposure through catheterization, surgical procedures, and medical devices. Latex-free products should be used throughout care.

## 7.14 Transition to Adult Care

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The transition from pediatric to adult-oriented SCI care is a critical period that should be planned, gradual, and ideally begin in early adolescence. Key domains include:

- **Medical management:** Identifying adult providers familiar with lifelong SCI management; transferring medical records and care plans
- **Vocational planning:** Career exploration, vocational rehabilitation services, supported employment
- **Independent living skills:** Self-management of bladder and bowel programs, skin care, medication management, nutritional planning
- **Sexual health:** Comprehensive education on fertility, reproductive options, sexual function, and safe practices
- **Psychosocial support:** Transition inherently involves loss of the family-centered pediatric care model; adult services expect greater patient independence; mental health screening and support are essential
- **Insurance and financial planning:** Navigating the transition from pediatric to adult coverage; disability benefits; durable medical equipment funding

> **Clinical Pearl:** The shift from family-centered pediatric care to the patient-centered adult model is one of the most challenging aspects of transition for young people with SCI. Systematic transition planning — beginning by age 14 to 16 — significantly improves continuity of care and reduces medical complications in early adulthood.
