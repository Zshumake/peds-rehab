## 1. GUIDELINES FOUND

### 1.1 Orthotic Guidelines

- **AACPDM (American Academy for Cerebral Palsy and Developmental Medicine) Care Pathways: Ankle-Foot Orthoses for Children with Cerebral Palsy.**
  - Evidence-based pathway for AFO prescription
  - Emphasis on matching orthosis to gait deviation and GMFCS level

- **International Society for Prosthetics and Orthotics (ISPO) Consensus Conference on Lower Limb Orthotic Management of Cerebral Palsy (2009).**
  - Morris C, Bowers R, Ross K, Stevens P, Phillips D. *Prosthetics and Orthotics International*. 2011;35(4):350-359.
  - AFO prescription should be based on specific gait deviations
  - Systematic approach to orthotic selection by gait classification

- **Scoliosis Research Society (SRS) / AAOS Guidelines on Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis:**
  - Bracing indicated for curves 25-45 degrees in skeletally immature patients (Risser 0-2)
  - BrAIST (Bracing in Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis Trial) confirmed efficacy

### 1.2 Assistive Technology / Seating Guidelines

- **RESNA (Rehabilitation Engineering and Assistive Technology Society of North America) Position Papers on Wheelchair Seating.**
  - Evidence-based approach to wheelchair seating assessment
  - Pelvis as foundation of seating

- **WHO Guidelines on Provision of Manual Wheelchairs in Less-Resourced Settings (2008).**

- **AACPDM Systematic Reviews on Power Mobility:**
  - Evidence supports early power mobility exposure (12-18 months)
  - Promotes exploration, social interaction, cognitive development

### 1.3 Outcome Measure Guidelines

- **AACPDM Outcome Measures Recommendations:**
  - GMFM-66 as gold standard evaluative measure for CP motor function
  - WeeFIM for functional independence
  - PEDI/PEDI-CAT for functional capability

- **CanChild Centre for Childhood Disability Research — GMFCS Implementation Guidelines.**
  - Palisano RJ, Rosenbaum P, et al. Stable classification system

### 1.4 Spina Bifida Guidelines

- **Spina Bifida Association Guidelines for the Care of People with Spina Bifida (2018, updated 2020).**
  - Comprehensive orthotic recommendations by neurological level
  - Mobility expectations by level

## 2. LANDMARK TRIALS & PIVOTAL STUDIES

### 2.1 AFO Studies

- **Romkes J, Brunner R (2002).** "Comparison of a dynamic and a hinged ankle-foot orthosis by gait analysis in patients with hemiplegic cerebral palsy." *Gait & Posture*. 15(1):18-24.
  - Compared hinged vs dynamic AFOs in hemiplegic CP
  - Hinged AFO improved knee kinematics at initial contact

- **Brehm MA, Harlaar J, Schwartz M (2008).** "Effect of ankle-foot orthoses on walking efficiency and gait in children with cerebral palsy." *J Rehabil Med*. 40(7):529-534.
  - AFOs improved walking efficiency (decreased energy expenditure)

- **Aboutorabi A, Arazpour M, Ahmadi Bani M, et al. (2017).** "Efficacy of ankle foot orthoses types on walking in children with cerebral palsy: A systematic review." *Ann Phys Rehabil Med*. 60(6):393-402.
  - Systematic review: solid and hinged AFOs both effective
  - Hinged AFOs superior for facilitating more normal gait kinematics

### 2.2 BrAIST (Scoliosis Bracing)

- **Weinstein SL, Dolan LA, Wright JG, Dobbs MB (2013).** "Effects of bracing in adolescents with idiopathic scoliosis." *N Engl J Med*. 369(16):1512-1521.
  - Landmark RCT: Bracing significantly reduced progression to surgical threshold (>50 degrees)
  - Success rate 72% with bracing vs 48% observation
  - Dose-response relationship: >12 hr/day wear associated with better outcomes
  - **Board pearl:** BrAIST trial confirmed bracing efficacy; **16-23 hours/day recommended**

### 2.3 Early Power Mobility

- **Jones MA, McEwen IR, Hansen L (2003).** "Use of power mobility for a young child with spinal muscular atrophy." *Phys Ther*. 83(3):253-262.

- **Butler C, Okamoto GA, McKay TM (1983).** "Powered mobility for very young disabled children." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 25(4):472-474.
  - Demonstrated that children as young as 24 months could operate power wheelchairs

- **Lynch A, Ryu JC, Agrawal S, Galloway JC (2009).** "Power mobility training for a 7-month-old infant with spina bifida." *Pediatric Physical Therapy*. 21(4):362-368.
  - Extended power mobility concept to infancy

- **Kenyon LK, Farris JP, Aldrich NJ, Rhodes S (2021).** "Reframing our approach to power mobility for young children." *Pediatric Physical Therapy*. 33(1):E1-E7.
  - Evidence supporting power mobility access from 12-18 months of age
  - Conceptual framework: mobility as exploration tool, not just transportation

### 2.4 GMFCS Development & Validation

- **Palisano R, Rosenbaum P, Walter S, et al. (1997).** "Development and reliability of a system to classify gross motor function in children with cerebral palsy." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 39(4):214-223.
  - Original GMFCS development paper
  - 5-level classification system

- **Palisano RJ, Rosenbaum P, Bartlett D, Livingston MH (2008).** "Content validity of the expanded and revised Gross Motor Function Classification System." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 50(10):744-750.
  - GMFCS-E&R: Extended to include 12-18 year age band

- **Hanna SE, Rosenbaum PL, Bartlett DJ, et al. (2009).** "Stability and decline in gross motor function among children and youth with cerebral palsy aged 2 to 21 years." *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 51(4):295-302.
  - GMFCS levels are STABLE over time (Board pearl)
  - Motor function growth curves by GMFCS level

### 2.5 GMFM-66 Development

- **Russell DJ, Rosenbaum PL, Avery LM, Lane M (2002).** *Gross Motor Function Measure (GMFM-66 & GMFM-88) User's Manual*. Cambridge University Press.
  - Definitive manual for GMFM administration

- **Russell DJ, Avery LM, Rosenbaum PL, et al. (2000).** "Improved scaling of the Gross Motor Function Measure for children with cerebral palsy: evidence of reliability and validity." *Phys Ther*. 80(9):873-885.
  - Rasch analysis converting GMFM-88 to GMFM-66
  - Interval-level measurement allowing meaningful change scores

### 2.6 PEDI / PEDI-CAT

- **Haley SM, Coster WJ, Ludlow LH, et al. (1992).** *Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory (PEDI): Development, Standardization, and Administration Manual*. Boston University.

- **Haley SM, Coster WJ, Dumas HM, et al. (2012).** "PEDI-CAT: Development, standardization, and administration of the Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory Computer Adaptive Test." *J Pediatr Rehabil Med*. 5(1):1-14.
  - Computer adaptive version: shorter administration time
  - 276 items reduced to ~15-20 per domain via CAT algorithm

## 3. RECENT EVIDENCE (2020-2024)

  - Concept of "tuning" AFO-footwear combination to optimize shank-to-vertical angle

- **Powered mobility devices for young children:**
  - Explorer Mini, Permobil Explorer Mini — designed for ages 12+ months
  - Wizzybug (UK charity-loaned powered wheelchair for children 14 months-5 years)

### 3.3 Outcome Measure Updates

- **PEDI-CAT ASD version:** Adapted for autism spectrum disorder population
- **Bayley-4 (2019):** Updated norms; 5 scales covering 1-42 months
- **ASQ-3 and ASQ:SE-2:** Continued validation across diverse populations
- **PROMIS Pediatric:** Patient-reported outcome measures for children with chronic conditions

## 4. EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### 4.1 Orthotic Use in CP

- **AFO usage:** ~50-80% of children with CP use some form of lower extremity orthosis
- **AFO most commonly prescribed orthosis in pediatric rehabilitation**
- **GMFCS Level & orthotic use:**
  - Level I: ~30% use AFOs
  - Level II: ~60-70% use AFOs
  - Level III: ~80%+ use AFOs/KAFOs + gait aids
  - Level IV-V: Standing frames, seating systems, positioning devices

### 4.2 Spina Bifida & Ambulation by Level

- **Thoracic level:** <10% community ambulators as adults; primary wheelchair users
- **High lumbar (L1-L2):** ~10-30% household/exercise ambulators; most use wheelchair for community
- **Mid-lumbar (L3):** ~50% community ambulators with KAFOs and crutches
- **Low lumbar (L4-L5):** ~80% community ambulators with AFOs
- **Sacral (S1-S3):** ~95%+ community ambulators; may need SMOs or no orthotics

### 4.3 Scoliosis

- **Prevalence of AIS:** 2-3% of adolescents (10-16 years)
- **Gender:** Female predominance 8:1 for curves requiring treatment
- **Risk of progression:** Higher with younger age, skeletal immaturity (Risser 0-1), larger curve magnitude
- **Neuromuscular scoliosis:**
  - CP: 25% (GMFCS I) to 75-100% (GMFCS V)
  - DMD: 90%+ after loss of ambulation
  - SMA: 60-90% depending on type
  - Spina bifida: 60-90% (thoracic level)

### 4.4 AAC Device Usage

- **~2 million Americans use AAC** (ASHA data)
- **CP:** ~25-30% of children with CP have communication impairments requiring AAC
- **Most common AAC need:** Dysarthria secondary to CP (spastic or dyskinetic)

## 5. CLINICAL SCALES & OUTCOME MEASURES

### 5.1 Gross Motor Function Classification System (GMFCS) — Expanded & Revised

- **Citation:** Palisano R, Rosenbaum P, Walter S, et al. (1997). *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 39(4):214-223. Updated: Palisano RJ, et al. (2008). *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 50(10):744-750.
- **Purpose:** Classification (descriptive/predictive); NOT evaluative
- **Age bands:** <2, 2-4, 4-6, 6-12, 12-18 years
- **5 Levels:**

| Level | Description | Key Features |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **I** | Walks without limitations | May have limitations in advanced motor skills (running, jumping); no gait aids |
| **II** | Walks with limitations | Difficulty with uneven surfaces, stairs without railing; may use handheld mobility device for long distances; minimal ability to run/jump |
| **III** | Walks using handheld mobility device | Uses crutches, walker, or canes indoors and outdoors; may use wheelchair for long distances |
| **IV** | Self-mobility with limitations; may use powered mobility | Transported or uses power wheelchair in community; may walk short distances with walker and physical assistance at home |
| **V** | Transported in manual wheelchair | Severely limited self-mobility even with assistive technology; dependent for all mobility and transfers |

- **Board pearls:**
  - GMFCS is a CLASSIFICATION system, not an evaluative outcome measure
  - GMFCS level is **STABLE over time** — does NOT change with intervention (Board classic)
  - GMFCS level by age 2 is predictive of adult mobility
  - GMFCS predicts ambulation potential, orthotic needs, surgical timing
  - GMFCS I-III: likely community ambulators
  - GMFCS IV-V: primarily wheelchair users

### 5.2 Gross Motor Function Measure (GMFM-66 and GMFM-88)

- **Citation:** Russell DJ, et al. (2000). *Phys Ther*. 80(9):873-885. Manual: Russell DJ, Rosenbaum PL, Avery LM, Lane M (2002).
- **Purpose:** EVALUATIVE measure — detects change in gross motor function over time
- **GMFM-88:**
  - 88 items in 5 dimensions:
    - A: Lying & Rolling (17 items)
    - B: Sitting (20 items)
    - C: Crawling & Kneeling (14 items)
    - D: Standing (13 items)
    - E: Walking, Running, Jumping (24 items)
  - Each item scored 0-3 (0 = does not initiate; 3 = completes)
  - Ordinal scale; dimension scores are percent of maximum

- **GMFM-66:**
  - 66 items selected from the 88 using Rasch analysis
  - **Interval-level scale** (unlike GMFM-88 which is ordinal)
  - Score range: 0-100
  - Administered via computer software (Gross Motor Ability Estimator, GMAE)
  - **Board pearl:** GMFM-66 is PREFERRED for research because it provides interval-level data and item difficulty hierarchy
  - Can compute from a subset of items

- **Motor Growth Curves:**
  - Hanna SE, et al. (2009). *Dev Med Child Neurol*. 51(4):295-302.
  - Motor development trajectories by GMFCS level
  - **GMFCS I:** Approaches ceiling (~90-100) by age 5-6
  - **GMFCS II:** Plateaus ~70-80 by age 5-7
  - **GMFCS III:** Plateaus ~50-60 by age 5-7
  - **GMFCS IV:** Plateaus ~30-40 by age 4-6
  - **GMFCS V:** Plateaus ~15-25 by age 3-5
  - **Board pearl:** GMFCS IV-V may show DECLINE in motor function after age 7-8 (as body outgrows motor capacity)

### 5.3 WeeFIM (Functional Independence Measure for Children)

- **Citation:** Msall ME, DiGaudio K, Rogers BT, et al. (1994). "The Functional Independence Measure for Children (WeeFIM): conceptual basis and pilot use in children with developmental disabilities." *Clin Pediatr*. 33(7):421-430.
- **Age range:** 6 months to 7 years (developmental age); also for older children/adolescents with disabilities
- **Structure:** **18 items** across **3 domains:**

| Domain | Items | Number |
|--------|-------|--------|
| **Self-care** | Eating, grooming, bathing, dressing upper, dressing lower, toileting | 8 items (includes sphincter control: bladder, bowel) |
| **Mobility** | Transfers (chair/wheelchair, toilet, tub/shower), locomotion (walk/wheelchair, stairs) | 5 items |
| **Cognition** | Comprehension, expression, social interaction, problem solving, memory | 5 items |

- **Scoring:** **7-point ordinal scale** per item:
  - 7 = Complete independence
  - 6 = Modified independence (device, extra time)
  - 5 = Supervision/setup
  - 4 = Minimal assistance (performs 75%+)
  - 3 = Moderate assistance (performs 50%+)
  - 2 = Maximal assistance (performs 25%+)
  - 1 = Total assistance (performs <25%)
- **Score range:** 18 (total dependence) to 126 (complete independence)
- **Board pearl:** WeeFIM is a measure of burden of care / functional independence; derived from adult FIM; 18 items, 7-point scale, 3 domains

### 5.4 PEDI and PEDI-CAT

#### PEDI (Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory)
- **Citation:** Haley SM, Coster WJ, Ludlow LH, et al. (1992). Boston University.
- **Age range:** 6 months to 7.5 years (developmental age)
- **Structure:** **3 domains:**
  - Self-care (73 items)
  - Mobility (59 items)
  - Social function (65 items)
- **Two scales per domain:**
  - **Functional Skills (Capability):** What the child CAN do (scored 0 = unable, 1 = capable)
  - **Caregiver Assistance (Performance):** How much help the child needs (6-point scale: 5 = independent to 0 = total assistance)
- **Board pearl:** PEDI measures BOTH capability (what child can do) AND performance (what child does do with caregiver assistance)
- **Modification scale:** Amount of equipment/environmental modification needed

#### PEDI-CAT (Computer Adaptive Test version)
- **Citation:** Haley SM, Coster WJ, Dumas HM, et al. (2012). *J Pediatr Rehabil Med*. 5(1):1-14.
- **Age range:** Birth to 20 years (expanded range)
- **Structure:** **4 domains:**
  - Daily Activities (68 items)
  - Mobility (75 items)
  - Social/Cognitive (60 items)
  - Responsibility (51 items) — NEW domain
- **Administration:** Computer adaptive; ~15-20 items per domain (vs full PEDI 197 items)
- **Scoring:** Item response theory; scaled scores 20-80
- **Board pearl:** PEDI-CAT adds Responsibility domain (child's role in managing own life tasks); faster administration via CAT algorithm

### 5.5 Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development, 4th Edition (Bayley-4)

- **Citation:** Bayley N, Aylward GP (2019). *Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development*, 4th ed. Pearson.
- **Age range:** **1-42 months** (16 days to 42 months, 15 days)
- **Structure:** **5 scales:**
  - **Cognitive** (mental/play-based items)
  - **Language** — Receptive Communication + Expressive Communication
  - **Motor** — Fine Motor + Gross Motor
  - **Social-Emotional** (questionnaire)
  - **Adaptive Behavior** (questionnaire)
- **Administration:** Directly administered (cognitive, language, motor); caregiver-completed (social-emotional, adaptive)
- **Scoring:** Raw scores converted to:
  - Scaled scores (subtests): mean 10, SD 3
  - Composite scores: mean 100, SD 15
  - Percentile ranks, age equivalents, growth scores
- **Board pearls:**
  - Bayley-4 is a DIAGNOSTIC measure (norm-referenced), not a screener
  - Identifies developmental delay: composite score <70 = significant delay (>2 SD below mean)
  - Updated from Bayley-III (2006) with improved norms
  - Used extensively in high-risk infant follow-up (NICU graduates, premature infants)

### 5.6 Denver Developmental Screening Test II (Denver II)

- **Citation:** Frankenburg WK, Dodds J, Archer P, et al. (1992). "The Denver II: a major revision and restandardization of the Denver Developmental Screening Test." *Pediatrics*. 89(1):91-97.
- **Age range:** Birth to 6 years
- **Purpose:** SCREENING tool (not diagnostic)
- **Structure:** **4 domains:**
  - **Personal-Social** (e.g., smiles, feeds self, dresses)
  - **Fine Motor-Adaptive** (e.g., grasps, draws, copies)
  - **Language** (e.g., babbles, says words, follows commands)
  - **Gross Motor** (e.g., sits, walks, jumps)
- **125 items** total; age-based norms shown on single-page form
- **Scoring:** Each item rated:
  - Pass (P)
  - Fail (F)
  - No opportunity (NO)
  - Refusal (R)
- **Interpretation:**
  - **Normal:** No delays, maximum 1 caution
  - **Suspect:** 2+ cautions and/or 1+ delays
  - **Untestable:** Refusals impair interpretation
- **Board pearls:**
  - Denver II is a SCREENING tool, NOT a diagnostic instrument
  - High sensitivity but limited specificity (many false positives)
  - Should be followed by diagnostic testing (e.g., Bayley) if abnormal
  - 4 domains (Board classic question)

### 5.7 Ages and Stages Questionnaire, 3rd Edition (ASQ-3)

- **Citation:** Squires J, Bricker D (2009). *Ages & Stages Questionnaires, Third Edition (ASQ-3)*. Paul H. Brookes Publishing.
- **Age range:** 1-66 months (21 age-specific questionnaires)
- **Purpose:** Parent-completed SCREENING tool
- **5 domains:**
  - Communication
  - Gross Motor
  - Fine Motor
  - Problem Solving
  - Personal-Social
- **30 items per questionnaire** (6 per domain)
- **Scoring:** Each item: Yes (10), Sometimes (5), Not Yet (0)
  - Domain scores compared to cutoffs
  - Scores below cutoff: referral for further evaluation
- **Board pearl:** ASQ-3 is PARENT-COMPLETED screening (vs Denver II which is clinician-administered); more practical for population screening

### 5.8 Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM)

- **Citation:** Law M, Baptiste S, Carswell A, et al. (1990). *Canadian Occupational Performance Measure*. CAOT Publications.
- **Purpose:** Client-centered outcome measure; identifies priorities for intervention
- **Structure:**
  - Semi-structured interview identifying problems in self-care, productivity, leisure
  - Client rates importance (1-10), performance (1-10), satisfaction (1-10)
  - Reassessment detects change in performance and satisfaction
- **Clinically significant change:** 2+ point change in performance or satisfaction
- **Board pearl:** COPM is a CLIENT-CENTERED / family-centered measure; identifies goals meaningful to family (aligns with ICF participation)

### 5.9 Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS)

- **Citation:** Kiresuk TJ, Sherman RE (1968). "Goal attainment scaling: A general method for evaluating comprehensive community mental health programs." *Community Mental Health Journal*. 4(6):443-453.
- **Structure:**
  - Individualized goals with 5 levels:
    - -2 = much less than expected
    - -1 = less than expected
    - 0 = expected level (goal met)
    - +1 = more than expected
    - +2 = much more than expected
  - T-score: mean 50, SD 10
- **Board pearl:** GAS is individualized and goal-specific; allows comparison across different patients with different goals; useful for BoNT-A outcome studies

## 6. BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD

### 6.1 AFO Types and Biomechanics

#### Supramalleolar Orthosis (SMO)
- **Design:** Extends just above malleoli; trim lines below level of malleolar tips
- **Biomechanics:** Controls hindfoot valgus/varus; provides mediolateral stability at subtalar joint
- **Indications:**
  - Pronation/pes planovalgus (most common indication)
  - Mild hypotonia
  - Low muscle tone (Down syndrome typical use)
  - GMFCS Level I-II with foot/ankle instability only
- **Board pearl:** SMO does NOT control ankle dorsiflexion/plantarflexion; only controls coronal plane at subtalar joint

#### Posterior Leaf Spring (PLS) AFO
- **Design:** Thin, narrow posterior upright; flexible at ankle; rigid footplate
- **Biomechanics:** Prevents foot drop (dorsiflexion assist); allows some plantarflexion
- **Indications:**
  - Foot drop / drop foot gait
  - Weak dorsiflexors with adequate quadriceps strength
  - Hemiplegia with dorsiflexor weakness only
  - GMFCS Level I-II hemiplegic CP
- **Board pearl:** PLS provides dorsiflexion ASSIST but minimal control of knee or stance phase stability; NOT appropriate for crouch gait

#### Solid AFO (Rigid AFO)
- **Design:** Solid construction; trim lines anterior to malleoli; no ankle motion allowed
- **Biomechanics:**
  - Blocks all ankle motion (no dorsiflexion or plantarflexion)
  - Controls ankle in all planes
  - Creates rigid lever for stance phase stability
  - Can be set in slight dorsiflexion (5 degrees) for "tuning"
- **Indications:**
  - Moderate-severe spasticity with equinus
  - Poor selective motor control
  - Crouch gait (set in slight DF to control excessive DF)
  - Recurvatum gait (set in slight DF)
  - GMFCS Level II-III-IV
- **Board pearl:** Solid AFO is the most restrictive standard AFO; limits third rocker (toe-off); may slow gait velocity but improves stability

#### Hinged AFO
- **Design:** Rigid with mechanical hinge at ankle; allows dorsiflexion, blocks plantarflexion (with PF stop)
- **Biomechanics:**
  - Allows tibial advancement over foot in stance (controlled dorsiflexion)
  - PF stop prevents equinus (blocks PF beyond 90 degrees)
  - Allows third rocker (heel rise)
  - Some models have adjustable DF/PF stops
- **Indications:**
  - Equinus gait with adequate quadriceps strength
  - Post-BoNT-A injection (to maintain range while allowing motion)
  - GMFCS Level I-II with dynamic equinus but preserved strength
- **Board pearl:** Hinged AFO requires adequate quadriceps strength to control tibial advancement; if quads are weak, patient will collapse into crouch
- **Board pearl:** Hinged AFO is CONTRAINDICATED in crouch gait (allowing DF worsens crouch)

#### Ground Reaction AFO (GRAFO) / Floor Reaction AFO
- **Design:** Solid AFO with anterior tibial shell (clamshell or strap); set in 5 degrees DF
- **Biomechanics:**
  - Ground reaction force is directed ANTERIOR to knee joint center
  - Creates KNEE EXTENSION moment during stance
  - Converts crouch gait toward more upright posture
- **Indications:**
  - **CROUCH GAIT** (most important indication) — Board classic
  - Weak quadriceps with excessive dorsiflexion in stance
  - GMFCS Level II-III
- **Contraindications:**
  - Knee flexion contracture >10-15 degrees (cannot generate extension moment)
  - Severe equinus (wrong problem)
- **Board pearl:** GRAFO is THE orthosis for crouch gait; the anterior shell and DF ankle setting push GRF anterior to knee

### 6.2 AFO Prescription by CP Gait Type (Board High-Yield)

| Gait Pattern | Description | Recommended AFO |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| **True equinus** (Group 1) | Toe-walking, no knee involvement | PLS or Hinged AFO (with PF stop) |
| **Jump gait** (Group 2) | Equinus + knee flexion + hip flexion | Solid AFO or Hinged AFO |
| **Apparent equinus** (Group 3) | Knee flexion → apparent equinus | Solid AFO or GRAFO |
| **Crouch gait** (Group 4) | Excessive dorsiflexion + knee flexion | **GRAFO** (Ground Reaction AFO) |
| **Stiff knee gait** | Reduced knee flexion in swing | AFO alone insufficient; BoNT-A to rectus femoris or distal rectus transfer |

- **Board pearl:** The Winters-Gage-Hicks classification of hemiplegic gait:
  - Type I: Drop foot in swing only → PLS AFO
  - Type II: True equinus throughout gait cycle → Hinged or Solid AFO
  - Type III: Equinus + knee recurvatum or stiff knee → Solid AFO
  - Type IV: Equinus + knee + hip + pelvis involvement → Solid AFO + consider surgical

### 6.3 KAFO/HKAFO by Spina Bifida Level

| Neurological Level | Innervated Muscles | Orthosis | Ambulatory Status |
|-------------------|-------------------|---------|------------------|
| **Thoracic** | No LE motor | HKAFO or parapodium/standing frame | Standing/exercise only; wheelchair primary |
| **High lumbar (L1-L2)** | Hip flexors (iliopsoas) | KAFO + forearm crutches | Household/exercise ambulation; wheelchair community |
| **L3** | Quadriceps | KAFO + forearm crutches | Household to limited community ambulators |
| **L4** | Tibialis anterior (dorsiflexion) | AFO (hinged or solid) + crutches or canes | Community ambulators with assistive devices |
| **L5** | Peroneals, gluteus medius (partial), hip extensors (partial) | AFO (may be PLS or SMO) | Community ambulators; Trendelenburg gait common |
| **S1-S2** | Gastrocnemius-soleus, gluteals | SMO or shoe insert, or no orthosis | Community ambulators; mild gait deviations |
| **S2-S3** | Foot intrinsics, bladder/bowel | None or SMO | Normal or near-normal ambulation; bladder issues persist |

- **Board pearl:** Functional level often 1-2 levels BELOW anatomic level due to weakness, orthopedic deformities, obesity, energy expenditure
- **Board pearl:** Ambulation declines with age in spina bifida — many L3-L4 children who walk with KAFOs transition to wheelchair as adolescents/adults (energy cost)

### 6.4 Reciprocating Gait Orthosis (RGO)

- **Design:** Bilateral HKAFOs linked by cable/mechanism at hips; hip flexion on one side drives hip extension on contralateral side
- **Mechanism:** Reciprocal cable coupling allows energy-efficient reciprocal gait pattern
- **Indication:** High-level spina bifida (thoracic, L1-L2) or thoracic SCI desiring upright ambulation
- **Board pearl:** RGO allows reciprocal gait in patients with no voluntary hip/knee control; however, energy expenditure remains very high (3-5x normal) and most children transition to wheelchair for community mobility

### 6.5 Spinal Orthoses

#### Milwaukee Brace (Cervicothoracolumbosacral Orthosis — CTLSO)
- **Design:** Pelvic girdle + neck ring with 1 anterior and 2 posterior uprights; lateral pressure pads
- **Indication:** Historically for thoracic scoliosis curves with apex above T8
- **Board pearl:** Largely replaced by TLSO braces; poor cosmesis and compliance; now rarely used
- **Still used for:** High thoracic curves, Scheuermann kyphosis (some centers)

#### TLSO — Boston Brace
- **Design:** Prefabricated symmetrical module, custom-modified with pads
- **Type:** Lumbar lordosis reduction (corrective forces through pad placement)
- **Indication:** Lumbar and thoracolumbar scoliosis (apex T8 and below)
- **Wear time:** **16-23 hours/day** (BrAIST trial: dose-response demonstrated)
- **Board pearl:** Most commonly prescribed scoliosis orthosis in North America

#### TLSO — Wilmington Brace
- **Design:** Custom-molded total-contact TLSO (CAD/CAM or plaster mold)
- **Indication:** Similar to Boston; total-contact design
- **Board pearl:** Custom-molded provides better fit; may improve compliance in some patients

#### Charleston Bending Brace
- **Design:** Nighttime-only brace; overcorrection (hypercorrection) principle
- **Wear time:** Nighttime only (8-10 hours)
- **Indication:** Single lumbar/thoracolumbar curves <35 degrees
- **Board pearl:** Nighttime-only option; may improve compliance; not for double curves or large curves

#### Providence Brace
- **Design:** Nighttime-only; CAD/CAM fabricated; hypercorrection
- **Similar to Charleston but different corrective forces**

#### Scoliosis Bracing Rules (Board Must-Know)
- **Indication:** AIS curves 25-45 degrees in Risser 0-2 (skeletally immature)
- **Goal:** Prevent progression to surgical threshold (>50 degrees)
- **Wear time:** 16-23 hours/day for TLSO (BrAIST: success rate increased with >12 hr/day)
- **Curves >45-50 degrees:** Bracing NOT effective; surgical referral
- **Curves <25 degrees:** Observation only
- **Neuromuscular scoliosis:** Bracing less effective long-term; often progresses despite bracing; surgical fusion may be needed earlier

### 6.6 Early Power Mobility (Board High-Yield)

- **Age for introduction:** As early as **12-18 months** (current evidence/practice)
- **Rationale:**
  - Exploration drives cognitive and social development
  - Restriction of mobility leads to learned helplessness
  - Self-initiated movement promotes spatial cognition, language, social interaction
  - "Experience-dependent development" — mobility is a means, not just an end
- **Candidacy:** Any child with mobility limitations who could benefit from independent exploration
  - CP GMFCS III-V
  - Spina bifida (thoracic/high lumbar)
  - SMA
  - Other neuromuscular conditions
- **Board pearl:** There is NO minimum cognitive level required to learn power mobility; children with significant intellectual disability can learn joystick operation
- **Board pearl:** Early power mobility does NOT reduce motivation to walk (historically feared but disproven)

### 6.7 AAC Devices

- **Types:**
  - **Low-tech:** Picture boards, communication books, eye-gaze boards
  - **Mid-tech:** Single-message devices (BigMack), simple multi-message devices (GoTalk)
  - **High-tech:** Speech-generating devices (SGD) — tablet-based apps (Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life), dedicated devices (Tobii Dynavox)
- **Access methods:**
  - Direct selection (touch, eye gaze)
  - Switch scanning (single/dual switch)
  - Head pointing
  - Eye-tracking/eye-gaze
- **Board pearl:** AAC is NOT a "last resort" — should be introduced early and alongside speech therapy (multimodal communication)
- **Board pearl:** AAC does NOT inhibit speech development (common misconception; research shows it can FACILITATE verbal language)

### 6.8 Seating Hierarchy (Board Classic)

**Principle: Proximal stability enables distal function**

**Seating assessment and intervention sequence:**
1. **PELVIS** — Foundation; must be stable and neutral
   - Correct pelvic obliquity, rotation, posterior tilt
   - Firm seat; contoured base; ASIS/PSIS alignment
2. **TRUNK** — Supported once pelvis is stable
   - Lateral trunk supports if needed
   - Anterior trunk support for forward lean
3. **HEAD** — Supported last
   - Head support/headrest once trunk is stabilized
   - May need chin support, forehead strap, or circumferential head support

**Board pearl:** ALWAYS address pelvis FIRST in seating; the pelvis is the base of support from which trunk and head control develop. "Fix the pelvis, free the hands."

**Additional seating principles:**
- 90-90-90 rule: 90 degrees at hips, knees, ankles (starting position; modified as needed)
- Tilt-in-space: maintains seated angle while recumbent position; redistributes pressure
- Recline: extends hip angle; opens hip angle
- Standing program: weight-bearing; bone density; pressure relief
- Pressure mapping: for children at risk of pressure injury (insensate, SB, SCI)
