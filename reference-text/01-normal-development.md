## Section 1: Gross Motor Milestones

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Gross motor development follows a cephalocaudal (head-to-toe) and proximal-to-distal progression. Mastery of antigravity control in prone precedes sitting, which precedes standing and walking. The following table summarizes the highest-yield gross motor milestones for board preparation.

| Age | Milestone | Board Pearl |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| Newborn | Physiologic flexion posture; complete head lag on pull-to-sit | Flexor tone predominates in full-term newborns |
| 2 months | Lifts head 45 degrees in prone | Beginning of antigravity head control |
| 3 months | Lifts head 90 degrees in prone; props on forearms; minimal head lag | Head control emerges; forearm propping signals upper trunk strength |
| 4 months | Props on extended arms; rolls prone to supine; no head lag | Absence of head lag by 4 months is expected |
| 6 months | Rolls both directions; sits with support (tripod); briefly sits unsupported | Tripod sitting transitions to independent sitting |
| 9 months | Crawls well; pulls to stand; cruises along furniture | Mobility and upright posture emerging |
| 12 months | Walks independently (range 9-15 months) | 97% of children walk by 18 months |
| 15 months | Walks well; stoops to recover objects; begins stiff-legged running | Stable gait with improved balance |
| 18 months | Runs stiffly; walks upstairs with hand held (marking time); kicks ball | Marking time = two feet on each step |
| 24 months | Runs well; walks up and down stairs marking time; jumps with both feet | Bilateral coordination for jumping |
| 3 years | Alternates feet going upstairs; rides tricycle; stands on one foot 1-2 sec | Reciprocal stair climbing is a 3-year milestone |
| 4 years | Hops on one foot; alternates feet going downstairs; catches large ball | Downstairs alternation follows upstairs by ~1 year |
| 5 years | Skips; hops on each foot; heel-to-toe tandem walk | Skip = the signature 5-year gross motor skill |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The stair-climbing progression is a board favorite: marking time up at 2 years, alternating feet up at 3 years, alternating feet down at 4 years. Think "2-3-4 stair rule."

> **Clinical Pearl:** Independent walking typically occurs at 12 months. Failure to walk by 18 months warrants evaluation. The 97th percentile for walking onset is 18 months.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The World Health Organization Multicentre Growth Reference Study established normative windows of achievement for six fundamental gross motor milestones across diverse populations. That study confirmed that while individual variation exists, the sequence of motor acquisition is remarkably consistent across cultures and ethnicities. The "windows of achievement" represent the range during which 95% of typically developing children attain each skill: sitting without support (3.8-9.2 months), standing with assistance (4.8-11.4 months), hands-and-knees crawling (5.2-13.5 months), walking with assistance (5.9-13.7 months), standing alone (6.9-16.9 months), and walking alone (8.2-17.6 months).

Environmental and cultural factors can influence the timing of milestone acquisition without necessarily indicating pathology. For example, prone sleeping recommendations (back-to-sleep campaigns) have been associated with mild delays in prone-based milestones such as rolling and crawling, though these typically resolve without intervention. Similarly, children in cultures with extensive carrying practices may show earlier standing and walking but later crawling milestones.

Preterm infants require age correction for milestone assessment. The standard practice is to subtract the weeks of prematurity from the chronological age, yielding a "corrected age" that more accurately reflects developmental expectations. This correction is applied until at least 24 months of age, with some experts recommending correction until 36 months for infants born before 28 weeks gestation.

## Section 2: Fine Motor Milestones

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Fine motor development follows a predictable progression from reflexive grasping to precise voluntary manipulation. Two high-yield sequences dominate board examinations: the grasp progression and the drawing/copying progression.

**Grasp Development Progression:**

| Stage | Age | Description |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| Reflexive grasp | 0-2 months | Involuntary palmar grasp reflex |
| Ulnar palmar grasp | 3-4 months | Objects held against ulnar side of palm |
| Palmar grasp | 4-5 months | Whole-hand crude grasp |
| Radial palmar grasp | 5-6 months | Thumb begins to participate |
| Radial digital grasp | 7-8 months | Object held in fingertips with thumb opposition |
| Inferior (scissors) pincer | 8-9 months | Crude thumb-index opposition |
| Superior (neat) pincer | 10-12 months | Precise tip-to-tip thumb-index opposition |
| Voluntary release | 12 months | Controlled letting go of objects |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The palmar grasp reflex must integrate (disappear by 4-6 months) before voluntary grasp can develop. Persistence of the palmar grasp reflex beyond 6 months suggests upper motor neuron pathology.

**Block Tower Progression:**

| Age | Blocks |
|-----|--------|
| 12 months | 2 blocks |
| 15 months | 2-3 blocks |
| 18 months | 3-4 blocks |
| 24 months | 6-7 blocks |
| 30 months | 8 blocks |
| 36 months | 9-10 blocks; bridge of 3 blocks |

**Drawing and Copying Progression:**

| Age | Skill |
|-----|-------|
| 12-15 months | Scribbles spontaneously |
| 2 years | Imitates vertical line and circular strokes |
| 2.5 years | Imitates horizontal line |
| 3 years | Copies circle; imitates cross |
| 4 years | Copies cross (+); draws person with 3+ parts |
| 4.5 years | Copies square |
| 5 years | Copies triangle; draws person with 6+ parts |
| 6 years | Copies diamond; writes first name |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The shape-copying mnemonic is "Circle, Cross, Square, Triangle, Diamond" corresponding roughly to ages 3, 4, 4.5, 5, and 6 years. The distinction between "imitates" (watches examiner draw, then copies) and "copies" (reproduces from a static model) matters: imitation is easier and precedes copying.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Fine motor development is driven by maturation of the corticospinal tract and concurrent refinement of visual-motor integration circuits. The radial side of the hand develops functional dominance earlier than the ulnar side, reflecting the maturation pattern of the corticospinal projections to the hand motor cortex. This ulnar-to-radial shift in grasp is clinically significant because delayed radial grasp development may signal neurological compromise.

Hand dominance typically establishes between 18 and 36 months of age, with most children showing a clear preference by age 3 years. Notably, consistent hand preference before 12-18 months is a red flag that should prompt evaluation for contralateral hemiparesis, as it may indicate asymmetric motor function from conditions such as cerebral palsy or perinatal stroke.

The development of in-hand manipulation skills (translation, shift, rotation of objects within one hand) occurs between 2 and 7 years and is critical for functional tasks like buttoning, writing, and tool use. These skills are not commonly tested on boards but are therapeutically relevant for occupational therapy goal-setting.

## Section 3: Language Development

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Language milestones divide into receptive (understanding) and expressive (production) components. The board emphasizes several critical age markers.

| Age | Receptive | Expressive |
|-----|-----------|------------|
| Newborn | Alerts to sound; prefers human voice | Differentiated crying |
| 2 months | Recognizes familiar voices | Cooing (vowel sounds); social smile |
| 4 months | Turns toward sound source | Laughing; beginning consonant sounds |
| 6 months | Responds to name | Babbling (consonant-vowel combinations: "ba-ba-ba") |
| 9 months | Understands "no"; understands gestures | "Mama/dada" nonspecific; gestures (waving, pointing) |
| 12 months | Follows one-step commands with gesture | First true words (1-3 words); "mama/dada" specific |
| 15 months | Points to desired objects | 4-6 words; uses jargon |
| 18 months | Points to body parts; follows simple commands | 10-25 words; points to indicate wants |
| 24 months | Follows 2-step commands | 50+ words; 2-word phrases; 50% intelligible to strangers |
| 30 months | Understands prepositions (in, on, under) | 200+ words; 2-3 word sentences; uses pronouns |
| 36 months | Knows age, sex, full name | 250-1000 words; 3-word sentences; 75% intelligible |
| 4 years | Understands colors; follows complex commands | Full sentences (4-5 words); tells stories; 100% intelligible |
| 5 years | Defines words; counts to 10+ | Fluent speech; 5-6 word sentences; correct grammar |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The speech intelligibility rule is frequently tested: 50% intelligible at 2 years, 75% at 3 years, 100% at 4 years. This applies to unfamiliar listeners -- parents often understand more.

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Babbling" (consonant-vowel combinations) at 6 months is a critical milestone. Absence of babbling by 12 months is a red flag for hearing impairment, autism, or other communication disorders.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Language acquisition is one of the most complex neurodevelopmental achievements, requiring integration of auditory processing, oral-motor coordination, cognitive processing, and social interaction circuits. Receptive language consistently outpaces expressive language, with children understanding far more words than they can produce at any given age.

The phenomenon of the "vocabulary explosion" typically occurs between 18 and 24 months, when children rapidly expand their vocabulary from approximately 50 words to several hundred over a period of weeks to months. This coincides with the onset of word combinations (2-word phrases) and marks a critical transition in linguistic development.

Late talkers -- children with expressive vocabulary below the 10th percentile at 18-24 months with otherwise normal development -- represent approximately 10-20% of toddlers. While the majority (50-70%) resolve spontaneously by age 3-4 years, they remain at elevated risk for later reading difficulties and academic challenges. Risk factors for persistent language disorder include family history of language or learning disability, limited gesture use, and reduced receptive language skills.

Bilingual language acquisition deserves special consideration. Bilingual children may exhibit smaller vocabularies in each individual language compared to monolingual peers, but their total vocabulary across both languages is typically comparable or exceeds monolingual norms. Code-switching (alternating between languages within a conversation) is a normal bilingual phenomenon and should not be confused with language confusion or disorder.

## Section 4: Social-Emotional Development

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

| Age | Social-Emotional Milestone | Board Significance |
|-----|---------------------------|-------------------|
| 2 months | Social smile (in response to face/voice) | One of the most frequently tested milestones |
| 3 months | Recognizes parent; anticipatory excitement | Social engagement emerging |
| 6 months | Stranger anxiety emerging; enjoys peek-a-boo | Recognition of familiar vs. unfamiliar |
| 9 months | Separation anxiety peaks (9-18 months); object permanence | Attachment behaviors; Piaget's sensorimotor stage |
| 12 months | Gives objects on request; attachment behaviors | Joint attention and reciprocity |
| 18 months | Beginning pretend play; parallel play | Symbolic play is an important cognitive marker |
| 24 months | Parallel play predominates; removes clothing | Play alongside but not truly with peers |
| 36 months | Group play beginning; shares with prompting; knows gender | Transition toward cooperative play |
| 4 years | Cooperative play; imaginative play; has friends | True interactive play with shared goals |
| 5 years | Competitive games; follows rules; understands right/wrong | Rule-governed play and moral reasoning |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The social smile at 2 months is one of the most commonly tested milestones on the PM&R boards. Absence of social smile by 2 months warrants evaluation for visual impairment, hearing loss, or neurodevelopmental concerns.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The play progression is a board staple: solitary play (infancy) --> parallel play (2 years) --> associative play (3 years) --> cooperative play (4 years). Cooperative play requires theory of mind, which develops around 4-5 years.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Social-emotional development is intimately linked to attachment theory, originally described by Bowlby and subsequently classified by Ainsworth through the Strange Situation paradigm. Secure attachment, observed in approximately 60-65% of typically developing infants, is characterized by using the caregiver as a secure base for exploration, distress upon separation, and positive greeting upon reunion. Insecure attachment patterns (avoidant, ambivalent, disorganized) occur more frequently in children with developmental disabilities and in those exposed to adverse childhood experiences.

Separation anxiety, which peaks between 9 and 18 months, and stranger anxiety, which typically emerges around 6-8 months, are both normal developmental phenomena reflecting the maturation of object permanence and attachment relationships. These phenomena should be distinguished from pathological anxiety, which is characterized by persistence beyond the expected developmental window and interference with age-appropriate functioning.

The development of pretend play is a cognitive milestone with significant implications for language and social development. Pretend play typically emerges around 18 months (simple pretend actions) and becomes increasingly elaborate through the preschool years. Absence or significant delay in pretend play is a hallmark feature of autism spectrum disorder and should prompt further evaluation.

## Section 5: Primitive Reflexes

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Primitive reflexes are brainstem-mediated motor patterns present in early infancy that are subsequently inhibited as the cerebral cortex matures. Their persistence beyond expected integration ages is a hallmark of upper motor neuron pathology, most notably cerebral palsy.

| Reflex | Emergence | Integration (Disappears) | Clinical Significance if Persistent |
|--------|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Moro | Birth (28 wk GA) | 3-6 months | UMN lesion, CP; asymmetry = brachial plexus injury or clavicle fracture |
| Palmar grasp | Birth (28 wk GA) | 4-6 months | Must integrate for voluntary grasp; persistence = UMN lesion |
| Plantar grasp | Birth (28 wk GA) | 9-12 months | Must integrate for independent standing |
| Rooting | Birth (28 wk GA) | 3-4 months (awake) | Frontal lobe lesion if persistent |
| Sucking | Birth (28 wk GA) | 4-6 months | Brainstem dysfunction |
| ATNR | Birth-2 weeks | 4-6 months | Interferes with midline hand function and rolling |
| STNR | 4-6 months | 8-12 months | Interferes with hands-and-knees crawling |
| TLR | Birth | 6 months | CP; vestibular dysfunction |
| Galant (trunk incurvation) | Birth | 2-6 months | Can interfere with sitting if persistent |
| Stepping/walking | Birth | 2-3 months | Reappears as voluntary walking ~12 months |
| Positive support | Birth | 2-4 months | Persistent in CP |
| Landau | 3-4 months (emerges) | 12-24 months | Absent in hypotonic/floppy infants |
| Parachute (anterior) | 6-9 months (emerges) | Persists for life | Asymmetry suggests hemiparesis |
| Lateral protective | 6-8 months (emerges) | Persists for life | Absence = impaired sitting balance |
| Posterior protective | 9-12 months (emerges) | Persists for life | Absence = impaired sitting balance |

> **Clinical Pearl:** An obligatory ATNR (the infant is locked in the "fencer" posture for >30 seconds and cannot break free) is ALWAYS pathological at any age. A non-obligatory ATNR (transient posturing that the infant can override) is normal until 4-6 months.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The STNR is unique among primitive reflexes because it emerges at 4-6 months (not at birth). It must integrate by 8-12 months to allow reciprocal crawling on hands and knees. The STNR causes arm extension with head extension and arm flexion with head flexion -- the opposite of what is needed for crawling.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The parachute reflex (anterior protective extension) emerges at 6-9 months and does NOT integrate -- it persists throughout life. Asymmetry of the parachute reflex is a clinical clue to hemiplegia.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Primitive reflexes are mediated by brainstem and spinal cord circuits that operate without cortical input. As myelination of descending cortical pathways progresses during the first year of life, these reflexes are suppressed (integrated) by higher cortical centers. The model of cortical inhibition of brainstem reflexes provides the conceptual framework for understanding why persistence of primitive reflexes signals failure of cortical maturation or cortical injury.

The asymmetric tonic neck reflex (ATNR) deserves special attention for its clinical implications. When the head is turned to one side, the ATNR produces extension of the arm and leg on the face side and flexion on the occiput side (the "fencer" position). This reflex serves a developmental purpose by introducing the infant to hand-eye coordination (the extended arm enters the visual field), but it must integrate to allow midline hand activities such as bilateral reaching and hand-to-mouth movement.

Postural reactions (righting, equilibrium, and protective extension reactions) emerge later in infancy and persist throughout life. These reactions require cortical and cerebellar integration and reflect a higher level of neurological maturation than primitive reflexes. Their absence or delayed emergence may indicate neurological compromise.

| Postural Reaction | Emergence | Significance |
|-------------------|-----------|--------------|
| Head righting (neck on body) | 1-2 months | Aligns head with gravity |
| Body righting on body | 4-6 months | Enables segmental rolling |
| Equilibrium (prone) | ~6 months | Balance response |
| Equilibrium (sitting) | ~7-8 months | Balance response |
| Equilibrium (standing) | ~12-15 months | Balance response |
| Protective extension (forward) | 6-7 months | Protects from forward falls |
| Protective extension (lateral) | 7-8 months | Protects from lateral falls |
| Protective extension (posterior) | 9-10 months | Protects from backward falls |

## Section 6: Developmental Screening Tools

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The distinction between screening and diagnostic instruments is fundamental.

| Tool | Type | Ages | Domains | Key Features | Sensitivity/Specificity |
|------|------|------|---------|--------------|------------------------|
| Denver II | Screening | Birth-6 years | 4: Gross Motor, Fine Motor-Adaptive, Language, Personal-Social | 125 items; clinician-administered; results = normal/suspect/untestable | Sens 56-83% / Spec 43-80% |
| ASQ-3 | Screening | 1-66 months | 5: Communication, Gross Motor, Fine Motor, Problem Solving, Personal-Social | 21 age-specific forms; parent-completed; 10-15 min parent, 2-3 min to score | Sens 70-90% / Spec 76-91% |
| Bayley-4 | Diagnostic (gold standard) | 1-42 months | 5: Cognitive, Language, Motor, Social-Emotional, Adaptive Behavior | Clinician-administered; Composite Mean=100, SD=15; Subtest Mean=10, SD=3 | Gold standard reference |
| BINS | Screening | 3-24 months | 4 conceptual areas: neurological, receptive, expressive, cognitive | 6 item sets; 10-15 min; risk categories: low/moderate/high | Quick office-based screener |
| PEDS | Screening | 0-8 years | Multiple | 10-item parent interview; identifies parental concerns | Sens 74-79% / Spec 70-80% |
| M-CHAT-R/F | ASD Screening | 16-30 months | ASD-specific | 20 items; parent-report; Follow-Up interview reduces false positives | Sens 85-92% / Spec with F/U 95-99% |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Denver II is a SCREENING tool; Bayley is a DIAGNOSTIC tool. This distinction is a classic board question. The Denver II does NOT generate a developmental quotient. The Bayley is the gold standard for quantifying developmental level in infants and toddlers.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ASQ-3 has largely supplanted the Denver II in many clinical settings because it can be parent-completed, has better psychometric properties, and is more time-efficient. It generates scores in a "monitoring zone" between clear pass and clear referral.

**AAP Recommended Screening Schedule:**
- Developmental surveillance: Every well-child visit
- Standardized developmental screening: 9, 18, and 30 months
- ASD-specific screening: 18 and 24 months

**Denver II Interpretation:**
- Normal: No delays, maximum 1 caution
- Suspect: 1 or more delays and/or 2 or more cautions
- Untestable: Too many refusals
- "Caution" = failure on item at the 75-90th percentile line
- "Delay" = failure on item entirely to the left of the age line (>90th percentile should have achieved)

**Developmental Quotient (DQ):**
- DQ = (developmental age / chronological age) x 100
- DQ <70 is roughly equivalent to >2 SD below mean
- Use corrected age for denominator in premature infants

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The 2022 CDC/AAP milestone revision represented a significant shift in developmental surveillance methodology. The updated milestones were revised to reflect behaviors that at least 75% of children demonstrate at a given age, moving away from the previous approach that used the 50th percentile. This shift was designed to provide greater clinical clarity and reduce the ambiguity associated with "most children can do this by" language. The revision also eliminated vague qualifiers such as "may" and "begins," replacing them with observable, binary criteria.

The updated milestone checklists now span 12 age-specific checklists covering every recommended well-child visit from 2 months through 5 years, with new checklists added for 15 and 30 months. Approximately 60% of the original milestones were retained with modifications, while about 40% were newly added based on research evidence.

Telehealth-based developmental screening expanded significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic. The ASQ-3 has been validated for remote parent-completed administration, making it particularly suitable for telehealth visits. The Bayley-4 added digital administration capabilities, though formal assessment still requires skilled clinical observation.

Emerging technologies for developmental screening include eye-tracking paradigms that can detect early attentional differences in infants at risk for ASD and machine learning algorithms that analyze infant movement patterns from home videos. While promising, these approaches remain investigational and have not yet replaced standard screening tools in clinical practice.

## Section 7: Red Flags for Referral

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Motor Red Flags:**
- No head control by 4 months
- Not sitting independently by 9 months
- Not pulling to stand by 12 months
- Not walking by 18 months
- Not running by 24 months
- Persistent fisting of hands beyond 3 months
- Persistent head lag beyond 4 months
- Hand preference before 18 months (suggests contralateral hemiparesis)
- Persistent toe walking beyond 3 years
- Regression of motor milestones at any age

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hand preference before 18 months is a CLASSIC board question. Early hand dominance should raise concern for contralateral hemiplegia (e.g., from hemiplegic cerebral palsy or perinatal stroke). Typical hand dominance does not establish until age 2-3 years.

**Language Red Flags:**
- No babbling by 12 months
- No single words by 16 months
- No 2-word phrases by 24 months
- No pointing or gestures by 12 months
- Any loss of language at any age
- Not following simple commands by 18 months
- Not 50% intelligible by 2 years
- Not 75% intelligible by 3 years
- Not 100% intelligible by 4 years

**General Red Flags:**
- Loss of any previously acquired skill at any age warrants urgent evaluation
- Differential includes neurodegenerative disease, Rett syndrome, ASD regression (~25-30% of ASD), Landau-Kleffner syndrome, seizure disorder, metabolic disease

> **Clinical Pearl:** Developmental regression -- loss of previously acquired skills -- is ALWAYS a red flag that demands urgent evaluation. While approximately 25-30% of children with ASD demonstrate language regression (typically around 18-24 months), neurodegenerative and metabolic disorders must be excluded.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The concept of "red flags" in developmental assessment serves as a clinical heuristic for identifying children who require expedited evaluation. However, the specificity of individual red flags is limited -- many children with a single red flag will ultimately be found to have typical development or a mild, self-resolving delay. The predictive value increases substantially when multiple red flags are present across domains or when red flags are accompanied by abnormal neurological examination findings.

The distinction between developmental delay, developmental disability, developmental dissociation, and developmental deviance is conceptually important for the clinician:

- **Developmental delay**: Performance below expected level in one or more domains, typically defined as >25% delay or >1.5-2 SD below the mean. Implies potential for catch-up with appropriate intervention.
- **Developmental disability**: A more permanent condition implying chronic functional limitation. The term is generally reserved for children older than 5-7 years when the permanence of deficits is more certain.
- **Developmental dissociation**: Markedly different rates of development across domains. For example, a child with autism may demonstrate normal motor development but significantly delayed language and social skills.
- **Developmental deviance**: Acquisition of skills in a non-sequential order. For example, a child who walks before crawling, or who demonstrates echolalia before meaningful speech. Deviance is particularly characteristic of ASD.

## Section 8: Autism Spectrum Disorder Screening

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Epidemiology:**
- Prevalence: 1 in 36 children (2.8%) per CDC ADDM Network 2020 data (published 2023)
- Previous estimates: 1 in 44 (2018), 1 in 54 (2016), 1 in 68 (2012)
- Male:Female ratio approximately 3.8:1 (trending lower as female recognition improves)
- Median age of diagnosis: ~4.5 years (goal is diagnosis before age 3)
- Co-occurring intellectual disability: 31-38%

**ASD Red Flags (Under 2 Years):**
- No social smile by 2 months
- Poor eye contact
- No babbling by 12 months
- No gestures (pointing, waving) by 12 months
- No single words by 16 months
- No 2-word spontaneous (not echolalic) phrases by 24 months
- Any loss of language or social skills at any age
- No response to name by 12 months
- No shared enjoyment / joint attention by 14 months
- Lack of pretend play by 18 months

**M-CHAT-R/F Screening:**
- 20-item parent-report questionnaire; ages 16-30 months
- Scoring: Low risk (0-2), Medium risk (3-7, administer Follow-Up interview), High risk (8-20, refer immediately)
- Follow-Up interview reduces false-positive rate substantially
- Sensitivity: 85-92%; Specificity with Follow-Up: 95-99%
- Positive predictive value with Follow-Up: 47.5% for ASD specifically; 94.6% for any developmental delay or concern
- AAP recommends universal screening at 18 and 24 months

**DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for ASD:**

**Criterion A** -- Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction (ALL 3 required):
1. Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
2. Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors
3. Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships

**Criterion B** -- Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities (at least 2 of 4):
1. Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech
2. Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines
3. Highly restricted, fixated interests abnormal in intensity or focus
4. Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input (new in DSM-5)

**Criterion C** -- Present in early developmental period
**Criterion D** -- Cause clinically significant impairment
**Criterion E** -- Not better explained by intellectual disability alone

**Severity Levels:**
- Level 1: "Requiring support"
- Level 2: "Requiring substantial support"
- Level 3: "Requiring very substantial support"

> **Clinical Pearl:** Key DSM-5 changes: Asperger syndrome, PDD-NOS, and autistic disorder were merged into a single ASD diagnosis. The criteria went from three domains (social, communication, RRB) to two (social communication combined, and RRB). Sensory processing symptoms were added as a criterion. A new diagnosis, Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder, was created for those with social communication deficits WITHOUT restricted/repetitive behaviors.

> **Clinical Pearl:** For the ASD diagnosis in DSM-5, ALL 3 social communication criteria are required PLUS at least 2 of 4 restricted/repetitive behavior criteria. Social Communication Disorder cannot be diagnosed if full ASD criteria are met.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The evolving epidemiology of ASD reflects both genuine increased awareness and broadened diagnostic criteria. The consistent upward trend in prevalence is driven primarily by improved surveillance, earlier identification, and recognition of ASD in previously underidentified populations (females, racial/ethnic minorities, individuals without intellectual disability). There is no persuasive evidence that the true incidence of ASD has increased at rates consistent with the observed prevalence changes.

Early detection of ASD remains a priority, with emerging evidence supporting reliable diagnosis as early as 12-14 months in specialized centers. Eye-tracking technology and computational approaches analyzing infant social attention patterns have shown promise for identifying at-risk infants, but these tools remain investigational.

The sex ratio in ASD has narrowed from the historically cited 4:1 male-to-female ratio toward approximately 3.8:1, reflecting improved recognition of ASD presentations in females. Female ASD presentation may differ phenotypically, with girls more likely to demonstrate social "camouflaging," more complex imaginative play, and fewer overt repetitive behaviors, potentially contributing to later diagnosis.

Emerging research supports genetic and neurobiological underpinnings of ASD, with heritability estimates ranging from 50-90%. De novo variants, copy number variants, and common genetic variants all contribute to ASD risk. More than 100 genes have been implicated, with no single gene accounting for more than 1-2% of cases. Syndromic forms of ASD (associated with Fragile X, Tuberous Sclerosis, Rett syndrome, among others) account for approximately 10-25% of all ASD cases.

## Section 9: IDEA and Early Intervention

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is the federal law governing special education and early intervention services for children with disabilities in the United States.

| Feature | Part C (Early Intervention) | Part B (Special Education) |
|---------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Ages | Birth to 3 years | 3-21 years |
| Service plan | IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan) | IEP (Individualized Education Program) |
| Focus | Family-centered | Child/student-centered |
| Setting | Natural environments (home, daycare, community) | School-based; Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) |
| Eligibility | State-defined; typically >25% delay or >1.5-2 SD below mean in 1+ domain, OR established condition | Must qualify under 1 of 13 disability categories |
| Cost | Free to families regardless of income | Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) |
| Transition | At age 3, transition to Part B | Continues through age 21 |
| Referral | Any concerned party; evaluation within 45 days | Through school district |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The IFSP vs. IEP distinction is a board favorite. IFSP is FAMILY-centered (birth-3, Part C, natural environments). IEP is CHILD-centered (3-21, Part B, school-based). Know this cold.

**Part C Services May Include:**
- Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language pathology
- Special instruction, audiology, vision services
- Psychology, social work
- Assistive technology, service coordination

**Part B Key Principles:**
- Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)
- Least Restrictive Environment (LRE): Educate with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate
- 13 disability categories for eligibility
- Related services: PT, OT, SLP, transportation, counseling
- Section 504: Alternative for students who do not qualify for an IEP but need accommodations

**Child Find Mandate:**
- IDEA requires states to identify, locate, and evaluate ALL children with disabilities from birth through age 21
- This includes children in private schools, homeless children, and children of migrant families

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The evidence base for early intervention is robust, with meta-analyses demonstrating moderate-to-large effect sizes for early developmental interventions across multiple domains. The rationale is grounded in the concept of neuroplasticity -- the developing brain's heightened capacity for reorganization and adaptation in response to environmental input and therapeutic intervention. This plasticity is greatest during the first 2-3 years of life, creating a critical window during which intervention has the highest potential yield.

Family-centered practice, the philosophical foundation of Part C services, recognizes that the family is the constant in the child's life and that professional support should empower families rather than create dependency on professionals. IFSP goals address family priorities and are implemented within the child's natural routines and environments, consistent with evidence that skills learned in context generalize more readily than those learned in clinical settings.

The transition from Part C to Part B at age 3 represents a significant shift in service delivery philosophy and can be disorienting for families. Advance planning for this transition should begin at least 6 months before the child's third birthday. Clinicians play an important role in advocating for continued services and ensuring families understand their rights under both parts of the law.

## Section 10: Developmental Delay Definitions

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Key Terminology:**

- **Developmental delay**: Significant delay in one or more developmental domains. Implies potential for catch-up. Typically defined as >25% delay or >1.5-2 SD below the mean in one or more areas.
- **Global developmental delay (GDD)**: Reserved for children under 5 years. Defined as significant delay (>2 SD below mean) in 2 or more developmental domains. Used when systematic IQ testing is not feasible due to age.
- **Intellectual disability (DSM-5)**: Requires deficits in BOTH intellectual functions AND adaptive functioning, with onset during the developmental period. Severity is classified by adaptive functioning level, NOT by IQ score.
- **Developmental disability**: A more permanent condition implying chronic limitation. Term generally used after age 5-7 years.

**Intellectual Disability Severity (DSM-5):**
- Severity determined by adaptive functioning (conceptual, social, practical domains) -- not IQ
- Historical IQ cutoffs (still referenced): Mild 50-70; Moderate 35-50; Severe 20-35; Profound <20
- Prevalence: 1-2% of the population (Mild ~85%, Moderate ~10%, Severe ~3-4%, Profound ~1-2%)

**Developmental Delay Prevalence:**
- Approximately 15-17% of children ages 3-17 have one or more developmental disabilities
- Global developmental delay: ~1-3% of children under 5
- Specific learning disabilities: ~5-10% of school-age children

**Prematurity and Developmental Risk:**
- Extremely preterm (<28 weeks): 25-50% have significant neurodevelopmental impairment
- Very preterm (28-32 weeks): 10-25% significant impairment
- Late preterm (34-36 weeks): Increased risk compared to term, but majority develop normally
- Corrected age used until at least 2 years (some recommend 3 years for extremely preterm)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In DSM-5, the severity of intellectual disability is classified by ADAPTIVE FUNCTIONING, not IQ score. This was a significant change from previous editions. The Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales and ABAS-3 are commonly used measures of adaptive functioning.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Corrected age = chronological age minus weeks of prematurity. Always use corrected age for developmental assessment of preterm infants until at least 2 years. Example: A 12-month-old born at 28 weeks (12 weeks early) has a corrected age of 9 months -- developmental expectations should match a 9-month-old.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The evaluation of a child with global developmental delay follows the algorithm outlined by the American Academy of Neurology and Child Neurology Society practice parameter. This systematic approach includes a detailed history (prenatal, perinatal, postnatal, family, social), physical examination including dysmorphology assessment, neurological examination, and tiered laboratory and imaging studies.

First-tier investigations typically include chromosomal microarray analysis (CMA), which has largely replaced the standard karyotype as the first-line genetic test. CMA identifies clinically significant copy number variants in approximately 15-20% of children with unexplained GDD or intellectual disability. Fragile X testing (FMR1 CGG repeat analysis) is recommended for all children with unexplained intellectual disability due to the relatively high prevalence and recurrence risk implications of Fragile X syndrome. Metabolic screening (amino acids, organic acids, thyroid function) and brain MRI are also part of the initial evaluation.

Second-tier investigations may include whole exome sequencing (WES), which identifies a causative variant in an additional 25-40% of previously undiagnosed cases. The diagnostic yield of next-generation sequencing continues to improve as databases of pathogenic variants expand.

Speech and language disorders in DSM-5 include language disorder (persistent difficulties in acquisition and use of language across modalities), speech sound disorder (difficulty with speech sound production), childhood-onset fluency disorder (stuttering), and social (pragmatic) communication disorder (difficulty with the social use of verbal and nonverbal communication). The last category is new in DSM-5 and captures individuals with social communication challenges who do not meet criteria for ASD due to the absence of restricted/repetitive behaviors.
