## Section 1: Definition and Epidemiology

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Definition (Rosenbaum 2007):** Cerebral palsy describes a group of permanent disorders of the development of movement and posture, causing activity limitation, that are attributed to non-progressive disturbances that occurred in the developing fetal or infant brain. The motor disorders of CP are often accompanied by disturbances of sensation, perception, cognition, communication, and behavior; by epilepsy; and by secondary musculoskeletal problems.

**Key features of the definition:**
- "Permanent" -- the condition is lifelong
- "Non-progressive disturbance" -- the brain lesion itself does not worsen, BUT the clinical manifestations change over time (contractures develop, hip displacement occurs, scoliosis progresses)
- "Developing fetal or infant brain" -- injury must occur during the prenatal, perinatal, or early postnatal period (generally accepted as before age 2 years)

**Epidemiology:**
- Prevalence: 2.0-3.5 per 1,000 live births (most commonly cited: 2-2.5 per 1,000)
- CP is the most common motor disability in childhood
- Prevalence has remained relatively stable despite advances in neonatal care
- Prevalence in very preterm (<32 weeks): approximately 60-80 per 1,000
- Prevalence in extremely preterm (<28 weeks): approximately 100-150 per 1,000
- Prevalence in term infants: approximately 1-1.5 per 1,000

> **Clinical Pearl:** CP is the MOST COMMON motor disability in childhood. Despite improvements in neonatal intensive care, the overall prevalence of CP has not declined significantly because increased survival of very preterm infants has offset reductions in CP from other causes.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The prevalence of CP has shown complex epidemiological trends over the past several decades. In high-income countries, improvements in obstetric and neonatal care -- including antenatal corticosteroids, surfactant therapy, therapeutic hypothermia for HIE, and antenatal magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection -- have reduced the incidence of CP per at-risk infant. However, these same advances have improved survival of extremely preterm infants who are at highest risk for CP, creating a stable or even slightly increasing overall prevalence.

Internationally, CP prevalence varies substantially based on access to perinatal care, with rates in low-income countries estimated at 2-10 times higher than in high-income settings. Population-based CP registries (such as the Australian Cerebral Palsy Register, Surveillance of Cerebral Palsy in Europe, and Swedish CPUP program) have been essential for accurate epidemiological monitoring and for establishing evidence-based surveillance programs.

## Section 2: Etiology and Risk Factors

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Risk Factors:**
- Prematurity is the single greatest risk factor for CP
- Only approximately 10% of CP is attributable to birth asphyxia
- Many cases result from prenatal factors that are difficult to identify or prevent

| Timing | Risk Factors | Common MRI Pattern |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Prenatal (1st-2nd trimester) | Brain malformations, infections (TORCH), genetic/chromosomal, multiple gestation, intrauterine growth restriction | Malformations, migration disorders |
| Prenatal (3rd trimester) | Periventricular leukomalacia (preterm), intrauterine stroke, placental insufficiency | White matter injury (PVL), focal infarcts |
| Perinatal | Birth asphyxia/HIE, neonatal stroke, IVH grade III-IV, meningitis | Basal ganglia/thalamic injury, watershed injury |
| Postnatal | Meningitis/encephalitis, traumatic brain injury, near-drowning, kernicterus | Variable depending on mechanism |

**MRI Findings in CP:**

| MRI Pattern | Frequency | CP Type Association | Typical Timing |
|-------------|-----------|--------------------| --------------|
| White matter injury/PVL | ~45% | Spastic diplegia | Preterm (24-34 weeks) |
| Grey matter/basal ganglia injury | ~13% | Dyskinetic, spastic quadriplegia | Term, acute asphyxia |
| Cortical/subcortical lesions | ~10% | Variable | Term |
| Brain malformations | ~11% | Variable (often ataxic or mixed) | 1st-2nd trimester |
| Focal infarcts | ~7% | Spastic hemiplegia | Term, MCA territory |
| Normal MRI | ~10-14% | More common in mild CP, ataxia | Any |

> **Clinical Pearl:** PVL (periventricular leukomalacia) is the most common neuropathology in preterm CP. Corticospinal fibers serving the lower extremities run closest to the lateral ventricles, which is why PVL typically produces spastic diplegia (legs more affected than arms).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Only about 10% of CP is caused by birth asphyxia. This is one of the most commonly tested facts. Most CP results from prenatal factors.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The understanding of CP etiology has expanded substantially in recent years with advances in neuroimaging and genomics. MRI is abnormal in approximately 86-90% of children with CP, making it the neuroimaging modality of choice. The pattern of MRI abnormality correlates with the timing of brain injury and the resulting CP phenotype, providing a window into pathogenesis.

Periventricular leukomalacia results from vulnerability of pre-myelinating oligodendrocytes during the 24-34 week gestational period. These precursor cells are exquisitely sensitive to ischemia, inflammation, and excitotoxicity. The periventricular location reflects the "watershed" vascular territory between penetrating branches of the lenticulostriate and thalamostriate arteries. Because descending motor fibers from the leg representation area of the motor cortex run closest to the ventricles, PVL characteristically produces spastic diplegia with relative sparing of upper extremity function.

Basal ganglia and thalamic injury in term infants reflects the high metabolic demand of these structures, making them particularly vulnerable to acute, severe hypoxia-ischemia. This pattern is most associated with sentinel events (complete placental abruption, cord prolapse, uterine rupture) and typically produces dyskinetic CP or severe spastic quadriplegia.

Emerging evidence indicates that 14-31% of CP cases may have a genetic or genomic contribution, challenging the traditional view that CP is exclusively an acquired brain injury. Whole exome sequencing has identified de novo variants in approximately 10-14% of previously "unexplained" CP cases. Genetic testing should be considered when MRI is normal, when there is a positive family history, or when CP presents with atypical features.

**Neuroprotective Strategies:**
- Antenatal magnesium sulfate given to mothers at risk of preterm delivery (<32 weeks) reduces the rate of CP by approximately 30-40% (NNT ~63). This is now standard of care.
- Therapeutic hypothermia for term or near-term infants with moderate-severe HIE (cooling to 33.5 degrees C for 72 hours, initiated within 6 hours of birth) reduces the combined outcome of death or major disability from approximately 60% to 45% (NNT 6-7). This has not eliminated CP from HIE but has significantly reduced its incidence.

## Section 3: Early Detection (Novak 2017)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The 2017 international clinical practice guideline by Novak and colleagues established an evidence-based pathway for early CP detection before 5 months corrected age, substantially earlier than the previous average diagnostic age of 12-24 months.

**Recommended Assessment Battery (Before 5 Months Corrected Age):**

| Tool | What It Assesses | Key Finding | Sensitivity | Specificity |
|------|-----------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| General Movements Assessment (GMA) | Spontaneous infant movements | Absent fidgety movements (3-5 months) = best single predictor | 98% | 91% |
| MRI (term equivalent) | Brain structure | Identifies lesion pattern and predicts CP subtype | 86-89% | 89% |
| HINE | Standardized neurological examination | Global scores predict GMFCS level | 90% | 85-90% |
| Combined approach | All three tools together | Maximum diagnostic accuracy | >97% | >99% |

**General Movements Assessment (Prechtl Method):**
- Writhing movements (birth to 8 weeks): Cramped-synchronized pattern = high risk for CP
- Fidgety movements (3-5 months corrected age): ABSENT fidgety movements = best single early predictor of CP
- The combination of absent fidgety movements on GMA plus abnormal MRI yields >95% accuracy for predicting CP

**HINE Scoring Thresholds:**
- Score >57 out of 78 at 3 months: Normal
- Score <50 at 3 months: CP likely
- Score <40 at 3 months: Likely GMFCS III-V (severe motor impairment)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Novak 2017 guideline is one of the most important recent advances in pediatric rehabilitation. The key message: CP can and should be diagnosed before 5 months corrected age in high-risk infants using the combination of GMA + MRI + HINE. Absent fidgety movements on GMA is the single best predictor of CP.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Early detection enables early intervention during the period of maximal neuroplasticity. Emerging evidence supports activity-based, goal-directed early intervention starting before 6 months corrected age, including programs such as GAME (Goals-Activity-Motor Enrichment).

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The paradigm shift toward early CP detection represents a fundamental change in the field. Historically, clinicians adopted a "wait and see" approach, often delaying diagnosis until motor abnormalities were unequivocal -- typically around 12-24 months. This delay meant that the critical window of early neuroplasticity was frequently missed.

The General Movements Assessment, developed by Prechtl, is based on the observation that the developing nervous system generates specific patterns of spontaneous movement that can be qualitatively assessed by trained observers. Normal fidgety movements are small, elegant, circular movements that occur continuously in awake infants between 3 and 5 months corrected age. Their absence is the strongest individual predictor of CP. Importantly, the GMA requires specific training for reliable scoring, and inter-rater reliability depends on adequate training.

The HINE provides a standardized, scorable neurological examination that yields a global optimality score predictive of both CP diagnosis and severity. It assesses cranial nerve function, posture, movements, tone, reflexes, and reactions. Its advantage over traditional neurological examination is its quantitative nature, allowing tracking over time and correlation with functional outcomes.

Early signs that should prompt referral for the GMA-MRI-HINE battery include: persistent hand fisting beyond 4 months, head lag beyond 4 months, absent or asymmetric reaching by 5 months, inability to sit with support by 6 months, hand preference before 12 months, and any regression of motor milestones.

## Section 4: Classification Systems

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

CP is classified using multiple parallel systems that capture different dimensions of function. Each system uses a 5-level structure where Level I represents the mildest limitation and Level V the most severe.

#### GMFCS (Gross Motor Function Classification System)

The GMFCS-E&R (Expanded and Revised) classifies gross motor function across 5 age bands: <2, 2-4, 4-6, 6-12, and 12-18 years.

**GMFCS Level Descriptions (6-12 year age band):**

| Level | Description | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|
| I | Walks without limitations; limitations in advanced skills (running, jumping, speed, balance) | Walks without limitations |
| II | Walks with limitations; difficulty with uneven surfaces, inclines, long distances, crowds; may use wheeled mobility for long distances | Walks without assistive device but with limitations |
| III | Walks using hand-held mobility device (crutches, walker) indoors; wheeled mobility for community distances | Requires hand-held walking device |
| IV | Self-mobility with limitations; transported or uses power mobility in most settings; standing for transfers with assistance | Primarily wheelchair; may stand for transfers |
| V | Transported in manual wheelchair in all settings; severely limited antigravity head/trunk control | Fully dependent for mobility |

**Key GMFCS Distinctions:**
- I vs. II: Level I walks without limitations; Level II has limitations (uneven terrain, stairs, distance)
- II vs. III: Level II walks without assistive device; Level III needs a hand-held device
- III vs. IV: Level III walks with device; Level IV is primarily wheelchair-bound, may stand for transfers
- IV vs. V: Level IV has some self-mobility; Level V is fully dependent for mobility

**GMFCS Facts:**
- GMFCS is DESCRIPTIVE, not prescriptive
- Level is generally stable after age 2 (especially after age 5)
- Based on USUAL performance, not best performance
- Approximately 60% of individuals with CP are ambulatory (GMFCS I-III)

**GMFCS Distribution in Population-Based Studies:**
- GMFCS I: ~35%
- GMFCS II: ~20%
- GMFCS III: ~10-15%
- GMFCS IV: ~15-20%
- GMFCS V: ~15-20%

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most commonly tested GMFCS distinction is Level II vs. Level III. Level II walks independently (no device) but with limitations. Level III uses a hand-held walking device (crutches or walker). This is the "device" dividing line.

#### GMFCS Motor Growth Curves

Motor development trajectories differ dramatically by GMFCS level:
- GMFCS I-II: Motor skills improve through adolescence, then plateau
- GMFCS III: Peak motor function around age 6-8, then DECLINE in adolescence
- GMFCS IV: Peak around age 5-7, then decline
- GMFCS V: Very limited motor development; peak around age 3-5, then decline

> **Clinical Pearl:** Anticipatory guidance is critical: families of children at GMFCS III-V should be counseled that gross motor function is expected to DECLINE during adolescence. This decline is due to increasing body mass, loss of flexibility, and reduced physical activity -- not progression of the brain lesion.

#### MACS (Manual Ability Classification System)

Classifies how children with CP use their hands to handle objects in daily activities. Ages 4-18 years (Mini-MACS for 1-4 years).

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| I | Handles objects easily and successfully; minor limitations in dexterity |
| II | Handles most objects with somewhat reduced quality/speed; may use alternative strategies |
| III | Handles objects with difficulty; needs help to prepare/modify activities |
| IV | Handles limited selection of easily managed objects in adapted situations; requires continuous support |
| V | Does not handle objects; requires total assistance |

#### CFCS (Communication Function Classification System)

Classifies everyday communication performance using ALL methods (speech, gestures, AAC, eye gaze).

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| I | Effective sender AND receiver with unfamiliar AND familiar partners |
| II | Effective but slower paced with unfamiliar AND/OR familiar partners |
| III | Effective sender AND receiver with FAMILIAR partners only |
| IV | Inconsistent sender AND/OR receiver with familiar partners |
| V | Seldom effective sender AND receiver even with familiar partners |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The CFCS measures communication function regardless of method. A child who communicates effectively using an eye-gaze AAC device with both familiar and unfamiliar partners would be CFCS Level I or II, even if they have no speech.

#### EDACS (Eating and Drinking Ability Classification System)

Five levels from Level I (eats and drinks safely and efficiently) to Level V (unable to eat or drink safely; tube feeding may be considered).

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The multi-dimensional classification approach using parallel functional systems (GMFCS, MACS, CFCS, EDACS) reflects the modern understanding that CP affects multiple functional domains and that these domains may be affected to different degrees in the same individual. A child may be GMFCS IV (limited self-mobility) but MACS II (handles most objects with some reduction) and CFCS I (effective communicator), highlighting the importance of assessing each domain independently.

The GMFM-66 and GMFM-88 are outcome measurement tools that complement the classification systems:
- GMFM-88 is an ordinal scale with 88 items across 5 dimensions (Lying/Rolling, Sitting, Crawling/Kneeling, Standing, Walking/Running/Jumping). Each item is scored 0-3.
- GMFM-66 uses Rasch analysis to create an interval scale from a 66-item subset. Because it is an interval scale, change scores can be meaningfully compared. It is more sensitive to change than the GMFM-88 and can be estimated from a subset of items using the Gross Motor Ability Estimator software.

The critical distinction: GMFM-88 is ordinal (all items must be tested). GMFM-66 is interval (Rasch-derived, more sensitive to change, can estimate from subset).

## Section 5: Motor Phenotypes

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Distribution by Motor Type:**
- Spastic CP: ~80-85% of all CP
- Dyskinetic CP: ~10-15%
- Ataxic CP: ~5-6%
- Mixed: ~5-10%

#### Spastic CP

Spasticity is defined as a velocity-dependent increase in muscle tone with hyperactive stretch reflexes (Lance definition). Key features include:
- "Positive" UMN signs: Spasticity, hyperreflexia, clonus, extensor plantar responses (Babinski sign)
- "Negative" UMN signs: Weakness, loss of selective motor control, fatigability

**Spastic Subtypes by Topography:**

| Subtype | Distribution | Frequency | Typical MRI | Clinical Features |
|---------|-------------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Unilateral (hemiplegia) | One side; arm usually > leg | ~30% | Focal infarct (MCA territory) or unilateral PVL | Most common in term infants; arm typically more affected |
| Bilateral - diplegia | Both legs > arms | ~35% | PVL (bilateral white matter injury) | Most common in preterm; corticospinal leg fibers near ventricles |
| Bilateral - quadriplegia | All four limbs + trunk/oromotor | ~15-20% | Severe HIE, extensive bilateral injury | GMFCS IV-V typically; often term with severe asphyxia |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Hypertonia Assessment Tool (HAT) can help differentiate spasticity (velocity-dependent), rigidity (velocity-independent, "lead-pipe"), and dystonia (sustained involuntary contractions causing twisting). This differentiation is critical because treatment approaches differ.

#### Dyskinetic CP

- Accounts for approximately 10-15% of all CP
- Subdivided into dystonic (~75% of dyskinetic) and choreoathetoid (~25% of dyskinetic)
- Most commonly associated with basal ganglia/thalamic injury from term asphyxia
- Also historically associated with kernicterus (bilirubin encephalopathy)
- Characterized by involuntary, uncontrolled, recurring, and occasionally stereotyped movements
- Dystonic type: Sustained or intermittent muscle contractions causing twisting and repetitive movements or abnormal postures
- Choreoathetoid type: Irregular, unpredictable, involuntary movements (chorea = rapid, dance-like; athetosis = slow, writhing)

#### Ataxic CP

- Accounts for approximately 5-6% of all CP
- Characterized by loss of orderly muscular coordination, resulting in inaccurate movements
- Clinical features: Intention tremor, dysmetria, wide-based gait, poor balance, difficulty with rapid alternating movements
- MRI may show cerebellar malformations or may be normal
- Normal MRI is more common in ataxic CP than in other subtypes

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The distinction between spasticity and dystonia has significant therapeutic implications and is a common source of confusion. Spasticity is velocity-dependent -- faster passive stretch produces greater resistance -- and is mediated by the stretch reflex arc at the spinal cord level due to loss of descending inhibition from the corticospinal and reticulospinal tracts. Dystonia, in contrast, is a movement disorder characterized by sustained or intermittent muscle contractions that cause abnormal postures and is driven by basal ganglia dysfunction.

Many children with CP have mixed hypertonia (co-existing spasticity and dystonia), and the relative contributions of each can be difficult to disentangle clinically. The Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS), while widely used, has limitations in this regard because it measures resistance to passive stretch without distinguishing between spasticity, dystonia, rigidity, and contracture. The Tardieu Scale offers better differentiation: it measures the angle of muscle reaction ("catch") at fast velocity (R1) versus the passive ROM at slow velocity (R2). The dynamic component (R2 - R1) represents the spastic contribution; if R2 - R1 is large, spasticity predominates; if R2 - R1 is small, contracture predominates.

## Section 6: Associated Conditions

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

CP is NOT just a motor disorder. Associated conditions are common and often more functionally limiting than the motor impairment itself.

| Associated Condition | Prevalence | Key Points |
|---------------------|------------|------------|
| Chronic pain | 50-75% | Often underrecognized; increases with age |
| Intellectual disability | ~50% | Ranges from 30-65% depending on CP type; highest in quadriplegia |
| Speech/language disorders | 40-60% | Dysarthria, language delay, communication impairment |
| Feeding/swallowing difficulties | 40-90% | Highest in GMFCS IV-V; aspiration risk |
| Epilepsy | 25-45% | More common in hemiplegic and quadriplegic CP |
| Visual impairment | 20-40% | Cortical visual impairment most common |
| Bladder control problems | 30-60% | Underrecognized |
| Behavioral/emotional problems | 25-40% | ADHD, anxiety, depression |
| Hip displacement | ~35% overall | Up to 90% in GMFCS V |
| GERD | 50-70% | Contributes to feeding difficulties and respiratory compromise |
| Constipation | 50-75% | Immobility, smooth muscle dysfunction, medications |
| Scoliosis | 25-50% | Up to 60-70% in non-ambulatory CP |
| Drooling/sialorrhea | ~20-40% | Oral motor dysfunction, not excess production |
| Hearing impairment | 10-20% | Sensorineural; higher in kernicterus |
| Osteopenia/low bone density | Up to 77% | In moderate-severe CP; fracture risk |
| Sleep disorders | ~20% | Multifactorial |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Chronic pain affects 50-75% of individuals with CP and is frequently underrecognized and undertreated. It increases with age and is associated with musculoskeletal deformity, spasticity, and reduced quality of life. Every CP visit should include a pain assessment.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Fracture risk is elevated in CP, with a lifetime prevalence of 9-16%. Approximately 75% of fractures occur in the lower limb. Osteopenia is driven by immobility, nutritional deficiency, anticonvulsant use, and limited weight-bearing.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The burden of associated conditions in CP scales with motor severity. Children at GMFCS IV-V have the highest rates of intellectual disability, epilepsy, visual impairment, feeding difficulties, and musculoskeletal complications. However, even children at GMFCS I-II frequently experience pain, behavioral challenges, and learning difficulties that impact quality of life.

Feeding and nutritional management is a major clinical challenge in severe CP. Dysphagia with aspiration risk affects the majority of children at GMFCS IV-V, and gastrostomy tube feeding is eventually required in approximately 7% of all CP and a much higher proportion of GMFCS V patients. The decision to place a gastrostomy is influenced by growth failure, aspiration pneumonia risk, and caregiver burden related to oral feeding. Gastroesophageal reflux is common and may require fundoplication in conjunction with gastrostomy.

Drooling (sialorrhea) in CP results from impaired oral motor control and infrequent swallowing rather than excess saliva production. Management options include anticholinergic medications (glycopyrrolate is preferred due to less CNS penetration than scopolamine or atropine), botulinum toxin injection into the salivary glands, and surgical procedures (salivary gland duct ligation or rerouting).

## Section 7: Spasticity Management Ladder

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The evidence-based approach to spasticity management follows a progressive ladder:

**Step 1: Rehabilitation and Conservative Management (All Patients)**
- Stretching, positioning, serial casting
- Orthotics (AFOs, KAFOs, SMOs)
- Therapeutic exercise and strengthening
- Adaptive equipment and seating

**Step 2: Focal Spasticity -- Chemodenervation**
- Botulinum toxin A (see Section 8)
- Phenol/alcohol nerve blocks (see below)

**Step 3: Generalized Spasticity -- Oral Medications**

| Medication | Mechanism | Dosing | Key Side Effects | Board Pearl |
|-----------|-----------|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| Baclofen | GABA-B agonist (central) | Start 0.5 mg/kg/day div BID-TID; max 2 mg/kg/day | Sedation, weakness; withdrawal seizures if stopped abruptly | Central mechanism; GABA-B |
| Diazepam | GABA-A agonist (central) | 0.1-0.3 mg/kg/day div BID-QID; max 2 mg/dose | Sedation, dependence, tolerance; wean slowly | Central mechanism; GABA-A |
| Dantrolene | Inhibits Ca2+ release from SR (peripheral) | Start 0.5 mg/kg once daily; max 2 mg/kg QID (100 mg/dose) | Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs; 28% case-fatality for liver disease), weakness | ONLY peripheral-acting antispasticity agent |
| Tizanidine | Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist (central) | Start 1-2 mg QHS; max 24 mg/day | Sedation, dry mouth, hepatotoxicity | Central mechanism; alpha-2 |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The board loves asking about mechanisms: Dantrolene is the ONLY antispasticity medication that acts PERIPHERALLY (at the muscle level, inhibiting calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum). Baclofen (GABA-B), diazepam (GABA-A), and tizanidine (alpha-2 adrenergic) all act centrally.

**Phenol/Alcohol Nerve Blocks:**
- Phenol (5-7% aqueous solution): Motor point or peripheral nerve block
- Mechanism: Protein denaturation and axonal destruction
- Onset: Minutes (vs. days for botulinum toxin)
- Duration: 3-12 months (up to 18 months)
- Advantages over botulinum toxin: Longer lasting, less expensive, immediate onset, no systemic dose ceiling
- Disadvantages: Painful injection, risk of dysesthesias (avoid mixed sensory-motor nerves)
- Classic application: Obturator nerve block for hip adductor spasticity (obturator nerve is predominantly motor, so dysesthesia risk is low)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenol nerve blocks are ideal for large muscles and predominantly motor nerves. The obturator nerve block for hip adductor spasticity is the classic clinical application because the obturator nerve is primarily motor, minimizing the risk of painful dysesthesias.

**Step 4: Intrathecal Baclofen Pump (see Section 9)**

**Step 5: Orthopedic Surgery (see Section 12)**

**Step 6: Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (see Section 10)**

**Dystonia Management:**
- First-line: Baclofen (same dosing as for spasticity)
- Second-line: Gabapentin (5-15 mg/kg/day TID, max 2400 mg/day), clonidine (start 0.025-0.05 mg/day BID, max 0.6 mg/day), or benzodiazepines
- Deep brain stimulation: For refractory dystonia targeting GPi (globus pallidus internus), typically age 7+ years

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The "traffic light" systematic review by Novak and colleagues (2020) provides the most comprehensive evidence synthesis for CP interventions:

**Green light (strong evidence -- DO IT):**
- Botulinum toxin A for spasticity (upper and lower limb)
- Bisphosphonates for low bone density
- Casting and orthotics
- CIMT and bimanual therapy for hemiplegia
- Context-focused therapy (goals in natural environments)
- Diazepam for short-term spasticity management
- Fitness training
- Goal-directed training
- Hip surveillance
- Home-based programs
- Intrathecal baclofen for generalized spasticity
- Selective dorsal rhizotomy
- SEMLS

**Red light (evidence against -- DON'T DO IT):**
- Craniosacral therapy
- Hyperbaric oxygen therapy
- NDT/Bobath as sole intervention for motor outcomes
- Sensory integration therapy (as traditionally defined)

The intervention evidence supports a shift toward task-specific, goal-directed, and activity-based therapies and away from passive, therapist-directed approaches. CIMT (constraint-induced movement therapy) and bimanual intensive therapy (HABIT) have the strongest evidence for upper extremity interventions in hemiplegic CP. CIMT improves unimanual capacity of the affected hand, while bimanual therapy improves coordination between hands.

## Section 8: Botulinum Toxin

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Mechanism:** Botulinum toxin blocks presynaptic acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction by cleaving SNARE proteins (specifically SNAP-25). This produces temporary chemical denervation of the injected muscle.

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Onset: 24-72 hours (clinically apparent within 1 week)
- Peak effect: 2-6 weeks (reevaluate at 6-8 weeks post-injection)
- Duration: 3-6 months (some effect for up to 8 months)
- Minimum re-injection interval: 3 months

**Dosing (OnabotulinumtoxinA / Botox):**
- Individual muscle: 1-6 U/kg per muscle
- Total body dose: Maximum 400 U total or 12-16 U/kg (do not exceed)
- Large muscles (gastrocnemius/soleus): 3-6 U/kg per side
- Small muscles (tibialis posterior, FCU): 1-2 U/kg per muscle

**Dosing (AbobotulinumtoxinA / Dysport):**
- Approximately 2.5-3:1 ratio to Botox (2.5-3 units Dysport ≈ 1 unit Botox)
- NOT interchangeable; dosing must be formulation-specific

**Adverse Effects:**
- Local: Pain, bruising, weakness (desired to a degree)
- Systemic (3.6% of injections): Fatigue, generalized weakness, flu-like symptoms
- Severe systemic events requiring hospitalization: 0.7%
- Risk factors for adverse events: GMFCS IV-V, underlying dysphagia, history of aspiration pneumonia
- FDA black box warning in pediatrics regarding potential for distant spread of toxin
- Neutralizing antibodies: 3-10% after repeated injections (may reduce efficacy)

**Contraindications:**
- Known hypersensitivity
- Concurrent aminoglycoside use (potentiates neuromuscular blockade)
- Myasthenia gravis or other neuromuscular junction disorders
- Infection at injection site

**Localization Techniques:**
- EMG guidance, electrical stimulation, or ultrasound for accurate muscle targeting

**Important Limitations:**
- Does NOT prevent contractures long-term
- Does NOT reduce the need for orthopedic hip surgery
- Repeated injections may lose efficacy due to neutralizing antibody formation

> **Clinical Pearl:** Botulinum toxin is NOT FDA-approved for pediatric spasticity in the United States -- it is used off-label. Despite this, it is one of the most commonly used and evidence-supported interventions in CP spasticity management.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Risk of systemic adverse events from botulinum toxin is highest in children at GMFCS IV-V, particularly those with existing dysphagia or aspiration risk. These patients require careful dose selection and monitoring.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The mechanism of botulinum toxin involves binding to presynaptic nerve terminals, internalization via receptor-mediated endocytosis, and cleavage of SNARE complex proteins required for acetylcholine vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane. Different serotypes cleave different SNARE proteins: type A (the most commonly used) cleaves SNAP-25. Recovery of neuromuscular transmission requires sprouting of new nerve terminals and formation of new synaptic connections, a process that takes 3-6 months.

The clinical application of botulinum toxin in CP requires integration of treatment goals with patient-specific factors. Goals may include improving function, reducing pain, facilitating orthotics or splinting, delaying or preventing contracture, improving hygiene access, or facilitating casting. Treatment should always be combined with physical and occupational therapy to maximize functional gains during the window of reduced spasticity.

## Section 9: Intrathecal Baclofen

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Mechanism:** Programmable pump implanted subcutaneously in the abdomen delivers baclofen directly to the intrathecal space via a catheter with its tip typically at T10-L1.

**Indications:**
- Generalized spasticity or dystonia not adequately managed by oral medications
- Most commonly used in GMFCS IV-V
- Requires trial dose (25-100 mcg intrathecal via lumbar puncture) before pump implantation

**Advantages:**
- 100-1000x lower dose than oral baclofen (typical dose 50-1000 mcg/day)
- Fewer systemic side effects (less sedation)
- Dose is adjustable; can be programmed for different rates throughout the day

**Complications:**
- Adverse effects in up to 50%: Lethargy, confusion, hypotonia
- Catheter complications: Infection, seroma, CSF leak, kink, disconnection, migration
- Pump malfunction: Battery failure, mechanical failure

**ITB WITHDRAWAL -- MEDICAL EMERGENCY:**
- Causes: Catheter kink/disconnect, pump failure, empty reservoir, programming error
- Symptoms: Rebound spasticity, fever, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, pruritis, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, multi-organ failure, DEATH
- Mimics: Malignant hyperthermia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, autonomic dysreflexia
- Treatment: Restore ITB delivery immediately; oral/IV/rectal baclofen; benzodiazepines; cyproheptadine; ICU admission

**ITB OVERDOSE:**
- Causes: Programming error, pump malfunction, catheter migration
- Symptoms: Drowsiness, respiratory depression, hypotonia, flaccidity, coma, bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia
- Treatment: Supportive care (airway, ventilation); CSF withdrawal if feasible; empty/turn off pump

> **Clinical Pearl:** ITB withdrawal = HYPER-symptoms (fever, tachycardia, spasticity, hypertension). ITB overdose = HYPO-symptoms (hypotension, hypothermia, hypotonia, respiratory depression). This distinction is a board favorite.

> **Clinical Pearl:** ITB withdrawal is life-threatening and can be fatal. Any patient with an ITB pump who presents with fever, increased spasticity, and autonomic instability should be treated as ITB withdrawal until proven otherwise. The pump should be interrogated immediately and oral baclofen initiated while troubleshooting.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The decision to implant an ITB pump involves balancing potential benefits (reduced spasticity/dystonia, improved comfort, easier caregiving) against significant surgical risks and the lifelong commitment to pump management. Patient selection should involve a multidisciplinary team including physiatry, neurosurgery, physical therapy, and the family.

The intrathecal baclofen trial is a critical pre-implantation step. A single bolus dose (typically 50 mcg in children, 25 mcg in very young/small children) is administered via lumbar puncture, and the patient is observed for 4-8 hours. A positive response is defined as a clinically meaningful reduction in spasticity (typically 1+ point on the Modified Ashworth Scale) without excessive side effects. Some centers use a continuous trial via an external catheter over several days for more definitive assessment.

Pump refills are required every 1-6 months depending on dose and reservoir volume. Patients and families must understand the importance of keeping refill appointments, as reservoir depletion leads to withdrawal. Battery life is approximately 5-7 years, after which surgical pump replacement is required.

## Section 10: Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Mechanism:** Selective surgical sectioning of dorsal (sensory) nerve rootlets at L1-S1/S2, interrupting the afferent limb of the gamma loop reflex arc to permanently reduce spasticity.

**Candidacy Criteria:**
- Age: 3-8 years (some centers extend to 10-12 years)
- Motor type: Pure spastic DIPLEGIA (no significant dystonia or athetosis)
- GMFCS Level: II-III (ambulatory or near-ambulatory)
- Adequate underlying voluntary motor strength
- No fixed contractures
- Good selective motor control
- Motivated patient and family
- MRI pattern: PVL preferred (predicts better outcomes)

**Outcomes (Meta-analysis of 3 RCTs, 90 children):**
- SDR + PT improved spasticity compared to PT alone (mean Ashworth reduction -1.2 points)
- Modest improvement in gross motor function (GMFM-66 +2.6 points vs. PT alone)
- Greater improvements in GMFCS II compared to GMFCS III

**Long-Term Outcomes (20-28 year follow-up):**
- Durable spasticity reduction maintained >10 years
- Motor improvements sustained in GMFCS I, attenuated in GMFCS II-III, not sustained in GMFCS IV-V
- Approximately 40% needed additional botulinum toxin or orthopedic surgery
- Quality of life improvements across most domains

**Complications (~10%):**
- Sensory changes and dysesthesias (most common)
- Bladder dysfunction (usually transient)
- Weakness (if excessive rootlets sectioned)
- Spinal deformity risk (lordosis, scoliosis) from laminectomy

> **Clinical Pearl:** SDR is for spastic DIPLEGIA at GMFCS II-III. It does NOT work for dystonia or athetosis. The ideal candidate has PVL on MRI, good underlying strength, no contractures, and good selective motor control. SDR permanently reduces spasticity but does not eliminate the need for ongoing therapy and potential orthopedic intervention.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The technique of SDR involves a limited laminectomy (typically single-level L1 in the modern approach) to access the cauda equina. Dorsal rootlets from L1 through S1 or S2 are identified and tested with intraoperative EMG. Rootlets demonstrating abnormal (sustained, irradiating) responses to electrical stimulation are selectively sectioned, while those with normal responses are preserved. The proportion of rootlets sectioned varies but is typically 50-70%.

The three randomized controlled trials comparing SDR + PT to PT alone consistently demonstrated reductions in spasticity and modest improvements in gross motor function. However, the clinical significance of the motor gains has been debated, as the GMFM improvements, while statistically significant, were small in absolute terms. Longer-term follow-up studies have shown more clinically meaningful functional differences, with SDR patients demonstrating better gait parameters and mobility at 5-10 years post-surgery compared to non-surgical controls.

Patient selection is the strongest predictor of outcome. Children with pure spasticity (no dystonia), GMFCS II-III, PVL on MRI, and good underlying strength consistently show the best results. Extending SDR to GMFCS IV-V patients or those with significant dystonia has yielded disappointing motor outcomes, though palliative benefits (comfort, ease of care) may still be achieved.

## Section 11: Gait Patterns and Orthotics

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### Rodda Classification for Spastic Hemiplegia

| Type | Ankle | Knee | Hip | Management |
|------|-------|------|-----|------------|
| Type I: Drop Foot | Dorsiflexion deficit in swing only | Normal or recurvatum | Normal | AFO sufficient |
| Type II: True Equinus | Equinus in stance AND swing | Normal or recurvatum | Normal | AFO + possible BoNT/surgery |
| Type III: Equinus + Knee | Equinus | Flexion in stance | Flexion | Multi-level treatment needed |
| Type IV: Equinus + Knee + Hip | Equinus | Flexion | Flexion + internal rotation | Requires SEMLS |

#### Rodda Classification for Spastic Diplegia

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| True Equinus | Bilateral equinus; knees extended or recurvatum |
| Jump Gait | Bilateral equinus, knee flexion, hip flexion, anterior pelvic tilt (classic diplegic pattern) |
| Apparent Equinus | Normal ankle ROM but equinus appearance due to excessive proximal flexion |
| Crouch Gait | Excessive dorsiflexion, knee flexion, hip flexion; calcaneal gait pattern |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Crouch gait is often IATROGENIC -- it can result from overlengthening of the Achilles tendon or isolated hamstring lengthening. It can also develop naturally during adolescent growth in GMFCS III-IV. Crouch gait leads to patella alta, patellofemoral pain, and eventually patellar fracture if not addressed.

#### AFO Types in CP

| AFO Type | Ankle Motion | Indication | Board Pearl |
|----------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| Solid AFO | Fixed; prevents equinus | GMFCS III-V; severe spasticity | Limits push-off but provides maximum stability |
| Hinged AFO (articulated) | Allows dorsiflexion; blocks plantarflexion | GMFCS I-II with equinus tendency | Allows third rocker (push-off) in gait |
| Posterior leaf spring (PLS) | Flexible posterior strut | Mild drop foot; GMFCS I-II | Assists dorsiflexion in swing phase |
| Ground reaction AFO (GRAFO) | Rigid; anterior tibial shell | CROUCH GAIT (knee flexion in stance) | Creates knee extension moment; contraindicated in equinus |
| DAFO | Thin, flexible, foot plate | Low tone or mild spasticity | Proprioceptive input |
| SMO | Below malleoli | Hindfoot valgus/varus; hypotonia | No ankle motion restriction |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The GRAFO (Ground Reaction AFO) is specifically indicated for CROUCH GAIT. It creates a knee extension moment by blocking excessive dorsiflexion. It is CONTRAINDICATED in equinus or fixed knee flexion contracture.

#### Key Examination Tests

| Test | What It Assesses | Positive Finding |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Silfverskiold | Gastrocnemius vs. soleus tightness | DF improves with knee flexion = gastrocnemius tight; does NOT improve = soleus tight |
| Duncan-Ely (Ely) | Rectus femoris spasticity | Prone, rapid knee flex → hip flexes off table = positive |
| Thomas test | Hip flexion contracture | Supine, flex opposite hip → tested hip flexes off table |
| Phelps test | "Confused equinus" | Ankle DF with knee flexed AND hip extended |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Silfverskiold test is a board classic: Ankle dorsiflexion is measured with the knee extended (stretches gastrocnemius, a 2-joint muscle) and with the knee flexed (relaxes gastrocnemius, isolates soleus). If dorsiflexion improves with knee flexion, the gastrocnemius is the culprit (gastroc recession may be indicated). If dorsiflexion does NOT improve, the soleus is involved (Achilles lengthening may be needed).

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Three-dimensional instrumented gait analysis is the gold standard for surgical decision-making in ambulatory CP (GMFCS I-III) before SEMLS. Gait analysis provides kinematics (joint angles), kinetics (joint moments and powers), EMG (muscle activation timing), and energy expenditure data. This information allows precise identification of the mechanical problems underlying each patient's gait deviation and guides surgical planning.

Lever arm dysfunction refers to skeletal malalignment (femoral anteversion, external tibial torsion, foot deformity) that reduces the efficiency of muscles acting across joints. For example, increased femoral anteversion causes the hip to internally rotate during walking, placing the hip abductors and extensors at a mechanical disadvantage. Correction of lever arm dysfunction through derotation osteotomies is a key component of SEMLS.

Stiff knee gait (reduced peak knee flexion in swing phase) results from overactivity of the rectus femoris during swing. The Duncan-Ely test and swing-phase EMG of the rectus femoris confirm the diagnosis. Treatment involves rectus femoris transfer (to the sartorius, gracilis, or iliotibial band), which converts the rectus from a knee extensor in swing to a knee flexor.

## Section 12: Hip Surveillance

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Hip Displacement Risk by GMFCS Level:**

| GMFCS Level | Risk of Hip Displacement |
|-------------|------------------------|
| I | ~1% |
| II | ~15% |
| III | ~40% |
| IV | ~70% |
| V | ~90% |

**Pathomechanics:** Spastic/overactive hip adductors and flexors, femoral anteversion, acetabular dysplasia, and lack of weight-bearing lead to progressive hip subluxation and eventual dislocation.

**Migration Percentage (Reimers Index):** Percentage of the femoral head uncovered laterally by the acetabulum on AP pelvis radiograph.

| MP | Classification | Management |
|----|---------------|------------|
| <30% | Normal | Observation, therapy, positioning |
| 30-40% | At risk | BoNT to adductors/flexors + bracing (hip abduction orthosis) |
| 40-50% | Subluxation | Soft tissue surgery (adductor/psoas release) |
| >50% | Significant subluxation | Bony reconstruction (VDRO +/- pelvic osteotomy) |
| >90-100% | Dislocation | Salvage procedures if symptomatic |

**Hip Surveillance Schedule (Based on CPUP/AACPDM):**

| GMFCS Level | Surveillance Frequency |
|-------------|----------------------|
| I | Hip X-ray at ages 2 and 6 years |
| II | Annual hip X-ray until skeletal maturity |
| III-V | Hip X-ray every 6 months until age 8, then annually |

**Natural History Without Intervention:** Progressive subluxation leads to dislocation, causing pain, windswept deformity, and difficulty with perineal hygiene and sitting.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Swedish CPUP program demonstrated that systematic hip surveillance paired with timely intervention reduced the rate of hip dislocation requiring surgery from approximately 8% to nearly 0%. This is one of the strongest arguments for population-based surveillance in CP.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nearly 50% of non-ambulatory children with quadriplegic CP develop hip subluxation by age 5. Hip surveillance must begin early and continue through skeletal maturity in at-risk patients.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The CPUP (Cerebral Palsy Follow-Up Programme), initiated in Sweden in 1994, established the model for population-based hip surveillance that has since been adopted internationally. The program demonstrated that standardized radiographic surveillance with migration percentage measurement, combined with timely intervention at predefined thresholds, can virtually eliminate painful hip dislocation in CP.

The management algorithm is based on migration percentage thresholds. Conservative measures (BoNT to adductors, hip abduction bracing) are employed when the MP reaches 30-40%. Soft tissue surgery (adductor and psoas release) is considered at MP 40-50%. When the MP exceeds 50%, bony reconstruction becomes necessary, typically involving a femoral varus derotation osteotomy (VDRO) combined with a pelvic osteotomy (Dega, Pemberton, or San Diego procedure) to improve acetabular coverage. Salvage procedures for established painful dislocation include proximal femoral resection-interposition arthroplasty, which is a palliative procedure for pain relief rather than a reconstructive operation.

## Section 13: Scoliosis

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

- Prevalence: 25-50% of all CP; up to 60-70% in non-ambulatory CP (GMFCS IV-V)
- Increases with GMFCS level: 2% in GMFCS I, approximately 30% in GMFCS IV-V
- Curve patterns: Typically long C-shaped thoracolumbar curves (unlike the S-shaped curves of idiopathic scoliosis), often with pelvic obliquity
- Bracing may slow progression but does NOT prevent surgery in neuromuscular scoliosis
- Surgical indications: >40-50 degree progressive curve interfering with sitting, age >10 years
- Surgery: Posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation to pelvis (unit rod or pelvic fixation)
- Complication rates for surgery: 25-50% (significantly higher than idiopathic scoliosis)
- Most common surgical complications: Infection, pneumonia, blood loss
- Growing rods (including magnetically controlled MAGEC rods) used in young children (<10 years) with progressive curves >40-50 degrees who are skeletally immature

> **Clinical Pearl:** Neuromuscular scoliosis in CP typically presents as a long, sweeping C-shaped curve with pelvic obliquity, unlike the shorter S-shaped curves of adolescent idiopathic scoliosis. Bracing does not prevent the need for surgery in neuromuscular scoliosis but may be used to support sitting posture and delay surgery.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The decision to perform spinal fusion in CP requires careful risk-benefit analysis. The high complication rate (25-50%) reflects the medical complexity of these patients, who often have poor nutritional status, respiratory compromise, and limited physiological reserve. However, observational data suggest that spinal fusion may improve survival -- one study found mortality rates of 8.7 per 1,000 follow-up years in non-surgical patients versus 5.3 per 1,000 in surgically treated patients.

Preoperative optimization is essential and typically includes nutritional supplementation, pulmonary rehabilitation (including secretion clearance and non-invasive ventilation training), and management of GERD and seizures. The surgical approach involves posterior instrumentation and fusion from the upper thoracic spine to the pelvis, correcting both the spinal curve and the pelvic obliquity.

## Section 14: SEMLS (Single-Event Multilevel Surgery)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

- Concept: Address ALL gait abnormalities in one surgical session, replacing the previous approach of repeated "birthday surgeries"
- Optimal timing: GMFCS I-III, ages 6-12 years (after gait maturation at ~age 6, before skeletal maturity)
- Requires preoperative 3D instrumented gait analysis for surgical planning
- Common procedures combined in SEMLS: Psoas lengthening, adductor release, hamstring lengthening, rectus femoris transfer, gastrocnemius or Achilles lengthening, femoral derotation osteotomy, tibial derotation osteotomy

**Advantages over serial single-level surgeries:**
- Fewer total surgical episodes and anesthesia events
- Fewer rehabilitation periods (one intensive rehab vs. multiple)
- Addresses biomechanical interactions between levels simultaneously
- Reduces compensatory gait patterns that develop after single-level correction

> **Clinical Pearl:** 3D instrumented gait analysis is the gold standard for SEMLS planning. It prevents the common error of treating a compensatory mechanism rather than the primary problem -- for example, apparent equinus (which looks like toe-walking but is actually caused by excessive hip and knee flexion) should not be treated with Achilles lengthening.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The evolution from serial single-level procedures to SEMLS represented a paradigm shift in CP orthopedic management. The rationale is rooted in the interdependence of musculoskeletal segments during gait -- correcting one level without addressing the others can create new compensatory patterns or unmask previously hidden problems. For example, isolated hamstring lengthening without addressing gastrocnemius spasticity can convert a jump gait into a crouch gait, an iatrogenic complication that is more functionally limiting than the original pattern.

The typical SEMLS rehabilitation protocol involves intensive inpatient rehabilitation for 1-2 weeks followed by outpatient therapy for 6-12 months. Maximum functional improvement is typically achieved at 12-24 months post-surgery. Families should be counseled that the early post-operative period may involve temporary functional decline before improvement occurs.

## Section 15: Prognosis and Aging with CP

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Ambulation Prognosis:**
- GMFCS I-II: Will walk independently throughout life (though may have increasing difficulty in adulthood)
- GMFCS III: Many transition from walking with device to wheelchair use during adolescence
- GMFCS IV-V: Non-ambulatory; focus on supported sitting, standing transfers (IV), or total dependency (V)
- Motor growth curves predict peak function and subsequent decline by GMFCS level

**Aging with CP:**
- Musculoskeletal deterioration is progressive: contractures, joint degeneration, pain
- Cervical spondylotic myelopathy occurs earlier (4th-5th decade) in CP due to abnormal movement patterns
- Fatigue is increasingly recognized as a major issue in adults with CP
- Mental health: Depression and anxiety are common in adults with CP
- Life expectancy: Most individuals with CP at GMFCS I-III have near-normal life expectancy; significant reduction at GMFCS IV-V (particularly with feeding difficulties and respiratory compromise)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The transition from pediatric to adult care is a critical vulnerability period for individuals with CP. Many adults with CP experience functional decline due to progressive musculoskeletal changes, reduced access to rehabilitation services, and increasing pain and fatigue. Proactive transition planning should begin in early adolescence.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The concept of premature aging in CP has gained increasing attention. Adults with CP report declining function beginning in their 20s-30s -- decades earlier than the general population. Contributing factors include chronic musculoskeletal strain from abnormal movement patterns, progressive joint degeneration, decreasing activity levels, and weight gain. Fatigue is cited as one of the most limiting symptoms by adults with CP.

Cervical myelopathy from spondylotic changes represents an under-recognized complication in adults with CP, particularly those with dyskinetic movements. The repetitive abnormal neck postures and movements accelerate cervical disc degeneration and stenosis, potentially causing new neurological decline that may be mistaken for natural CP progression.

Preventive strategies for healthy aging with CP include lifelong fitness programs (adapted for functional level), weight management, bone health optimization (calcium, vitamin D, weight-bearing when possible, bisphosphonates for osteoporosis), pain management, and mental health support.
