# Chapter 8: Genetic & Chromosomal Syndromes — Research Notes

## GUIDELINES FOUND

### AAP Health Supervision Guidelines for Down Syndrome
- **Bull MJ, Trotter T, Santoro SL, et al. "Health Supervision for Children and Adolescents with Down Syndrome." Pediatrics. 2022;149(5):e2022057010.**
  - Updated from prior 2011 guidelines (Bull MJ, Committee on Genetics. Pediatrics. 2011;128(2):393-406)
  - Comprehensive cradle-to-adult screening schedule
  - Key screening recommendations:
    - **Cardiac**: Echocardiogram in all newborns regardless of murmur (40-50% have CHD)
    - **Thyroid**: TSH at birth, 6 months, 12 months, then annually
    - **Hearing**: ABR at birth or by 1 month; audiologic evaluation every 6 months until age 3, then annually
    - **Vision**: Ophthalmologic evaluation by 6 months, then annually (refractive errors in 50-70%)
    - **Cervical spine**: Lateral cervical radiographs (flexion/extension + neutral) between ages 3-5 years; earlier if symptomatic
    - **Hematology**: CBC at birth (polycythemia, TMD/transient myeloproliferative disorder in ~10%)
    - **Celiac disease**: Tissue transglutaminase IgA antibody starting at age 2, or sooner if symptomatic
    - **Sleep**: Polysomnography by age 4 (obstructive sleep apnea in 50-75%)
    - **Atlantoaxial instability**: Screening radiographs; ADI >4.5 mm is abnormal
    - **Growth**: DS-specific growth charts (Zemel BS et al. Pediatrics. 2015;136(5):e1204-e1211)

### Special Olympics Atlantoaxial Instability Policy
- Special Olympics requires cervical spine radiographs (flexion/extension views) for all athletes with Down syndrome before participation in high-risk sports
- High-risk sports: gymnastics, diving, pentathlon, butterfly stroke, diving starts in swimming, high jump, soccer, alpine skiing, equestrian sports
- Athletes with ADI >4.5 mm or symptoms of spinal cord compression are restricted from these activities
- **Board Pearl**: The 10-30% prevalence of atlantoaxial instability is well-established, but only 1-2% are symptomatic

### ACMG Guidelines on Genetic Testing
- **Miller DT, Adam MP, Aradhya S, et al. "Consensus statement: chromosomal microarray is a first-tier clinical diagnostic test for individuals with developmental disabilities or congenital anomalies." Am J Hum Genet. 2010;86(5):749-764.**
- Chromosomal microarray (CMA) recommended as first-tier test for unexplained developmental delay/intellectual disability, ASD, or multiple congenital anomalies
- Diagnostic yield: 15-20% (vs. 3% for traditional karyotype)
- Limitation: cannot detect balanced translocations or point mutations
- **Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)**: Second-tier test when CMA is non-diagnostic; diagnostic yield 25-40% in undiagnosed cases
  - Manickam K, et al. "Exome and genome sequencing for pediatric patients with congenital anomalies or intellectual disability." Genet Med. 2021;23(3):458-468.
- **Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)**: Emerging as potential first-line; diagnostic yield 30-50% in some studies
  - Clark MM, et al. "Meta-analysis of the diagnostic and clinical utility of genome and exome sequencing and chromosomal microarray in children with suspected genetic diseases." NPJ Genom Med. 2018;3:16.

## LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
1. **Pueschel SM, Scola FH. "Atlantoaxial instability in individuals with Down syndrome: epidemiologic, radiographic and clinical studies." Pediatrics. 1987;80(4):555-560.**
   - Established prevalence of atlantoaxial instability at 10-30% in Down syndrome
   - Only 1-2% symptomatic requiring surgical intervention
   - Advocated for screening cervical spine radiographs

2. **Freeman SB, Bean LH, Allen EG, et al. "Ethnicity, sex, and the incidence of congenital heart defects: a report from the National Down Syndrome Project." Genet Med. 2008;10(3):173-180.**
   - Documented cardiac defect rates in DS: overall ~44%
   - Endocardial cushion defect (atrioventricular septal defect/AVSD) most common (40-45% of cardiac defects)
   - VSD: ~35%; ASD: ~8%; PDA: ~7%; TOF: ~4%

3. **Roizen NJ, Patterson D. "Down's syndrome." Lancet. 2003;361(9365):1281-1289.**
   - Comprehensive review establishing the modern clinical profile
   - Documented Brushfield spots (speckled iris) prevalence ~38-60%
   - Single palmar crease (simian crease) in ~45%

### Prader-Willi Syndrome
4. **Carrel AL, Myers SE, Whitman BY, Allen DB. "Benefits of long-term GH therapy in Prader-Willi syndrome: a 4-year study." J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2002;87(4):1581-1585.**
   - Demonstrated GH therapy improves body composition, linear growth, muscle mass, and physical function
   - Led to FDA approval of GH for PWS (2000)

5. **Cassidy SB, Schwartz S, Miller JL, Driscoll DJ. "Prader-Willi syndrome." Genet Med. 2012;14(1):10-26.**
   - Definitive clinical review
   - Chromosome 15q11.2-q13 paternal deletion (65-75%) or maternal uniparental disomy (20-30%)
   - Diagnostic testing: DNA methylation analysis (gold standard, detects >99%)
   - Neonatal hypotonia with poor feeding → childhood hyperphagia and obesity

### Rett Syndrome
6. **Amir RE, Van den Veyver IB, Wan M, et al. "Rett syndrome is caused by mutations in X-linked MECP2, encoding methyl-CpG-binding protein 2." Nat Genet. 1999;23(2):185-188.**
   - Identification of MECP2 gene mutations as the cause of Rett syndrome
   - Landmark in understanding X-linked dominant conditions

7. **Neul JL, Kaufmann WE, Glaze DG, et al. "Rett syndrome: revised diagnostic criteria and nomenclature." Ann Neurol. 2010;68(6):944-950.**
   - Updated diagnostic criteria for typical and atypical Rett
   - Regression in language/hand skills after 6-18 months of normal development
   - Hand stereotypies (wringing, washing, squeezing) are hallmark

### Fragile X Syndrome
8. **Hagerman RJ, Berry-Kravis E, Hazlett HC, et al. "Fragile X syndrome." Nat Rev Dis Primers. 2017;3:17065.**
   - CGG trinucleotide repeat expansion on FMR1 gene (Xq27.3)
   - Normal: 5-44 repeats; Premutation: 55-200; Full mutation: >200
   - Macro-orchidism develops in puberty (~80% of post-pubertal males)
   - Most common inherited cause of intellectual disability
   - Second most common genetic cause of ID after Down syndrome

### Williams Syndrome
9. **Morris CA. "Williams Syndrome." In: Adam MP, et al., eds. GeneReviews. 1999 [Updated 2023].**
   - 7q11.23 microdeletion including elastin gene (ELN)
   - "Cocktail party personality": overfriendly, excessive sociability, empathy
   - Supravalvular aortic stenosis (SVAS): 75%
   - Peripheral pulmonary stenosis common in infancy
   - Infantile hypercalcemia (15-50%)
   - Elfin facies, stellate iris pattern

### Angelman Syndrome
10. **Williams CA, Driscoll DJ, Dagli AI. "Clinical and genetic aspects of Angelman syndrome." Genet Med. 2010;12(7):385-395.**
    - Chromosome 15q11.2-q13 maternal deletion (68-75%)
    - "Happy puppet" syndrome: frequent laughter, hand flapping
    - Severe seizures (80-90%), typically onset 1-3 years
    - Near absence of speech; ataxic gait
    - EEG pattern: high-amplitude 2-3 Hz delta activity

## EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 700 live births (most common chromosomal condition) |
| Maternal age association | Risk increases exponentially: ~1/1,500 at age 20; ~1/350 at age 35; ~1/100 at age 40; ~1/25 at age 45 |
| CHD prevalence | 40-50% (AVSD most common) |
| Atlantoaxial instability | 10-30% radiographic; 1-2% symptomatic |
| Hypothyroidism | 15-20% |
| Hearing loss | 75% (conductive, sensorineural, or mixed) |
| Alzheimer disease | 50-70% by age 60 |
| Leukemia risk | 10-30x increased (ALL and AML); TMD in 10% of neonates |
| Celiac disease | 5-16% |
| Obstructive sleep apnea | 50-75% |
| Life expectancy | ~60 years (improved from 25 years in 1983) |
| Duodenal atresia | ~5% (30% of duodenal atresia cases have DS) |
| Hirschsprung disease | 2-15x increased risk |

### Trisomy 18 (Edwards Syndrome)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 5,000-8,000 live births |
| Survival | 50% die within first week; 90% die by age 1 year; 5-10% survive beyond 1 year |
| Female predominance | 3:1 (F:M) among live births |
| Cardiac defects | >90% (VSD, PDA most common) |
| Key features | Rocker-bottom feet, overlapping fingers (index over 3rd, 5th over 4th), clenched fists, micrognathia, prominent occiput |

### Trisomy 13 (Patau Syndrome)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 10,000-16,000 live births |
| Survival | Median survival 7-10 days; 90% die within first year |
| Cardiac defects | 80% |
| Key features | Holoprosencephaly (midline brain defects), polydactyly (post-axial), cutis aplasia (scalp defects), microphthalmia/cyclopia, cleft lip/palate |

### Turner Syndrome (45,X)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 2,000-2,500 live female births |
| Spontaneous abortion | 99% of 45,X conceptions miscarry |
| Cardiac defects | 25-45% (bicuspid aortic valve most common ~15-30%; coarctation of aorta ~7-18%) |
| Short stature | Near universal; mean adult height 147 cm (4'10") without treatment |
| Ovarian failure | 90%+ (streak ovaries; primary amenorrhea) |
| Key features | Webbed neck (pterygium colli), shield chest (broad chest with widely spaced nipples), lymphedema of hands/feet in neonates |

### Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 500-1,000 live male births |
| Typical presentation | Tall stature, long limbs, gynecomastia (40-75%), small firm testes, azoospermia |
| IQ | Average IQ 85-90; verbal > performance discrepancy |
| Testosterone | Low; replacement often needed in adolescence |
| Diagnosis | Often delayed until puberty or adulthood; 75% remain undiagnosed |

### Prader-Willi Syndrome
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 10,000-30,000 live births |
| Genetic mechanism | Paternal 15q11-q13 deletion (65-75%); maternal UPD (20-30%); imprinting defect (1-3%) |
| Obesity | Nearly universal without intervention; onset typically ages 2-8 |
| GH deficiency | 40-100% depending on criteria |

### Angelman Syndrome
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 12,000-20,000 live births |
| Genetic mechanism | Maternal 15q11-q13 deletion (68-75%); paternal UPD (3-7%); UBE3A mutation (11%); imprinting defect (3%) |
| Seizures | 80-90% |

### Rett Syndrome
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 10,000-15,000 live female births |
| Gene | MECP2 (Xq28) — de novo mutation in >99% |
| Almost exclusively female | Males with MECP2 mutation typically die in infancy (severe neonatal encephalopathy) |
| Regression onset | 6-18 months after period of normal development |

### Fragile X Syndrome
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | ~1 in 4,000 males; ~1 in 8,000 females |
| Full mutation | >200 CGG repeats on FMR1 gene |
| Intellectual disability | Moderate in males; ~50% of females have some cognitive impairment |
| Premutation carriers | 1 in 250-800 females; 1 in 250-810 males |
| FXTAS | Fragile X-associated tremor/ataxia syndrome in premutation carriers (typically >50 years) |

### Williams Syndrome
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | 1 in 7,500-10,000 live births |
| SVAS | 75% |
| Mean IQ | 50-60 |

### Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD)
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Prevalence | Estimated 2-5% of school-age children in the US and Western Europe |
| FAS (full syndrome) | 0.5-3 per 1,000 live births |
| Leading preventable cause of intellectual disability | — |
| Facial features | Smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, short palpebral fissures |
| CNS | Microcephaly, intellectual disability (mean IQ 70), behavioral problems |
| Cardiac | VSD, ASD |
| No safe amount of alcohol in pregnancy | Per AAP, ACOG, Surgeon General |

### TORCH Infections
| Infection | Key Features |
|---|---|
| **T**oxoplasmosis | Intracranial calcifications (scattered/diffuse), hydrocephalus, chorioretinitis. Classic triad: chorioretinitis + hydrocephalus + intracranial calcifications |
| **O**ther (Syphilis) | Hutchinson teeth, saddle nose, saber shins, snuffles, interstitial keratitis |
| **R**ubella | Sensorineural hearing loss (#1 feature), cataracts, PDA (classic cardiac lesion), "blueberry muffin" rash |
| **C**MV | Most common congenital infection; periventricular calcifications, sensorineural hearing loss (progressive), hepatosplenomegaly, petechiae. Leading non-genetic cause of sensorineural hearing loss |
| **H**erpes (HSV) | Skin vesicles, encephalitis, keratoconjunctivitis; acquired during delivery |
| Zika (newer addition) | Microcephaly (severe), intracranial calcifications (cortical/subcortical junction), arthrogryposis, macular scarring |

### VACTERL Association
| Component | Feature |
|---|---|
| **V** | Vertebral anomalies (hemivertebrae, butterfly vertebrae) |
| **A** | Anal atresia (imperforate anus) |
| **C** | Cardiac defects (VSD most common) |
| **T** | Tracheoesophageal fistula (TE fistula with esophageal atresia) |
| **E** | Esophageal atresia |
| **R** | Renal anomalies (renal agenesis, horseshoe kidney) |
| **L** | Limb anomalies (radial ray defects, thumb anomalies) |
- Diagnosis: 3 or more features required
- Non-random association, not a syndrome (no single unifying cause identified)
- Incidence: ~1 in 10,000-40,000 live births

## CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS

### Atlantoaxial Instability (AAI) Measurement
- **Atlantodental interval (ADI)**: Distance between posterior aspect of anterior arch of C1 and anterior aspect of odontoid process of C2
  - Normal in children: <4.5 mm
  - Abnormal/unstable: >4.5 mm (some sources use >5 mm)
  - Measured on lateral flexion/extension cervical radiographs
  - Symptomatic AAI: myelopathic signs (hyperreflexia, clonus, extensor plantar responses, bowel/bladder dysfunction, neck pain, torticollis)

### Denver Developmental Screening Test (Denver II)
- Screens 4 domains: gross motor, fine motor-adaptive, language, personal-social
- Ages birth to 6 years
- Used in DS and other genetic syndromes to track developmental milestones
- DS-specific milestone ranges established by Palpalardo et al.

### Ages and Stages Questionnaire (ASQ-3)
- Parent-completed developmental screening tool
- Widely used in early intervention referral decisions

### Intellectual Disability Classification (DSM-5 / AAIDD)
| Level | IQ Range | Support Level |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 50-70 | Intermittent |
| Moderate | 35-50 | Limited |
| Severe | 20-35 | Extensive |
| Profound | <20 | Pervasive |
- DS average IQ: 40-55 (moderate range)
- DSM-5 emphasizes adaptive functioning over IQ scores

### Genetic Testing Hierarchy
| Test | Resolution | Detects | Diagnostic Yield |
|---|---|---|---|
| Karyotype | 5-10 Mb | Aneuploidies, large structural changes, balanced translocations | 3% for unexplained DD/ID |
| FISH | Targeted | Known microdeletion/duplication syndromes | Targeted only |
| Chromosomal Microarray (CMA) | 50-100 kb | CNVs (deletions/duplications) | 15-20% for unexplained DD/ID |
| Whole Exome Sequencing (WES) | Single nucleotide | Point mutations in coding regions (~1.5% of genome) | 25-40% |
| Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS) | Single nucleotide + structural | All variants including non-coding | 30-50% |
| DNA Methylation Analysis | Epigenetic | Imprinting disorders (PWS, AS) | Gold standard for PWS/AS |
| Trinucleotide repeat analysis | Repeat expansion | Fragile X, myotonic dystrophy, Huntington | Targeted |

## BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD PEARLS

### Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
1. **Most common chromosomal cause of intellectual disability**: 1 in 700 live births
2. **Endocardial cushion defect (AVSD)** is the most common cardiac defect (40-45% of CHD in DS) — BOARD CLASSIC
3. **Atlantoaxial instability**: 10-30% radiographic, 1-2% symptomatic; ADI >4.5 mm abnormal; screening radiographs at age 3-5
4. **Special Olympics**: Lateral cervical spine films required before high-risk sports participation
5. **Single palmar crease** (simian crease): ~45%
6. **Brushfield spots**: White/light-colored speckles on iris periphery, seen in ~38-60%
7. **Hypotonia**: Present in nearly 100% of newborns; central hypotonia
8. **Duodenal atresia**: "Double bubble sign" on abdominal X-ray; ~30% of duodenal atresia cases have DS
9. **Leukemia risk**: 10-30x increased; both ALL and AMKL (acute megakaryoblastic leukemia); transient myeloproliferative disorder (TMD) in ~10% of neonates
10. **Hypothyroidism**: Screen TSH annually
11. **Alzheimer disease**: Amyloid precursor protein gene on chromosome 21; clinical dementia by age 55-60 in 50-70%
12. **Hearing loss**: 75% cumulative incidence; conductive > sensorineural
13. **OSA**: 50-75%; polysomnography by age 4
14. **Cervical spine**: Hypoplastic posterior arch of C1 and os odontoideum also contribute to instability

### Trisomy 18 (Edwards Syndrome)
15. **Overlapping fingers**: Index finger overlaps 3rd, 5th overlaps 4th — PATHOGNOMONIC
16. **Rocker-bottom feet**: Convex sole with prominent calcaneus
17. **Clenched fists**: Persistent
18. **Prominent occiput**, micrognathia, low-set ears
19. **>90% die within first year**; cardiac defects in >90%
20. **Female predominance**: 3:1 among live births

### Trisomy 13 (Patau Syndrome)
21. **Holoprosencephaly**: Failure of prosencephalon to divide; spectrum from alobar to lobar
22. **Postaxial polydactyly**: Extra digit on ulnar/fibular side
23. **Cutis aplasia**: Scalp defect (midline)
24. **Microphthalmia/cyclopia** in severe cases
25. **Median survival 7-10 days**

### Turner Syndrome (45,X)
26. **Webbed neck** (pterygium colli) — CLASSIC
27. **Coarctation of aorta** — classic cardiac association (7-18%); bicuspid aortic valve most common cardiac defect (15-30%)
28. **Shield chest**: Broad chest with widely spaced, inverted nipples
29. **Short stature**: Respond to GH therapy; SHOX gene haploinsufficiency
30. **Streak ovaries**: Primary amenorrhea, infertility; estrogen replacement at puberty
31. **Neonatal lymphedema**: Puffy hands and feet at birth
32. **Normal intelligence** but nonverbal learning disability common; visuospatial deficits
33. **Horseshoe kidney**: ~30%
34. **Cystic hygroma**: Detected prenatally on ultrasound

### Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)
35. **Tall stature** with long limbs (eunuchoid body habitus)
36. **Gynecomastia**: 40-75%; increased breast cancer risk (20x male general population)
37. **Small firm testes**, azoospermia, infertility
38. **Verbal IQ typically lower** than performance IQ (opposite of Turner)
39. **Often undiagnosed until puberty** or infertility workup
40. **Testosterone replacement** starting at puberty

### Prader-Willi Syndrome
41. **Neonatal hypotonia** with poor feeding → childhood **hyperphagia** and **obesity** (two-phase pattern)
42. **Paternal deletion 15q11-q13** (65-75%); compare with maternal deletion = Angelman
43. **PWS and Angelman**: Same chromosomal region, different parent of origin = GENOMIC IMPRINTING
44. **GH therapy**: FDA-approved; improves height, body composition, muscle mass
45. **Hypogonadism**: Cryptorchidism in males, delayed puberty
46. **Small hands and feet**: Acromicria
47. **Behavioral**: Skin picking, temper tantrums, perseveration
48. **DNA methylation analysis**: Gold standard diagnostic test (detects >99%)
49. **Hypothalamic dysfunction**: Drives hyperphagia, temperature instability, high pain threshold

### Angelman Syndrome
50. **"Happy puppet" syndrome**: Frequent laughing, hand flapping, ataxic gait
51. **Maternal deletion 15q11-q13** (compare paternal = PWS)
52. **UBE3A gene**: Key gene; maternally expressed (imprinted)
53. **Severe intellectual disability**: Near absence of speech
54. **Seizures**: 80-90%; characteristic EEG with high-amplitude 2-3 Hz delta activity
55. **Fascination with water**: Classic behavioral feature

### Rett Syndrome
56. **MECP2 gene mutation** (Xq28); X-linked dominant; almost exclusively females
57. **Normal development to 6-18 months** then regression
58. **Hand stereotypies**: Wringing, washing, squeezing — HALLMARK; loss of purposeful hand use
59. **Four stages**: Stage I (stagnation 6-18mo), Stage II (regression 1-4yr), Stage III (plateau/pseudostationary), Stage IV (late motor deterioration)
60. **Breathing irregularities**: Hyperventilation while awake, breath holding, air swallowing
61. **Seizures**: 60-80%
62. **Scoliosis**: Progressive; common indication for surgical intervention
63. **Acquired microcephaly**: Head circumference decelerates after birth (normal at birth)

### Williams Syndrome
64. **"Cocktail party personality"**: Overly friendly, excessive sociability, strong verbal skills relative to visuospatial deficits
65. **Supravalvular aortic stenosis (SVAS)**: 75% — CLASSIC association
66. **7q11.23 microdeletion** including elastin (ELN) gene
67. **Infantile hypercalcemia**: 15-50%
68. **Elfin facies**: Broad forehead, periorbital fullness, stellate iris, long philtrum, full lips
69. **Peripheral pulmonary stenosis**: Common in infancy; may resolve
70. **Cognitive**: Mean IQ 50-60; strong verbal/auditory skills, poor visuospatial; musical ability

### Fragile X Syndrome
71. **CGG trinucleotide repeat expansion** on FMR1 gene (Xq27.3)
72. **Repeat thresholds**: Normal <45; intermediate 45-54; premutation 55-200; full mutation >200
73. **Macro-orchidism**: Post-pubertal, 80% of affected males — CLASSIC
74. **Large ears, long face, prominent jaw**: Post-pubertal facial features
75. **Most common inherited cause of intellectual disability** (DS is most common overall genetic cause)
76. **Anticipation**: Premutation expands in subsequent generations (maternal transmission)
77. **FXTAS**: Fragile X-associated tremor/ataxia syndrome in premutation carrier males >50 years
78. **FXPOI**: Fragile X-associated primary ovarian insufficiency in premutation carrier females
79. **Behavioral**: ADHD, anxiety, autism features (~30%), hand flapping, gaze aversion
80. **Mitral valve prolapse**: ~50%

### TORCH Infections — Board Pearls
81. **CMV**: Most common congenital infection; periventricular calcifications (vs. scattered in toxo)
82. **Toxoplasmosis**: Diffuse/scattered intracranial calcifications; classic triad = chorioretinitis + hydrocephalus + calcifications
83. **Rubella**: PDA is classic cardiac lesion; sensorineural hearing loss is #1 feature; "blueberry muffin" rash
84. **Syphilis**: Hutchinson teeth, interstitial keratitis, CN VIII deafness = Hutchinson triad
85. **CMV vs. Toxo calcifications**: CMV = periventricular; Toxo = scattered/diffuse — CLASSIC BOARD DISTINCTION

### VACTERL
86. **Three or more features** required for diagnosis
87. **Not a syndrome** (no single genetic cause); association
88. **Radial ray defects**: Absent/hypoplastic thumb or radius
89. **Most common cardiac defect**: VSD

### Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders
90. **Leading preventable cause of intellectual disability**
91. **Facial features**: Smooth philtrum, thin upper lip (vermillion border), short palpebral fissures — require all 3 for FAS diagnosis
92. **No safe amount of alcohol in pregnancy**
93. **CNS dysfunction**: Microcephaly, cognitive deficits, behavioral problems (ADHD-like)
94. **Growth deficiency**: Prenatal and/or postnatal growth restriction
95. **Cardiac defects**: VSD, ASD
