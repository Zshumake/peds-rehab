## [COMPREHENSIVE] Introduction

Brachial plexus birth injury (BPBI) is one of the most consequential complications of the birth process, resulting in partial or complete paralysis of the affected upper extremity. Despite advances in obstetric care, brachial plexus birth injury has not been eliminated, and its management remains a complex interdisciplinary endeavor involving neonatology, pediatric neurology, hand surgery, orthopedic surgery, rehabilitation medicine, and therapy services.

The spectrum of outcomes in brachial plexus birth injury is remarkably wide. The majority of infants with upper plexus injuries achieve satisfactory spontaneous recovery through conservative management alone, while a subset with severe injuries (particularly total plexus palsy with Horner syndrome) will require microsurgical intervention and may still be left with significant long-term functional limitations. Between these extremes lies a challenging clinical population requiring careful serial assessment to determine the trajectory of recovery and the optimal timing of intervention.

For the rehabilitation physician, brachial plexus birth injury demands competency in several domains: understanding the anatomy and pathophysiology of nerve injury to localize lesions and predict recovery, applying standardized assessment tools to track recovery and guide surgical decision-making, prescribing and supervising therapy programs that prevent contractures and optimize functional development, recognizing secondary musculoskeletal complications (particularly glenohumeral dysplasia), and counseling families through what is often a prolonged and uncertain recovery process.

This chapter provides a comprehensive review of brachial plexus birth injury from anatomy through long-term management, emphasizing the clinical assessment tools, surgical decision-making frameworks, and rehabilitation principles most relevant to board certification and clinical practice.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Brachial Plexus Anatomy

### Structural Organization

The brachial plexus is formed by the ventral rami of spinal nerve roots C5 through T1. Understanding its layered anatomical organization from roots to terminal branches is essential for localizing the level of injury and predicting the resulting functional deficits.

**Roots** (C5, C6, C7, C8, T1) emerge from the intervertebral foramina and converge to form three **trunks**: the upper trunk (C5 + C6), the middle trunk (C7 alone), and the lower trunk (C8 + T1). Each trunk divides into anterior and posterior **divisions**, producing six divisions total. These divisions recombine to form three **cords**, named according to their anatomic relationship to the axillary artery: the lateral cord (anterior divisions of the upper and middle trunks), the posterior cord (all three posterior divisions), and the medial cord (anterior division of the lower trunk). The cords give rise to the five major **terminal branches**: the musculocutaneous, axillary, radial, median, and ulnar nerves.

**Table 5.1: Brachial Plexus Organization**

| Level | Components | Formation |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Roots | C5, C6, C7, C8, T1 | Ventral rami of spinal nerves |
| Trunks | Upper (C5-C6), Middle (C7), Lower (C8-T1) | Root convergence |
| Divisions | 6 total (anterior and posterior from each trunk) | Trunk bifurcation |
| Cords | Lateral, Posterior, Medial | Division recombination |
| Terminal branches | Musculocutaneous, Axillary, Radial, Median, Ulnar | Cord termination |

### Critical Branches from Roots and Trunks

Several branches arise proximal to the cords and are clinically significant for localizing injury:

- **Dorsal scapular nerve** (C5 root): Innervates the rhomboid muscles. Weakness suggests C5 root involvement proximal to the trunk.
- **Long thoracic nerve** (C5, C6, C7 roots): Innervates the serratus anterior. Injury produces scapular winging.
- **Suprascapular nerve** (upper trunk): Innervates the supraspinatus and infraspinatus (shoulder abduction and external rotation). Frequently involved in Erb palsy.
- **Phrenic nerve** (C3, C4, C5 roots): Innervates the diaphragm. Ipsilateral hemidiaphragm paralysis occurs in approximately 5% of brachial plexus birth injuries and is more common with total palsy.

### Cord-to-Terminal Branch Relationships

- **Lateral cord** gives rise to the musculocutaneous nerve (elbow flexion via biceps and brachialis) and contributes the lateral head of the median nerve.
- **Posterior cord** gives rise to the axillary nerve (deltoid, teres minor) and the radial nerve (triceps, wrist/finger extensors).
- **Medial cord** gives rise to the ulnar nerve, contributes the medial head of the median nerve, and gives off the medial pectoral nerve and medial cutaneous nerves of the arm and forearm.

### Functional Myotome Mapping

Understanding the myotomal innervation of the upper extremity muscles is essential for clinical localization:

**Table 5.1b: Key Myotomes and Functional Correlates**

| Root Level | Key Muscles | Primary Function | Clinical Test |
|------------|------------|-----------------|---------------|
| C5 | Deltoid, biceps, supraspinatus, infraspinatus | Shoulder abduction, external rotation, elbow flexion | Arm abduction against gravity |
| C6 | Biceps, brachioradialis, wrist extensors | Elbow flexion, forearm supination, wrist extension | Elbow flexion against gravity |
| C7 | Triceps, wrist flexors, finger extensors | Elbow extension, wrist flexion, finger extension | Finger extension |
| C8 | FDP, FDS, finger flexors | Finger flexion, grip strength | Grip strength |
| T1 | Intrinsic hand muscles | Fine motor, finger abduction/adduction | Finger spread |

## [BOARD] Nerve Injury Classification

**Table 5.2: Seddon and Sunderland Nerve Injury Classification**

| Seddon | Sunderland | Pathology | Prognosis |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Neurapraxia | Grade I | Focal demyelination; axon intact | Complete recovery, weeks to months |
| Axonotmesis | Grade II | Axon disrupted, endoneurium intact | Good recovery (axon regrows ~1 mm/day) |
| Axonotmesis | Grade III | Axon + endoneurium disrupted, perineurium intact | Partial recovery, aberrant reinnervation |
| Axonotmesis | Grade IV | Neuroma-in-continuity (epineurium intact) | No spontaneous recovery; surgery needed |
| Neurotmesis | Grade V | Complete nerve transection | No spontaneous recovery; surgery required |
| -- | Grade VI (Mackinnon) | Mixed injury pattern along nerve | Variable prognosis |

In brachial plexus birth injuries, the injury spectrum ranges from stretch-type neurapraxia (most common, best prognosis) through rupture (axon disruption with variable recovery) to root avulsion (root torn from the spinal cord, worst prognosis with no possibility of spontaneous recovery).

> **Clinical Pearl**: Root avulsion (preganglionic injury) can be distinguished from rupture by specific clinical and electrodiagnostic findings. The sensory nerve action potential (SNAP) is preserved despite clinical sensory loss because the dorsal root ganglion remains connected to the peripheral nerve. Additional signs suggesting avulsion include Horner syndrome (T1), elevated hemidiaphragm (phrenic nerve, C3-C5), scapular winging (long thoracic nerve, C5-C7), and rhomboid weakness (dorsal scapular nerve, C5).

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Pathophysiology of Birth Injury

The mechanism of brachial plexus birth injury involves lateral traction on the neck and shoulder during delivery, separating the head from the shoulder. In vertex (head-first) deliveries, lateral traction on the head away from the ipsilateral shoulder stretches the upper plexus (C5-C6), producing the classic Erb-Duchenne pattern. In breech deliveries, lateral traction on the trunk and arms preferentially stretches the lower plexus (C8-T1), although upper plexus injuries still predominate overall.

The severity of stretch determines whether the injury results in neurapraxia (focal demyelination with full recovery expected), axonotmesis (axonal disruption with variable recovery depending on the integrity of surrounding connective tissue structures), or root avulsion (preganglionic separation from the spinal cord with no potential for spontaneous recovery).

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Classification of Brachial Plexus Birth Injuries

### Erb-Duchenne Palsy (Upper Plexus, C5-C6)

Erb-Duchenne palsy is the most common type of brachial plexus birth injury, accounting for approximately 70-80% of all cases. The C5 and C6 roots are affected, sometimes extending to include C7 (termed "extended Erb palsy").

The muscles affected include the deltoid (C5-C6, axillary nerve), supraspinatus and infraspinatus (C5-C6, suprascapular nerve), biceps (C5-C6, musculocutaneous nerve), brachialis (C5-C6), brachioradialis (C5-C6, radial nerve), supinator, and external rotators of the shoulder.

The resulting classic posture is the "waiter's tip" position: the arm is adducted and internally rotated at the shoulder, the elbow is extended, the forearm is pronated, and the wrist is flexed. The movements lost include shoulder abduction, external rotation, elbow flexion, and forearm supination. Critically, hand grip is preserved because C8-T1 function remains intact.

The prognosis for Erb-Duchenne palsy is the best among all brachial plexus birth injury patterns, with 70-92% showing spontaneous recovery depending on severity criteria and how "recovery" is defined. Most recovery occurs within the first 3-6 months.

### Klumpke Palsy (Lower Plexus, C8-T1)

Isolated Klumpke palsy involving only C8-T1 is very rare as a birth injury, accounting for less than 1-2% of cases. Some experts question whether true isolated lower plexus birth injury exists in neonates at all, though it is well described in adult traumatic plexus injuries.

The affected muscles include the intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, lumbricals, thenar eminence, hypothenar eminence), wrist flexors, and finger flexors (particularly the flexor digitorum profundus to the ring and small fingers). The clinical presentation features a claw hand deformity (metacarpophalangeal hyperextension with interphalangeal flexion), weak grip, and sensory loss along the medial arm, forearm, and hand.

Horner syndrome (ipsilateral miosis, ptosis, and anhidrosis) indicates T1 root avulsion, as the preganglionic sympathetic fibers from T1 that travel to the superior cervical ganglion are disrupted. The prognosis is generally poorer than for upper plexus injuries due to the avulsion mechanism.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Horner syndrome in the setting of brachial plexus birth injury indicates T1 root involvement and carries a poor prognosis. It is one of the most important prognostic indicators and should be specifically sought on examination of every infant with brachial plexus birth injury.

### Total (Complete) Plexus Palsy (C5-T1)

Total plexus palsy accounts for approximately 20-30% of brachial plexus birth injuries and involves all roots from C5 through T1. The affected infant presents with a flail arm showing complete paralysis of the entire upper extremity, absent Moro reflex on the affected side, no grip, and no voluntary arm movement.

Horner syndrome is common and indicates a poor prognosis. Phrenic nerve palsy (causing an elevated hemidiaphragm and potential respiratory distress) is associated with total palsy. The prognosis is the worst of all injury patterns, with spontaneous recovery less likely, less complete, and most likely to require surgical intervention.

### Narakas Classification

The Narakas classification is the most widely used system for stratifying brachial plexus birth injury severity and is an important board-tested framework.

**Table 5.3: Narakas Classification of Brachial Plexus Birth Injuries**

| Group | Roots | Description | Spontaneous Recovery |
|-------|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| I | C5-C6 | Upper Erb palsy | ~90% |
| II | C5-C7 | Extended Erb palsy (upper + middle trunk) | ~65% |
| III | C5-T1 | Total palsy without Horner syndrome | ~50% |
| IV | C5-T1 | Total palsy with Horner syndrome | <5% |

Narakas Group IV (total palsy with Horner syndrome) essentially mandates surgical exploration, as no meaningful spontaneous recovery is expected. This classification was originally described by Narakas in 1987 and remains central to clinical decision-making.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The Narakas classification provides a practical framework linking injury pattern to prognosis and surgical decision-making. Group I injuries almost always recover spontaneously. Group IV injuries almost never recover spontaneously and require early surgical exploration. Groups II and III fall in the intermediate range, requiring careful serial assessment to guide management.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Epidemiology and Risk Factors

### Incidence

The overall incidence of brachial plexus birth injury ranges from 0.4 to 4.6 per 1,000 live births depending on the population studied, with recent United States estimates of approximately 0.4-1.5 per 1,000 live births. The incidence has been declining due to improved obstetric practices and increased cesarean section rates for anticipated difficult deliveries. However, brachial plexus birth injury has not been eliminated and still occurs even in cesarean deliveries (approximately 1-4% of cases), indicating that intrauterine positioning and forces may contribute to the injury mechanism.

### Risk Factors

**Table 5.4: Risk Factors for Brachial Plexus Birth Injury**

| Category | Risk Factor | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Maternal/Fetal | Shoulder dystocia | Strongest risk factor (~100x increased risk); present in ~50% of cases |
| | Macrosomia (>4,000 g) | OR 4-14x; particularly >4,500 g |
| | Maternal diabetes | Associated with macrosomia |
| | Maternal obesity | Increases delivery complication risk |
| | Multiparity | Increased risk |
| | Post-term pregnancy (>42 weeks) | Associated with macrosomia |
| | Previous child with BPBI | Recurrence risk ~5-15% |
| Delivery | Prolonged second stage | Increased traction forces |
| | Instrumental delivery | Forceps, vacuum extraction |
| | Breech presentation | Associated with lower plexus injuries |
| | Assisted traction | During delivery |

Two important board-tested caveats warrant emphasis. First, approximately 50% of brachial plexus birth injuries occur without documented shoulder dystocia, and some cases occur without any identifiable risk factors, which has important medicolegal implications. Second, bilateral brachial plexus birth injury is rare (2-4% of cases) and is associated with breech delivery and very large birth weight.

### Associated Injuries

The most common associated injury is ipsilateral clavicle fracture (10-15%), which may actually be somewhat protective by absorbing energy that would otherwise be transmitted to the plexus. Other associations include humeral fractures (proximal humerus or shaft), torticollis (from sternocleidomastoid injury or postural causes), phrenic nerve palsy (approximately 5%, more common with total palsy), facial nerve palsy (from forceps or birth trauma), and rarely, cervical spinal cord injury.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Assessment Scales and Evaluation

### Active Movement Scale

The Active Movement Scale (AMS), developed by Curtis and colleagues at the Hospital for Sick Children in Toronto, is the most widely used standardized assessment tool for evaluating motor function in infants with brachial plexus birth injury. Each of 15 muscle groups is graded on a 0-7 scale based on observation of spontaneous and stimulated movements.

**Table 5.5: Active Movement Scale Scoring**

| Score | Description | Position |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| 0 | No contraction | Gravity eliminated |
| 1 | Contraction, no movement | Gravity eliminated |
| 2 | Movement <=1/2 range | Gravity eliminated |
| 3 | Movement >1/2 range | Gravity eliminated |
| 4 | Full range movement | Gravity eliminated |
| 5 | Movement <=1/2 range | Against gravity |
| 6 | Movement >1/2 range | Against gravity |
| 7 | Full movement | Against gravity |

The 15 movements assessed include shoulder flexion, abduction, adduction, internal rotation, and external rotation; elbow flexion and extension; forearm pronation and supination; wrist flexion and extension; and finger flexion, extension, thumb flexion, and thumb extension. Serial AMS assessments are used to track recovery over time and serve as a key input for surgical decision-making.

### Toronto Test Score

The Toronto Test Score, developed by Michelow and colleagues at the Hospital for Sick Children, is an early predictor of recovery used to identify infants who may benefit from microsurgical intervention. It is calculated as the sum of AMS scores for five specific movements assessed at 3 months of age: elbow flexion, elbow extension, wrist extension, finger extension, and thumb extension.

A total score above 3.5 at 3 months predicts good spontaneous recovery, supporting continued conservative management. A total score of 3.5 or below predicts poor spontaneous recovery and identifies candidates for surgical exploration.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The Toronto Test at 3 months is the most widely used screening tool for identifying candidates for microsurgical reconstruction. It established the 3-month evaluation as a critical decision-making time point and remains central to the clinical assessment algorithm for brachial plexus birth injury.

### Cookie Test

The Cookie Test (also known as the Toronto Cookie Test) is a simple, practical, and functionally meaningful assessment that evaluates the ability to bring the hand to the mouth, testing combined elbow flexion and forearm supination (C5-C6 function). The child is offered a cookie or attractive object, and the ability to bring it to the mouth is observed.

Failure to achieve the Cookie Test (inability to bring the hand to the mouth with active elbow flexion and supination) by 6-9 months of age is considered an indication for microsurgical reconstruction by many surgeons. The test directly assesses the most functionally important movement in upper Erb palsy and is widely referenced in the brachial plexus birth injury literature.

### Towel Test

The Towel Test assesses the ability to remove a towel placed over the face, testing shoulder and elbow function. Inability to remove the towel from the face at 6 months of age is an additional indicator of poor recovery.

### Mallet Classification

The Mallet Classification is a grading system for global shoulder function in children with brachial plexus birth injury, typically assessed after age 3-4 years. Five shoulder movements are graded on a scale of I (no function) to V (normal function): global abduction, global external rotation, hand to neck, hand to back, and hand to mouth. The Mallet system is widely used for evaluating surgical outcomes and long-term follow-up.

### Additional Assessment Tools

- **Gilbert and Raimondi Scale**: Hand function grading system (grades 0-V for grasp patterns).
- **Modified Gilbert Shoulder Scale**: Shoulder function grading for surgical outcomes.
- **Brachial Plexus Outcome Measure (BPOM)**: Activity-based functional assessment.
- **Pediatric Outcomes Data Collection Instrument (PODCI)**: Parent-reported outcome measure.

### Imaging

High-resolution MRI of the brachial plexus can visualize root avulsions, pseudomeningoceles, and neuromas, and is increasingly used for surgical planning. MRI neurography with 3-Tesla imaging provides excellent anatomical detail. CT myelography, the historical gold standard for root avulsion detection, has been largely replaced by MRI. Shoulder imaging (radiographs and MRI) is important for monitoring glenohumeral joint development, posterior subluxation or dislocation, and glenoid retroversion during long-term follow-up.

### Electrodiagnostic Studies

EMG and nerve conduction studies play an important supporting role in the evaluation of brachial plexus birth injury, though clinical assessment remains the primary basis for decision-making. Studies are typically performed at 3-4 weeks of age (after Wallerian degeneration has occurred) and repeated at 3-6 months. The initial study at 3-4 weeks establishes the extent and severity of the injury once sufficient time has elapsed for Wallerian degeneration to produce detectable electrophysiological changes.

Nerve conduction studies show reduced or absent compound muscle action potential (CMAP) amplitudes in affected nerve distributions. The severity of CMAP amplitude reduction correlates with the degree of axonal loss. Sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) provide crucial information for distinguishing preganglionic from postganglionic injuries.

EMG findings in the acute phase (after 2-3 weeks) include fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves, indicating active denervation. The distribution of denervation potentials helps map the extent of injury across root levels and individual muscles. On follow-up EMG at 3-6 months, the presence of nascent motor unit potentials (small, polyphasic units) indicates early reinnervation and is a favorable prognostic sign. Absence of reinnervation potentials at 3-6 months supports consideration of surgical intervention.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Preserved sensory nerve action potentials (SNAPs) in the setting of clinical sensory loss is a key electrodiagnostic finding suggesting root avulsion (preganglionic injury). In avulsion, the dorsal root ganglion remains connected to the peripheral nerve, so sensory conduction is preserved even though the sensory fibers are disconnected from the spinal cord. This finding has direct implications for surgical planning, as avulsed roots cannot be grafted and nerve transfers from extraplexal donors must be used instead.

### Practical Limitations of Electrodiagnostics in Infants

Several practical challenges limit the utility of electrodiagnostic studies in neonates and infants:

- Cooperation is limited; sedation may be required for reliable testing, which adds procedural complexity and risk.
- Normative values for nerve conduction studies in neonates differ significantly from adult values due to ongoing myelination; age-appropriate reference ranges must be used.
- EMG electrode placement in very small muscles requires technical expertise in pediatric electrodiagnosis.
- The clinical examination and serial AMS assessments remain more practical and reliable for guiding surgical decision-making in most cases.
- Electrodiagnostic studies are most valuable for detecting root avulsion (preserved SNAP with denervation) and for confirming the extent of injury when clinical findings are ambiguous.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Natural History and Recovery

### Spontaneous Recovery Patterns

The natural history of brachial plexus birth injury is characterized by a high overall rate of spontaneous recovery, but with marked variation based on injury severity.

**Table 5.6: Spontaneous Recovery by Injury Pattern**

| Injury Pattern | Spontaneous Recovery Rate | Timeline |
|---------------|--------------------------|----------|
| Upper plexus (C5-C6) | 80-92% | Most recovery within 3-6 months |
| Extended Erb (C5-C7) | ~65% | Most recovery within 3-6 months |
| Total palsy without Horner | ~50% | Early hand function return (2-3 months) is favorable |
| Total palsy with Horner | <5% | Essentially requires surgical intervention |

Most spontaneous recovery occurs in the first 3-6 months, with continued improvement possible up to 12-24 months. After 18-24 months, further spontaneous recovery is unlikely.

### Predictors of Poor Recovery

Predictors of poor recovery can be identified at sequential time points:

- **At birth**: Total palsy, Horner syndrome, phrenic nerve palsy.
- **At 1 month**: No biceps function, no finger extension.
- **At 3 months**: Toronto Test score of 3.5 or below, no biceps function (AMS below 6 for elbow flexion against gravity), no finger or wrist extension, failure of the Cookie Test.
- **At 6 months**: Absence of biceps function is virtually universally accepted as an indication for surgical exploration.
- **At 9 months**: Persistent functional deficits; some centers still consider primary nerve surgery up to this point.

### Long-Term Sequelae

Even with satisfactory nerve recovery, long-term musculoskeletal complications develop due to the effects of residual muscle imbalance on the growing skeleton:

- **Shoulder internal rotation contracture**: The most common and functionally significant long-term problem, caused by dominant internal rotator strength with weak external rotators.
- **Glenohumeral dysplasia**: Progressive posterior subluxation of the humeral head, glenoid retroversion, and glenoid biconcavity, driven by the muscle imbalance acting on the developing glenohumeral joint.
- **Muscle co-contractions**: Simultaneous activation of antagonist muscles (particularly shoulder internal and external rotators), resulting from aberrant reinnervation patterns.
- **Elbow flexion contracture**: Common with C5-C6 injuries.
- **Forearm supination deficit**: A common functional limitation affecting bimanual tasks.
- **Limb length discrepancy**: The affected arm may be shorter.
- **Psychosocial impact**: Body image concerns, participation limitations, and reduced quality of life, particularly in adolescence. Children and adolescents with visible upper extremity asymmetry may experience bullying, social withdrawal, and activity avoidance. Proactive psychosocial support and peer mentoring programs can mitigate these effects.

### Factors Influencing Long-Term Functional Outcome

The long-term functional outcome after brachial plexus birth injury depends on the interaction of multiple factors:

1. **Initial injury severity**: Narakas Group I injuries have fundamentally different long-term trajectories than Group IV injuries, even with optimal treatment.

2. **Quality and timing of nerve recovery**: Rapid, early recovery (within 2-3 months) produces better outcomes than delayed recovery, even when the same ultimate degree of muscle reinnervation is achieved. This likely reflects the importance of motor endplate viability and cortical motor representation.

3. **Aberrant reinnervation**: Misdirected axonal regeneration produces co-contractions (simultaneous activation of antagonist muscles), which are a major source of functional limitation. Co-contraction of shoulder internal and external rotators is particularly disabling and contributes to glenohumeral dysplasia.

4. **Skeletal growth effects**: The developing musculoskeletal system remodels in response to the mechanical forces acting upon it. Muscle imbalance during periods of rapid growth drives progressive skeletal deformity (glenohumeral dysplasia, radial head subluxation).

5. **Compliance with therapy**: Consistent daily ROM exercises and functional use of the affected limb significantly influence long-term outcomes.

6. **Surgical interventions**: Timely and appropriate primary nerve surgery and secondary reconstructive procedures can substantially improve functional outcomes when spontaneous recovery is inadequate.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Management

### Conservative Management

Initial management for all brachial plexus birth injuries is conservative. Gentle passive range-of-motion exercises begin within the first 1-2 weeks after birth (once initial edema resolves) and are taught to parents. Key movements to maintain include shoulder external rotation and abduction, elbow extension, forearm supination, and wrist and finger extension. These exercises are critical for preventing contractures that would compromise the functional benefit of any nerve recovery.

Positioning should avoid prolonged postures that reinforce contracture patterns. Splinting is generally not recommended in the neonatal period but may be introduced later for specific contracture prevention (for example, supination splints or wrist extension splints). Sensory stimulation of the affected limb is encouraged to maintain cortical representation.

Serial clinical assessments using the Active Movement Scale should be performed monthly during the first 3-6 months and then quarterly thereafter.

### Occupational and Physical Therapy Protocols

The therapy program for brachial plexus birth injury evolves through distinct phases:

**Neonatal Phase (0-2 weeks)**: Education and counseling for parents. Gentle handling instructions. Avoid excessive passive ranging in the acute period while edema and inflammation are resolving. Support the affected arm during feeding and positioning.

**Early Intervention Phase (2 weeks - 3 months)**: Structured passive range-of-motion program taught to parents, performed multiple times daily. Focus on shoulder external rotation, shoulder abduction, elbow extension, forearm supination, wrist extension, and finger extension. Sensory stimulation to the affected limb through touch, texture, and temperature variation to maintain cortical representation and prevent sensory neglect. Positioning strategies to avoid sustained internal rotation and adduction.

**Active Recovery Phase (3-12 months)**: Continue passive ROM. Introduce age-appropriate activities that encourage active use of the affected limb. Constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT) principles may be adapted for infants to promote use of the affected arm. Weight-bearing through the affected upper extremity during tummy time and supported sitting. Bimanual activities to encourage integration of the affected arm into functional tasks.

**Toddler and Preschool Phase (1-5 years)**: Transition from passive to active-assisted and active exercises. Goal-directed functional training. Strengthening of recovered muscles. Splinting and orthotic management as needed for residual contractures. Adaptation of play activities to promote upper extremity function. Monitoring for glenohumeral dysplasia and internal rotation contracture.

**School-Age Phase (>5 years)**: Sport and recreation adaptation. Continued contracture management. Psychosocial support for body image and peer relationships. Assessment for late reconstructive procedures if functional deficits persist.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Parent education and engagement are the single most important factors in the conservative management of brachial plexus birth injury. Parents perform the daily ROM exercises, monitor recovery, and reinforce functional use of the affected limb. Empowering parents with knowledge and technique through structured teaching sessions is essential to optimizing outcomes.

### The Microsurgical Timing Controversy

The optimal timing of primary nerve surgery for brachial plexus birth injury remains one of the most debated topics in the field. Three main approaches have been advocated, each with supporting evidence and limitations.

**Table 5.7: Microsurgical Timing Approaches**

| Approach | Timing | Proponents | Rationale | Indications | Criticism |
|----------|--------|------------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Early | 3 months | Gilbert, Tassin (France); European centers | Motor endplate viability declines with time; earlier reinnervation yields better outcomes | No biceps recovery, Toronto score <=3.5 at 3 months | May operate on some who would recover spontaneously |
| Intermediate | 6 months | Clarke, Curtis (Toronto); most North American centers | More observation time; Toronto test at 3 months screens, surgery at 6 months for failures | No antigravity elbow flexion, failed Cookie Test at 6 months | May miss optimal window for some |
| Late | 9 months | Selected centers | Maximize spontaneous recovery; reduce unnecessary surgery | No useful hand function, persistent total palsy | Poorer shoulder outcomes with later surgery |

Recent evidence trends toward earlier intervention, particularly for severe injuries (Narakas Groups III-IV). A key finding from surgical outcome studies is that patients operated on within 3 months required no secondary procedures, while all patients operated on after 1 year required secondary reconstruction.

**Current consensus (approximate)**: Most centers perform surgical exploration at 3-6 months for total palsy with Horner syndrome and at 6-9 months for upper plexus injuries failing to recover. No universal consensus exists, and practice varies by center and surgeon preference.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The trend in brachial plexus birth injury management is toward earlier surgical referral, particularly for severe injuries. Early referral at 3 months for evaluation (not necessarily surgery) allows timely assessment and surgical planning if the injury fails to recover. A child who has not recovered biceps function (antigravity elbow flexion) by 6 months of age should be considered a candidate for microsurgical intervention at most centers.

### Surgical Options

#### Primary Nerve Surgery

**Nerve grafting** using autologous sural nerve grafts is the most common primary reconstruction technique. Grafts are used to bridge gaps after resection of neuroma-in-continuity, placed as interposition grafts from root or trunk level to the target nerve.

**Nerve transfers (neurotization)** have become increasingly central to brachial plexus reconstruction:

- **Oberlin transfer**: Transfer of a fascicle from the ulnar nerve (typically the fascicle innervating the flexor carpi ulnaris) to the motor branch of the biceps (musculocutaneous nerve). This is the most well-known nerve transfer for brachial plexus reconstruction. The rationale is that a single expendable fascicle from a functioning nerve provides a short-distance, high-quality motor input directly to the target muscle. Originally described by Oberlin and colleagues in 1994.

- **Double Oberlin (Mackinnon modification)**: Combines the classic Oberlin transfer (ulnar fascicle to biceps motor branch) with a median nerve fascicle transfer to the brachialis branch, providing dual innervation for elbow flexion.

- **Spinal accessory nerve to suprascapular nerve transfer**: Restores shoulder abduction and external rotation by transferring the spinal accessory nerve (which innervates the trapezius) to the suprascapular nerve (which innervates the supraspinatus and infraspinatus).

- **Intercostal nerve transfers**: Historically used to restore elbow flexion when local donors are unavailable.

- **Contralateral C7 transfer**: Rarely used in brachial plexus birth injury, involves transferring the contralateral C7 root across to the injured side.

**Neurolysis** (freeing the nerve from surrounding scar tissue) has a limited role in brachial plexus birth injury management.

#### Surgical Decision-Making Algorithm

A practical algorithm for surgical decision-making integrates the assessment tools discussed above:

1. **At birth**: Document injury pattern (Narakas group), associated injuries, Horner syndrome status.
2. **At 1 month**: Begin serial AMS assessments. If Narakas Group IV (total palsy + Horner), begin surgical planning and MRI/electrodiagnostic evaluation.
3. **At 3 months**: Calculate Toronto Test Score. If score <=3.5, refer to brachial plexus surgery team for evaluation. If score >3.5, continue conservative management with close monitoring.
4. **At 6 months**: Assess Cookie Test and biceps function (AMS for elbow flexion). If no antigravity elbow flexion or failed Cookie Test, microsurgical intervention is indicated at most centers.
5. **At 9-12 months**: For patients with partial but incomplete recovery, reassess functional deficits and determine whether primary nerve surgery is still beneficial or whether secondary reconstructive procedures are more appropriate.
6. **After 12 months**: Primary nerve surgery is generally not beneficial. Focus shifts to secondary procedures (tendon transfers, osteotomies) for residual deficits.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The decision to proceed with microsurgery is not an emergency but should not be unduly delayed. Motor endplate viability declines over time, and delayed reinnervation produces poorer outcomes than timely intervention. The critical window for primary nerve surgery is generally considered to be before 9-12 months of age, with earlier surgery (3-6 months) yielding superior outcomes for severe injuries.

#### Secondary and Late Reconstruction

When primary nerve surgery does not achieve adequate function, or for residual deficits persisting after nerve recovery, secondary procedures are performed.

**Shoulder procedures**:
- Subscapularis slide or release (Carlioz procedure) for internal rotation contracture.
- Latissimus dorsi and teres major transfer to external rotators (L'Episcopo-type procedures).
- Botulinum toxin A injection into internal rotators (subscapularis, pectoralis major) to reduce internal rotation contracture and promote external rotation remodeling, often used as a bridge to surgery or adjunct to therapy.
- Humeral rotation osteotomy for fixed internal rotation deformity, typically performed after sufficient skeletal growth (age 4-5 years or older).

**Elbow procedures**:
- Steindler flexorplasty (transfer of the forearm flexor-pronator mass more proximally on the humerus to augment elbow flexion). This procedure is most effective when the forearm flexor-pronator muscles have adequate strength (MRC grade 4 or better) and the elbow has a passive range of motion allowing full flexion.
- Free functioning muscle transfer (FFMT) using the gracilis muscle as a free flap for elbow flexion when no local donors are available, primarily in total palsy. The gracilis is harvested with its neurovascular pedicle (obturator nerve, medial circumflex femoral artery) and transferred to the arm, with the nerve coaptation to an available motor nerve (intercostal, spinal accessory, or contralateral C7).
- Triceps-to-biceps transfer for elbow flexion when triceps function is strong but elbow flexion is absent.

**Forearm and hand procedures**:
- Pronator teres rerouting to function as a supinator for persistent supination deficit. The pronator teres is detached from its insertion, passed around the radius, and reinserted on the dorsal aspect, converting it from a pronator to a supinator.
- Wrist and hand tendon transfers for residual deficits, tailored to the specific pattern of weakness.
- Tenodesis procedures for wrist or finger positioning when active motor power is insufficient for functional tendon transfers.

### Postoperative Rehabilitation After Microsurgery

Postoperative rehabilitation following primary nerve surgery is a structured process that requires close communication between the surgical and rehabilitation teams:

- **Immobilization phase** (0-3 weeks): The arm is immobilized in a position that protects the nerve repair site. An arm sling or body wrap is typically used. No passive or active ranging of the operated extremity.
- **Gentle mobilization phase** (3-6 weeks): Gradual introduction of gentle passive ROM. Begin with gravity-eliminated positions. Avoid stretching across the repair site.
- **Active-assisted phase** (6-12 weeks): Begin active-assisted exercises as nerve regeneration permits. Sensory stimulation to the affected limb. Encourage developmental activities that promote cortical reorganization.
- **Active strengthening phase** (3-12 months post-surgery): As reinnervation occurs (typically beginning 3-6 months after surgery, progressing over 12-24 months), progressive active exercises and functional training are introduced. The rate of axonal regeneration is approximately 1 mm per day, so the timing of reinnervation depends on the distance from the repair site to the target muscle.
- **Functional integration phase** (12+ months): Focus on incorporating recovered function into bimanual activities, self-care tasks, and play. Address compensatory movement patterns that may have developed during the period of paralysis.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Shoulder Dysplasia

### Pathogenesis

Posterior glenohumeral dysplasia is one of the most important long-term complications of brachial plexus birth injury. It develops because muscle imbalance (dominant internal rotators, weak external rotators) exerts asymmetric forces on the developing, cartilaginous neonatal glenoid. Over time, this produces progressive posterior subluxation of the humeral head, posterior wear of the glenoid with increasing retroversion, and eventual glenoid biconcavity.

### Classification and Monitoring

The Waters classification uses MRI to categorize the severity of glenohumeral deformity based on the relationship of the humeral head to the glenoid and the degree of glenoid retroversion. The classification system ranges from mild posterior subluxation with concentric glenoid to severe posterior dislocation with biconcave glenoid deformity. This classification guides surgical decision-making: mild deformity may respond to soft tissue procedures alone (releases and transfers), while severe bony deformity may require osteotomy or, in extreme cases, arthroplasty considerations at skeletal maturity.

Serial imaging (MRI or ultrasound) is recommended to monitor joint development, particularly during the first 2-3 years of life when the glenoid is most susceptible to remodeling under the influence of muscle forces. Ultrasound is advantageous in young infants because it can be performed without sedation and provides dynamic assessment of humeral head position relative to the glenoid. MRI becomes the preferred modality as the child grows, providing detailed assessment of cartilaginous and bony anatomy, muscle quality, and labral integrity.

### Prevention and Management

Prevention centers on early recognition and treatment of internal rotation contracture through aggressive physical therapy, timely botulinum toxin injections, and surgical releases or transfers when indicated.

**Table 5.8: Shoulder Dysplasia Management Ladder**

| Severity | Intervention |
|----------|-------------|
| Early contracture | Aggressive PT, positioning, passive ROM |
| Moderate contracture | Botulinum toxin to internal rotators; continued PT |
| Mild dysplasia | Anterior release + tendon transfer |
| Moderate subluxation | Closed/open reduction + tendon transfer |
| Severe retroversion | Glenoid anteversion osteotomy |
| Severe fixed internal rotation | Humeral rotation osteotomy |

> **Clinical Pearl**: Shoulder dysplasia in brachial plexus birth injury is a progressive condition driven by muscle imbalance acting on the growing skeleton. Early and aggressive management of internal rotation contracture through physical therapy, botulinum toxin, and timely surgical intervention can prevent or mitigate glenohumeral remodeling. Delayed treatment allows irreversible bony changes that limit reconstructive options.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Prognosis and Counseling

### Prognostic Framework

Prognosis in brachial plexus birth injury is determined by the interaction of injury severity (root levels involved, degree of nerve damage), timing and quality of recovery, and the response to treatment. The Narakas classification provides the initial prognostic framework, with refinement based on serial assessment using the AMS, Toronto Test, and functional tests.

**Table 5.9: Prognostic Indicators Summary**

| Indicator | Favorable | Unfavorable |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Injury pattern | Upper plexus (C5-C6) | Total palsy (C5-T1) |
| Horner syndrome | Absent | Present |
| Phrenic nerve palsy | Absent | Present |
| Biceps function at 3 months | AMS >=6 (antigravity) | AMS <3 |
| Toronto Test at 3 months | >3.5 | <=3.5 |
| Cookie Test at 6-9 months | Positive (hand to mouth) | Negative |
| Towel Test at 6 months | Positive (removes towel) | Negative |
| EMG reinnervation | Present on follow-up | Absent |
| SNAP with sensory loss | Absent (postganglionic) | Present (preganglionic avulsion) |

### Outcome Measures for Long-Term Follow-Up

Several validated outcome measures help quantify functional status and track changes over time in children with brachial plexus birth injury:

- **Mallet Classification**: As described above, grades global shoulder function across five movements (I-V). Most useful after age 3-4 years. Serves as the standard outcome measure for evaluating shoulder-related surgical interventions.
- **Raimondi Hand Scale**: Grades hand function from 0 (no function, complete paralysis) to V (normal function), evaluating grasp patterns and fine motor capability. Particularly relevant for total plexus injuries.
- **Brachial Plexus Outcome Measure (BPOM)**: An activity-based functional assessment that evaluates the child's ability to perform real-world tasks requiring the affected upper extremity.
- **Assisting Hand Assessment (AHA)**: While originally developed for children with hemiplegic cerebral palsy, the AHA has been applied to children with brachial plexus birth injury to evaluate how effectively the affected hand is used as an assisting hand in bimanual activities.
- **Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM)**: A client-centered outcome measure that captures the child's (or parent's) priorities for occupational performance and satisfaction with performance.

### Counseling Families

When counseling families regarding brachial plexus birth injury, several principles guide the discussion. Most infants with upper plexus injuries will recover good function with conservative management. The first 3 months represent a critical observation period during which the trajectory of recovery becomes apparent. The 3-month Toronto Test and 6-month clinical assessment are key decision points for surgical referral.

Families should understand that even with excellent nerve recovery, long-term musculoskeletal complications (particularly shoulder internal rotation contracture and glenohumeral dysplasia) may develop and require ongoing surveillance and potential intervention. Consistent daily range-of-motion exercises performed by parents are among the most important determinants of long-term functional outcome.

For total palsy with Horner syndrome, families should be counseled early that surgical intervention will almost certainly be required, and referral to a specialized brachial plexus center should occur promptly.

### Medicolegal Considerations

Brachial plexus birth injury is one of the most common causes of obstetric malpractice litigation. Several points are relevant for the rehabilitation physician:

- Brachial plexus birth injury can occur without any documented obstetric negligence. Approximately 50% of cases occur without documented shoulder dystocia, and cases have been documented following cesarean delivery.
- The presence of brachial plexus birth injury alone does not establish malpractice; the standard of care requires evaluation of whether the delivery management was appropriate given the clinical circumstances.
- Accurate and detailed clinical documentation of the injury pattern, recovery trajectory, functional outcomes, and therapeutic interventions is essential, as these records may be requested in litigation proceedings years after the birth.
- Rehabilitation physicians may be asked to provide expert testimony regarding functional outcomes, disability rating, life care planning, and the expected cost of ongoing medical and rehabilitation care.

### Long-Term Follow-Up Framework

Children with brachial plexus birth injury require structured long-term follow-up that extends through skeletal maturity:

**Table 5.11: Long-Term Follow-Up Schedule**

| Age | Assessment Focus | Key Interventions |
|-----|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0-3 months | Serial AMS, Toronto Test at 3 months | Passive ROM, parent education |
| 3-6 months | Recovery trajectory, surgical candidacy | Continue ROM; surgical referral if indicated |
| 6-12 months | Cookie Test, residual deficits | Microsurgery if indicated; active exercises |
| 1-3 years | Glenohumeral development, contractures | Shoulder imaging, botulinum toxin, therapy |
| 3-5 years | Mallet Classification, shoulder function | Tendon transfers, osteotomies as needed |
| 5-12 years | Functional integration, sport participation | Adaptation, psychosocial support |
| 12-18 years | Final skeletal growth, body image | Late reconstruction, transition planning |
