## 12.1 The Spasticity Management Ladder

`[BOARD]`

Spasticity management in pediatric rehabilitation follows a structured, stepwise approach that matches the intensity and invasiveness of treatment to the severity and distribution of hypertonia. The management ladder, endorsed by guidelines from the American Academy of Neurology (AAN), the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), and the American Academy for Cerebral Palsy and Developmental Medicine (AACPDM), provides a framework for clinical decision-making.

### The Six Steps

| Step | Intervention | Indication | Reversibility |
|------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | Physical therapy, positioning, stretching, serial casting, orthotics | All patients with spasticity | Fully reversible |
| 2 | Oral medications (baclofen, diazepam, dantrolene, tizanidine) | Generalized spasticity | Reversible |
| 3 | Focal chemodenervation (botulinum toxin, phenol/alcohol neurolysis) | Focal or segmental spasticity | Reversible (BoNT) / Semi-permanent (phenol) |
| 4 | Intrathecal baclofen pump | Severe generalized spasticity not responsive to oral agents | Reversible (pump can be removed) |
| 5 | Selective dorsal rhizotomy | Selected patients with spastic diplegia | Irreversible |
| 6 | Orthopedic surgery | Fixed contractures and bony deformities | Irreversible |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The spasticity management ladder is not strictly sequential. Many children receive interventions from multiple steps simultaneously. For example, a child may use oral baclofen for generalized tone reduction while also receiving botulinum toxin injections to address focal equinus, all while continuing a comprehensive therapy and orthotic program.

Physical management forms the foundation at every level. Stretching programs, proper positioning, serial casting for fixed or dynamic contractures, and orthotic devices are universally recommended as first-line interventions and should continue alongside all pharmacologic and surgical treatments (Delgado et al., 2010; NICE CG145, 2012).

## 12.2 Oral Antispasticity Medications

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 12.2.1 Baclofen (Oral)

**Mechanism of action:** Baclofen is a gamma-aminobutyric acid type B (GABA-B) receptor agonist. It exerts its antispasticity effect primarily through presynaptic inhibition at the spinal cord level. By binding to GABA-B receptors on presynaptic terminals in the dorsal horn, baclofen inhibits the release of excitatory neurotransmitters including glutamate and aspartate, thereby reducing monosynaptic and polysynaptic reflex activity.

**Dosing:** In children, oral baclofen is typically initiated at 0.5 mg/kg/day divided into two to three doses, with weekly titration by 0.5 mg/kg/day. The target dose range is 1-2 mg/kg/day, with a maximum of approximately 60 mg/day in children and 80 mg/day in adolescents and adults. The 2010 AAN Practice Parameter rated oral baclofen as Level C evidence for reducing spasticity in children with cerebral palsy (Delgado et al., 2010).

**Adverse effects:** The most common side effects include sedation, drowsiness, fatigue, generalized hypotonia, dizziness, nausea, and constipation. Cognitive dulling may occur, particularly at higher doses. The sedative effects can be beneficial when spasticity interferes with sleep.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Oral baclofen has poor central nervous system penetration, with only approximately 5% of an oral dose crossing the blood-brain barrier. This pharmacokinetic limitation is precisely why intrathecal delivery is so much more effective: direct cerebrospinal fluid delivery allows 100- to 1000-fold dose reduction while achieving superior spasticity control.

**Withdrawal:** Abrupt discontinuation of oral baclofen can precipitate a withdrawal syndrome featuring seizures, hallucinations, rebound spasticity, and autonomic instability. The medication must always be tapered gradually when being discontinued (Coffey et al., 2002).

### 12.2.2 Dantrolene Sodium

**Mechanism of action:** Dantrolene is unique among antispasticity agents because it acts peripherally at the skeletal muscle level rather than centrally. It inhibits calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum by binding to the ryanodine receptor (RyR1), thereby reducing excitation-contraction coupling and weakening muscle contraction.

`[BOARD]`

**Dosing:** Pediatric dosing begins at 0.5 mg/kg once daily and is titrated gradually to a maximum of 2 mg/kg four times daily (not exceeding 100 mg per single dose or 12 mg/kg/day). Some references cite a maximum of 400 mg/day for older adolescents and adults. The AAN Practice Parameter rated dantrolene as Level C evidence for spasticity reduction (Delgado et al., 2010).

**Hepatotoxicity:** The most critical adverse effect of dantrolene is idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity, which can be fatal. Case-fatality rates for dantrolene-associated liver disease have been reported as high as 28%. Risk factors include doses exceeding 400 mg/day, treatment duration beyond 60 days, female sex, and concurrent hepatotoxic medications. Liver function tests must be monitored at baseline and monthly for the first six months of therapy. Dantrolene is contraindicated in patients with active liver disease.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Dantrolene is the only peripherally acting antispasticity agent in common clinical use. All other oral antispasticity medications (baclofen, diazepam, tizanidine) act centrally on the brain or spinal cord. This distinction is a recurring board examination topic. Dantrolene's peripheral mechanism also makes it the drug of choice for malignant hyperthermia, where intravenous dantrolene is the first-line treatment.

Other side effects include drowsiness, dizziness, diarrhea, general weakness, and photosensitivity.

### 12.2.3 Tizanidine

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

**Mechanism of action:** Tizanidine is a centrally acting alpha-2 adrenergic agonist. It reduces spasticity by enhancing presynaptic inhibition of excitatory amino acid release in the spinal cord, thereby decreasing polysynaptic reflex activity. Its mechanism is similar to that of clonidine.

**Dosing:** In children, tizanidine is typically started at 1-2 mg at bedtime and titrated by 2-4 mg every three to seven days. Maximum doses range from 24-36 mg/day. Pediatric dosing is not well established, and its use in children is largely off-label, extrapolated from adult data.

**Adverse effects:** Sedation is the most common side effect and can be severe. Dry mouth, hypotension, and dizziness also occur frequently. Hepatotoxicity has been reported, necessitating periodic liver function monitoring, though the risk is lower than with dantrolene.

**Drug interactions:** Tizanidine is metabolized by the cytochrome P450 1A2 (CYP1A2) enzyme system. It is contraindicated with potent CYP1A2 inhibitors such as fluvoxamine and ciprofloxacin, which can dramatically increase tizanidine levels and cause profound hypotension and sedation.

**Evidence level:** The AAN Practice Parameter rated tizanidine as Level C for pediatric spasticity management based on insufficient pediatric-specific evidence (Delgado et al., 2010).

### 12.2.4 Benzodiazepines (Diazepam)

`[BOARD]`

**Mechanism of action:** Diazepam enhances gamma-aminobutyric acid type A (GABA-A) receptor-mediated inhibition at both spinal and supraspinal levels. This mechanism is distinct from baclofen, which acts on GABA-B receptors.

**Dosing:** Diazepam is dosed at 0.1-0.3 mg/kg/day divided two to four times daily, with a maximum of 2 mg per dose in young children. The 2010 AAN Practice Parameter rated diazepam as Level A evidence for short-term reduction of spasticity in children with cerebral palsy, making it the only oral antispasticity medication to achieve Level A recommendation (Delgado et al., 2010).

**Adverse effects:** Despite its strong evidence for efficacy, diazepam's side effect profile limits its long-term utility. Key concerns include sedation, cognitive impairment, tolerance with chronic use, physical dependence, and respiratory depression. Behavioral disinhibition may occur in some children. As with baclofen, the medication must be tapered to avoid withdrawal seizures.

| Medication | Mechanism | Site of Action | AAN Level | Key Side Effect |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| Baclofen | GABA-B agonist | Spinal cord (central) | C | Sedation; withdrawal seizures |
| Dantrolene | Ryanodine receptor blocker | Skeletal muscle (peripheral) | C | Hepatotoxicity |
| Tizanidine | Alpha-2 agonist | Spinal cord (central) | C | Sedation; CYP1A2 interactions |
| Diazepam | GABA-A agonist | Spinal + supraspinal (central) | A | Sedation; tolerance; dependence |

## 12.3 Botulinum Toxin in Pediatric Spasticity

`[BOARD]`

### 12.3.1 Mechanism of Action

Botulinum toxin type A (BoNT-A) produces its therapeutic effect by blocking neuromuscular transmission. The toxin is internalized at the presynaptic terminal of the neuromuscular junction, where it cleaves SNARE complex proteins --- specifically, SNAP-25 in the case of BoNT-A. This cleavage prevents acetylcholine-containing vesicles from fusing with the presynaptic membrane, thereby blocking acetylcholine release and producing a chemical denervation of the target muscle.

The onset of clinical effect occurs within 24-72 hours, with peak effect typically at approximately two weeks. Duration of effect ranges from three to four months on average, though some patients may experience benefit for up to six to eight months. Functional recovery occurs through collateral axonal sprouting and eventual regeneration of the original neuromuscular junction.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Botulinum toxin does not affect sensory nerve fibers. Therefore, sensory side effects such as dysesthesia or numbness do not occur with BoNT-A injections. This contrasts with phenol neurolysis, which can cause painful dysesthesias when mixed sensorimotor nerves are injected.

### 12.3.2 Formulations and Dosing

Two formulations of BoNT-A are commonly used in pediatric practice: onabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) and abobotulinumtoxinA (Dysport). Their units are not interchangeable, and there is no precise conversion factor, though an approximate ratio of 1 unit Botox to 2.5-3 units Dysport is often cited.

| Parameter | OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) | AbobotulinumtoxinA (Dysport) |
|-----------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Maximum dose per session | 12-16 U/kg (max 400 U total) | 30 U/kg (max 1000 U total) |
| Maximum per injection site | 50 U | 125 U |
| Typical dilution | 100 U in 1-2 mL saline | 300-500 U in 1.5-2.5 mL |
| Onset | 24-72 hours | 24-72 hours |
| Peak effect | ~2 weeks | ~2 weeks |
| Duration | 3-4 months | 3-4 months |
| Minimum reinjection interval | 3 months | 3 months |

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 12.3.3 Dosing by Muscle Group (OnabotulinumtoxinA)

| Target Muscle | Dose Range (U/kg) | Common Indication |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Gastrocnemius (both heads) | 3-6 | Equinus gait |
| Soleus | 2-3 | Equinus gait |
| Medial hamstrings | 3-6 each | Crouch gait, scissoring |
| Lateral hamstrings | 3-6 each | Crouch gait |
| Hip adductors | 2-6 per side | Scissoring |
| Tibialis posterior | 1-2 | Varus foot deformity |
| Biceps brachii | 1-3 | Elbow flexion posturing |
| Pronator teres | 0.5-1.5 | Forearm pronation |
| Flexor carpi ulnaris | 0.5-1.5 | Wrist flexion posturing |

### 12.3.4 Evidence Base

The landmark randomized controlled trial by Koman and colleagues (2000) established the efficacy of BoNT-A for equinus gait in children with cerebral palsy, demonstrating significant improvement in gait patterns and physician-rated outcomes compared with placebo. The Novak et al. (2020) systematic review assigned a green traffic light (effective) to BoNT-A for reducing spasticity and improving gait in CP.

The European Consensus statements (Love et al., 2010; Heinen et al., 2010) provide detailed recommendations for dosing, muscle selection, injection technique, and integration with goal-directed therapy programs. Current practice trends emphasize ultrasound-guided injection for improved targeting accuracy, multilevel injection protocols, and combining BoNT-A with structured rehabilitation programs.

### 12.3.5 Adverse Effects and Safety

Local side effects include injection site pain, bruising, and excessive weakness of the injected muscle. Systemic adverse events, while uncommon, are clinically significant. A single-center analysis of 2,219 injection sessions reported systemic adverse events in 3.6% of sessions, with severe events requiring hospitalization in 0.7%. Risk factors for systemic complications include higher GMFCS levels (IV-V), underlying dysphagia, and history of aspiration pneumonia.

In 2009, the FDA issued a black box warning regarding the potential for distant spread of toxin effect, leading to generalized weakness, dysphagia, and respiratory compromise, particularly in children with underlying neuromuscular conditions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Neutralizing antibody formation occurs in 3-10% of patients after repeated injections and represents the primary mechanism of secondary non-response. The risk of antibody formation is minimized by using the lowest effective dose, maintaining a minimum three-month interval between injection sessions, and avoiding booster injections.

Important limitations of BoNT-A must be understood: it does not prevent contracture development in the long term, and it has not been shown to reduce the need for orthopedic hip surgery in children at risk for hip displacement.

## 12.4 Phenol and Alcohol Neurolysis

`[BOARD]`

### 12.4.1 Phenol Neurolysis

Phenol (5-7% aqueous solution) produces chemical neurolysis through protein denaturation, causing Wallerian degeneration of the targeted nerve. The onset of effect is immediate (within minutes), which is a distinct advantage over BoNT-A. Duration of effect is variable, typically lasting three to twelve months, with an average of approximately six months.

**Common targets include:**
- Obturator nerve motor branches (hip adductor spasticity)
- Musculocutaneous nerve (elbow flexor spasticity)
- Tibial nerve branches (ankle plantarflexor spasticity)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenol should ideally be injected into pure motor nerves or motor branches to avoid painful dysesthesias. When a mixed sensorimotor nerve is injected, the resultant demyelination of sensory fibers can produce severe, persistent dysesthetic pain that may be more disabling than the original spasticity. This is why the obturator nerve motor branch is such an ideal target --- it is a pure motor nerve to the adductors.

**Advantages over BoNT-A:** Immediate onset, longer duration, lower cost, no risk of neutralizing antibody formation, and no dose ceiling per session.

**Disadvantages:** Risk of painful dysesthesias with mixed nerve injection, tissue necrosis at the injection site, vascular injury, less precise targeting, and the requirement for nerve stimulator or ultrasound guidance.

### 12.4.2 Alcohol (Ethanol) Neurolysis

Ethanol (45-100%) produces a similar mechanism of chemical neurolysis. It is used less frequently in pediatric practice than phenol. Alcohol injections tend to be more painful at the time of administration and may produce more tissue necrosis.

## 12.5 Intrathecal Baclofen Therapy

`[BOARD]`

### 12.5.1 Overview and Pump Specifications

Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) therapy delivers baclofen directly to the cerebrospinal fluid via a surgically implanted programmable pump (typically the Medtronic SynchroMed II). The pump reservoir is placed in the abdomen, and a catheter is threaded into the thoracic intrathecal space. Catheter tip placement at T10-T12 targets lower extremity spasticity, while placement at C5-T2 addresses upper extremity involvement.

Typical intrathecal doses range from 50 to 1,500 mcg/day, with average pediatric doses of 200-400 mcg/day --- representing a 100- to 1,000-fold reduction compared to equivalent oral doses. The pump requires refilling every one to six months depending on dose and concentration. Battery life is approximately five to seven years, after which surgical pump replacement is necessary.

The Novak et al. (2020) systematic review assigned ITB a green traffic light for reducing spasticity in CP. A Dutch randomized controlled trial by Hoving et al. (2009) demonstrated significant reduction in both spasticity and pain with ITB therapy compared with placebo, as well as improved ease of care for children with severe spastic cerebral palsy.

Adverse effects are reported in up to 50% of patients and include lethargy, confusion, and hypotonia. Catheter-related complications include infection, seroma formation, and cerebrospinal fluid leak. Pump malfunction represents a potentially life-threatening complication.

### 12.5.2 ITB Trial Protocol

`[BOARD]`

Before permanent pump implantation, all candidates undergo a screening trial to confirm responsiveness to intrathecal baclofen.

**Protocol:**
1. A lumbar puncture is performed and a bolus dose of baclofen is injected into the intrathecal space.
2. The starting trial dose is 25-50 mcg in children and 50 mcg in adolescents and adults.
3. If no response is observed, the trial may be repeated at 75 mcg and then 100 mcg, with doses administered at least 24 hours apart.
4. A positive trial is defined as a reduction of at least one point on the Modified Ashworth Scale in the target muscle groups.
5. Onset of effect from a bolus dose occurs within 30-60 minutes.
6. Peak effect occurs at approximately four hours.
7. Duration of effect is four to eight hours.

> **Clinical Pearl:** If a patient does not respond to a 100 mcg trial dose of intrathecal baclofen, they are generally considered not to be a candidate for ITB pump implantation. This trial failure should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis (e.g., dystonia rather than spasticity) or re-evaluation of treatment goals.

### 12.5.3 ITB Withdrawal: A Medical Emergency

`[BOARD]`

ITB withdrawal is among the most dangerous complications in pediatric rehabilitation and must be recognized immediately. It occurs when intrathecal baclofen delivery is abruptly interrupted.

**Causes:**
- Catheter kink, fracture, disconnection, or migration
- Pump malfunction or battery depletion
- Missed refill appointment leading to empty reservoir
- Programming error

**Clinical presentation (progressive):**

| Stage | Symptoms | Timeline |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Early | Return of baseline spasticity, pruritus (especially facial/truncal), diaphoresis, anxiety, irritability | Hours after interruption |
| Intermediate | Hyperthermia (temperature >40C / 104F), tachycardia, labile blood pressure, rigidity, altered mental status | 24-72 hours |
| Severe / Life-threatening | Rhabdomyolysis (elevated CK, myoglobinuria, acute renal failure), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), seizures, multi-organ failure, cardiac arrest | 48-96 hours if untreated |

**Case-fatality rate:** Reported mortality ranges from 10-22% in published case series, underscoring the severity of this condition (Coffey et al., 2002).

**Differential diagnosis:** ITB withdrawal can mimic several other conditions: malignant hyperthermia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, serotonin syndrome, autonomic dysreflexia (in patients with concurrent spinal cord pathology), and sepsis. The key distinguishing factor is the temporal relationship to baclofen delivery disruption.

**Management:**
1. **Restore intrathecal baclofen delivery as rapidly as possible.** Evaluate the pump for residual volume, interrogate for programming errors, and assess catheter integrity (plain radiographs, pump interrogation, dye study).
2. **High-dose oral baclofen** can serve as a temporizing measure, though oral bioavailability is poor and this alone is often insufficient.
3. **Intravenous benzodiazepines** (diazepam) provide GABAergic support and treat rigidity and seizures.
4. **Cyproheptadine** has been reported in case series as an adjunctive agent.
5. **Intensive care unit admission** is essential for patients with moderate to severe withdrawal.
6. **Aggressive supportive care:** external cooling for hyperthermia, intravenous fluids, renal monitoring for rhabdomyolysis, and correction of coagulopathy.
7. **Dantrolene** may be considered for severe rigidity unresponsive to other measures.

### 12.5.4 ITB Overdose

`[BOARD]`

ITB overdose, while also potentially fatal, is generally more readily managed than withdrawal.

**Causes:**
- Programming error (dose set too high)
- Pump malfunction delivering excess drug
- Accidental refill into subcutaneous pocket rather than pump reservoir (pocket fill)

**Clinical presentation:**
- Excessive hypotonia and flaccidity
- Progressive drowsiness, obtundation, and coma
- Respiratory depression (the primary life-threatening concern)
- Bradycardia and hypotension
- Seizures (rare)

**Management:**
1. **Airway management and ventilatory support** are the immediate priorities.
2. **Lumbar puncture** to withdraw 30-40 mL of cerebrospinal fluid can reduce intrathecal drug levels.
3. **Pump should be stopped** or set to minimum dose.
4. **Physostigmine** has been reported to provide some benefit in case reports, though it is not considered standard therapy.
5. Supportive care until drug effect wears off (half-life in CSF approximately 1-5 hours).

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key distinction between ITB withdrawal and overdose can be remembered by their cardinal features: **Withdrawal = Hyperthermia + Rigidity + Rhabdomyolysis** (resembling malignant hyperthermia). **Overdose = Respiratory Depression + Flaccidity + Coma** (resembling anesthetic overdose). Both are emergencies, but withdrawal carries a higher mortality rate and is more difficult to manage acutely.

## 12.6 Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy

`[BOARD]`

### 12.6.1 Candidate Selection

Selective dorsal rhizotomy (SDR) is a neurosurgical procedure that permanently reduces spasticity by selectively sectioning dorsal (sensory) nerve rootlets at the lumbosacral level. The ideal candidate profile is well established and represents a high-yield board topic.

**Ideal SDR candidate:**
- **Age:** 3-8 years (some centers extend to 2-10 years)
- **Diagnosis:** Spastic diplegia (bilateral spastic cerebral palsy)
- **GMFCS Level:** II or III (ambulatory with or without assistive devices)
- **Tone pattern:** Pure spasticity with no dystonia, athetosis, or ataxia
- **Underlying strength:** Adequate voluntary motor control beneath the spasticity
- **Trunk and head control:** Sufficient to maintain upright posture
- **Cognitive ability:** Sufficient to participate in intensive postoperative physical therapy
- **Contractures:** No significant fixed contractures (or contractures correctable at time of surgery)
- **MRI pattern:** Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL), the pattern seen in ex-premature infants, is the most favorable imaging finding
- **Motivation:** Family committed to intensive postoperative rehabilitation (typically 6-12 months)

**Contraindications:**
- Dystonia or mixed tone disorders
- Severe underlying weakness masked by spasticity
- Fixed contractures exceeding 15-20 degrees
- GMFCS IV-V (relative contraindication; some centers perform SDR for comfort and ease of care)
- Significant ataxia
- Severe cognitive impairment limiting physical therapy participation

### 12.6.2 Procedure and Outcomes

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

The procedure involves a laminectomy (traditionally L1-S1; some centers use a single-level L1 approach) with identification of dorsal rootlets using intraoperative electromyographic monitoring. Rootlets demonstrating abnormal responses are selectively sectioned, typically 50-70% of selected dorsal rootlets.

The meta-analysis by McLaughlin et al. (2002), combining three randomized controlled trials (Steinbok 1997, Wright 1998, McLaughlin 1998) with a total of 90 children with spastic diplegia, demonstrated that SDR combined with physical therapy was superior to physical therapy alone for reducing spasticity (mean Ashworth scale reduction of 1.2 points) and modestly improving motor function (GMFM-66 improvement of approximately 2.6 points). A large English cohort study of 137 children at GMFCS levels II-III showed GMFM-66 improvement of 3.2 points per year compared with an expected 1.9 points per year, with greater gains in GMFCS level II than level III.

Long-term follow-up studies extending 20-28 years post-SDR have demonstrated durable spasticity reduction, though motor improvements are most sustained in patients at GMFCS level I and attenuate at higher GMFCS levels. Approximately 10% of patients experience adverse events including wound infection and foot dysesthesias. Importantly, approximately 40% of SDR patients eventually require additional botulinum toxin injections or orthopedic surgery despite the procedure.

> **Clinical Pearl:** SDR is an irreversible procedure. Once dorsal rootlets are sectioned, spasticity reduction is permanent. This permanence is both its greatest advantage (no ongoing medication costs or pump maintenance) and its greatest risk (if weakness is unmasked or balance worsens, the spasticity cannot be restored). Careful patient selection is therefore paramount.

## 12.7 Pediatric Pain Assessment Scales

`[BOARD]`

Accurate pain assessment in children requires developmentally appropriate tools. The selection of a pain scale depends on the child's age, cognitive ability, and communication capacity.

### 12.7.1 FLACC Scale

The FLACC (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) scale is a behavioral observation tool validated for children aged 2 months to 7 years and for nonverbal or cognitively impaired patients of any age (Merkel et al., 1997).

| Category | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 |
|----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **F**ace | No particular expression or smile | Occasional grimace or frown, withdrawn | Frequent to constant quivering chin, clenched jaw |
| **L**egs | Normal position or relaxed | Uneasy, restless, tense | Kicking or legs drawn up |
| **A**ctivity | Lying quietly, normal position | Squirming, shifting, tense | Arched, rigid, or jerking |
| **C**ry | No cry (awake or asleep) | Moans or whimpers, occasional complaint | Crying steadily, screams or sobs |
| **C**onsolability | Content, relaxed | Reassured by occasional touching, distractible | Difficult to console or comfort |

**Total score: 0-10.** Interpretation: 0 = comfortable; 1-3 = mild discomfort; 4-6 = moderate pain; 7-10 = severe pain.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The revised FLACC (r-FLACC) has been validated specifically for children with cognitive impairment. It incorporates individualized pain descriptors provided by caregivers who know the child's baseline behaviors, improving the sensitivity and specificity of pain detection in this challenging population.

### 12.7.2 Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale

The Wong-Baker FACES scale (Wong and Baker, 1988) is a self-report tool appropriate for children aged 3 years and older. It presents six faces representing increasing levels of pain, scored as 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10. The child selects the face that best represents their current pain experience. The scale has been validated across multiple cultures and languages.

### 12.7.3 Other Pediatric Pain Tools

| Scale | Type | Age Range | Scoring |
|-------|------|-----------|---------|
| FLACC | Behavioral observation | 2 months - 7 years; nonverbal patients | 0-10 |
| Wong-Baker FACES | Self-report | 3+ years | 0-10 (even numbers) |
| Numeric Rating Scale (NRS) | Self-report | 8+ years | 0-10 |
| NIPS (Neonatal Infant Pain Scale) | Behavioral observation | Neonates | 0-7 (6 categories) |

## 12.8 Spasticity and Tone Assessment Scales

`[BOARD]`

### 12.8.1 Modified Ashworth Scale

The Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS) remains the most widely used clinical measure of spasticity (Bohannon and Smith, 1987). It is an ordinal scale that grades resistance to passive muscle stretch.

| Grade | Description |
|-------|------------|
| 0 | No increase in muscle tone |
| 1 | Slight increase in tone; catch and release or minimal resistance at end of range |
| 1+ | Slight increase; catch followed by minimal resistance through remainder of range (<1/2 ROM) |
| 2 | More marked increase through most of ROM; affected part easily moved |
| 3 | Considerable increase in tone; passive movement difficult |
| 4 | Affected part rigid in flexion or extension |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The MAS measures resistance to passive stretch but does not distinguish between spasticity (velocity-dependent), contracture (fixed shortening), and dystonia (involuntary sustained contraction). As typically administered, it is velocity-independent, which is a fundamental limitation. The Tardieu scale addresses this limitation.

### 12.8.2 Tardieu Scale

The Modified Tardieu Scale (Boyd and Graham, 1999) is a velocity-dependent assessment that differentiates the spastic component of increased tone from fixed contracture.

**Key measurements:**
- **R1:** The angle at which a catch or clonus is first felt during a fast passive stretch (the spastic component)
- **R2:** The full passive range of motion achieved during a slow stretch (reflecting true muscle length and any contracture)
- **Dynamic component = R2 - R1:** The difference between passive ROM and the angle of spastic catch

**Clinical interpretation:**
- **Large R2-R1 difference:** Predominantly spastic; likely to respond to antispasticity treatments (BoNT-A, baclofen, SDR)
- **Small R2-R1 difference:** Predominantly fixed contracture; requires mechanical or surgical intervention (serial casting, tendon lengthening)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Tardieu scale is superior to the Modified Ashworth Scale for guiding treatment decisions because it quantifies the relative contributions of spasticity and contracture. A child with an R2-R1 difference of 30 degrees at the ankle is an excellent candidate for botulinum toxin injection, while a child with an R2-R1 difference of only 5 degrees needs orthopedic intervention rather than chemodenervation.

## 12.9 Antiepileptic Drugs: Key Side Effects

`[BOARD]`

Epilepsy co-occurs in 25-45% of children with cerebral palsy, making antiepileptic drug (AED) pharmacology an essential knowledge area for the pediatric rehabilitation physician. The following table summarizes the most board-relevant side effects of commonly prescribed AEDs.

| AED | Key Side Effects (Board High-Yield) |
|-----|-------------------------------------|
| Valproic acid | Hepatotoxicity (highest risk in children <2 years with metabolic disease), pancreatitis, teratogenicity (neural tube defects), weight gain, thrombocytopenia, tremor, alopecia |
| Carbamazepine | Hyponatremia (SIADH), aplastic anemia, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (screen HLA-B*1502 in patients of Asian descent), diplopia, ataxia |
| Phenytoin | Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, osteoporosis, cerebellar atrophy, coarsened facial features, folate deficiency, teratogenicity; zero-order kinetics |
| Phenobarbital | Sedation, cognitive impairment, behavioral problems, vitamin D deficiency, osteomalacia |
| Levetiracetam | Behavioral and psychiatric effects (irritability, aggression, depression); otherwise relatively benign |
| Topiramate | Cognitive slowing, metabolic acidosis, nephrolithiasis, weight loss, acute angle-closure glaucoma |
| Lamotrigine | Stevens-Johnson syndrome (risk increased with rapid titration or concurrent valproic acid use), rash |
| Vigabatrin | Irreversible bilateral concentric visual field constriction; first-line for infantile spasms in tuberous sclerosis complex |
| Gabapentin | Sedation, dizziness, weight gain; used off-label for neuropathic pain and spasticity |
| Oxcarbazepine | Hyponatremia (more frequent than with carbamazepine), rash, dizziness |
| Zonisamide | Nephrolithiasis, oligohydrosis with hyperthermia, metabolic acidosis |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenytoin follows zero-order (saturation) kinetics, meaning that small dose adjustments can produce disproportionately large changes in serum drug levels. This makes phenytoin dosing inherently unpredictable compared with most other AEDs that follow first-order kinetics. Clinicians must monitor levels closely when adjusting phenytoin doses.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Valproic acid is contraindicated in children under 2 years of age with suspected metabolic disease due to the highest risk of fatal hepatotoxicity in this population. In older children, it remains a broadly effective AED but requires monitoring of liver function, complete blood count, and pancreatic enzymes.

## 12.10 DMD Corticosteroid Management

`[BOARD]`

### 12.10.1 Standard Corticosteroid Therapy

Corticosteroids remain the standard of care for Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD), providing the most robust evidence for altering the natural history of the disease. The landmark trial by Griggs et al. (1991) established 0.75 mg/kg/day of prednisone as the optimal dose, demonstrating prolongation of ambulation by two to five years.

| Feature | Prednisone | Deflazacort |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Dose | 0.75 mg/kg/day | 0.9 mg/kg/day |
| Initiation | Age 4-6 years (motor plateau or early decline) | Age 4-6 years |
| Weight gain | More significant | Less (major clinical advantage) |
| Cataracts | Lower risk | Higher risk (posterior subcapsular) |
| Efficacy | Prolongs ambulation 2-5 years; preserves respiratory and cardiac function | Similar efficacy |
| FDA status | Generic, widely available | FDA-approved for DMD (2017) |

The FOR-DMD trial (McDonald et al., 2022) compared daily prednisone, daily deflazacort, and intermittent prednisone, confirming that all regimens provide benefit. Daily deflazacort was associated with less weight gain but a higher incidence of cataracts.

**Alternative dosing regimens** include weekend-only dosing (10 mg/kg given over the weekend) and 10-days-on/10-days-off schedules, which may reduce side effects while preserving some therapeutic benefit.

### 12.10.2 Vamorolone (Agamree)

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Vamorolone, a dissociative corticosteroid, received FDA approval in October 2023 for the treatment of DMD in patients aged 2 years and older. It was developed to retain the anti-inflammatory and membrane-stabilizing properties of traditional corticosteroids while dissociating the glucocorticoid receptor transactivation that mediates many classic steroid side effects. The phase 3 VISION-DMD trial demonstrated efficacy comparable to prednisone with less bone mineral density loss and fewer behavioral side effects. This represents a significant advance in the long-term management of DMD.

### 12.10.3 Monitoring Requirements

All boys on long-term corticosteroid therapy for DMD require systematic monitoring:

- **Growth:** Height and weight at each visit; growth velocity tracking
- **Body composition:** BMI; dietary counseling
- **Bone density:** DEXA scanning; vertebral fracture assessment
- **Ophthalmology:** Annual screening for posterior subcapsular cataracts
- **Blood pressure and glucose:** Screening for metabolic syndrome
- **Behavioral health:** Mood changes, irritability, sleep disturbance
- **Adrenal function:** Risk of adrenal insufficiency with chronic use; stress-dose steroid protocols for surgery/illness

> **Clinical Pearl:** Corticosteroids should be initiated when motor function plateaus or begins to decline, typically between ages 4 and 6. Starting steroids while motor function is still improving may expose the child to side effects without clear benefit. Conversely, starting too late misses the window of maximum benefit. The Birnkrant et al. (2018) guidelines in Lancet Neurology provide the current comprehensive framework for DMD care decisions.

## 12.11 Gene Therapies for Neuromuscular Disease

`[BOARD]`

### 12.11.1 SMA Gene Therapies

Three disease-modifying therapies are now available for spinal muscular atrophy, each with a distinct mechanism, route of delivery, and evidence base. All target the fundamental molecular defect in SMA --- insufficient levels of survival motor neuron (SMN) protein.

| Feature | Nusinersen (Spinraza) | Onasemnogene (Zolgensma) | Risdiplam (Evrysdi) |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Mechanism | Antisense oligonucleotide modifying SMN2 splicing (exon 7 inclusion) | AAV9 gene replacement delivering functional SMN1 gene | Small molecule SMN2 splicing modifier |
| Route | Intrathecal injection | Single IV infusion | Oral liquid (daily) |
| FDA approval | December 2016 | May 2019 | August 2020 |
| Dosing schedule | Loading: days 0, 14, 28, 63; maintenance every 4 months | One-time dose | Daily |
| Approved ages | All ages | <2 years | >=2 months |
| Approximate cost | ~$375,000/year | ~$2.1 million (one-time) | ~$100,000-340,000/year |
| Key trial | ENDEAR (Type 1); CHERISH (Types 2/3) | START; STR1VE | FIREFISH (Type 1); SUNFISH (Types 2/3) |

**Key trial results:**

- **ENDEAR** (Finkel et al., 2017): In SMA type 1, 51% of nusinersen-treated infants achieved motor milestones versus 0% in the sham control group. Combined death or permanent ventilation occurred in 39% of treated versus 68% of sham patients.

- **CHERISH** (Mercuri et al., 2018): In later-onset SMA (types 2/3), nusinersen treatment produced a mean HFMSE improvement of +3.9 points versus -1.0 in the sham group, a clinically meaningful difference exceeding the threshold of 3 points.

- **START** (Mendell et al., 2017): All 15 infants with SMA type 1 treated with high-dose onasemnogene were alive at 20 months without permanent ventilation (versus 8% in historical controls). Eleven of 12 high-dose patients achieved independent sitting.

- **FIREFISH** (Darras et al., 2021): Risdiplam-treated infants with SMA type 1 achieved sitting without support in 29-41% at 12 months; 85-90% were alive without permanent ventilation.

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

**Pre-symptomatic treatment:** The addition of SMA to the United States Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) in 2018 has enabled newborn screening across all 50 states. Screening detects approximately 95% of SMA cases via homozygous deletion of SMN1 exon 7 (point mutations may be missed). Pre-symptomatic treatment with nusinersen (NURTURE trial) and onasemnogene (SPR1NT trial) has demonstrated near-normal motor development in infants treated before symptom onset, fundamentally transforming the natural history of SMA.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma) was the most expensive drug in the world at its launch, at approximately $2.1 million per dose. However, as a one-time intravenous infusion, the total lifetime treatment cost may be comparable to or lower than the cumulative cost of indefinite nusinersen maintenance (intrathecal injections every four months for life) or daily risdiplam. All patients receiving onasemnogene require liver function monitoring and prednisolone pre-treatment due to risks of hepatotoxicity and thrombotic microangiopathy.

### 12.11.2 DMD Gene Therapies

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

**Exon-skipping antisense oligonucleotides** represent the first class of gene-targeted therapies approved for DMD. These agents cause specific exons to be skipped during pre-mRNA processing, producing a truncated but partially functional dystrophin protein (similar to the milder Becker muscular dystrophy phenotype).

| Agent | Brand Name | Target | FDA Approval | Applicable Mutations |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Eteplirsen | Exondys 51 | Exon 51 skipping | September 2016 | ~13% of DMD patients |
| Golodirsen | Vyondys 53 | Exon 53 skipping | December 2019 | ~8% of DMD patients |
| Viltolarsen | Viltepso | Exon 53 skipping | August 2020 | ~8% of DMD patients |
| Casimersen | Amondys 45 | Exon 45 skipping | February 2021 | ~8% of DMD patients |

All are administered as weekly intravenous infusions. All received FDA accelerated approval based on surrogate endpoints (dystrophin production), with confirmatory trials ongoing.

**Gene replacement therapy:** Delandistrogene moxeparvovec (Elevidys), an AAV-based gene therapy delivering a micro-dystrophin construct, received FDA accelerated approval in June 2023 for ambulatory boys with DMD aged 4-5 years. This represents the first gene replacement approach for DMD.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Unlike SMA where all patients share the same molecular defect (SMN1 deletion), DMD arises from hundreds of different mutations in the dystrophin gene. Exon-skipping therapies are therefore mutation-specific, with each agent applicable to only a subset of patients. Collectively, the four approved exon-skipping agents cover approximately 30% of DMD mutations amenable to skipping strategies.

## 12.12 Dystonia Management in Children

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Dystonia frequently coexists with spasticity in children with cerebral palsy, particularly in those with dyskinetic CP subtypes. The Hypertonia Assessment Tool (HAT) helps differentiate spasticity, dystonia, and rigidity in clinical practice.

**First-line:** Oral baclofen (same GABA-B-mediated mechanism and dosing as for spasticity)

**Second-line options:**
- Gabapentin: 5-15 mg/kg/day divided three times daily, titrated to a maximum of 2,400 mg/day
- Clonidine: initiated at 0.025-0.05 mg/day divided twice daily, titrated to a maximum of 0.6 mg/day
- Benzodiazepines

**Refractory dystonia:** Deep brain stimulation (DBS) targeting the globus pallidus internus (GPi) is considered for children with severe dystonia unresponsive to medication, generally in patients aged 7 years and older (Fehlings et al., 2018).

## 12.13 Growth Plate Effects of Medications

`[BOARD]`

The pediatric growth plate (physis) is vulnerable to medication effects. Understanding which medications can affect skeletal growth is clinically relevant and frequently tested.

| Medication/Agent | Effect on Growth Plate |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Chronic corticosteroids | Suppress growth velocity via GH axis and IGF-1 reduction; do not cause premature closure but slow growth; associated osteopenia increases fracture risk |
| Fluoroquinolones | Theoretical risk to growth plate cartilage based on animal studies; FDA black box warning; generally avoided in children <18 years |
| Retinoids (isotretinoin) | Case reports of premature epiphyseal closure; associated hyperostosis |
| Chemotherapy (methotrexate) | Can affect growth plate function; radiation to growth plates causes premature closure and asymmetric growth |
| Excessive vitamin A | Premature closure of growth plates |
| Sex steroids (testosterone, estrogen) | Accelerate skeletal maturation and epiphyseal fusion; precocious puberty leads to early fusion and short adult stature |
| Growth hormone | Does NOT cause premature closure; accelerates growth velocity; used therapeutically in GH deficiency, Turner syndrome, Prader-Willi syndrome |
| Bisphosphonates | No direct growth plate effect; increase bone mineral density; used in osteogenesis imperfecta and DMD-related osteoporosis |

> **Clinical Pearl:** In boys with DMD on chronic corticosteroids, growth suppression and osteoporosis are major management challenges. Vertebral compression fractures are common but often asymptomatic and underdiagnosed. Routine lateral spine imaging and DEXA scanning should be integrated into DMD surveillance protocols, with bisphosphonate therapy considered for those with significant bone mineral density loss or fractures.

## 12.14 Pediatric NSAID Use

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

| Medication | Dosing | Age | Key Considerations |
|-----------|--------|-----|-------------------|
| Ibuprofen | 5-10 mg/kg/dose q6-8h; max 40 mg/kg/day | >6 months | Most commonly used pediatric NSAID |
| Naproxen | 5-7 mg/kg/dose q12h; max 20 mg/kg/day | >2 years | Longer half-life allows BID dosing |
| Ketorolac | 0.5 mg/kg IV/IM q6h (max 30 mg/dose) | Short-term only | Maximum 5 days; acute pain only |
| Acetaminophen | 10-15 mg/kg/dose q4-6h; max 75 mg/kg/day | All ages | Not an NSAID; no anti-inflammatory effect |
| Celecoxib | Weight-based dosing | >2 years | FDA-approved for juvenile idiopathic arthritis |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Aspirin should be avoided in children under 19 years of age due to the risk of Reye syndrome, a potentially fatal hepatic encephalopathy associated with aspirin use during viral illnesses. Celecoxib (a selective COX-2 inhibitor) is the only NSAID with specific FDA approval for use in juvenile idiopathic arthritis.
