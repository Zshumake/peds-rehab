## [COMPREHENSIVE] Introduction to Neuromuscular Diseases in Pediatric Rehabilitation

Neuromuscular diseases (NMDs) encompass a broad and clinically diverse group of inherited and acquired conditions affecting the motor unit at any level: the anterior horn cell, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, or skeletal muscle itself. In pediatric rehabilitation, these disorders present unique challenges because they unfold against the backdrop of normal growth and development. A child with a progressive neuromuscular condition must not only contend with declining function but must do so during periods when peers are rapidly acquiring new motor, cognitive, and social skills.

The past two decades have witnessed a transformation in the management of pediatric neuromuscular diseases. The introduction of disease-modifying therapies for spinal muscular atrophy and Duchenne muscular dystrophy, the expansion of newborn screening programs, and the evolution of multidisciplinary care models have fundamentally altered the natural history and prognosis of conditions that were previously considered uniformly devastating. The rehabilitation physician plays a central role in this evolving landscape, coordinating care across medical disciplines, prescribing adaptive equipment and orthotic devices, guiding exercise programs that balance activity with tissue protection, managing the transition from ambulatory to non-ambulatory phases, and supporting quality of life across the lifespan.

This chapter examines the major neuromuscular diseases encountered in pediatric rehabilitation practice: the dystrophinopathies (DMD and BMD), spinal muscular atrophy, Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, Friedreich ataxia, the congenital myopathies, and myotonic dystrophy. For each condition, the discussion addresses genetics and pathophysiology, clinical presentation and natural history, diagnostic evaluation, current treatment options, and rehabilitation-specific management considerations.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy

### Genetics and Pathophysiology

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) is the most common and most severe of the childhood-onset muscular dystrophies, affecting approximately 1 in 3,500 to 5,000 live male births worldwide. The disease follows an X-linked recessive inheritance pattern, caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene located at Xp21.2. The dystrophin gene is the largest known human gene, spanning approximately 2.4 megabases and containing 79 exons. It encodes dystrophin, a 427-kDa rod-shaped cytoskeletal protein that serves as a critical mechanical link between the intracellular actin cytoskeleton and the extracellular matrix through the dystrophin-associated glycoprotein complex (DAGC).

The most common mutations are large deletions (60-70% of cases), concentrated in two mutational hotspot regions: exons 44-55 and exons 2-20. Duplications account for 5-15% and point mutations or small insertions/deletions account for the remaining 25-30%. In DMD, these mutations disrupt the translational reading frame (out-of-frame mutations), producing a truncated, nonfunctional, and rapidly degraded dystrophin protein. Approximately 30% of cases arise from de novo mutations with no prior family history.

The complete absence of functional dystrophin destabilizes the sarcolemma during muscle contraction, resulting in unregulated calcium influx, protease activation, oxidative stress, chronic inflammation, and progressive myofiber necrosis. Over time, skeletal muscle is replaced by fibrotic and adipose tissue, resulting in progressive weakness and functional decline.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Female carriers of DMD mutations are usually asymptomatic but 2.5-10% develop symptoms (manifesting carriers) due to skewed X-inactivation. All female carriers should receive periodic cardiac screening, as cardiomyopathy may develop independently of skeletal muscle involvement.

### Clinical Presentation and Natural History

Boys with DMD typically present between ages 2 and 5 years, though the diagnostic delay remains significant, with the average age at confirmed diagnosis still approximately 4-5 years despite symptom onset much earlier. Early signs include delayed motor milestones (walking often delayed to 15-18 months), frequent falls, difficulty ascending stairs, toe-walking, and calf pseudohypertrophy (caused by replacement of muscle tissue with fat and fibrotic tissue rather than true muscular hypertrophy). Weakness follows a proximal-to-distal gradient, affecting the pelvic girdle before the shoulder girdle, and the lower extremities before the upper extremities.

The Gower sign is a classic compensatory maneuver in which the child uses the hands to "climb up" the thighs and legs when rising from the floor, reflecting proximal hip and knee extensor weakness. The child first rolls prone, pushes up to hands and knees, then progressively extends by pressing the hands against the shins, knees, and thighs to achieve an upright position. This sign is typically evident by age 3-5 years and is one of the most frequently tested clinical findings on board examinations. Although the Gower maneuver is characteristic of DMD, it is not specific and can be observed in any condition causing proximal lower extremity weakness, including SMA type III, limb-girdle muscular dystrophies, and inflammatory myopathies.

The gait pattern in DMD is characterized by hyperlordosis (compensating for hip extensor weakness), a wide-based waddling gait (compensating for hip abductor weakness, also known as Trendelenburg gait), and progressive toe-walking as ankle plantarflexion contractures develop. The lumbar lordosis serves to shift the center of gravity posterior to the hip joint axis, reducing the demand on weakening hip extensors. As the disease progresses, boys develop a characteristic compensated gait pattern that maintains ambulation through a series of biomechanical adaptations including increased anterior pelvic tilt, knee hyperextension, and equinus positioning at the ankles.

> **Clinical Pearl**: CK is the most sensitive initial screening test for DMD. Values are markedly elevated at 10,000-50,000 IU/L (50-100 times normal), present from birth even before clinical symptoms appear. CK peaks in early childhood and gradually declines as muscle mass is lost.

Cognitive involvement occurs in approximately one-third of boys with DMD. The mean IQ is approximately 85 (one standard deviation below the population mean), with roughly 30% meeting criteria for intellectual disability (IQ below 70). Deficits are particularly notable in verbal working memory. These cognitive findings are attributed to the absence of the brain-expressed Dp71 dystrophin isoform.

Loss of independent ambulation typically occurs between ages 9 and 13, with corticosteroid therapy extending the ambulatory period by 2-5 years. After loss of ambulation, progressive neuromuscular scoliosis develops in over 90% of patients. Dilated cardiomyopathy becomes detectable in most patients by ages 10-15, with fibrosis characteristically beginning in the posterolateral left ventricular wall. Progressive restrictive lung disease follows a predictable pattern: forced vital capacity (FVC) peaks around ages 12-14 and then declines at approximately 5-8% per year. Nocturnal hypoventilation precedes daytime respiratory failure.

With contemporary multidisciplinary care including corticosteroids, cardiac therapies, and ventilatory support, survival into the 30s and beyond has become increasingly common, though historical survival without such interventions was limited to the late teens and early 20s. A meta-analysis of survival data found the median life expectancy for boys born after 1990 to be approximately 28 years, extending to approximately 30 years with ventilatory support.

The natural history of DMD is often conceptualized in clinical stages that guide rehabilitation planning:

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of a young boy presenting with proximal weakness, elevated CK, and calf pseudohypertrophy includes several conditions that must be distinguished from DMD:

- **Becker muscular dystrophy**: Later onset, slower progression, ambulation maintained past age 16; in-frame dystrophin mutations.
- **Limb-girdle muscular dystrophies (LGMD)**: Heterogeneous group with autosomal inheritance; specific protein deficiencies on immunohistochemistry distinguish subtypes.
- **Spinal muscular atrophy type III**: Proximal weakness with Gower sign, but CK is normal or only mildly elevated, tongue fasciculations may be present, and EMG shows a neurogenic pattern rather than myopathic.
- **Inflammatory myopathies**: Dermatomyositis and polymyositis present with proximal weakness and elevated CK but have skin findings (dermatomyositis), inflammatory infiltrates on biopsy, and response to immunosuppression.
- **Emery-Dreifuss muscular dystrophy**: Early contractures (elbows, Achilles tendons, posterior neck) with cardiac conduction defects; emerin or lamin A/C mutations.
- **Congenital muscular dystrophies**: Earlier onset (birth or first months), hypotonia, possible structural brain abnormalities (Walker-Warburg, muscle-eye-brain disease).

### Diagnosis

The diagnostic approach has shifted away from invasive muscle biopsy toward genetic-first strategies. Multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA) is the first-line genetic test, detecting deletions and duplications that account for 70-80% of cases. If MLPA is negative, next-generation sequencing identifies point mutations and small indels. Muscle biopsy is now reserved for cases where genetic testing is inconclusive and demonstrates dystrophic changes (fiber size variation, central nucleation, necrotic and regenerating fibers, endomysial fibrosis, fatty infiltration) with absent dystrophin on immunohistochemistry and western blot.

Electromyography shows a myopathic pattern (low amplitude, short duration, polyphasic motor unit potentials with early recruitment) but is not typically required when genetic testing is available. Newborn CK-based screening programs have been piloted in multiple countries, and expert consensus supports their implementation, although universal newborn screening for DMD has not yet been adopted.

### Corticosteroid Treatment

Corticosteroids remain the only pharmacological treatment with robust evidence for slowing DMD disease progression. Current guidelines recommend initiation when motor function plateaus, typically between ages 4 and 6, before the onset of motor decline.

**Table 4.1: Corticosteroid Regimens in DMD**

| Agent | Dose | Key Considerations |
|-------|------|--------------------|
| Prednisone/prednisolone | 0.75 mg/kg/day | Most extensively studied; daily dosing most common |
| Deflazacort | 0.9 mg/kg/day | FDA approved 2017; may cause less weight gain; higher cataract risk |
| Vamorolone | Weight-based dosing | FDA approved October 2023; dissociative steroid with fewer metabolic side effects |

The benefits of corticosteroid therapy include prolongation of ambulation by 2-5 years, stabilization or improvement of muscle strength, delayed onset and reduced severity of scoliosis, slowed decline in pulmonary function, and potential delay in cardiomyopathy onset. These benefits extend beyond the ambulatory phase, and current practice is to continue corticosteroid therapy indefinitely, adjusting doses for side effects.

Side effects are significant and include Cushingoid weight gain, growth retardation, behavioral changes, cataracts (more common with deflazacort), osteoporosis with vertebral compression fractures, adrenal suppression, glucose intolerance, and immunosuppression. The FOR DMD trial (Finding the Optimum Regimen for Duchenne) compared daily prednisone, daily deflazacort, and intermittent prednisone, finding the most favorable risk-benefit profile with daily deflazacort.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Live vaccines are contraindicated in patients receiving immunosuppressive doses of corticosteroids. Varicella immunity must be confirmed before initiating corticosteroid therapy. Adrenal insufficiency from chronic corticosteroid use is a potentially life-threatening complication requiring stress-dose hydrocortisone during illness or surgery.

### The Birnkrant 2018 DMD Care Considerations

The Birnkrant 2018 care considerations, published as a landmark three-part series in the Lancet Neurology, represent the most comprehensive and widely cited guideline framework for DMD management. These guidelines emphasize a multidisciplinary care model involving neurology, pulmonology, cardiology, orthopedics, endocrinology, gastroenterology, rehabilitation medicine, and psychosocial support.

**Neuromuscular and Rehabilitation Management (Part 1)**:
- Genetic diagnosis is preferred over muscle biopsy as the first-line diagnostic approach.
- Corticosteroid initiation is recommended at the motor function plateau, typically ages 4-6.
- Structured stretching programs targeting the ankles, knees, hip flexors, and iliotibial bands should be performed 4-6 times per week.
- Standing programs are recommended for non-ambulatory patients to maintain bone density and prevent contractures.
- Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) should be used nightly to prevent or delay equinovarus contractures. Knee-ankle-foot orthoses (KAFOs) may prolong ambulation in selected settings.
- Regular submaximal aerobic exercise is encouraged; eccentric and high-resistance exercise should be avoided. Swimming and cycling are recommended even into the non-ambulatory phase.

**Respiratory, Cardiac, and Orthopedic Management (Part 2)**:
- Annual pulmonary function testing beginning at ages 5-6, increasing to every 6 months in the non-ambulatory phase.
- Full respiratory panel (FVC, MIP, MEP, peak cough flow, SpO2, pCO2) performed every 6 months in non-ambulatory patients.
- Lung volume recruitment (breath stacking) when FVC falls below 60% predicted.
- Mechanical assisted cough when FVC is below 50% or peak cough flow falls below 270 L/min.
- Nocturnal noninvasive ventilation when FVC drops below 50%, MIP falls below 60 cmH2O, or SpO2 falls below 95%.
- Baseline cardiac evaluation (ECG and echocardiography or cardiac MRI) at diagnosis or by age 6, then at minimum every 2 years until age 10, then annually. Cardiac MRI is superior to echocardiography for detecting early fibrosis.
- Prophylactic ACE inhibitor or ARB therapy is recommended by age 10 or at first sign of cardiac dysfunction. A perindopril trial demonstrated 10-year survival of 93% (perindopril) versus 66% (placebo). Eplerenone or spironolactone may serve as add-on therapy.
- Scoliosis monitoring with visual inspection annually during ambulation and every 6 months after ambulation loss. Spine radiography is obtained when a curve is detected, with orthopedic referral for curves exceeding 20 degrees.

**Primary Care and Emergency Management (Part 3)**:
- Stress-dose hydrocortisone protocols for adrenal crisis prevention during illness or procedures.
- Avoidance of succinylcholine (risk of rhabdomyolysis and fatal hyperkalemia) and cautious use of volatile halogenated anesthetics. Total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA) is the recommended anesthetic approach.
- Routine screening for depression, anxiety, and behavioral disorders.
- Advance care planning discussions beginning in adolescence.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Succinylcholine is absolutely contraindicated in DMD and all dystrophinopathies. Administration can trigger fatal hyperkalemia and rhabdomyolysis due to the unstable sarcolemma. Potent inhaled halogenated anesthetics also carry risk. Total intravenous anesthesia is the safest approach.

### Exon-Skipping and Gene-Based Therapies

The development of mutation-specific therapies has transformed the therapeutic landscape of DMD over the past decade. Antisense oligonucleotide-based exon-skipping therapies bind to pre-mRNA during splicing, causing specific exons to be "skipped" and restoring the translational reading frame to produce a truncated but partially functional dystrophin protein, effectively converting the DMD phenotype toward a milder BMD-like phenotype.

| Eteplirsen | Exondys 51 | 2016 | Exon 51 | ~13% | PMO antisense oligonucleotide |

Eteplirsen received accelerated FDA approval in 2016 based on a modest increase in dystrophin production on muscle biopsy (0.28-0.93% of normal), though its functional benefit remains debated.

Gene therapy using adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors to deliver a truncated micro-dystrophin gene has emerged as a promising approach. Delandistrogene moxeparvovec (Elevidys), using an AAVrh74 vector, received FDA accelerated approval in June 2023 for ambulatory boys with DMD ages 4-5. Challenges include pre-existing AAV immunity, immune responses to the vector or transgene, uncertain durability of expression, and the limitation of one-time dosing.

Additional therapeutic advances include givinostat, a histone deacetylase inhibitor with anti-fibrotic and anti-inflammatory properties (FDA approved March 2024 for DMD ages 6 and older based on the EPIDYS trial), and vamorolone, a dissociative steroid that provides anti-inflammatory effects with fewer traditional steroid side effects (FDA approved October 2023 based on the VISION-DMD trial). Ataluren, which promotes ribosomal read-through of premature stop codons in nonsense mutations (approximately 10-15% of DMD), is approved in the European Union but did not meet its primary endpoint in the ACT DMD trial and has not received FDA approval.

### Scoliosis Management in DMD

Neuromuscular scoliosis develops in over 90% of non-ambulatory patients with DMD, typically presenting as a progressive long C-shaped collapsing curve. Unlike idiopathic scoliosis, bracing is generally ineffective at preventing curve progression in DMD, though orthoses may provide sitting support and comfort.

Posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation is indicated when the Cobb angle exceeds 20-25 degrees in a growing child or exceeds 40-50 degrees in any patient. Earlier surgery is preferred while pulmonary function remains adequate, with an FVC above 30-40% considered the threshold for acceptable operative risk. Preoperative optimization includes comprehensive pulmonary function assessment, cardiac evaluation, nutritional status optimization, and stress-dose corticosteroid planning.

Spinal fusion improves sitting balance and quality of life. It does not improve pulmonary function but may slow its decline. Corticosteroid use has reduced and delayed the need for scoliosis surgery in many patients.

### Transition of Care and Adult Issues

As survival in DMD has extended into the third and fourth decades, transition of care from pediatric to adult-oriented services has become a critical issue. The Birnkrant 2018 guidelines specifically address transitions, recommending that planning begin in adolescence and encompass medical care coordination, educational and vocational planning, psychosocial development, and advance care planning.

Key adult-oriented concerns include:

- **Respiratory**: Many adults with DMD are ventilator-dependent (noninvasive or invasive). Access to respiratory care providers experienced with neuromuscular ventilation is essential. Emergency protocols for ventilator malfunction must be in place.
- **Cardiac**: Progressive cardiomyopathy requires ongoing cardiology follow-up. Heart failure management may include advanced therapies including cardiac transplantation in selected cases, though this remains controversial.
- **Psychosocial**: Depression, anxiety, and social isolation are common in young adults with DMD. Access to mental health services, peer support networks, and community integration programs is important. Sexuality and relationship counseling should be offered.
- **Pain**: Chronic pain from contractures, immobility, and osteoporosis-related fractures is underrecognized and undertreated in adults with DMD. Multimodal pain management approaches are preferred over opioid monotherapy.
- **Nutrition**: Dysphagia and gastroparesis worsen with disease progression. Nutritional monitoring, swallowing evaluation, and consideration of gastrostomy for inadequate oral intake are ongoing issues.
- **Bone health**: Osteoporosis from corticosteroid use, immobility, and muscle weakness places patients at risk for fragility fractures, particularly vertebral compression fractures. Vitamin D supplementation, DXA monitoring, and bisphosphonate therapy for fracture treatment are standard components of care.

### Palliative Care Integration

Palliative care principles should be integrated throughout the disease trajectory of DMD, not reserved for end-of-life situations. Early palliative care focuses on symptom management (pain, dyspnea, fatigue, depression), quality of life optimization, advance care planning discussions, and support for patients and families in decision-making about life-sustaining treatments. The goal is to align medical interventions with the patient's values and preferences while maintaining the best possible quality of life at every stage of the disease.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Advance care planning conversations should begin in adolescence for patients with DMD, while the patient is cognitively able to participate in decisions about their own care. Topics include preferences regarding mechanical ventilation (noninvasive versus tracheostomy), cardiopulmonary resuscitation, hospitalization, and goals for quality of life. These conversations should be revisited periodically as the disease progresses and the patient's perspective may evolve.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Becker Muscular Dystrophy

### The Reading Frame Hypothesis

Becker muscular dystrophy (BMD) is caused by mutations in the same dystrophin gene at Xp21.2 as DMD. The critical distinction lies in the type of mutation and its effect on the translational reading frame. The reading frame hypothesis, proposed by Monaco and colleagues in 1988, provides the molecular basis for distinguishing DMD from BMD: out-of-frame mutations disrupt the reading frame and produce a truncated, nonfunctional, rapidly degraded dystrophin protein, resulting in the severe DMD phenotype. In-frame mutations preserve the reading frame and allow production of a shortened but partially functional dystrophin protein, resulting in the milder BMD phenotype. This rule holds true in approximately 90-95% of cases.

### Clinical Distinctions from DMD

BMD affects approximately 1 in 18,000 to 30,000 live male births, making it 3-6 times less common than DMD. Symptom onset is later, typically between ages 5 and 15 years, though the range extends from childhood into the third decade. The defining clinical criterion distinguishing BMD from DMD is the retention of independent ambulation beyond age 16; loss of ambulation before age 16 classifies the patient as having the DMD phenotype regardless of the molecular finding.

CK is elevated but generally lower than in DMD (2,000-20,000 IU/L). Calf pseudohypertrophy is present. Cognition is generally normal or mildly affected. Contractures and scoliosis are less common and less severe.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Dilated cardiomyopathy in BMD can be disproportionately severe relative to skeletal muscle involvement. Cardiac failure may be the presenting complaint or the primary cause of morbidity, even in ambulatory patients. Cardiac disease is the leading cause of death in BMD, with mean age of death in the mid-40s. Aggressive cardiac surveillance and treatment with ACE inhibitors or ARBs is essential.

Management of BMD is primarily supportive, centered on cardiac monitoring (echocardiography at diagnosis and regularly thereafter), physical therapy, contracture prevention, and activity modification. The same anesthesia precautions as DMD apply (avoid succinylcholine, use TIVA). Corticosteroids are sometimes used but lack the robust evidence base available for DMD.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Spinal Muscular Atrophy

### Genetics and Pathophysiology

Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) is an autosomal recessive neurodegenerative disease caused by biallelic loss-of-function mutations in the survival motor neuron 1 (SMN1) gene on chromosome 5q13. The most common mutation is homozygous deletion of exon 7, present in approximately 95% of affected individuals. Loss of SMN protein leads to progressive degeneration of alpha motor neurons in the anterior horn of the spinal cord and brainstem motor nuclei, producing denervation atrophy of skeletal muscle.

The SMN2 gene is a paralogous "backup" gene that differs from SMN1 by a single C-to-T transition in exon 7. This nucleotide change causes predominant exon 7 skipping during splicing, so that SMN2 produces approximately 90% truncated, unstable protein (delta-7 SMN) and only approximately 10% full-length functional SMN protein. The number of SMN2 copies inversely correlates with disease severity, as each additional copy contributes a small additional amount of functional SMN protein.

SMA has an incidence of approximately 1 in 6,000 to 10,000 live births, with a carrier frequency of approximately 1 in 40 to 60 in the general population. SMA type I (Werdnig-Hoffmann disease) was historically the most common genetic cause of infant death, a distinction that has been transformed by the advent of disease-modifying therapies and newborn screening.

### Classification

**Table 4.3: Classification of Spinal Muscular Atrophy**

**Type I** presents with severe hypotonia ("floppy infant"), poor head control, weak cry, feeding difficulties, tongue fasciculations, and a characteristic bell-shaped chest resulting from intercostal muscle weakness with relative diaphragm sparing, producing paradoxical breathing. Reflexes are absent. Without treatment, death from respiratory failure typically occurs before age 2.

**Type II** children achieve independent sitting but never stand or walk independently. They develop progressive scoliosis (nearly universal), joint contractures, a fine hand tremor (polyminimyoclonus), and respiratory compromise. Survival into adulthood is common.

**Type III** patients achieve independent ambulation but may lose this ability later. The Gower sign may be present, and calf pseudohypertrophy can occur, mimicking DMD. CK may be mildly elevated (up to 4-10 times normal). Lifespan is normal.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The bell-shaped chest in SMA type I is caused by intercostal muscle weakness with preserved diaphragmatic strength. This produces paradoxical (abdominal) breathing, which is a clinically important distinguishing feature. Tongue fasciculations are another hallmark finding that should raise suspicion for SMA in any hypotonic infant.

### Diagnosis and Newborn Screening

Genetic testing is the first-line diagnostic approach. Quantitative analysis of SMN1 (confirming homozygous deletion or mutation) establishes the diagnosis. SMN2 copy number determination is critical for prognostication and treatment planning. EMG and nerve conduction studies show a denervation pattern (fibrillations, positive sharp waves, large-amplitude long-duration motor unit potentials reflecting chronic reinnervation, reduced recruitment) with reduced compound muscle action potential (CMAP) amplitudes and normal sensory nerve responses. Muscle biopsy, if performed, shows a neurogenic pattern with group atrophy.

Newborn screening for SMA was added to the United States Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) in 2018 and has since been implemented in most states. The screening test detects homozygous SMN1 exon 7 deletion from dried blood spots and identifies approximately 95% of cases (missing the small percentage caused by point mutations). Newborn screening has fundamentally transformed SMA outcomes by enabling presymptomatic treatment, which can achieve near-normal motor development.

### Disease-Modifying Therapies

Three FDA-approved therapies target different aspects of SMN protein biology, and all have demonstrated transformative efficacy compared to the natural history of untreated disease.

**Table 4.4: FDA-Approved SMA Therapies**

| Feature | Nusinersen (Spinraza) | Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma) | Risdiplam (Evrysdi) |
|---------|-----------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Mechanism | Antisense oligonucleotide; modifies SMN2 splicing | AAV9 gene replacement; delivers functional SMN1 | Small molecule; SMN2 splicing modifier |
| Route | Intrathecal injection | Single IV infusion | Oral (liquid), daily |
| Dosing schedule | Loading: days 1, 14, 28, 63; maintenance q4 months | One-time | Daily, continuous |
| FDA approval | December 2016 | May 2019 | August 2020 |
| Age indication | All ages | <2 years | >=2 months |

| Key trial (Type II/III) | CHERISH | -- | SUNFISH |
| Approximate cost | $125,000 per dose | ~$2,125,000 (one-time) | Annual (daily oral) |

**Nusinersen** was the first FDA-approved SMA treatment (December 2016). The ENDEAR trial enrolled 121 infants with SMA type I and demonstrated that 51% of nusinersen-treated infants achieved motor milestone responses versus 0% in the sham-control group, with significant improvement in event-free survival. The trial was stopped early due to overwhelming efficacy. The CHERISH trial in later-onset SMA (types II/III) showed significant improvement in the Hammersmith Functional Motor Scale Expanded (HFMSE) score of +4.0 versus -1.9 points. The NURTURE trial in presymptomatic infants demonstrated remarkable results, with the majority achieving sitting and walking milestones.

**Onasemnogene abeparvovec** is an AAV9-based gene replacement therapy delivering a functional SMN1 gene via a single intravenous infusion, approved for SMA patients under 2 years of age. The STR1VE trial showed that 59% of treated type I infants achieved independent sitting, and all survived to 14 months compared to 25% expected survival without permanent ventilation in the natural history. Long-term follow-up (START trial, 5-year data) confirms sustained efficacy. Safety concerns include hepatotoxicity (elevated transaminases requiring prednisolone pretreatment and monitoring), thrombotic microangiopathy (rare but serious), and thrombocytopenia. A 2024 meta-analysis of 57 studies reported overall survival rates of 95% with onasemnogene compared to 86% with risdiplam and 60% with nusinersen in type I SMA. Recent phase III trials (STEER and STRENGTH) have explored intrathecal onasemnogene administration and sequential therapy in previously treated patients, showing modest but clinically meaningful motor gains.

**Risdiplam** is an oral small-molecule SMN2 splicing modifier that crosses the blood-brain barrier, providing systemic SMN protein upregulation. The FIREFISH trial in type I SMA showed 29% achieved independent sitting at 12 months (versus 0% in natural history), with 85-90% alive without permanent ventilation. The SUNFISH trial in type II/III SMA demonstrated statistically significant improvement in MFM-32 scores. Risdiplam offers practical advantages: oral administration, no intrathecal injections, no concerns about pre-existing AAV immunity, and no age restriction (approved from 2 months of age).

> **Clinical Pearl**: The key principle across all SMA therapies is that earlier treatment, particularly presymptomatic treatment identified through newborn screening, produces dramatically better outcomes. Once motor neurons are lost, they cannot be replaced. High baseline function at the time of treatment initiation is the strongest predictor of favorable response.

### SMA Functional Assessment Scales

- **CHOP-INTEND** (Children's Hospital of Philadelphia Infant Test of Neuromuscular Disorders): 0-64 points; primary outcome measure for type I trials. Assesses 16 items of neuromuscular function in infants. A score below 40 is typical for untreated type I SMA; treated infants may achieve scores above 50-60.
- **HINE-2** (Hammersmith Infant Neurological Examination, Section 2): Motor milestone assessment for infants, evaluating head control, sitting, rolling, crawling, standing, and walking. Used as a primary or secondary outcome in type I trials.
- **HFMSE** (Hammersmith Functional Motor Scale Expanded): 0-66 points; primary outcome for type II/III trials. A change of 3 or more points is considered clinically meaningful. Assesses gross motor function including rolling, sitting, standing, and walking activities.
- **RULM** (Revised Upper Limb Module): 0-37 points; for non-ambulatory patients. Measures upper extremity function including reaching, grasping, and manipulation tasks that are functionally relevant for daily activities.
- **MFM-32** (Motor Function Measure): 0-96 across 3 dimensions (standing/transfers, axial/proximal, distal); validated for neuromuscular diseases ages 6-60. Used as the primary outcome measure in the SUNFISH trial of risdiplam.

### SMA Rehabilitation Considerations

Rehabilitation management of SMA has been transformed by disease-modifying therapies but remains essential regardless of treatment status. For type I SMA, priorities include respiratory support (secretion clearance, assisted cough, ventilatory management), nutritional support (addressing swallowing difficulties, gastrostomy when needed), positioning and seating to optimize posture and respiratory mechanics, and adaptive equipment to promote participation and development.

For type II SMA, rehabilitation focuses on maintaining and maximizing sitting function, preventing and managing scoliosis (which develops in nearly all patients), contracture prevention through stretching and orthotic management, standing programs using standing frames or standers, power mobility assessment and provision (typically by age 2-3 years), and upper extremity function preservation.

For type III SMA, the focus shifts to maintaining ambulation as long as safe and efficient, gait training with or without orthotic support, fall prevention strategies, and transition planning for potential loss of ambulation.

> **Clinical Pearl**: With disease-modifying therapies, many children with SMA are now achieving motor milestones that were never possible in the natural history of their disease. Rehabilitation professionals must adapt their assessment and treatment approaches to support these new functional trajectories. Children treated presymptomatically may achieve near-normal motor development but may still have subtle deficits in endurance, strength, or motor quality that benefit from therapeutic intervention.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease

### Overview

Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease (CMT), also known as hereditary motor and sensory neuropathy (HMSN), is the most common inherited peripheral neuropathy, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 2,500. It is a genetically heterogeneous group of disorders with more than 100 causative genes identified. Despite this genetic diversity, the clinical presentation is unified by a characteristic syndrome of length-dependent, distal-predominant motor and sensory neuropathy.

The hallmark clinical features include distal weakness and atrophy (particularly of the peroneal muscles), sensory loss in a stocking-glove distribution, pes cavus, hammertoes, depressed or absent deep tendon reflexes, and a steppage gait caused by foot drop. The classic description of the lower extremity appearance is "inverted champagne bottle" legs, reflecting prominent distal atrophy with preserved proximal musculature.

### Classification and Genetics

CMT is classified by inheritance pattern, pathophysiology (demyelinating versus axonal), and nerve conduction velocity. The fundamental electrophysiological distinction between demyelinating and axonal forms is defined by the median motor nerve conduction velocity threshold of 38 m/s.

**Table 4.5: CMT Classification**

| Type | Pathophysiology | Inheritance | NCV (Median Motor) | Key Gene/Locus | Histology |
|------|----------------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------|
| CMT1A | Demyelinating | AD | <38 m/s (often 15-30) | PMP22 duplication, 17p11.2 | Onion bulbs |
| CMT1B | Demyelinating | AD | <38 m/s | MPZ (myelin protein zero), 1q22 | Onion bulbs |
| CMT2A | Axonal | AD | >38 m/s | MFN2 (mitofusin 2), 1p36 | Axonal degeneration |
| CMT3 (Dejerine-Sottas) | Severe demyelinating | AD or AR | <10 m/s | PMP22, MPZ, or EGR2 (point mutations) | Prominent onion bulbs |
| CMT4 | Demyelinating | AR | <38 m/s | Multiple genes | Onion bulbs |
| CMTX1 | Intermediate | X-linked | 25-40 m/s (males) | GJB1 (connexin 32), Xq13 | Mixed features |

**CMT1A** is the most common form, representing approximately 50% of all CMT cases. It is caused by a 1.5-megabase duplication on chromosome 17p11.2 containing the PMP22 (peripheral myelin protein 22) gene. The pathophysiology results from a gene dosage effect: overexpression of PMP22 disrupts normal myelination. Onset typically occurs in the first two decades with slowly progressive distal weakness, foot drop, hand weakness, sensory loss, areflexia, pes cavus, and hammertoes.

The nerve biopsy hallmark of demyelinating CMT is the "onion bulb" formation, consisting of concentric layers of Schwann cell processes surrounding demyelinated and remyelinated axons, reflecting repeated cycles of demyelination and remyelination.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The reciprocal deletion of the 17p11.2 region (complementary to the CMT1A duplication) causes hereditary neuropathy with liability to pressure palsies (HNPP), characterized by episodic demyelinating neuropathies at compression sites (e.g., carpal tunnel, peroneal nerve at the fibular head). Nerve biopsy in HNPP shows "tomaculous" (sausage-shaped) myelin swellings.

**CMT2A** is the most common axonal subtype, caused by mutations in the MFN2 (mitofusin 2) gene. Motor nerve conduction velocities are normal or near-normal (greater than 38 m/s) because myelin is intact; reduced CMAP amplitudes reflect the underlying axonal loss. Nerve biopsy shows axonal degeneration without onion bulb formation. CMT2A is often more severe with earlier onset than CMT1A.

**CMT3 (Dejerine-Sottas disease)** represents a severe demyelinating neuropathy with onset in infancy, severely slowed NCVs (below 10 m/s), palpably enlarged nerves, and significant developmental delay. It is now often classified as a severe form of CMT1 rather than a separate entity.

**CMT4** subtypes are autosomal recessive demyelinating neuropathies that are generally more severe with earlier onset and more common in consanguineous populations.

**CMTX1** is the second most common CMT form overall (10-15% of cases), caused by mutations in the GJB1 gene encoding connexin 32 on the X chromosome. Males are more severely affected than females. NCVs are intermediate (25-40 m/s in males), falling between the demyelinating and axonal ranges. Transient central nervous system white matter lesions may occur.

### Rehabilitation Management

No disease-modifying pharmacotherapy is currently available for any form of CMT. Management is centered on rehabilitation and supportive care, and the rehabilitation physician often serves as the primary coordinator of care for these patients.

**Physical therapy** focuses on stretching (particularly the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia to address progressive equinovarus deformity), strengthening (avoiding eccentric overload that could accelerate axonal damage in already compromised nerves), balance training (to address proprioceptive deficits), and aerobic conditioning. A home exercise program is essential for long-term management.

**Orthotic management** is a cornerstone of CMT rehabilitation. Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) for foot drop are the most commonly prescribed intervention. The choice between rigid and articulating AFOs depends on the degree of ankle weakness and the patient's functional level. Custom molded shoe inserts (orthotics) address pes cavus deformity, redistribute plantar pressures, and reduce the risk of callosities and pressure injuries. Over-the-counter arch supports are generally insufficient for the degree of foot deformity in CMT.

**Surgical management** of foot deformities is frequently necessary as the disease progresses. Common procedures include plantar fasciotomy, posterior tibial tendon transfer to the dorsum of the foot (to improve dorsiflexion), calcaneal osteotomy to correct hindfoot varus, metatarsal osteotomies for forefoot correction, and hammer toe correction. Surgery is typically considered when orthotic management no longer adequately compensates for the deformity, when calluses or skin breakdown develops despite orthotics, or when the deformity is progressing despite conservative measures.

**Hand rehabilitation** becomes increasingly important as CMT progresses. Intrinsic hand muscle weakness leads to difficulty with fine motor tasks such as buttoning, writing, and manipulating small objects. Adaptive equipment (built-up handles, button hooks, zipper pulls), hand therapy to maintain ROM and strength, and custom hand splints may be indicated.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Vincristine (a chemotherapeutic agent) must be avoided in patients with CMT, as it can cause devastating, irreversible worsening of the neuropathy. This pharmacogenomic interaction is a frequently tested board topic. All patients with CMT should carry medical alert identification warning against vincristine use. Other potentially neurotoxic medications (nitrofurantoin, metronidazole, cisplatin, taxanes) should also be used with caution.

**Neuropathic pain** is common in CMT, affecting an estimated 50-70% of patients, and may be managed with gabapentin, pregabalin, or duloxetine. Pain can be multifactorial, resulting from neuropathic mechanisms, biomechanical stress from foot deformity, and musculoskeletal strain from compensatory gait patterns.

**Fatigue** is an underrecognized but prevalent symptom in CMT that significantly impacts quality of life and participation. Management includes energy conservation strategies, activity pacing, aerobic conditioning within tolerance, and treatment of contributing factors such as sleep disturbance and depression.

Genetic counseling is essential given the varied inheritance patterns across CMT subtypes.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Friedreich Ataxia

### Genetics and Pathophysiology

Friedreich ataxia (FRDA) is the most common hereditary ataxia, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 50,000 and a carrier frequency of approximately 1 in 100 in European-descent populations. It follows autosomal recessive inheritance, caused by mutations in the FXN gene on chromosome 9q21.11, which encodes the mitochondrial protein frataxin.

Approximately 96% of affected individuals are homozygous for a GAA trinucleotide repeat expansion in intron 1 of the FXN gene. Normal alleles contain 5-33 repeats, premutation alleles 34-65 repeats, and pathogenic alleles 66 to over 1,700 repeats. The expanded GAA repeat causes heterochromatin formation through DNA triplex (sticky DNA) structures, leading to transcriptional silencing and severely reduced frataxin protein levels (5-30% of normal).

Frataxin is a mitochondrial protein involved in iron-sulfur cluster assembly. Its deficiency leads to mitochondrial iron accumulation, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and cellular energy failure. The primary sites of pathology are the dorsal root ganglia, spinocerebellar tracts, corticospinal tracts, dorsal columns, and the heart (hypertrophy and fibrosis).

GAA repeat size inversely correlates with age of onset and directly correlates with disease severity; larger repeats produce earlier onset and more severe disease.

### Clinical Features

Onset typically occurs before age 25, with a mean age of 10-15 years. The neurological presentation includes progressive gait and limb ataxia (both cerebellar and sensory in nature), dysarthria, loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia from dorsal root ganglion and peripheral nerve involvement), loss of proprioception and vibration sense (dorsal column degeneration), positive Babinski sign (corticospinal tract involvement), and nystagmus.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The combination of areflexia (lower motor neuron finding) and Babinski sign (upper motor neuron finding) in a young patient with progressive ataxia is highly suggestive of Friedreich ataxia. This mixed pattern of upper and lower motor neuron signs is a classic board-tested association.

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is the most common cause of death, occurring in approximately 60% of patients. Concentric left ventricular hypertrophy may eventually transition to dilated cardiomyopathy. Cardiac arrhythmias are common. Skeletal manifestations include scoliosis (over 80%), pes cavus, and hammertoes (phenotypically overlapping with CMT). Diabetes mellitus develops in approximately 30% of patients due to pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction and insulin resistance. Optic atrophy and sensorineural hearing loss may occur.

Most patients become wheelchair-dependent within 10-15 years of symptom onset. Mean age of death is approximately 37 years, primarily from cardiac causes.

### Diagnosis

Genetic testing for GAA repeat expansion is definitive. Nerve conduction studies demonstrate an axonal sensory neuropathy (absent or reduced sensory nerve action potentials with normal motor nerve conduction velocities), an important electrophysiological distinction. Echocardiography reveals concentric left ventricular hypertrophy. MRI may show cervical spinal cord atrophy. Frataxin protein levels are reduced to 5-30% of normal.

### Treatment

Omaveloxolone (Skyclarys) was approved by the FDA in February 2023 as the first and only FDA-approved treatment for Friedreich ataxia. It is an Nrf2 activator that enhances expression of antioxidant genes, reduces oxidative stress, and improves mitochondrial function. The MOXIe trial (a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 2 trial in 103 patients aged 16-40) demonstrated improvement of 1.55 points on the modified Friedreich Ataxia Rating Scale (mFARS) versus worsening of 0.85 points in the placebo group (difference -2.40, p=0.014). The drug is taken as a 150-mg oral daily dose and is approved for patients aged 16 years and older.

Supportive management remains critical: physical therapy targeting balance, coordination, and strengthening; occupational therapy; speech therapy for dysarthria; cardiac monitoring and treatment (beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors); scoliosis management; diabetes management; and orthotic support for pes cavus.

### Rehabilitation in Friedreich Ataxia

The rehabilitation approach to Friedreich ataxia must address the unique challenges of progressive cerebellar and sensory ataxia. Key rehabilitation components include:

- **Gait and balance training**: Progressing from community ambulation with assistive devices (canes, walkers) to wheelchair mobility as the disease advances. Balance exercises that challenge proprioceptive and vestibular systems may slow functional decline.
- **Fall prevention**: Home safety assessments, grab bars, non-slip surfaces, and fall risk education for patients and families.
- **Adaptive technology**: Voice-activated technology, adapted computer access, and environmental control systems become increasingly important as upper extremity coordination declines.
- **Cardiac rehabilitation**: While formal cardiac rehab protocols have not been specifically validated in Friedreich ataxia, the principles of graded aerobic exercise within cardiac safety parameters apply.
- **Scoliosis management**: Monitoring and bracing for progressive scoliosis; surgical fusion when curves exceed 40-50 degrees or impair sitting balance.
- **Psychosocial support**: Friedreich ataxia typically manifests during adolescence, a period of critical identity formation. The progressive nature of the disease, combined with the cognitive awareness of declining function, places patients at elevated risk for depression and anxiety. Mental health screening and support should be integrated into routine care.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Friedreich ataxia is the most common hereditary ataxia, but ataxia in a pediatric patient has a broad differential diagnosis that includes posterior fossa tumors, Joubert syndrome, ataxia-telangiectasia, mitochondrial disorders, and vitamin E deficiency. The combination of progressive ataxia with areflexia, Babinski sign, cardiomyopathy, and scoliosis in a school-age child should prompt testing for GAA expansion in the FXN gene.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Congenital Myopathies

### Overview and Distinguishing Features

The congenital myopathies are a genetically diverse group of muscle diseases that present at birth or in infancy with hypotonia and weakness. They are defined by characteristic structural abnormalities visible on muscle biopsy. A critical distinction from the muscular dystrophies is that congenital myopathies are generally static or slowly progressive (rather than relentlessly degenerative), CK is normal or only mildly elevated, and biopsy shows structural changes (rods, cores, central nuclei) rather than the dystrophic triad of necrosis, regeneration, and fibrosis.

### Major Types

**Table 4.6: Congenital Myopathies — Classification by Biopsy Findings**

| Type | Biopsy Hallmark | Stain | Key Gene(s) | Inheritance | Critical Association |
|------|----------------|-------|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Nemaline myopathy | Nemaline rods (Z-disc-derived, alpha-actinin/actin) | Gomori trichrome | NEB (nebulin), ACTA1 (actin) | AR (NEB), AD/AR (ACTA1) | Facial weakness, respiratory insufficiency |
| Central core disease | Central cores (devoid of mitochondria/oxidative enzymes) | NADH / oxidative stains | RYR1 (ryanodine receptor 1) | AD (most), AR | Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility |
| Centronuclear/myotubular myopathy | Centrally located nuclei | H&E | MTM1 (X-linked), DNM2 (AD), BIN1 (AR) | Variable | X-linked form: severe neonatal, often fatal |
| Congenital fiber type disproportion | Type 1 fibers >12-25% smaller than type 2 | ATPase | Multiple (TPM3, ACTA1, RYR1, SELENON) | Variable | Often improves with age |

**Nemaline myopathy** spans a clinical spectrum from severe neonatal forms with respiratory failure at birth to mild childhood-onset forms. The characteristic finding on Gomori trichrome staining is the presence of nemaline rods, rod-shaped inclusions derived from Z-disc material composed of alpha-actinin and actin. Common features include facial weakness (elongated face), bulbar weakness, and proximal-predominant limb weakness. The most common genetic cause is mutations in NEB (nebulin, autosomal recessive).

**Central core disease** is caused by mutations in the RYR1 (ryanodine receptor 1) gene. Biopsy shows well-defined central cores devoid of mitochondria and oxidative enzyme activity running longitudinally through type 1 fibers, visible on NADH and other oxidative stains. Clinically, patients present with hypotonia, proximal weakness, motor delay, hip dislocation, and scoliosis. The disease is often non-progressive or only slowly progressive.

> **Clinical Pearl**: Central core disease and malignant hyperthermia susceptibility share a common genetic basis through RYR1 mutations. All patients with central core disease (and their family members carrying RYR1 mutations) must be treated as malignant hyperthermia-susceptible. Volatile halogenated anesthetics and succinylcholine are absolutely contraindicated. This anesthesia risk is a high-yield board topic.

**Centronuclear (myotubular) myopathy** is characterized by centrally located nuclei in many muscle fibers (normally, nuclei are located at the fiber periphery). The X-linked form (MTM1 gene, encoding myotubularin) is the most severe, presenting with profound neonatal hypotonia and respiratory failure, and is often fatal in infancy. The autosomal dominant form (DNM2 gene, encoding dynamin 2) is milder with later onset. The autosomal recessive form (BIN1 gene, encoding amphiphysin 2) is of intermediate severity.

**Congenital fiber type disproportion (CFTD)** is defined by type 1 fibers being significantly smaller (greater than 12-25% difference in mean diameter) than type 2 fibers without other specific structural abnormalities. Multiple genes have been implicated. The prognosis is generally favorable, with many patients showing improvement over time.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Myotonic Dystrophy

### Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1

Myotonic dystrophy type 1 (DM1, Steinert disease) is the most common muscular dystrophy in adults and one of the most complex multisystem genetic disorders. It follows autosomal dominant inheritance, caused by a CTG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the 3' untranslated region of the DMPK (DM protein kinase) gene on chromosome 19q13.3. Normal alleles contain 5-34 repeats; premutation alleles contain 35-49 repeats (unstable, may expand in offspring); and pathogenic alleles contain 50 to over 4,000 repeats.

The pathophysiology involves a toxic RNA gain-of-function mechanism. Expanded CUG repeats in the mRNA form hairpin structures that sequester RNA-binding proteins, particularly MBNL1 (muscleblind-like 1), causing widespread splicing dysregulation (a "spliceopathy") affecting multiple organ systems.

#### Anticipation

Genetic anticipation is a hallmark of DM1 and a major board concept. The CTG repeat tends to expand in successive generations, particularly with maternal transmission, producing earlier onset and more severe disease in offspring. A classic generational pattern is: grandparent with cataracts and mild myotonia, parent with classic adult-onset DM1, and child with congenital DM1 resulting from massive repeat expansion.

#### Clinical Forms

**Table 4.7: Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1 — Clinical Spectrum**

| Form | CTG Repeats | Onset | Key Features |
|------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| Premutation | 35-49 | Asymptomatic | No symptoms; repeat may expand in offspring |
| Mild/Late-onset | 50-150 | Middle-age/elderly | Cataracts, mild myotonia; normal lifespan |
| Classic adult | 100-1,000 | 10-30 years | Myotonia, distal weakness, cardiac conduction defects, cataracts, multisystem |
| Congenital | >1,000 (often 1,500-4,000+) | Birth | Severe hypotonia, respiratory failure, facial diplegia, intellectual disability |

**Classic adult DM1** presents with myotonia (delayed muscle relaxation after contraction, demonstrated as inability to release handgrip or by percussion myotonia), progressive distal-predominant weakness (unlike most myopathies, which are proximal), facial weakness with temporalis and masseter atrophy producing a "hatchet face" appearance, ptosis, and sternocleidomastoid weakness. Multisystem involvement includes cardiac conduction defects (AV block, bundle branch block) with risk of sudden cardiac death, respiratory muscle weakness with central apnea, gastrointestinal dysmotility, insulin resistance and diabetes, hypogonadism, thyroid dysfunction, cognitive decline, and posterior subcapsular cataracts (often the earliest detectable sign).

**Congenital DM1** is almost exclusively maternally transmitted and results from massive repeat expansions (typically over 1,000 repeats). It presents at birth with severe hypotonia, respiratory failure frequently requiring ventilatory support, feeding difficulties, arthrogryposis (joint contractures), facial diplegia, and a characteristic tented upper lip. Neonatal mortality is significant (approximately 25% in severe cases). Survivors develop moderate to severe intellectual disability, motor delays, and eventually the myotonia and progressive weakness typical of classic DM1 in childhood and adolescence.

> **Clinical Pearl**: When an infant presents with unexplained hypotonia and joint contractures, always examine the mother for clinical signs of myotonic dystrophy (grip myotonia, facial weakness, cataracts). The mother may be undiagnosed, and recognizing the maternal phenotype is key to diagnosing congenital DM1.

#### Diagnosis

Genetic testing for CTG repeat expansion in the DMPK gene is definitive. EMG characteristically shows myotonic discharges with waxing-and-waning amplitude and frequency, producing the classic "dive bomber" sound on audio monitor. Myotonic discharges may not be present in the congenital form during infancy. Slit lamp examination reveals posterior subcapsular cataracts.

### Myotonic Dystrophy Type 2

Myotonic dystrophy type 2 (DM2, also known as proximal myotonic myopathy or PROMM) is caused by a CCTG tetranucleotide repeat expansion in intron 1 of the CNBP gene on chromosome 3q21.3. Key distinctions from DM1 include: onset in adulthood (20-60 years), proximal-predominant rather than distal weakness, absence of a congenital form, and no clear genetic anticipation. DM2 is generally milder than DM1. While less relevant to pediatric rehabilitation, awareness of DM2 is important for differential diagnosis and board examinations.

### Rehabilitation Considerations in Myotonic Dystrophy

Management of myotonic dystrophy in pediatric rehabilitation encompasses several unique challenges:

**Congenital DM1**: Neonatal management requires respiratory support (ranging from supplemental oxygen to mechanical ventilation), feeding therapy (swallowing dysfunction is common, and gastrostomy may be required), and developmental stimulation from birth. Motor development is delayed, and many children require physical and occupational therapy throughout childhood. Cognitive impairment (moderate to severe in most cases) necessitates special education services and potentially speech-language pathology for communication support. Annual ophthalmologic screening for cataracts should begin in childhood. Cardiac monitoring (annual ECG at minimum, with Holter monitoring if conduction abnormalities are detected) is essential given the risk of cardiac arrhythmias and sudden death.

**Childhood-onset DM1**: Children who present later (school-age) may have more subtle findings initially, including learning difficulties, behavioral problems (often misdiagnosed as ADHD), mild facial weakness, and grip myotonia. Rehabilitation interventions focus on fine motor support, adaptive strategies for myotonia (warming up before activities, avoiding cold exposure, using broader grips), and exercise programs that balance activity with fatigue management. Excessive daytime somnolence is a significant functional impairment that may respond to modafinil or other wakefulness-promoting agents.

**Multisystem monitoring**: A comprehensive monitoring program for myotonic dystrophy should include annual cardiac evaluation (ECG, echocardiography, and Holter monitoring as indicated), pulmonary function testing (FVC, MIP, MEP), ophthalmologic examination (slit lamp for cataracts), glucose tolerance testing (for diabetes), thyroid function testing, and neurocognitive assessment. Swallowing evaluation should be performed if dysphagia symptoms develop.

**Anesthesia precautions**: Patients with myotonic dystrophy are at increased risk during anesthesia. Succinylcholine should be avoided as it may trigger severe myotonic crises. Volatile anesthetics should be used with caution. Cardiac conduction abnormalities may predispose to arrhythmias during procedures. Respiratory depressant effects of anesthetic agents may be exaggerated due to underlying respiratory muscle weakness and central hypoventilation. Preoperative cardiac and pulmonary evaluation is essential.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Exercise and Rehabilitation in Neuromuscular Diseases

### Principles of Exercise Prescription

Exercise prescription in neuromuscular diseases requires careful consideration of the balance between maintaining function and avoiding overwork injury. The historical recommendation for strict rest has been replaced by evidence supporting the benefits of appropriately prescribed exercise.

**General principles applicable across neuromuscular diseases include:**

1. **Submaximal aerobic exercise** is safe and beneficial in most neuromuscular conditions. Swimming and cycling are particularly recommended in DMD and SMA because they provide low-impact cardiovascular conditioning with reduced eccentric muscle loading.

2. **Eccentric and high-resistance exercise should be avoided** in dystrophic conditions (DMD, BMD), as the unstable sarcolemma makes dystrophic muscle fibers susceptible to contraction-induced damage. Moderate-intensity, low-impact strengthening is preferred.

3. **Stretching programs** are fundamental across all neuromuscular conditions to prevent contractures. In DMD, the Birnkrant guidelines recommend stretching of the ankles, knees, hip flexors, and iliotibial bands 4-6 times per week.

4. **Standing programs** for non-ambulatory patients help maintain bone density, prevent contractures, and provide cardiovascular and respiratory benefits. Standing frames, tilt tables, and supported standing devices are used.

5. **Orthotic management** varies by condition: night AFOs for DMD contracture prevention, daytime AFOs for CMT foot drop, custom footwear for pes cavus deformities.

6. **Assistive technology** should be introduced proactively, including power mobility when ambulation declines, upper extremity supports, and communication devices as needed.

7. **Respiratory muscle training** may benefit patients with progressive respiratory involvement, including inspiratory muscle training and lung volume recruitment techniques.

### Condition-Specific Rehabilitation Considerations

**DMD**: Emphasis on contracture prevention through daily stretching and night splinting, preservation of ambulation through gait aids and KAFOs when appropriate, transition to power mobility with supportive seating when ambulation becomes unsafe or inefficient, standing programs and weight-bearing after loss of ambulation, and respiratory support progression (cough assist, nocturnal NIV, daytime ventilation).

**SMA**: Rehabilitation goals are guided by SMA type and functional level. For type I, the focus is on positioning, respiratory management, and adaptive equipment. For types II and III, therapy addresses mobility optimization, scoliosis management, upper extremity function preservation, and participation. With the advent of disease-modifying therapies, rehabilitation goals are expanding as patients achieve previously unattainable motor milestones.

**CMT**: Focus on AFO management for foot drop, balance and fall prevention, hand therapy and adaptive equipment for fine motor limitations, pain management, and surgical rehabilitation after foot reconstruction procedures.

**Friedreich ataxia**: Balance and coordination training, fall prevention strategies, adaptive equipment for progressive limb ataxia (weighted utensils, adapted writing tools, voice-activated technology), speech therapy for dysarthria, and cardiac rehabilitation principles applied within the context of progressive neurological disease.

**Myotonic dystrophy**: For congenital DM1, initial rehabilitation focuses on respiratory management, feeding support, and developmental stimulation. As children grow, therapy addresses cognitive and motor delays, communication needs, and progressive weakness and myotonia. Adaptive strategies for the grip myotonia (such as using handles with broader grips, avoiding cold exposure which worsens myotonia) are practical considerations. Annual cardiac monitoring for conduction abnormalities should be integrated into the rehabilitation care plan.

### Overwork Weakness and Exercise Prescription Safety

The concept of overwork weakness (also called overuse weakness) is central to exercise prescription in neuromuscular diseases. First described in poliomyelitis survivors, overwork weakness refers to a paradoxical and potentially permanent decline in muscle strength resulting from exercise that exceeds the regenerative capacity of the diseased muscle. In dystrophic conditions, the unstable sarcolemma makes muscle fibers particularly vulnerable to contraction-induced damage, especially during eccentric (lengthening) contractions.

Evidence-based guidelines for exercise safety in neuromuscular diseases include:

1. **Rate of perceived exertion** should remain moderate (Borg scale 11-13 out of 20); patients should not exercise to the point of persistent muscle pain or increased weakness lasting more than 30 minutes post-exercise.
2. **Heart rate monitoring** during aerobic exercise, maintaining intensity at 50-70% of age-predicted maximum heart rate.
3. **Resistance exercise**, when appropriate, should use low resistance with higher repetitions rather than high resistance with low repetitions.
4. **Delayed-onset muscle soreness** lasting more than 24 hours or dark (myoglobinuric) urine after exercise indicates excessive intensity.
5. **Progressive decline in performance** with continued exercise suggests overwork and requires immediate reduction in exercise intensity.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The exercise prescription for a child with a neuromuscular disease should follow the principle of "use it carefully, but use it." Complete inactivity accelerates deconditioning, contracture development, and bone loss, while excessive or inappropriate exercise risks overwork weakness. The rehabilitation physician must individualize the exercise prescription based on the specific diagnosis, disease stage, current functional level, and the patient's response to activity.

### Assistive Technology and Adaptive Equipment

Assistive technology plays an increasingly important role as neuromuscular diseases progress:

- **Power mobility**: Timely provision of power wheelchairs prevents the social isolation and participation restrictions that occur when ambulation becomes unsafe or inefficient. In DMD, power mobility is typically introduced around ages 10-13, though earlier provision for community access is appropriate.
- **Seating and positioning**: Custom molded seating systems in wheelchairs optimize posture, reduce scoliosis progression, and improve respiratory mechanics. Tilt-in-space and recline functions assist with pressure relief and fatigue management.
- **Communication devices**: For conditions affecting bulbar function (SMA type I, congenital myotonic dystrophy), augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) systems may be needed.
- **Environmental controls**: Smart home technology, voice-activated systems, and switch-adapted devices promote independence in daily activities.
- **Upper extremity supports**: Mobile arm supports, balanced forearm orthoses, and robotic arm devices help maintain feeding, grooming, and computer access when arm strength declines.

## [BOARD] Diagnostic Approach to the Floppy Infant

The differential diagnosis of infantile hypotonia is one of the most commonly tested topics in pediatric rehabilitation board examinations. A systematic approach distinguishes central (upper motor neuron) from peripheral (lower motor neuron) causes.

| CK | Normal | Variable (elevated in dystrophies, normal in SMA/CMT) |
| Antigravity movements | Often preserved | Often reduced or absent |

Central causes of infantile hypotonia include hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy, chromosomal abnormalities (trisomy 21, Prader-Willi syndrome), metabolic disorders, structural brain malformations, and cerebral palsy. Peripheral causes include SMA, congenital muscular dystrophies, congenital myopathies, congenital myotonic dystrophy, neonatal myasthenia gravis, and congenital neuropathies.

> **Clinical Pearl**: The "floppy infant" examination should systematically assess tone (passive resistance to movement), strength (antigravity movements, spontaneous activity), reflexes (deep tendon reflexes, Moro, grasp), and associated findings (fasciculations, contractures, respiratory pattern, facial expression, eye movements). The pattern of these findings directs the diagnostic workup and differentiates central from peripheral causes. A hypotonic infant with brisk reflexes and preserved antigravity movements likely has a central cause, while a hypotonic infant with absent reflexes and poor antigravity activity likely has a peripheral neuromuscular cause.

## [BOARD] Genetic Counseling Considerations in Neuromuscular Diseases

Genetic counseling is an integral component of care for all pediatric neuromuscular diseases. Key principles include:

- **X-linked recessive (DMD/BMD)**: Mothers of affected boys have a 50% chance of being carriers. Each pregnancy of a carrier has a 25% chance of producing an affected son, 25% carrier daughter, 25% unaffected son, and 25% non-carrier daughter. Approximately 30% of DMD cases are de novo mutations, meaning the mother is not a carrier. Carrier testing (genetic analysis) and preimplantation/prenatal diagnosis are available.

- **Autosomal recessive (SMA, Friedreich ataxia)**: Parents are obligate carriers with a 25% recurrence risk per pregnancy. Carrier testing is available for family members. Population-based carrier screening for SMA is increasingly offered as part of preconception or prenatal care panels.

- **Autosomal dominant (CMT1A, myotonic dystrophy)**: Each child of an affected parent has a 50% risk of inheriting the mutation. Variable expressivity means family members with the same mutation may have very different clinical severity. In myotonic dystrophy, the phenomenon of anticipation (earlier onset and greater severity in successive generations, particularly with maternal transmission) adds complexity to genetic counseling.

- **Trinucleotide repeat disorders (Friedreich ataxia, myotonic dystrophy)**: The instability of repeat expansions means that repeat length may change between generations. In DM1, massive expansion during maternal transmission can produce congenital DM1 in a child of a mildly affected or even asymptomatic mother.
