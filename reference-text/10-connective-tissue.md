## [BOARD] Key Concepts

### Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA): Overview and ILAR Classification

JIA is the most common chronic rheumatic disease of childhood, with an incidence of 2 to 20 per 100,000 children per year and an estimated prevalence of 70,000 to 100,000 in the United States. It is defined as arthritis of unknown etiology beginning before age 16 years that persists for at least 6 weeks after exclusion of other causes. The International League of Associations for Rheumatology (ILAR) classification replaced older terminology (JRA and JCA) with a system recognizing 7 mutually exclusive subtypes (Petty et al., 2004).

The female-to-male ratio is 2:1 to 3:1 overall, though this varies considerably by subtype. Peak incidence occurs at 1 to 3 years in girls and 8 to 10 years in boys. Pannus formation (proliferative synovitis with granulation tissue invading cartilage and bone) is the pathologic hallmark, analogous to adult rheumatoid arthritis. TNF-alpha plays a pivotal role in inflammatory cell trafficking into the synovium in non-systemic subtypes, while systemic JIA is increasingly recognized as an autoinflammatory condition driven by innate immunity and the IL-1/IL-6 axis.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ANA is a marker of uveitis risk in JIA, not a marker of disease activity. ANA-positive oligoarticular JIA patients have the highest risk of developing chronic anterior uveitis, which is characteristically asymptomatic and requires screening by slit-lamp examination.

### ILAR Classification: The Seven JIA Subtypes

| Subtype | % of JIA | Sex Ratio | Peak Age | ANA | RF | HLA-B27 | Uveitis Risk | Defining Features |
|---------|----------|-----------|----------|-----|-----|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Oligoarticular** (persistent: 4 or fewer joints; extended: more than 4 after 6 months) | 50-60% | F >> M (5:1) | 1-5 yr | + (70-80%) | - | - | HIGH (20-30%) | Knee most common joint; asymmetric; highest uveitis risk; best overall prognosis |
| **RF-negative Polyarticular** (5 or more joints in first 6 months) | 15-20% | F > M (3:1) | Biphasic (1-3 and 9-14 yr) | + (25-40%) | - | - | Moderate (5-10%) | Symmetric involvement; large and small joints; variable course |
| **RF-positive Polyarticular** | 2-7% | F >> M (9:1) | Late childhood / adolescence | + (40-50%) | + | - | Low | Mimics adult RA; aggressive, erosive; rheumatoid nodules possible; worst articular prognosis |
| **Systemic (sJIA / Still disease)** | 10-15% | M = F | Any age (peak 1-5 yr) | - | - | - | Very low | Quotidian spiking fever, evanescent salmon-colored rash, serositis, hepatosplenomegaly; MAS risk 10-15% |
| **Enthesitis-related Arthritis (ERA)** | 5-10% | M >> F (7:1) | > 6 yr (peak 9-12 yr) | - | - | + (60-80%) | Moderate (acute anterior, SYMPTOMATIC) | Lower extremity large joints; enthesitis (Achilles, plantar fascia); sacroiliitis; may progress to ankylosing spondylitis |
| **Psoriatic Arthritis** | 5-8% | F > M (slight) | Biphasic (2-4 and 9-11 yr) | + (young onset) | - | - | Moderate (6-15%) | Dactylitis; nail pitting; psoriatic rash may precede or follow arthritis; bimodal clinical phenotype |
| **Undifferentiated** | 10-15% | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable | Does not fit single category or meets criteria for more than one |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Uveitis in JIA is typically anterior, chronic, and ASYMPTOMATIC — this is fundamentally different from ERA-associated uveitis, which is acute, symptomatic (red, painful eye), and similar to the uveitis seen in adult ankylosing spondylitis. Board questions frequently test this distinction.

**Oligoarticular JIA** is the most common subtype and carries the best articular prognosis. The persistent form (4 or fewer joints throughout the disease course) rarely causes significant joint damage. The extended form (more than 4 joints affected after the initial 6 months) has a less favorable prognosis, approaching that of polyarticular disease. The HLA associations most strongly linked to oligoarticular JIA include DR8, DR11, DR13, and DPw2.

**RF-positive Polyarticular JIA** is essentially adult rheumatoid arthritis occurring in childhood. It shares the same HLA-DR4 association and progresses to erosive disease in the majority. Rheumatoid nodules, while uncommon in children overall, are most frequently seen in this subtype.

**Systemic JIA** is unique among JIA subtypes in its pathogenesis (autoinflammatory rather than autoimmune), its clinical features (prominent systemic inflammation), and its treatment (IL-1 and IL-6 blockade rather than TNF inhibition). The quotidian fever pattern is characteristic: one or two daily temperature spikes to 39 degrees Celsius or higher, with rapid return to baseline or below. The evanescent salmon-colored rash appears during fever and fades between spikes. The sJIA-associated HLA is DRB1*11.

**Psoriatic JIA** has a bimodal onset pattern reflecting two distinct phenotypes. Younger patients (preschool age) resemble oligoarticular JIA with female predominance, ANA positivity, and small joint involvement with dactylitis. Older patients resemble adult psoriatic arthritis with approximately equal sex distribution, enthesitis, axial disease, and HLA-B27 positivity. Importantly, frank psoriasis is absent at presentation in approximately 50% of cases, and nail changes (pitting, onycholysis) are present in 50% to 80%.

### ACR/Arthritis Foundation 2019 Treatment Guidelines

The 2019 ACR/Arthritis Foundation guidelines established a treat-to-target approach with the goal of achieving clinically inactive disease (CID) within 6 months of diagnosis (Ringold et al., 2019; Angeles-Han et al., 2019).

**Stepwise Treatment for Non-Systemic JIA:**

1. **First-line:** NSAIDs (4 to 6 weeks trial) combined with intra-articular corticosteroid injections. Triamcinolone hexacetonide is preferred over triamcinolone acetonide due to longer duration of effect.
2. **Second-line:** Conventional DMARDs. Methotrexate is the first-line DMARD, given at 10 to 15 mg/m2/week (oral or subcutaneous). Leflunomide and sulfasalazine are alternatives.
3. **Third-line:** Biologic DMARDs. TNF inhibitors (etanercept, adalimumab) are first-line biologics for non-systemic JIA.
4. **Further escalation:** Abatacept, tocilizumab, tofacitinib for refractory polyarticular disease.

**Treatment for Systemic JIA:**

For sJIA, the guidelines strongly recommend IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab) or IL-6 inhibitors (tocilizumab) as first-line biologic therapy, over TNF inhibitors. This reflects the distinct autoinflammatory pathogenesis driven by the IL-1/IL-6 axis.

> **Clinical Pearl:** TNF inhibitors are the first-line biologics for non-systemic JIA subtypes, but they are NOT recommended as first-line for systemic JIA. For sJIA, IL-1 or IL-6 blockade is preferred because the disease is driven by innate immunity and the inflammasome rather than by adaptive immune-mediated TNF pathways.

**Wallace Criteria for Clinically Inactive Disease:**

A patient meets CID criteria when all of the following are satisfied:
1. No active arthritis
2. No fever, rash, serositis, splenomegaly, or generalized lymphadenopathy attributable to JIA
3. No active uveitis
4. Normal ESR and/or CRP
5. Physician global assessment at the best possible score
6. Morning stiffness lasting 15 minutes or less

### Uveitis Screening

The ACR 2019 guidelines stratify uveitis screening frequency based on JIA subtype, ANA status, age at onset, and disease duration (Angeles-Han et al., 2019).

| Risk Level | Patient Profile | Screening Frequency |
|------------|----------------|---------------------|
| High | Oligoarticular or RF-negative polyarticular; ANA positive; onset before age 7; disease duration less than 4 years | Every 3 months |
| Moderate | ANA positive with onset at age 7 or older, or disease duration 4 to 7 years | Every 6 months |
| Low | RF-positive polyarticular, sJIA, ERA, psoriatic arthritis, or disease duration greater than 7 years | Every 12 months |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Uveitis can develop even after JIA is in remission. Screening should continue as recommended regardless of the arthritis disease activity status.

### Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)

MAS is a life-threatening hyperinflammatory complication that occurs in 10% to 15% of sJIA patients, with a mortality rate of 8% to 22%. It represents a form of secondary hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) in which there is uncontrolled proliferation and activation of CD8-positive T cells and macrophages. Interferon-gamma is the pivotal cytokine driving this process. MAS can be triggered by infections (especially Epstein-Barr virus), medication changes, or disease flares.

**2016 Classification Criteria for MAS in sJIA (Ravelli et al., 2016):**

In a febrile patient with known or suspected sJIA, MAS is diagnosed when:
- **Ferritin greater than 684 ng/mL** PLUS any 2 of the following:
  - Platelet count 181,000/uL or less
  - AST greater than 48 U/L
  - Triglycerides greater than 156 mg/dL
  - Fibrinogen 360 mg/dL or less

> **Clinical Pearl:** The paradoxical laboratory pattern is pathognomonic for MAS: the ESR may DROP (because fibrinogen, an acute phase reactant that drives ESR, is being consumed) while the ferritin RISES dramatically (often exceeding 10,000 ng/mL). In a sJIA flare without MAS, both ESR and ferritin are typically elevated. This "ESR-ferritin dissociation" is a critical board concept.

**Treatment of MAS:**
- High-dose pulse intravenous methylprednisolone
- Cyclosporine A
- Anakinra (increasingly used as first-line in MAS complicating sJIA)
- Etoposide in refractory cases

### Juvenile Dermatomyositis (JDM)

JDM is the most common pediatric inflammatory myopathy, with an incidence of 2 to 4 per million children per year. Unlike adult dermatomyositis, JDM does not carry an increased risk of underlying malignancy.

**Diagnostic Criteria (Bohan and Peter, 1975):**

Definite JDM requires the characteristic skin rash plus 3 of 4 muscle criteria:
1. Symmetric proximal muscle weakness
2. Elevated muscle enzymes (CK, aldolase, AST, LDH)
3. Myopathic EMG changes (short, small, polyphasic motor unit potentials; fibrillations; positive sharp waves)
4. Muscle biopsy showing inflammation, necrosis, and characteristic perifascicular atrophy

**Hallmark Skin Findings:**

| Finding | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Heliotrope rash | Violaceous (purple-lilac) discoloration of the upper eyelids, often with periorbital edema — pathognomonic |
| Gottron papules | Erythematous or violaceous papules over the MCP, PIP, and DIP joints, knees, and elbows — pathognomonic |
| V-sign | Photosensitive erythematous rash over the anterior chest |
| Shawl sign | Rash over the upper back and shoulders |
| Nail fold capillary changes | Dilated, tortuous, and dropout capillaries visible on nail fold capillaroscopy; correlate with disease activity |

**Myositis-Specific Antibodies in JDM (Rider and Nistala, 2016):**

| Antibody | Clinical Association |
|----------|---------------------|
| Anti-NXP2 (anti-p140) | Most common antibody in JDM; calcinosis; severe muscle disease |
| Anti-TIF1-gamma (anti-p155/140) | Severe cutaneous disease; photosensitivity; in adults, associated with malignancy |
| Anti-MDA5 (anti-CADM-140) | Rapidly progressive interstitial lung disease; amyopathic dermatomyositis; skin ulceration |
| Anti-Mi-2 | Classic dermatomyositis; good prognosis |
| Anti-SRP | Necrotizing myopathy; severe disease |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Calcinosis (dystrophic calcification in skin, subcutaneous tissue, or muscle) affects 20% to 40% of JDM patients and is strongly associated with anti-NXP2 antibodies. It is particularly common when treatment is delayed or disease is inadequately controlled. Lipodystrophy (partial or generalized) is another distinctive complication of JDM.

**Disease Activity Monitoring:** The Childhood Myositis Assessment Scale (CMAS) evaluates 14 maneuvers testing strength, endurance, and function, with scores ranging from 0 to 52 (higher scores indicating better function).

### Pediatric Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

Pediatric SLE accounts for 15% to 20% of all SLE cases, with a peak onset at 12 to 14 years and a female-to-male ratio of 4:1 to 5:1 (less female predominant than adult SLE). Pediatric SLE tends to be more severe than adult-onset disease, with higher rates of nephritis (50% to 75% versus 25% to 50%), CNS involvement, and hematologic disease.

**2019 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria (Aringer et al., 2019):**

The entry criterion is an ANA titer of 1:80 or higher on HEp-2 cells. Once this is met, clinical and immunologic domains are scored, and a total of 10 points or more classifies a patient as having SLE. Domains include constitutional, hematologic, neuropsychiatric, mucocutaneous, serosal, musculoskeletal, renal, antiphospholipid, complement, and SLE-specific antibodies.

**Key Autoantibodies:**

| Antibody | Significance |
|----------|-------------|
| Anti-dsDNA | Most specific for SLE activity monitoring; correlates with nephritis |
| Anti-Smith | Most specific antibody overall for SLE diagnosis (but less sensitive) |
| Anti-histone | Drug-induced lupus |
| Anti-Ro/SSA and Anti-La/SSB | Neonatal lupus; congenital heart block; Sjogren syndrome overlap |
| Antiphospholipid antibodies | Thrombotic risk; anticardiolipin, anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I, lupus anticoagulant |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Lupus nephritis is the most significant predictor of morbidity and mortality in pediatric SLE. Class IV (diffuse proliferative) nephritis is the most common and most severe form. Low complement levels (C3 and C4) correlate with active disease, particularly nephritis.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Drug-induced lupus in adolescents is most commonly caused by minocycline (used for acne). It presents with arthritis, serositis, and anti-histone antibodies but does NOT cause nephritis or CNS disease.

### Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki disease is an acute, self-limited vasculitis of medium-sized arteries that primarily affects children younger than 5 years. It is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries, having supplanted rheumatic fever. Incidence is highest in children of Asian descent (approximately 300 per 100,000 children under 5 in Japan versus approximately 25 per 100,000 in the United States) (McCrindle et al., 2017).

**Diagnostic Criteria (AHA 2017):**

Classic (complete) Kawasaki disease requires fever for 5 days or more plus at least 4 of 5 clinical features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Conjunctival injection | Bilateral bulbar; non-exudative |
| Oral mucous membrane changes | Strawberry tongue; cracked, erythematous lips |
| Polymorphous exanthem | Variable rash — NOT vesicular |
| Extremity changes | Acute: edema, erythema of hands/feet; Convalescent: periungual desquamation |
| Cervical lymphadenopathy | At least 1.5 cm, usually unilateral |

**Incomplete Kawasaki disease** should be considered in any child with fever for 5 or more days and 2 to 3 classic features, particularly with supportive laboratory findings (CRP 3 mg/dL or greater, or ESR 40 mm/hr or greater) plus 3 or more supplemental criteria, or echocardiographic abnormalities.

**Treatment (Newburger et al., 1986; McCrindle et al., 2017):**
- **IVIG:** 2 g/kg as a single infusion within 10 days of fever onset
- **Aspirin:** High-dose (80 to 100 mg/kg/day in divided doses) during the acute febrile phase, then low-dose (3 to 5 mg/kg/day) through the convalescent phase (6 to 8 weeks) or until ESR and CRP normalize
- **Refractory KD:** Second IVIG dose, intravenous methylprednisolone, or infliximab (anti-TNF)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Untreated Kawasaki disease results in coronary artery aneurysms in approximately 25% of cases. Timely IVIG within 10 days of fever onset reduces this rate to less than 5%. Infants under 6 months are at highest risk for coronary complications and are more likely to have an incomplete presentation.

**Key Temporal Features:**
- Thrombocytosis is characteristic in the second to third week and platelet counts may exceed 1,000,000/uL
- Periungual desquamation occurs in the convalescent phase (2 to 3 weeks), NOT during the acute phase
- Echocardiograms should be obtained at diagnosis, at 2 weeks, and at 6 to 8 weeks minimum

### Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF) and the Revised Jones Criteria

ARF follows group A streptococcal (GAS) pharyngitis and can cause permanent cardiac damage. While declining in developed nations, it remains a major cause of acquired heart disease in developing countries.

**Revised Jones Criteria (2015) (Gewitz et al., 2015):**

Diagnosis requires evidence of preceding GAS infection (elevated ASO titer, positive throat culture, or positive rapid streptococcal test) PLUS:

| Component | Low-Risk Populations | Moderate/High-Risk Populations |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Major Criteria** | Carditis (clinical or subclinical on echo), polyarthritis, Sydenham chorea, erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules | Same as low-risk PLUS monoarthritis and polyarthralgia accepted as major |
| **Minor Criteria** | Fever 38.5 degrees C or higher, polyarthralgia, elevated ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval | Fever 38.0 degrees C or higher, monoarthralgia, elevated ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval |
| **Diagnosis** | 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor | 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Carditis is the most serious manifestation of ARF. The mitral valve is most commonly affected (mitral regurgitation). Subclinical carditis detected by Doppler echocardiography is now accepted as a major criterion under the 2015 revision.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Sydenham chorea may appear 2 to 6 months after the GAS infection and may be the sole manifestation of ARF. The arthritis of rheumatic fever is migratory, involves large joints, and is exquisitely responsive to aspirin or NSAIDs. Unlike JIA, the arthritis of ARF is NEVER chronic.

**Antibiotic Prophylaxis:**
Secondary prophylaxis with penicillin VK (250 mg twice daily) or benzathine penicillin G (1.2 million units intramuscularly every 4 weeks) is required. Duration depends on the presence and severity of carditis: 5 years (no carditis), 10 years or until age 21 (carditis without valve disease), or 10 years or until age 40 (carditis with persistent valvular disease).

### Lyme Disease

Lyme disease is caused by the spirochete Borrelia burgdorferi, transmitted by Ixodes ticks (deer ticks). Transmission requires a minimum of 36 to 48 hours of tick attachment. The disease is endemic in the northeastern and upper midwestern United States, with an estimated 476,000 cases per year.

**Clinical Stages:**

| Stage | Timing | Key Manifestations |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Early localized | 3-30 days post-bite | Erythema migrans (expanding annular rash 5 cm or larger); single sufficient for diagnosis without serology |
| Early disseminated | Weeks to months | Multiple EM lesions; facial nerve palsy (may be bilateral, nearly pathognomonic); carditis with AV block; meningitis |
| Late | Months to years | Lyme arthritis (mono/oligoarthritis; knee in 90%); encephalopathy |

**Two-Tier Serologic Testing (Wormser et al., 2006):**
1. ELISA (screening) — if positive or equivocal, proceed to:
2. Western blot (confirmatory)
   - IgM: 2 of 3 specific bands (valid only within first 30 days)
   - IgG: 5 of 10 specific bands
   - Modified two-tier testing (two sequential EIAs) was FDA-approved in 2019

> **Clinical Pearl:** Serology is NOT indicated for erythema migrans; the diagnosis is clinical, and treatment should be started empirically. Serology may be negative early in the disease. Facial nerve palsy in Lyme disease may be bilateral, which is almost pathognomonic for Lyme as a cause.

**Treatment:**
- Early or localized disease: Doxycycline (patients 8 years and older) or amoxicillin (younger than 8 years)
- Neuroborreliosis or carditis with advanced AV block: IV ceftriaxone
- Lyme arthritis: Oral doxycycline or amoxicillin for 28 days; IV ceftriaxone for refractory cases

### Septic Arthritis and the Kocher Criteria

Septic arthritis is a pediatric emergency with an incidence of 4 to 10 per 100,000 children per year, peaking in children younger than 3 years. Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative organism in children beyond the neonatal period. In neonates, Group B Streptococcus, S. aureus, and gram-negative organisms predominate. Kingella kingae is the most common pathogen in children aged 6 months to 4 years and may be missed on standard cultures (requiring special culture media or PCR).

**Kocher Criteria (Kocher et al., 1999):**

These four criteria distinguish septic arthritis from transient synovitis of the hip:

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| 1. Non-weight-bearing | Patient unable or unwilling to bear weight |
| 2. ESR greater than 40 mm/hr | Elevated inflammatory marker |
| 3. Fever greater than 38.5 degrees Celsius | Systemic inflammation |
| 4. WBC greater than 12,000/mm3 | Leukocytosis |

| Number of Criteria Present | Predicted Probability of Septic Arthritis |
|---------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| 0 | Less than 0.2% |
| 1 | 3% |
| 2 | 40% |
| 3 | 93.1% |
| 4 | 99.6% |

Caird et al. (2006) added a 5th criterion — CRP greater than 2.0 mg/dL — which had the strongest independent predictive value.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Septic arthritis of the hip is a surgical emergency because elevated intracapsular pressure can compromise the blood supply to the femoral head, leading to avascular necrosis. The hip joint is entirely intracapsular, making it uniquely vulnerable. Joint aspirate findings suggesting septic arthritis include WBC greater than 50,000/uL with more than 75% polymorphonuclear cells.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Transient synovitis is the most common cause of acute hip pain in children aged 3 to 8 years. It is benign, self-limited, and typically follows a viral illness. The Kocher criteria help clinicians determine which patients with hip effusion need urgent joint aspiration versus observation.

### Pediatric Spondyloarthropathies

The spondyloarthropathies in children are primarily represented by enthesitis-related arthritis (ERA), the ILAR subtype defined by arthritis and enthesitis, or arthritis or enthesitis with at least 2 of the following: sacroiliac tenderness or lumbosacral pain, HLA-B27 positivity, family history of HLA-B27-associated disease, acute symptomatic anterior uveitis, or onset in a male older than 6 years.

ERA predominantly affects boys (male-to-female ratio of 7:1) with onset after age 6 years. Enthesitis — inflammation at tendon and ligament insertions into bone — is the hallmark. The Achilles tendon insertion and plantar fascia are most commonly involved. Large joint arthritis of the lower extremities predominates early, with sacroiliitis and axial disease developing later. A significant proportion progress to ankylosing spondylitis in adulthood.

**Reactive Arthritis** is a post-infectious spondyloarthropathy triggered by enteric organisms (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia) or urogenital infection. It is HLA-B27 associated and typically presents as asymmetric oligoarthritis of the lower extremities, often with enthesitis and sometimes with urethritis and conjunctivitis (the classic triad).

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### JIA: Functional Assessment and Rehabilitation

The Childhood Health Assessment Questionnaire (CHAQ) measures functional ability across 8 domains: dressing, arising, eating, walking, hygiene, reach, grip, and activities. Scores range from 0 (no difficulty) to 3 (unable to perform), and the disability index is the mean of the 8 domain scores.

The JADAS (Juvenile Arthritis Disease Activity Score) integrates physician and parent assessments, active joint count, and ESR to provide a composite disease activity measure. JADAS-10, JADAS-27, and JADAS-71 vary by the number of joints included in the count. Clinically inactive disease thresholds are a JADAS-10 of 1 or less.

The ACR Pediatric Response Criteria (ACR Pedi 30/50/70/90) define improvement as 30%, 50%, 70%, or 90% improvement in 3 of 6 core set variables (physician global assessment, parent global assessment, functional ability, active joint count, joints with limited ROM, and ESR or CRP) with no more than 1 variable worsening by more than 30%.

Rehabilitation is integral to JIA management. Key principles include:
- Maintaining joint ROM through daily stretching programs
- Strengthening exercises tailored to disease activity (avoid high-impact during flares)
- Aquatic therapy for its combination of buoyancy support and resistance
- Splinting for joint protection (resting hand splints, knee extension splints)
- Aerobic conditioning to address the deconditioning common in chronic arthritis
- Occupational therapy for hand function and joint protection strategies
- Addressing psychosocial impacts including school participation, peer relationships, and transition to adult care

### JDM: Comprehensive Management

Treatment of JDM begins with systemic corticosteroids (typically intravenous methylprednisolone pulse therapy followed by oral prednisone). Methotrexate is the first-line steroid-sparing agent. IVIG is used for refractory skin disease. Hydroxychloroquine may be added for persistent skin manifestations.

Rehabilitation is crucial in JDM. During active disease, gentle active-assisted ROM is maintained while avoiding eccentric exercise that could worsen muscle inflammation. As disease control improves, progressive strengthening programs are introduced. Historically, exercise was avoided in inflammatory myopathy due to concerns about exacerbating inflammation, but current evidence supports supervised exercise as safe and beneficial for maintaining and improving strength, endurance, and function.

Sun protection is essential due to the photosensitive nature of JDM skin disease. Photoprotective clothing and broad-spectrum sunscreen are recommended.

Calcinosis management remains challenging. Prevention through early aggressive immunosuppressive therapy is the best strategy. Established calcinosis may be treated with colchicine, diltiazem, aluminum hydroxide, or surgical excision for symptomatic deposits, though evidence for medical therapy is limited.

### Pediatric SLE: Multisystem Considerations

Pediatric SLE requires a multidisciplinary approach given its potential to affect virtually every organ system. Nephritis management follows the ISN/RPS classification: Class III and IV nephritis require aggressive immunosuppression with mycophenolate mofetil or intravenous cyclophosphamide, while Class V (membranous) may respond to mycophenolate alone.

Hydroxychloroquine is recommended for all SLE patients (unless contraindicated) as it reduces flares, organ damage accrual, and mortality. Annual ophthalmologic screening for hydroxychloroquine retinal toxicity is recommended.

Neuropsychiatric SLE can manifest as seizures, psychosis, cognitive dysfunction, headache, or mood disorders. Distinguishing CNS lupus from infection or medication side effects (particularly corticosteroid-related neuropsychiatric effects) is a common clinical challenge.

Antiphospholipid syndrome occurs in 20% to 30% of pediatric SLE patients and increases the risk of venous and arterial thromboembolism, as well as pregnancy complications in adolescent patients.

### Kawasaki Disease: Long-Term Cardiac Follow-Up

Coronary artery aneurysms, when they occur, vary in prognosis based on size. Small aneurysms (less than 5 mm or Z-score less than 5) often resolve within 1 to 2 years. Medium aneurysms (5 to 8 mm or Z-score 5 to 10) may regress but carry ongoing risk. Giant aneurysms (8 mm or greater, or Z-score 10 or greater) rarely resolve and carry the highest risk of thrombosis, stenosis, and myocardial infarction. Risk stratification guides long-term antiplatelet and anticoagulation therapy, activity restrictions, and follow-up imaging.

MIS-C (Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children) associated with SARS-CoV-2 shares features with Kawasaki disease, including fever, mucocutaneous findings, and coronary involvement. However, MIS-C tends to affect older children, has more prominent gastrointestinal and cardiac dysfunction, and frequently involves shock. The ACR published clinical guidance for MIS-C management (Henderson et al., 2021) with treatment approaches overlapping with Kawasaki disease (IVIG, corticosteroids, anakinra).

### Rheumatic Fever: Global Perspective

While ARF has become uncommon in developed nations due to improved living conditions and access to antibiotics, it remains a major public health burden in developing countries, sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and indigenous populations in Australia and New Zealand. Rheumatic heart disease (chronic valvular disease resulting from recurrent or poorly treated ARF) is the leading cause of cardiovascular death in young people in many low- and middle-income countries.

The 2015 Jones Criteria revision addressed this disparity by creating separate criteria for low-risk and moderate-to-high-risk populations. In high-risk settings, monoarthritis (rather than only polyarthritis) and polyarthralgia are accepted as major criteria, and the fever threshold is lowered to 38.0 degrees Celsius, reflecting the observation that ARF in endemic populations may present with less florid features.

### Lyme Disease: Diagnostic Pitfalls

Several diagnostic pitfalls deserve emphasis. First, serologic testing should not be ordered for patients with erythema migrans because sensitivity is low in early infection and the diagnosis is clinical. Second, IgM Western blot is only valid within the first 30 days of symptoms; a positive IgM after 30 days is likely a false positive and should not be used for diagnosis. Third, serologic tests remain positive for years after successful treatment and do not indicate persistent infection. The modified two-tier approach (two sequential EIAs) offers improved sensitivity and specificity over the traditional ELISA-plus-Western-blot approach.

Post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome — persistent fatigue, musculoskeletal pain, and cognitive complaints after adequate antibiotic therapy — occurs in a minority of patients. Prolonged or repeated antibiotic courses are not recommended and have shown no benefit in randomized trials.
