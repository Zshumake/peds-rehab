# Chapter 11: Pediatric Burns & Cancer Rehabilitation — Research Notes

## GUIDELINES FOUND

### American Burn Association (ABA) Referral Criteria
- **ABA. "Advanced Burn Life Support Course Provider Manual." American Burn Association. 2018 Revision.**
  - Criteria for referral to a burn center:
    1. Partial-thickness burns >10% TBSA
    2. Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
    3. Third-degree (full-thickness) burns in any age group
    4. Electrical burns (including lightning)
    5. Chemical burns
    6. Inhalation injury
    7. Burns in patients with preexisting medical conditions that could complicate management
    8. Burns with concomitant trauma (when burn is greatest risk)
    9. Burns in children — hospitals without qualified personnel/equipment for pediatric care
    10. Patients requiring special social, emotional, or rehabilitative intervention (including NAT/suspected child abuse)

### ABA/ISBI Practice Guidelines for Burn Rehabilitation
- **Richard RL, Hedman TL, Quick CD, et al. "A clarion to burn rehabilitation specialists: it does make a difference." J Burn Care Res. 2015;36(2):e155.**
- **Serghiou M, Cowan A, Whitehead C. "Rehabilitation after a burn injury." Clin Plast Surg. 2009;36(4):675-686.**
  - Anti-deformity positioning, splinting, early mobilization, scar management
  - COMFORT = CONTRACTURE principle: Position opposite the expected contracture position

### COG (Children's Oncology Group) Survivorship Guidelines
- **Children's Oncology Group. "Long-Term Follow-Up Guidelines for Survivors of Childhood, Adolescent, and Young Adult Cancers." Version 6.0. 2023.**
  - Risk-based screening for late effects of cancer therapy
  - Organ-specific screening based on treatment exposures (anthracyclines → cardiac; cranial RT → neurocognitive; alkylating agents → gonadal)
  - Exercise recommendations for survivors: 150 min/week moderate or 75 min/week vigorous activity
  - Surveillance for secondary malignancies

### Exercise Guidelines for Pediatric Cancer
- **Wurz A, McLaughlin E, Chamorro-Vina C, et al. "Exercise during and after cancer treatment in children and adolescents." Semin Oncol Nurs. 2021;37(6):151227.**
- **Stout NL, Baima J, Swisher AK, et al. "A systematic review of exercise systematic reviews in the cancer literature (2005-2017)." PM R. 2017;9(9S2):S347-S384.**
  - Exercise is safe and beneficial during and after pediatric cancer treatment
  - Benefits: improved fitness, reduced fatigue, improved quality of life, maintenance of BMD, improved body composition
  - No increase in adverse events with supervised exercise programs

## LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### Burn Assessment
1. **Lund CC, Browder NC. "The estimation of areas of burns." Surg Gynecol Obstet. 1944;79:352-358.**
   - Developed the Lund-Browder chart for age-specific TBSA estimation
   - Accounts for proportional differences between children and adults (e.g., larger head, smaller legs in children)
   - Gold standard for pediatric burn surface area estimation

2. **Wallace AB. "The exposure treatment of burns." Lancet. 1951;1(6653):501-504.**
   - Described the Rule of Nines for rapid burn assessment in adults
   - Modified Rule of Nines adapted for children (see Epidemiology section)

### Burn Rehabilitation
3. **Schneider JC, Holavanahalli R, Helm P, et al. "Contractures in burn injury: defining the problem." J Burn Care Res. 2006;27(4):508-514.**
   - Documented contracture as the most common complication of burn injury
   - Established evidence base for aggressive anti-deformity positioning

4. **Engrav LH, Heimbach DM, Rivara FP, et al. "12-Year within-wound study of the effectiveness of custom pressure garment therapy." Burns. 2010;36(7):975-983.**
   - Pressure garment therapy: 23-25 mmHg pressure recommended
   - Wear 23 hours/day for 12-18 months (or until scar maturation)
   - Evidence for scar thickness reduction but not color/pliability

### Cancer Rehabilitation — Pediatric
5. **Marchese VG, Chiarello LA, Lange BJ. "Effects of physical therapy intervention for children with acute lymphoblastic leukemia." Pediatr Blood Cancer. 2004;42(2):127-133.**
   - PT intervention improved ankle ROM, knee extension strength, and functional mobility in children with ALL during treatment

6. **Ness KK, Krull KR, Jones KE, et al. "Physiologic frailty as a sign of accelerated aging among adult survivors of childhood cancer." J Clin Oncol. 2013;31(36):4496-4503.**
   - Childhood cancer survivors experience accelerated aging/premature frailty
   - Supports rehabilitation interventions to maintain function

7. **Hillmann A, Rosenbaum D, Schroter J, et al. "Electromyographic and gait analysis of forty-three patients after rotationplasty." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2000;82(2):187-196.**
   - Gait analysis after Van Ness rotationplasty: functional outcomes comparable to above-knee amputation with endoprosthesis

## RECENT EVIDENCE (2018-2024)

1. **Smolle C, Cambiaso-Daniel J, Forbes AA, et al. "Recent trends in burn epidemiology worldwide: a systematic review." Burns. 2017;43(2):249-257.**
   - Scald burns remain #1 cause in children globally
   - Declining burn mortality due to improved critical care

2. **Suman OE, Herndon DN. "Effects of cessation of a structured and supervised exercise conditioning program on lean mass and muscle strength in severely burned children." Arch Phys Med Rehabil. 2020.**
   - Exercise programs during burn recovery improve lean body mass and muscle strength; benefits lost when program stops

3. **COG Long-Term Follow-Up Guidelines Version 6.0. 2023.**
   - Updated survivorship screening recommendations (see Guidelines)

4. **Dixon SB, Chen Y, Yasui Y, et al. "Reduced morbidity and mortality in survivors of childhood acute lymphoblastic leukemia." J Clin Oncol. 2020;38(29):3418-3429.**
   - Improved survival in recent treatment eras; but late effects remain significant

5. **Wurz A, et al. Semin Oncol Nurs. 2021.** (See Guidelines — exercise during/after pediatric cancer treatment)

## EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### Pediatric Burns
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Incidence | ~100,000 children seek burn treatment annually in the US |
| Peak age | 1-5 years (toddlers/preschool) |
| Leading mechanism | **Scald burns** (#1 in children <5 years; 60-80% of pediatric burns) |
| Leading mechanism (older children) | Flame burns (#1 in children >5 years) |
| Contact burns | 3rd most common; include hot iron, stove, curling iron |
| Mortality | <3% overall for hospitalized pediatric burns (improved from historical rates) |
| Inhalation injury | Associated with 40-60% of burn deaths |
| NAT (non-accidental trauma) | 10-20% of pediatric burns; scald burns most common NAT mechanism |

### NAT (Non-Accidental Trauma) Burn Indicators
| Indicator | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stocking/glove distribution** | Symmetric, circumferential, clear demarcation lines (forced immersion) |
| **Sparing of flexion creases** | Child reflexively curls body; palms/soles/antecubital/popliteal fossae spared |
| **Uniform depth** | Suggests prolonged contact with single heat source |
| **Doughnut sign** | Central sparing of buttocks/perineum (contact with cool tub bottom in forced immersion) |
| **Delay in seeking care** | >2 hours delay suspicious |
| **Inconsistent history** | Story does not match burn pattern or developmental capability |
| **Multiple burns at different stages of healing** | Suggests repeated injury |
| **Burns to dorsum of hands** | Children reflexively withdraw; dorsal burns suggest forced contact |
| **Cigarette burns** | Circular, uniform, deep; often multiple |

### Modified Rule of 9s for Children vs. Adults
| Body Region | Adult (%) | Child (%) | Infant (%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Head/Neck | 9 | 18 | 18 |
| Each Upper Extremity | 9 | 9 | 9 |
| Anterior Trunk | 18 | 18 | 18 |
| Posterior Trunk | 18 | 18 | 18 |
| Each Lower Extremity | 18 | 14 | 14 |
| Perineum/Genitalia | 1 | 1 | 1 |
- **Board Pearl**: For children, the head is larger (18%) and legs are smaller (14% each) compared to adults (head 9%, legs 18% each)
- **Palmar method**: Patient's palm (including fingers) = ~1% TBSA; useful for scattered/small burns

### Lund-Browder Chart — Age-Specific TBSA Percentages

| Body Area | 0-1 yr | 1-4 yr | 5-9 yr | 10-14 yr | 15+ yr / Adult |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Head (total) | 19% | 17% | 13% | 11% | 7% |
| Neck | 2% | 2% | 2% | 2% | 2% |
| Anterior trunk | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% |
| Posterior trunk | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% | 13% |
| Each buttock | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% |
| Genitalia | 1% | 1% | 1% | 1% | 1% |
| Each upper arm | 4% | 4% | 4% | 4% | 4% |
| Each forearm | 3% | 3% | 3% | 3% | 3% |
| Each hand | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% | 2.5% |
| Each thigh | 5.5% | 6.5% | 8% | 8.5% | 9.5% |
| Each leg (below knee) | 5% | 5% | 5.5% | 6% | 7% |
| Each foot | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% | 3.5% |

- **Key Principle**: Head percentage decreases and leg percentage increases with age
- **Board Pearl**: Head loses ~1% per year of age (from 19% at birth to ~7% in adults); legs gain proportionally

### Burn Depth Classification
| Classification | Old Term | Depth | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Superficial | 1st degree | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters (sunburn) | Painful | 3-7 days; no scarring |
| Superficial partial-thickness | 2nd degree (superficial) | Epidermis + superficial (papillary) dermis | Pink, moist, blisters, blanches | Very painful | 7-14 days; minimal scarring |
| Deep partial-thickness | 2nd degree (deep) | Epidermis + deep (reticular) dermis | Pale, mottled, less moisture, may blister | Decreased sensation | 14-35 days; significant scarring, may need grafting |
| Full-thickness | 3rd degree | Entire epidermis + dermis (into subQ fat) | White, waxy, leathery, dry, eschar | Insensate | Will NOT heal spontaneously; requires excision + grafting |
| 4th degree (subdermal) | 4th degree | Through skin into muscle, bone, tendon | Charred, exposed deep structures | Insensate | Requires excision; amputation may be necessary |

### Pediatric Cancer Epidemiology
| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| Overall pediatric cancer incidence | ~16,000 new cases/year in US (ages 0-19) |
| Overall 5-year survival | ~85% (improved from <60% in 1970s) |
| Most common pediatric malignancy | **Leukemia** (~30% of all pediatric cancers) |
| Most common leukemia | **ALL** (~80% of pediatric leukemias; ~25% of all childhood cancers) |
| ALL peak age | 2-5 years |
| ALL prognosis | 5-year survival ~90% |
| AML | ~20% of pediatric leukemias; 5-year survival ~65-70% |
| Most common solid tumor | CNS tumors (~25% of pediatric cancers) |
| Most common extracranial solid tumor | Neuroblastoma |
| Most common primary bone tumor | **Osteosarcoma** |
| Second most common bone tumor | **Ewing sarcoma** |
| Wilms tumor (nephroblastoma) | Most common renal tumor in children; peak age 3-4 years; 5-year survival ~90% |
| Rhabdomyosarcoma | Most common soft tissue sarcoma in children |

## CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS

### Burn Assessment Scales

#### Lund-Browder Chart (see Epidemiology table above)
- Most accurate method for pediatric TBSA estimation
- Age-adjusted percentages account for proportional body growth

#### Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS)
| Parameter | Score Range |
|---|---|
| Pigmentation | 0-3 (normal to mixed) |
| Vascularity | 0-3 (normal to purple) |
| Pliability | 0-5 (normal to contracture) |
| Height/Thickness | 0-3 (flat to >5mm) |
| Total | 0-14 |
- Most widely used burn scar assessment tool
- Higher score = worse scar

#### Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale (POSAS)
- More comprehensive than VSS; includes patient perspective
- Observer scale: Vascularity, pigmentation, thickness, relief, pliability, surface area
- Patient scale: Pain, pruritus, color, stiffness, thickness, irregularity
- Each item scored 1-10; total 6-60 per scale

### Anti-Deformity Positioning — COMFORT = CONTRACTURE

**Principle**: Position the burned area in the OPPOSITE direction of expected contracture (anti-deformity position). Comfort positions promote contracture formation.

| Body Area | Expected Contracture | Anti-Deformity Position |
|---|---|---|
| **Neck (anterior)** | Flexion | Extension (no pillow; towel roll under shoulders) |
| **Neck (posterior)** | Extension | Slight flexion |
| **Neck (lateral)** | Lateral flexion toward burn | Lateral flexion AWAY from burn |
| **Axilla/Shoulder** | Adduction | Abduction 90 degrees; airplane splint |
| **Elbow (anterior)** | Flexion | Extension |
| **Elbow (posterior)** | Extension | Flexion 30-40 degrees |
| **Wrist (volar)** | Flexion | Extension 20-30 degrees |
| **Hand (dorsal)** | MCP hyperextension, IP flexion, thumb adduction ("claw hand") | MCP flexion 70-90 degrees, IP extension, thumb abduction/opposition ("intrinsic plus" or "safe position") |
| **Hip (anterior)** | Flexion | Extension (prone position encouraged); neutral rotation |
| **Knee (posterior)** | Flexion | Extension |
| **Ankle (anterior)** | Dorsiflexion | Neutral (90 degrees) |
| **Ankle (posterior/plantar)** | Plantarflexion | Neutral (90 degrees); posterior splint or ankle foot orthosis |

- **Board Pearl**: "COMFORT = CONTRACTURE" — the position of comfort is the position that leads to contracture
- **Board Pearl**: Hand dorsal burn positioning = "safe position" / "intrinsic plus" = MCPs flexed 70-90 degrees, IPs extended, thumb abducted and opposed

### Pediatric Cancer Classification

#### ALL (Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia)
| Feature | Data |
|---|---|
| Age | Peak 2-5 years |
| Cell type | B-cell precursor (85%); T-cell (15%) |
| Favorable prognosis | Age 1-9, WBC <50K, B-cell, hyperdiploidy (>50 chromosomes), t(12;21)/TEL-AML1 |
| Unfavorable prognosis | Age <1 or >10, WBC >50K, T-cell, MLL rearrangements, t(9;22)/Ph+, hypodiploidy |
| Treatment | Induction → Consolidation → Maintenance (2-3 years total); intrathecal chemotherapy for CNS prophylaxis |

#### AML (Acute Myeloid Leukemia)
| Feature | Data |
|---|---|
| Percentage | 20% of pediatric leukemias |
| **Auer rods** | Rod-shaped inclusions in blasts; PATHOGNOMONIC for AML (especially M3/APL) |
| Down syndrome | 10-20x increased AML risk; especially AMKL (acute megakaryoblastic leukemia) |
| APL (M3) | t(15;17)/PML-RARA; treated with ATRA + arsenic trioxide; DIC risk |

#### Osteosarcoma
| Feature | Data |
|---|---|
| Peak age | 10-25 years (adolescent growth spurt) |
| Location | **Metaphysis** of long bones; **distal femur** (#1), proximal tibia (#2), proximal humerus (#3) |
| Radiograph | "Sunburst" pattern; Codman triangle (periosteal elevation) |
| Histology | Malignant osteoid production by tumor cells |
| Metastases | Lung (#1 site); 15-20% have metastases at diagnosis |
| Treatment | Neoadjuvant chemotherapy → Wide surgical resection → Adjuvant chemotherapy |
| 5-year survival | ~70% localized; ~20-30% metastatic |
| Risk factors | Prior radiation, Paget disease (adults), hereditary retinoblastoma (RB1 mutation), Li-Fraumeni (TP53) |

#### Ewing Sarcoma
| Feature | Data |
|---|---|
| Peak age | 5-25 years (peak 10-15) |
| Location | **Diaphysis** of long bones (femur most common) AND **flat bones** (pelvis, ribs, scapula) |
| Radiograph | "Onion skin" periosteal reaction; permeative/moth-eaten pattern |
| Molecular | t(11;22)(q24;q12) EWSR1-FLI1 fusion in ~85% |
| Histology | Small round blue cell tumor |
| Treatment | Chemotherapy + surgery and/or radiation (Ewing is radiosensitive; osteo is NOT) |
| 5-year survival | ~70% localized; ~20-30% metastatic |

### Bone Tumor Location Map — Board Classic
| Tumor | Location in Bone | Location Predilection |
|---|---|---|
| **Osteosarcoma** | Metaphysis | Distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus |
| **Ewing sarcoma** | Diaphysis (and flat bones) | Femur > pelvis > ribs > tibia |
| **Osteochondroma** | Metaphysis (growing away from joint) | Distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus |
| **Giant cell tumor** | Epiphysis (extends to subchondral bone) | Distal femur > proximal tibia > distal radius |
| **Chondroblastoma** | Epiphysis | Proximal humerus > distal femur > proximal tibia |
| **Aneurysmal bone cyst** | Metaphysis (eccentric) | Posterior elements of spine; long bones |
| **Unicameral bone cyst** | Metaphysis (central) | Proximal humerus > proximal femur |
| **Eosinophilic granuloma (LCH)** | Diaphysis | Skull > femur > spine |

- **Board Pearl**: Osteosarcoma = METAPHYSIS; Ewing = DIAPHYSIS — MOST TESTED bone tumor distinction
- **Board Pearl**: Both osteosarcoma and Ewing favor the femur but in different parts of the bone
- **Board Pearl**: Giant cell tumor and chondroblastoma are the EPIPHYSEAL tumors

### Van Ness Rotationplasty
- Procedure: Resection of distal femur/proximal tibia with tumor; remaining limb rotated 180 degrees; ankle joint serves as knee joint
- Ankle plantar flexors function as "knee extensors"
- Result: Below-knee functional amputation level (rather than above-knee)
- Advantages: Biological joint, growth potential preserved in children, better prosthetic use than above-knee amputation
- Indications: Malignant bone tumors around knee in skeletally immature patients
- **Board Pearl**: Ankle becomes the knee; prosthesis attaches to rotated foot

## BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD PEARLS

### Pediatric Burns

#### Assessment
1. **Scald burns = #1 cause in children** <5 years (60-80% of pediatric burns)
2. **Lund-Browder chart**: Gold standard for pediatric TBSA; age-specific percentages
3. **Head = 18% in children** (vs. 9% in adults); **each leg = 14%** (vs. 18% in adults)
4. **Head loses ~1% per year** from 18% at birth toward 7% in adults; legs gain proportionally
5. **Palmar method**: Patient's palm + fingers = ~1% TBSA (useful for small/scattered burns)
6. **Superficial (1st degree) burns are NOT counted** in TBSA calculations for fluid resuscitation

#### Depth
7. **Superficial partial-thickness**: Pink, moist, blisters, VERY PAINFUL, heals 7-14 days
8. **Deep partial-thickness**: Pale, mottled, DECREASED sensation, heals 14-35 days with scarring
9. **Full-thickness**: White/leathery, INSENSATE, will NOT heal spontaneously
10. **Board Pearl**: Pain level inversely correlates with depth — most painful = most superficial

#### NAT (Non-Accidental Trauma) Burns
11. **Stocking/glove immersion pattern**: Symmetric, circumferential, clear waterline
12. **Doughnut sign**: Central buttock sparing in forced immersion (contact with cool tub bottom)
13. **Flexion crease sparing**: Child reflexively curls; antecubital/popliteal fossae spared
14. **Dorsal hand burns**: Suspicious (children reflexively withdraw hand; dorsal contact = forced)
15. **Cigarette burns**: Circular, deep, uniform, often multiple
16. **Delay in seeking care >2 hours**: Red flag for NAT
17. **10-20% of pediatric burns** are NAT

#### COMFORT = CONTRACTURE
18. **Neck anterior burn**: Extension (no pillow)
19. **Axilla burn**: Abduction 90 degrees (airplane splint)
20. **Anterior elbow burn**: Extension
21. **Dorsal hand burn**: "Safe position" / "Intrinsic plus" — MCPs flexed 70-90, IPs extended, thumb abducted
22. **Hip anterior burn**: Extension (prone positioning)
23. **Posterior knee burn**: Extension
24. **Ankle burn (plantarflexion contracture risk)**: Neutral 90 degrees (AFO or posterior splint)
25. **Board Pearl**: The position of comfort IS the position of contracture

#### Burn Rehabilitation Principles
26. **Pressure garments**: 23-25 mmHg; worn 23 hrs/day for 12-18 months until scar maturation
27. **Scar maturation**: 12-18 months (up to 2 years); scar is mature when flat, soft, and pale
28. **Silicone sheeting**: Improves scar pliability and thickness; mechanism debated (hydration theory)
29. **Exercise**: Active ROM and stretching begin within 24-48 hours of admission; continue through recovery
30. **Hypertrophic scar risk**: Deep partial-thickness and full-thickness burns; delayed healing >14-21 days
31. **Z-plasty**: Surgical technique to release linear contractures; increases length by redirecting tension

#### ABA Referral Criteria
32. **Partial-thickness >10% TBSA**, face/hands/feet/genitalia/joints, full-thickness, electrical, chemical, inhalation injury, special needs patients, suspected NAT

#### Fluid Resuscitation (for reference)
33. **Parkland formula**: 4 mL/kg/%TBSA for first 24 hours (half in first 8 hours from time of burn)
34. **Pediatric modification**: Add maintenance fluid (D5 with appropriate electrolytes) to Parkland formula for children <20 kg (limited glycogen stores → hypoglycemia risk)
35. **Goal**: Urine output 1-2 mL/kg/hr in children <30 kg; 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr in children >30 kg

### Pediatric Cancer Rehabilitation

#### ALL (Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia)
36. **80% of pediatric leukemias** and **~25% of all childhood cancers**
37. **Peak age 2-5 years**; B-cell precursor in 85%
38. **5-year survival ~90%**: Most curable childhood cancer
39. **Favorable prognosis**: Age 1-9, WBC <50K, hyperdiploidy, TEL-AML1 fusion
40. **Steroid myopathy**: Significant during treatment (dexamethasone/prednisone); contributes to weakness and functional decline
41. **AVN**: Late effect, especially of hips/knees; related to corticosteroid exposure
42. **Vincristine neuropathy**: Peripheral neuropathy (sensorimotor); foot drop, weakness; dose-limiting toxicity
43. **Methotrexate neurotoxicity**: Leukoencephalopathy, especially with intrathecal or high-dose IV; executive function deficits
44. **Cranial radiation**: Neurocognitive late effects (attention, processing speed, memory); growth hormone deficiency; secondary brain tumors
45. **Obesity**: Common late effect of ALL treatment

#### AML (Acute Myeloid Leukemia)
46. **Auer rods**: Needle-shaped crystalline inclusions in blast cytoplasm — PATHOGNOMONIC for AML
47. **APL (M3)**: t(15;17); treated with ATRA; DIC is major risk
48. **Down syndrome + AML**: Especially AMKL (acute megakaryoblastic leukemia); respond well to treatment

#### Osteosarcoma
49. **Metaphysis of long bones**: Distal femur #1 location — CLASSIC
50. **"Sunburst" pattern + Codman triangle**: Classic radiographic findings
51. **Peak during adolescent growth spurt**: Related to rapid bone growth
52. **Lung metastases**: #1 site of distant spread
53. **NOT radiosensitive**: Surgery + chemotherapy; radiation has limited role
54. **Limb salvage**: 85-90% of patients undergo limb-sparing surgery (endoprosthesis)
55. **Phantom limb pain**: Even in children; more common with amputation than limb salvage

#### Ewing Sarcoma
56. **Diaphysis of long bones AND flat bones**: Key anatomic distinction from osteosarcoma
57. **"Onion skin" periosteal reaction**: Layered periosteal new bone — CLASSIC
58. **t(11;22) EWSR1-FLI1 fusion**: Present in ~85%
59. **Small round blue cell tumor**: Histologic description (DDx includes neuroblastoma, lymphoma, rhabdomyosarcoma)
60. **RADIOSENSITIVE**: Unlike osteosarcoma; radiation can be used for local control when surgery not feasible
61. **Rare in African Americans**: Unlike osteosarcoma

#### Osteosarcoma vs. Ewing — Board Comparison
| Feature | Osteosarcoma | Ewing Sarcoma |
|---|---|---|
| Location in bone | Metaphysis | Diaphysis |
| Bone preference | Long bones (around knee) | Long bones + flat bones (pelvis, ribs) |
| Peak age | 10-25 (adolescent growth spurt) | 5-25 (peak 10-15) |
| Radiograph | Sunburst, Codman triangle | Onion skin, permeative |
| Radiosensitivity | No | Yes |
| Molecular | Complex karyotype; RB1, TP53 | t(11;22) EWSR1-FLI1 |
| Race | All races | Rare in African Americans |

#### Van Ness Rotationplasty
62. **Ankle becomes functional knee joint**: Rotated 180 degrees
63. **Ankle plantar flexors** serve as "knee extensors"
64. **Functional below-knee amputation level** (vs. above-knee without rotationplasty)
65. **Indicated in skeletally immature patients**: Preserves growth potential
66. **Better energy expenditure** than above-knee amputation

#### Cancer-Related Fatigue
67. **Most common symptom**: Reported by 50-90% of pediatric cancer patients during treatment
68. **Multifactorial**: Disease process, treatment effects (chemotherapy, radiation), anemia, deconditioning, sleep disruption, psychosocial factors
69. **Exercise is the most effective intervention**: Evidence supports aerobic and resistance training
70. **Energy conservation techniques**: Pacing, prioritization, sleep hygiene
71. **Persists into survivorship**: 20-30% of survivors report significant fatigue years after treatment completion

#### COG Survivorship — Late Effects
72. **Anthracycline cardiotoxicity**: Dose-dependent cardiomyopathy; echo screening recommended (starting 2 years after therapy, ongoing)
73. **Cranial radiation**: Neurocognitive deficits, GH deficiency, hypothyroidism, secondary CNS tumors
74. **Alkylating agents**: Gonadal dysfunction, infertility, secondary leukemia (MDS/AML)
75. **Bleomycin**: Pulmonary fibrosis; PFT screening
76. **Cisplatin**: Ototoxicity (high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss), nephrotoxicity
77. **Vincristine**: Peripheral neuropathy (motor and sensory); foot drop
78. **Secondary malignancies**: Overall 3-6x increased risk; related to radiation and alkylating agents
79. **Exercise recommendations**: 150 min/week moderate or 75 min/week vigorous activity for all survivors
80. **Bone health**: Reduced BMD from steroids, methotrexate, radiation, inactivity; DEXA screening recommended

#### Exercise Guidelines for Pediatric Cancer
81. **Safe during active treatment**: Supervised exercise does NOT increase adverse events
82. **Benefits during treatment**: Maintained fitness, reduced fatigue, improved QoL, maintained BMD
83. **Post-treatment**: Aerobic + resistance training improves cardiovascular fitness, strength, body composition, fatigue, psychosocial wellbeing
84. **Individualized**: Consider treatment phase, blood counts (avoid high-intensity if platelet <50K or ANC <500), central line precautions, bone metastases
