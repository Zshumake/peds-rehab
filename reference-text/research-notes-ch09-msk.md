# Chapter 9: Pediatric MSK — Bones, Joints & Orthopedics — Research Notes

## GUIDELINES FOUND

### DDH (Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip)
- **AAP Clinical Practice Guideline: "Early Detection of Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip." Pediatrics. 2000;105(4):896-905.**
  - Universal physical examination screening at birth and well-child visits
  - Ultrasound recommended for girls born breech at 6 weeks or if examination is equivocal
  - Imaging: Ultrasound preferred <4-6 months (femoral head is cartilaginous); radiographs after 4-6 months (ossification begins)
  - Risk factors: female sex (4-8:1 F:M), breech presentation, family history, firstborn, oligohydramnios, swaddling with legs extended

- **AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline: "Detection and Nonoperative Management of Pediatric Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip in Infants up to Six Months of Age." 2014 (updated 2022).**
  - Limited evidence to recommend universal ultrasound screening (vs. selective/targeted screening)
  - Strong recommendation for Pavlik harness as first-line treatment for DDH diagnosed <6 months

### Scoliosis
- **SRS (Scoliosis Research Society) / AAOS / POSNA / AAP Position Statement: "Screening for the Early Detection of Idiopathic Scoliosis in Adolescents." 2015.**
  - Support screening at ages 10-12 (girls) and 13-14 (boys) or twice during adolescence
  - Adam's forward bend test; scoliometer threshold >=7 degrees for referral

- **BrAIST Trial (Bracing in Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis Trial): Weinstein SL, Dolan LA, Wright JG, Dobbs MB. "Effects of bracing in adolescents with idiopathic scoliosis." N Engl J Med. 2013;369(16):1512-1521.**
  - LANDMARK RCT (prematurely stopped due to efficacy)
  - Bracing significantly reduced progression to surgical threshold (>=50 degrees)
  - Success rate: 72% with bracing vs. 48% with observation
  - Dose-response: >=13 hours/day wear time had 90-93% success rate
  - Eligible patients: Risser 0-2, Cobb angle 25-40 degrees, age 10-15

### Clubfoot
- **Ponseti Method: Ponseti IV. "Congenital clubfoot: fundamentals of treatment." Oxford University Press. 1996.**
  - Gold standard for initial treatment of idiopathic clubfoot
  - Serial manipulation and casting (typically 5-7 casts, changed weekly)
  - Percutaneous Achilles tenotomy in ~80% of cases
  - Foot abduction orthosis (FAO/Denis Browne bar) for 4 years to prevent relapse
  - Success rate: 90-95% when properly applied

### Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease
- **Herring JT, Kim HT, Browne R. "Legg-Calve-Perthes disease. Part I: Classification of radiographs with use of the modified lateral pillar and Stulberg classifications." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2004;86(10):2103-2120.**
  - Multicenter study establishing prognostic value of lateral pillar classification

### Salter-Harris Classification
- **Salter RB, Harris WR. "Injuries involving the epiphyseal plate." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 1963;45(3):587-622.**
  - Original description of the 5-type classification

## LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### DDH
1. **Graf R. "The diagnosis of congenital hip-joint dislocation by the ultrasonic compound treatment." Arch Orthop Trauma Surg. 1980;97(2):117-133.**
   - Established ultrasound classification of infant hip (Graf classification)
   - Type I: Normal (alpha angle >60 degrees)
   - Type IIa: Immature, physiologic <3 months (alpha 50-59 degrees)
   - Type IIb: Immature >3 months, delayed ossification (alpha 50-59 degrees)
   - Type IIc: Critical zone (alpha 43-49 degrees)
   - Type III: Subluxated (alpha <43 degrees)
   - Type IV: Dislocated

2. **Pavlik A. "Stirrups as an aid in the treatment of congenital dysplasias of the hip in children." 1957 (translated).**
   - Pavlik harness: Flexion 100-110 degrees, abduction <70 degrees
   - Success rate: 85-95% when applied before 6 months
   - Contraindication: Do not use >6 months of age; risk of avascular necrosis increases with forced abduction
   - Avascular necrosis rate with proper use: 0-5%
   - Wear full-time for 6-12 weeks, then wean

### Clubfoot (Talipes Equinovarus)
3. **Pirani S, Outerbridge HK, Sawatzky B, Stothers K. "A reliable method of clinically evaluating a virgin clubfoot evaluation." 21st SICOT Congress. 1999.**
   - Pirani scoring system: 6 parameters, scored 0/0.5/1 each (total 0-6)
   - Hindfoot: severity of posterior crease, emptiness of heel, rigidity of equinus
   - Midfoot: curvature of lateral border, severity of medial crease, lateral head of talus coverage
   - Higher score = more severe deformity; score 0 = corrected

4. **Morcuende JA, Dolan LA, Dietz FR, Ponseti IV. "Radical reduction in the rate of extensive corrective surgery for clubfoot using the Ponseti method." Pediatrics. 2004;113(2):376-380.**
   - Demonstrated Ponseti method reduces surgical intervention rates from ~70-90% (with traditional methods) to <5%

### Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease
5. **Herring JT, Kim HT, Browne R. J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2004;86(10):2103-2120.**
   - Lateral pillar classification (Herring classification):
     - Group A: Full lateral pillar height maintained — good prognosis
     - Group B: >50% lateral pillar height maintained — intermediate prognosis
     - Group B/C: Exactly 50% lateral pillar height — border group, poorer than B
     - Group C: <50% lateral pillar height — poor prognosis
   - Best predictor of outcome, especially in children >8 years

6. **Stulberg SD, Cooperman DR, Wallensten R. "The natural history of Legg-Calve-Perthes disease." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 1981;63(7):1095-1108.**
   - Stulberg classification (outcome at skeletal maturity):
     - Class I: Normal spherical head
     - Class II: Spherical, coxa magna, short neck, steep acetabulum
     - Class III: Ovoid (non-spherical) but congruent
     - Class IV: Flat head, flat acetabulum
     - Class V: Flat head, normal acetabulum (incongruent)
     - I-II: Good long-term; III-IV: Fair; V: Poor (early arthritis)

### SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis)
7. **Loder RT, Richards BS, Shapiro PS, et al. "Acute slipped capital femoral epiphysis: the importance of physeal stability." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 1993;75(8):1134-1140.**
   - Classification: Stable (able to weight-bear) vs. Unstable (unable to weight-bear)
   - Stable: 0-10% AVN risk; Unstable: 24-47% AVN risk
   - Treatment: In situ percutaneous screw fixation for stable SCFE

### Scoliosis — BrAIST
8. **Weinstein SL, Dolan LA, Wright JG, Dobbs MB. N Engl J Med. 2013;369(16):1512-1521.**
   - See Guidelines section above — this is the definitive bracing trial

9. **Weinstein SL, Dolan LA, Spratt KF, et al. "Health and function of patients with untreated idiopathic scoliosis: a 50-year natural history study." JAMA. 2003;289(5):559-567.**
   - 50-year follow-up: Untreated AIS patients had back pain and cosmetic concerns but most had productive lives
   - Curves >50 degrees continued to progress in adulthood at ~1 degree/year

### OI (Osteogenesis Imperfecta)
10. **Sillence DO, Senn A, Danks DM. "Genetic heterogeneity in osteogenesis imperfecta." J Med Genet. 1979;16(2):101-116.**
    - Original Sillence classification: 4 types (now expanded to >15 molecular subtypes)

11. **Glorieux FH, Bishop NJ, Plotkin H, et al. "Cyclic administration of pamidronate in children with severe osteogenesis imperfecta." N Engl J Med. 1998;339(14):947-952.**
    - IV pamidronate reduces fracture rate, improves BMD, and decreases bone pain in children with severe OI
    - Became standard of care for moderate-severe OI

## RECENT EVIDENCE (2018-2024)

1. **AAOS DDH Clinical Practice Guideline (2022 update).** Evidence-based review supporting Pavlik harness, closed reduction, and highlighting need for improved screening.

2. **Dobbs MB, Gurnett CA. "Update on clubfoot: etiology and treatment." Clin Orthop Relat Res. 2009;467(5):1146-1153** and **Gurnett CA, et al. "Asymmetric lower-limb malformations in individuals with homeobox gene mutations." Am J Hum Genet. 2008.**
   - Genetic basis of clubfoot emerging; PITX1 gene mutations; multifactorial etiology

3. **Nault ML, Parent S, Phan P, et al. "A modified Risser grading system predicts the curve acceleration phase of female adolescent idiopathic scoliosis." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2010;92(5):1073-1081.**
   - Refined Risser grading to better predict growth velocity and curve progression risk

4. **Roposch A, Liu LQ, Hefti F, et al. "Standardized diagnostic criteria for developmental dysplasia of the hip in early infancy." Clin Orthop Relat Res. 2011;469(12):3451-3461.**
   - Effort to standardize DDH diagnostic criteria

5. **Divi SN, et al. "BrAIST Long-Term Follow-Up." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2020.** Extension data supporting durability of bracing benefit.

## CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS

### DDH Clinical Tests
| Test | Description | What It Detects |
|---|---|---|
| **Barlow test** | Adduction + posterior force to hip in 90-degree flexion | Dislocatable hip (hip is IN joint; test pushes it OUT) |
| **Ortolani test** | Abduction + anterior lift of hip in 90-degree flexion | Reducible hip (hip is OUT; test puts it back IN) — "clunk" of reduction |
| **Galeazzi sign** | Compare knee heights with hips/knees flexed | Unilateral dislocation — affected side knee lower |
| **Trendelenburg sign** | Stand on affected leg; pelvis drops on opposite side | Weak hip abductors / unstable hip (older child) |

- **Board Pearl**: Barlow = provocative (makes it dislocate); Ortolani = reductive (puts it back in)
- **Board Pearl**: "Barlow is Bad" (dislocates); "Ortolani is OK" (reduces)

### Graf Ultrasound Classification (see Landmark Studies above)

### Clubfoot — CAVE Mnemonic
| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **C** | Cavus (high medial arch from forefoot pronation) |
| **A** | Adductus (forefoot adduction) |
| **V** | Varus (hindfoot varus/inversion) |
| **E** | Equinus (ankle plantarflexion from tight Achilles) |

- Correction order in Ponseti method: CAVE — correct Cavus first (supinating forefoot), then Adductus and Varus simultaneously with serial casting, then Equinus last (Achilles tenotomy)

### Pirani Score (see Landmark Studies above)
- Total score 0-6; sum of hindfoot (0-3) and midfoot (0-3) subscores
- Each of 6 parameters: 0 (normal), 0.5 (moderate), 1 (severe)

### Legg-Calve-Perthes — Herring Lateral Pillar Classification
| Group | Lateral Pillar Height | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| A | Full height maintained | Good |
| B | >50% height maintained | Intermediate |
| B/C | ~50% height | Guarded |
| C | <50% height | Poor |

### Legg-Calve-Perthes — Catterall Classification (historical)
| Group | Head Involvement | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| I | <25% anterior head | Good |
| II | <50% head | Good |
| III | >50% head | Fair |
| IV | Whole head | Poor |

### Legg-Calve-Perthes — Waldenstrom Stages
| Stage | Radiographic Findings |
|---|---|
| Initial/Necrosis | Increased density of femoral head; joint space widening |
| Fragmentation | Head becomes fragmented; mixed lytic/sclerotic areas |
| Re-ossification | New bone formation; head begins to reform |
| Remodeling | Final head shape established by skeletal maturity |

### Scoliosis — Cobb Angle Measurement
- **Measurement**: Angle between perpendicular lines drawn from the superior endplate of the most tilted vertebra above and the inferior endplate of the most tilted vertebra below
- **Treatment thresholds**:
  - <10 degrees: Not scoliosis (normal variant)
  - 10-24 degrees: Observation, serial radiographs every 6-12 months in growing child
  - 25-40 degrees: Brace (TLSO/Boston brace); >=13 hrs/day per BrAIST
  - 40-50 degrees (or >50 degrees): Surgical intervention (posterior spinal fusion)
  - >50 degrees: Likely to progress even after skeletal maturity (~1 degree/year)

### Risser Sign (Iliac Apophysis Ossification)
| Grade | Ossification | Growth Remaining |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | No ossification | Significant growth remaining |
| 1 | 0-25% lateral ossification | — |
| 2 | 25-50% | — |
| 3 | 50-75% | — |
| 4 | 75-100% (not fused) | Minimal growth |
| 5 | Complete fusion | Skeletal maturity reached |
- **Board Pearl**: Risser 0-1 with Cobb >20 degrees = highest progression risk; bracing most effective at Risser 0-2

### Salter-Harris Classification (SALTR Mnemonic)
| Type | Mnemonic | Fracture Pattern | Frequency | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | **S**lipped / **S**traight across | Through physis only (epiphysis slips) | 6% | Good |
| II | **A**bove | Through physis + metaphysis (Thurston-Holland fragment) | 75% | Good |
| III | **L**ower / be**L**ow | Through physis + epiphysis (intra-articular) | 8% | Fair (need anatomic reduction) |
| IV | **T**hrough / **T**wo | Through metaphysis + physis + epiphysis | 10% | Fair-poor (ORIF usually needed) |
| V | **R**ammed / c**R**ush | Crush injury to physis | 1% | Poor (growth arrest common) |

- **Board Pearl**: Types III, IV, V have highest risk of growth disturbance
- **Board Pearl**: Thurston-Holland fragment = pathognomonic for Type II
- **Board Pearl**: Type I may have normal X-ray; diagnosed by tenderness over physis
- **Board Pearl**: Peterson Type VI (missing physis from trauma/infection/burn) added later

## BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD PEARLS

### DDH
1. **Barlow** = dislocatable (hip is in, test pushes it out); **Ortolani** = reducible (hip is out, test puts it back in) — MOST TESTED CONCEPT
2. **Galeazzi sign**: Unequal knee heights with hips/knees flexed = unilateral dislocation
3. **Pavlik harness**: First-line for DDH <6 months; flexion 100-110 degrees, abduction <70 degrees
4. **Pavlik harness contraindications**: Age >6 months, teratologic dislocation, failed 3-4 week trial
5. **Avascular necrosis** risk: Forced abduction (>70 degrees) or pressure in Pavlik harness
6. **Left hip predominance** (60%): Left hip is more commonly affected because LOA (left occiput anterior) is most common fetal position, placing left hip against maternal sacrum
7. **Ultrasound before 6 months; X-ray after 4-6 months** (ossific nucleus appears)
8. **Asymmetric skin folds**: Suggestive but NOT diagnostic (present in 25% of normal infants)

### Clubfoot
9. **CAVE mnemonic**: Cavus, Adductus, Varus, Equinus — four components
10. **Ponseti method**: Serial casting (weekly x 5-7), then Achilles tenotomy (80%), then bracing (FAO x 4 years)
11. **Correct CAVE in order**: Cavus first, then adductus/varus, equinus LAST
12. **Pirani score**: 0-6; guides treatment decisions
13. **Relapse rate**: 20-40% without bracing compliance; <5% with compliance
14. **Bilateral**: 50% of cases
15. **Associated conditions**: Myelomeningocele, arthrogryposis, trisomy 18, amniotic band syndrome

### Legg-Calve-Perthes
16. **Age 4-10 years** (peak 5-7); M:F 4-5:1
17. **Herring lateral pillar classification**: Most important prognostic classification
18. **Prognosis**: Age at onset is KEY — children <6 years have better prognosis; >8 years have poor prognosis
19. **Stages**: Initial (necrosis) → Fragmentation → Re-ossification → Remodeling (Waldenstrom stages)
20. **Bilateral in 10-15%**: Consider endocrine causes (hypothyroidism, GH deficiency) if bilateral
21. **"Head at risk" signs**: Gage sign, calcification lateral to epiphysis, lateral subluxation, horizontal physis
22. **Treatment**: <6 years + Group A-B: observation; >8 years + Group B/C-C: containment (femoral/pelvic osteotomy)

### SCFE
23. **Classic patient**: Overweight adolescent boy (10-16 years)
24. **Presentation**: Antalgic gait, externally rotated leg, knee/thigh pain (referred pain!!)
25. **Exam**: Obligatory external rotation with hip flexion (Drehmann sign)
26. **Bilateral**: 18-50%; consider prophylactic pinning of contralateral side (especially if <10 years, endocrine disorder)
27. **Stable vs. Unstable**: Ability to weight-bear determines classification; Unstable has 24-47% AVN risk
28. **Treatment**: In situ single screw fixation (do NOT reduce stable SCFE — increased AVN risk)
29. **EMERGENT**: Unstable SCFE requires urgent surgical stabilization
30. **Frog-leg lateral X-ray**: Best view to detect early SCFE; Klein line (line along superior femoral neck) fails to intersect femoral head
31. **"Ice cream falling off the cone"**: Classic radiographic analogy
32. **Do NOT manipulate/reduce a stable SCFE**: Forced reduction increases AVN risk dramatically

### Osgood-Schlatter Disease
33. **Traction apophysitis**: Tibial tubercle
34. **Patellar tendon insertion**: Repetitive traction during growth
35. **Self-limited**: Resolves with skeletal maturity
36. **Treatment**: Activity modification, ice, NSAIDs, quadriceps stretching, patellar tendon strap
37. **Sinding-Larsen-Johansson**: Same mechanism but at inferior pole of patella (compare)

### Scoliosis
38. **BrAIST trial**: Bracing effective (72% vs 48%); >=13 hrs/day wear = 90-93% success
39. **Cobb angle thresholds**: <10 not scoliosis; 10-24 observe; 25-40 brace; >40-50 surgery
40. **Risser 0-1 + Cobb >20 degrees**: Highest progression risk
41. **Right thoracic curve**: Most common pattern in AIS
42. **Left thoracic curve**: Red flag — obtain MRI to rule out syringomyelia, Chiari, tumor
43. **Curve progression after maturity**: Curves >50 degrees continue at ~1 degree/year
44. **Adams forward bend test**: Screening test; scoliometer >=7 degrees = referral
45. **Scoliosis in NM disease**: Different from idiopathic; long C-shaped curves, more progressive, often need earlier surgery

### Torticollis (Congenital Muscular Torticollis)
46. **Head tilts toward and chin rotates away** from affected SCM
47. **Right SCM predominance** (75% right-sided)
48. **SCM mass (fibromatosis colli)**: 20% have palpable mass; resolves by 4-6 months
49. **Associated DDH**: Screen hip ultrasound in all CMT patients (10-20% co-occurrence)
50. **Associated plagiocephaly**: 80-90%
51. **Treatment**: Stretching (passive range of motion), positioning; 90-95% resolve with conservative management by age 1 year
52. **Surgery**: SCM release if not resolved by 12-18 months despite therapy
53. **Must rule out**: Posterior fossa tumor, Klippel-Feil, atlantoaxial rotary subluxation, ocular torticollis

### OI (Osteogenesis Imperfecta)
54. **Type I (mild)**: Most common; autosomal dominant; blue sclerae, normal height or mildly short, fractures decrease after puberty
55. **Type II (perinatal lethal)**: Most severe; die in utero or shortly after birth; beaded ribs, severe deformity
56. **Type III (severe deforming)**: Severe; compatible with life but progressive deformity, very short stature, dentinogenesis imperfecta, gray/blue sclerae
57. **Type IV (moderate)**: Variable; normal-gray sclerae; moderate severity
58. **Blue sclerae**: Classic in Type I (thin sclera allows choroid color to show)
59. **Dentinogenesis imperfecta**: Opalescent teeth (Type I [50%], III, IV)
60. **Wormian bones**: Extra bones in cranial sutures
61. **Hearing loss**: ~50% in Type I (mixed conductive/sensorineural); typically starts in 2nd-3rd decade
62. **Bisphosphonates (pamidronate/zoledronate)**: Increase BMD, reduce fracture rate, decrease bone pain
63. **Collagen Type I defect**: COL1A1/COL1A2 gene mutations (quantitative in Type I, qualitative in types II-IV)
64. **Board Pearl**: Must distinguish from NAT/child abuse — both present with multiple fractures; OI has other features (blue sclerae, FH, wormian bones)

### Salter-Harris
65. **Type II is most common** (75%)
66. **Type I may have normal X-ray**: Diagnosed clinically by tenderness at physis
67. **Thurston-Holland fragment**: Triangular metaphyseal fragment = Type II
68. **Types III-V**: Higher risk of growth arrest; III and IV require anatomic reduction (ORIF)
69. **Type V**: Crush injury, worst prognosis, often retrospective diagnosis

### Sports Injuries — Pediatric Specific
70. **Little League elbow**: Medial epicondyle apophysitis (traction injury from valgus stress in throwing)
71. **Little League shoulder**: Proximal humeral physis stress fracture (widened proximal humeral physis)
72. **Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD)**: Capitellum (lateral elbow) in throwers; lateral knee in adolescents
73. **Pitch count limits**: USA Baseball guidelines to prevent overuse injuries

### Limping Child — Differential by Age
| Age | Key Diagnoses |
|---|---|
| 1-3 years | Toddler's fracture, septic arthritis, DDH (late presentation), JIA, leukemia |
| 4-10 years | Legg-Calve-Perthes, septic arthritis, transient synovitis, JIA, Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma |
| 11-16 years | SCFE, Osgood-Schlatter, OCD, stress fractures, osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma |
| Any age | Septic arthritis (emergency!), osteomyelitis, fracture, malignancy, JIA |

- **Board Pearl**: Transient synovitis vs. septic arthritis — Kocher criteria (see Ch 10)
- **Board Pearl**: Toddler's fracture — spiral fracture of distal tibia; often occult on initial X-ray
