# Chapter 5: Brachial Plexus Birth Injuries (BPBI) — Research Notes

---

## SECTION 1: ANATOMY & PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

### 1.1 Brachial Plexus Anatomy

- **Roots**: C5, C6, C7, C8, T1 (ventral rami of spinal nerves)
- **Trunks**:
  - Upper trunk: C5 + C6
  - Middle trunk: C7
  - Lower trunk: C8 + T1
- **Divisions**: Each trunk splits into anterior and posterior divisions (6 total)
- **Cords** (named by relationship to axillary artery):
  - Lateral cord: Anterior divisions of upper + middle trunks → musculocutaneous nerve, lateral contribution to median nerve
  - Posterior cord: All three posterior divisions → axillary nerve, radial nerve
  - Medial cord: Anterior division of lower trunk → ulnar nerve, medial contribution to median nerve, medial pectoral nerve, medial cutaneous nerves of arm and forearm
- **Terminal branches**: Musculocutaneous, axillary, radial, median, ulnar nerves
- **Key branches from roots/trunks**: Dorsal scapular nerve (C5 root), long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7 roots), suprascapular nerve (upper trunk), nerve to subclavius (upper trunk)

### 1.2 Nerve Injury Classification (Seddon / Sunderland)

| Seddon | Sunderland | Pathology | Prognosis |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Neurapraxia | Grade I | Focal demyelination; axon intact | Complete recovery, weeks-months |
| Axonotmesis | Grade II | Axon disrupted, endoneurium intact | Good recovery (axon regrows ~1 mm/day) |
| Axonotmesis | Grade III | Axon + endoneurium disrupted, perineurium intact | Partial recovery, aberrant regeneration |
| Axonotmesis | Grade IV | Axon, endoneurium, perineurium disrupted; epineurium intact (neuroma in continuity) | No spontaneous recovery; surgery needed |
| Neurotmesis | Grade V | Complete transection of nerve | No spontaneous recovery; surgery required |
| — | Grade VI (Mackinnon) | Mixed injury pattern along nerve | Variable |

### 1.3 Pathophysiology of Birth Injury

- **Mechanism**: Lateral traction/stretch on the neck and shoulder during delivery, separating the head from the shoulder
  - Vertex delivery: Lateral traction on head away from affected shoulder (ipsilateral shoulder impacted)
  - Breech delivery: Lateral traction on trunk/arms; lower plexus more commonly affected
- **Injury types**: Stretch (most common), rupture, avulsion (root torn from spinal cord — worst prognosis, no spontaneous recovery)
- **Root avulsion signs**: Winged scapula (long thoracic nerve, C5-C7), rhomboid weakness (dorsal scapular nerve, C5), Horner syndrome (T1 sympathetic fibers), elevated hemidiaphragm (phrenic nerve, C3-C5), absent paraspinal muscle denervation on EMG (suggests preganglionic injury)

## SECTION 2: CLASSIFICATION OF BRACHIAL PLEXUS BIRTH INJURIES

### 2.1 Erb-Duchenne Palsy (Upper Plexus — C5-C6)

- **Most common type**: ~70-80% of all BPBI
- **Roots involved**: C5, C6 (sometimes C7 — "extended Erb palsy")
- **Muscles affected**: Deltoid (C5,6 — axillary n.), supraspinatus/infraspinatus (C5,6 — suprascapular n.), biceps (C5,6 — musculocutaneous n.), brachialis (C5,6), brachioradialis (C5,6 — radial n.), supinator, external rotators of shoulder
- **Classic posture ("Waiter's Tip")**: Arm adducted and internally rotated at shoulder, elbow extended, forearm pronated, wrist flexed. **High-yield board description.**
- **Lost movements**: Shoulder abduction, external rotation, elbow flexion, forearm supination
- **Preserved**: Hand grip (C8, T1 — intact)
- **Prognosis**: Best among BPBI types; 70-92% show spontaneous recovery (depending on severity and definition of "recovery")

### 2.2 Klumpke Palsy (Lower Plexus — C8-T1)

- **Rare as isolated birth injury**: <1-2% of BPBI (much more common in adult traumatic plexus injuries)
- **Roots involved**: C8, T1
- **Muscles affected**: Intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, lumbricals, thenar, hypothenar), wrist flexors, finger flexors (FDP to ring/small finger), forearm flexors
- **Clinical**: Claw hand deformity (MCP hyperextension, IP flexion), weak grip, sensory loss medial arm/forearm/hand
- **Horner syndrome**: Ipsilateral miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis — indicates T1 root avulsion (disruption of preganglionic sympathetic fibers from T1 that travel to superior cervical ganglion). **Board pearl**: Horner syndrome with BPBI indicates T1 involvement and poor prognosis.
- **Prognosis**: Generally poorer than upper plexus injuries due to avulsion mechanism

### 2.3 Total (Complete) Plexus Palsy (C5-T1)

- **Frequency**: ~20-30% of BPBI
- **All roots**: C5 through T1
- **Clinical**: Flail arm — complete paralysis of the entire upper extremity; absent Moro reflex on affected side, no grip, no arm movement
- **Associated**: Horner syndrome (common — indicates poor prognosis), phrenic nerve palsy (elevated hemidiaphragm), respiratory distress
- **Prognosis**: Worst; spontaneous recovery less likely and less complete; most likely to require surgical intervention

### 2.4 Narakas Classification (4 Groups)

**Important classification system for BPBI — board-relevant.**

| Group | Roots | Description | Prognosis |
|-------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Group I | C5-C6 | Upper Erb palsy | Best — ~90% spontaneous recovery |
| Group II | C5-C7 | Extended Erb palsy (upper + middle trunk) | ~65% spontaneous recovery |
| Group III | C5-T1 | Total palsy without Horner syndrome | ~50% spontaneous recovery |
| Group IV | C5-T1 | Total palsy WITH Horner syndrome | Worst — <5% spontaneous recovery; indicates root avulsion |

- **Reference**: Narakas AO. Obstetrical brachial plexus injuries. In: Lamb DW, ed. The Paralyzed Hand. Edinburgh: Churchill Livingstone; 1987:116-135.
- **Significance**: Narakas Group IV (total palsy + Horner) essentially mandates surgical exploration — no meaningful spontaneous recovery expected

## SECTION 3: EPIDEMIOLOGY & RISK FACTORS

### 3.1 Incidence

- **Overall incidence**: 0.4-4.6 per 1,000 live births (varies by population and era)
- **Recent US estimates**: ~0.4-1.5 per 1,000 live births
- **Declining trend**: Due to improved obstetric practices, increased cesarean section rates for anticipated difficult deliveries
- **Despite improvements**: Has NOT been eliminated — still occurs even with cesarean deliveries (rare but documented)

### 3.2 Risk Factors

#### Maternal/Fetal Risk Factors
- **Shoulder dystocia**: Strongest risk factor (~100x increased risk); present in ~50% of BPBI cases; however, ~50% of BPBI occurs WITHOUT documented shoulder dystocia
- **Macrosomia**: Birth weight >4,000 g (particularly >4,500 g); OR 4-14x
- **Maternal diabetes** (gestational or pre-gestational): Associated with macrosomia
- **Maternal obesity**
- **Multiparity**
- **Post-term pregnancy** (>42 weeks)
- **Previous child with BPBI** (recurrence risk ~5-15%)

#### Delivery Risk Factors
- **Prolonged second stage of labor**
- **Instrumental delivery** (forceps, vacuum extraction)
- **Breech presentation** (associated with lower plexus injuries)
- **Assisted traction during delivery**

#### Other Considerations
- **Board pearl**: BPBI can occur in cesarean deliveries (rare, ~1-4% of BPBI cases), indicating that intrauterine positioning/forces may contribute
- **Board pearl**: BPBI can occur without ANY identifiable risk factors — this is important for medicolegal considerations
- **Bilateral BPBI**: Rare (~2-4%); associated with breech delivery, very large infants

### 3.3 Associated Injuries

- **Clavicle fracture**: Most common associated injury (~10-15%); ipsilateral; actually may be somewhat protective (absorbs energy)
- **Humeral fracture**: Proximal humerus or shaft
- **Torticollis**: From SCM injury or postural
- **Phrenic nerve palsy**: C3-C5; elevated hemidiaphragm; ~5% of BPBI; more common with total palsy
- **Facial nerve palsy**: From forceps or birth trauma
- **Cervical spinal cord injury**: Rare but must be considered with severe presentations

## SECTION 4: ASSESSMENT SCALES & EVALUATION

### 4.1 Active Movement Scale (AMS)

- **Developed by**: Curtis et al., Hospital for Sick Children, Toronto
- **Reference**: Curtis C, Stephens D, Clarke HM, et al. The Active Movement Scale: an evaluative tool for infants with obstetrical brachial plexus palsy. Journal of Hand Surgery (American). 2002;27(3):470-478.
- **Purpose**: Standardized assessment of motor function in each muscle group for infants with BPBI
- **Scoring**: Each muscle group graded on 0-7 scale based on observation of spontaneous and stimulated movements:
  - 0 = No contraction (gravity eliminated)
  - 1 = Contraction, no movement (gravity eliminated)
  - 2 = Movement ≤1/2 range (gravity eliminated)
  - 3 = Movement >1/2 range (gravity eliminated)
  - 4 = Full range movement (gravity eliminated)
  - 5 = Movement ≤1/2 range (against gravity)
  - 6 = Movement >1/2 range (against gravity)
  - 7 = Full movement (against gravity)
- **Movements tested**: 15 movements total — shoulder flexion, abduction, adduction, internal rotation, external rotation; elbow flexion, extension; forearm pronation, supination; wrist flexion, extension; finger flexion, extension; thumb flexion, extension
- **Clinical use**: Serial assessments to track recovery; guides surgical decision-making; widely used outcome measure in BPBI research

### 4.2 Toronto Test Score (Toronto Score)

- **Developed by**: Michelow et al., Hospital for Sick Children, Toronto
- **Reference**: Michelow BJ, Clarke HM, Curtis CG, et al. The natural history of obstetrical brachial plexus palsy. Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery. 1994;93(4):675-680.
- **Purpose**: Early predictor of recovery; used to identify infants who may benefit from microsurgical intervention
- **Method**: Sum of AMS scores for elbow flexion, elbow extension, wrist extension, finger extension, thumb extension at age 3 months
- **Scoring interpretation**:
  - **Total score >3.5 at 3 months**: Predicts good spontaneous recovery; continue conservative management
  - **Total score ≤3.5 at 3 months**: Predicts poor spontaneous recovery; surgical exploration recommended
- **Board pearl**: The Toronto score at 3 months is used as a screening tool to identify candidates for early microsurgical reconstruction
- **Significance**: Helped establish the 3-month evaluation time point as critical for decision-making

### 4.3 Cookie Test (Toronto Cookie Test)

- **Purpose**: Functional assessment of the ability to bring the hand to the mouth — tests elbow flexion and forearm supination (C5-C6 function)
- **Method**: Child is offered a cookie (or attractive object); ability to bring it to mouth is observed
- **Clinical significance**: Tests the most functionally important movement in upper Erb palsy — combined elbow flexion and supination
- **Decision-making**: Failure to achieve "cookie test" (inability to bring hand to mouth with active elbow flexion and supination) by 6-9 months is considered an indication for microsurgical reconstruction by many surgeons
- **Simple, practical, and functionally meaningful test** — widely referenced in BPBI literature

### 4.4 Mallet Classification (Shoulder Function)

- **Purpose**: Grades global shoulder function in children with BPBI (typically assessed after age 3-4 years)
- **Reference**: Mallet J. Obstetrical paralysis of the brachial plexus. II. Therapeutics. Treatment of sequelae. Priority for the treatment of the shoulder. Method for the expression of results. Revue de Chirurgie Orthopedique et Reparatrice de l'Appareil Moteur. 1972;58(Suppl 1):166-168.
- **Grades**: I-V for each of 5 movements:
  - Global abduction
  - Global external rotation
  - Hand to neck
  - Hand to back
  - Hand to mouth
- **Grade I**: No function
- **Grade V**: Normal function
- **Widely used** for evaluating surgical outcomes and long-term follow-up

### 4.5 Additional Assessment Tools

- **Gilbert and Raimondi Scale**: Hand function grading system for BPBI
- **Raimondi Hand Scale**: 0-V for hand function (grasp patterns)
- **Modified Gilbert Shoulder Scale**: Shoulder function grading
- **Pediatric Outcomes Data Collection Instrument (PODCI)**: Parent-reported outcome measure
- **Brachial Plexus Outcome Measure (BPOM)**: Activity-based functional assessment

### 4.6 Imaging

- **Ultrasound**: Can identify pseudomeningoceles (suggesting root avulsion), nerve thickening; limited role
- **MRI**: High-resolution MRI of brachial plexus — can visualize root avulsions, pseudomeningoceles, neuromas; increasingly used for surgical planning; MRI neurography with 3T provides excellent detail
- **CT myelography**: Historical gold standard for root avulsion detection; largely replaced by MRI
- **Shoulder imaging**: X-ray/MRI to assess glenohumeral joint development, posterior subluxation/dislocation, glenoid retroversion (important for long-term follow-up)

### 4.7 Electrodiagnostics

- **Timing**: EMG/NCS typically performed at 3-4 weeks (after Wallerian degeneration has occurred) and repeated at 3-6 months
- **NCS**: Reduced/absent CMAP amplitudes in affected nerve distributions; sensory nerve responses may be preserved in avulsion (preganglionic injury — DRG intact, so SNAP preserved despite clinical sensory loss). **Board pearl**: Preserved SNAP with clinical sensory loss suggests root avulsion.
- **EMG**: Fibrillations/positive sharp waves (denervation); reduced/absent recruitment; presence of MUPs on follow-up EMG indicates reinnervation
- **Limitations**: Difficult in infants; limited cooperation; requires sedation in some cases

## SECTION 5: NATURAL HISTORY & RECOVERY

### 5.1 Spontaneous Recovery Rates

- **Overall**: ~70-92% of BPBI show some spontaneous recovery (varies widely by definition of "recovery" and injury pattern)
- **Upper plexus (C5-C6)**: ~80-92% spontaneous recovery with conservative management; most recovery occurs within first 3-6 months
- **Extended Erb (C5-C7)**: ~65% spontaneous recovery
- **Total palsy without Horner**: ~50% spontaneous recovery; recovery pattern matters — early return of hand function (within 2-3 months) suggests better prognosis
- **Total palsy with Horner**: <5% meaningful spontaneous recovery — essentially requires surgical intervention
- **Timeline**: Most spontaneous recovery occurs in first 3-6 months; continued improvement possible up to 12-24 months; after 18-24 months, further spontaneous recovery unlikely

### 5.2 Predictors of Poor Recovery

- **At birth**: Total palsy, Horner syndrome, phrenic nerve palsy
- **At 1 month**: No biceps function, no finger extension
- **At 3 months** (most commonly used decision point):
  - Toronto test score ≤3.5
  - No biceps function (AMS <6 for elbow flexion against gravity)
  - No finger/wrist extension
  - Cookie test failure
- **At 6 months**: No biceps function is virtually universally accepted indication for surgical exploration
- **At 9 months**: Persistent functional deficits — some centers still consider primary nerve surgery up to this point

### 5.3 Long-Term Sequelae

- **Muscle co-contractions**: Simultaneous activation of antagonist muscles (especially shoulder internal/external rotators) — from aberrant reinnervation
- **Shoulder internal rotation contracture**: Most common and functionally significant long-term problem; leads to posterior glenohumeral subluxation/dislocation and glenoid dysplasia
- **Glenohumeral dysplasia**: Progressive posterior subluxation of humeral head, glenoid retroversion, biconcavity; caused by muscle imbalance (strong internal rotators, weak external rotators) acting on growing skeleton
- **Elbow flexion contracture**: Common with C5-C6 injuries
- **Forearm supination deficit**: Common functional limitation
- **Limb length discrepancy**: Affected arm may be shorter
- **Joint contractures**: Various locations
- **Functional limitations**: Reaching overhead, bimanual tasks, grooming, dressing
- **Psychosocial impact**: Body image concerns, participation limitations, reduced quality of life

## SECTION 6: MANAGEMENT

### 6.1 Conservative Management (Initial Phase)

- **First-line for all BPBI**: Initial management is conservative/observation
- **Gentle ROM exercises**: Begin within first 1-2 weeks after birth (once initial edema resolves); parents taught to perform gentle passive ROM to prevent contractures, especially:
  - Shoulder external rotation and abduction
  - Elbow extension
  - Forearm supination
  - Wrist and finger extension
- **Positioning**: Avoid prolonged positions that reinforce contracture patterns
- **Splinting**: Generally NOT recommended in neonatal period; may be used later for specific contracture prevention (e.g., wrist extension splint, supination splint)
- **Sensory stimulation**: Tactile stimulation to affected limb to promote cortical representation
- **Parent education**: Handling, positioning, ROM exercises, activity encouragement
- **Serial assessment**: Regular clinical evaluations using AMS; typically monthly in first 3-6 months, then quarterly

### 6.2 Microsurgical Timing Controversy (3-6-9 Month Debate)

**This is a high-yield board topic — the optimal timing of primary nerve surgery remains debated.**

#### 3-Month Approach (Early Surgery)
- **Proponents**: Gilbert, Tassin (France); many European centers
- **Rationale**: Motor endplate viability declines with time; earlier reinnervation leads to better outcomes; more time for motor learning after reinnervation
- **Evidence**: Gilbert A, Tassin JL. Surgical repair of the brachial plexus in obstetric paralysis. Chirurgie. 1984;110(1):70-75.
- **Indications at 3 months**: No biceps recovery (AMS <3 for elbow flexion), Toronto score ≤3.5
- **Criticism**: May operate on some infants who would have recovered spontaneously

#### 6-Month Approach (Intermediate)
- **Proponents**: Clarke, Curtis (Toronto/Hospital for Sick Children); many North American centers
- **Rationale**: More time to observe recovery; Toronto test at 3 months used as screening, with surgery at 6 months for those failing to recover
- **Evidence**: Clarke HM, Curtis CG. An approach to obstetrical brachial plexus injuries. Hand Clinics. 1995;11(4):563-580.
- **Indications at 6 months**: Failure of biceps recovery (no antigravity elbow flexion), failure of cookie test
- **Most widely accepted** timing in current practice

#### 9-Month Approach (Late Surgery)
- **Proponents**: Some centers, particularly those emphasizing maximizing spontaneous recovery
- **Rationale**: Allow maximum time for spontaneous recovery; reduce unnecessary surgery
- **Indication**: No useful hand function, persistent total palsy
- **Criticism**: May miss optimal window for reinnervation; poorer outcomes for shoulder reported with later surgery

#### Current Consensus (Approximate)
- **Most centers**: Surgical exploration at 3-6 months for total palsy with Horner syndrome; 6-9 months for upper plexus injuries failing to recover
- **No universal consensus exists** — practice varies by center and surgeon preference
- **Trend**: Moving toward earlier intervention, particularly for severe injuries (Narakas Group III-IV)
- **Key reference**: Hale HB, Bae DS, Waters PM. Current concepts in the management of brachial plexus birth palsy. Journal of Hand Surgery (American). 2010;35(2):322-331.

### 6.3 Surgical Options

#### Primary Nerve Surgery (Microsurgical Reconstruction)
- **Nerve grafting**: Sural nerve grafts (autologous) used to bridge gaps after resection of neuroma; most common primary reconstruction technique; interposition grafts from root/trunk to target nerve
- **Nerve transfers (Neurotization)**:
  - **Oberlin transfer**: Transfer of fascicle(s) from ulnar nerve (typically FCU fascicle) to the motor branch of biceps (musculocutaneous nerve); most well-known nerve transfer for BPBI
  - **Reference**: Oberlin C, Beal D, Leechavengvongs S, et al. Nerve transfer to biceps muscle using a part of ulnar nerve for C5-C6 avulsion of the brachial plexus: anatomical study and report of four cases. Journal of Hand Surgery (American). 1994;19(2):232-237.
  - Double Oberlin (Mackinnon modification): Ulnar fascicle to biceps motor branch + median fascicle to brachialis branch
  - Spinal accessory nerve to suprascapular nerve transfer (for shoulder abduction/external rotation)
  - Intercostal nerve transfers (for elbow flexion, historically)
  - Contralateral C7 transfer (rarely used in BPBI)
- **Neurolysis**: Freeing nerve from scar tissue (external neurolysis); limited role in BPBI

#### Secondary/Late Procedures (After Primary Surgery or For Residual Deficits)

**Shoulder:**
- **Muscle release/tendon transfer for internal rotation contracture**:
  - Subscapularis slide/release (Carlioz procedure)
  - Latissimus dorsi and teres major transfer to external rotators (L'Episcopo-type procedures)
  - Anterior shoulder release with tendon transfer
- **Shoulder arthrodesis**: Rarely in children; last resort for severe dysplasia
- **Botulinum toxin A**: Injection into internal rotators (subscapularis, pectoralis major) to reduce internal rotation contracture and promote external rotation remodeling; used as adjunct to therapy and sometimes as bridge to surgery
- **Reference**: DeMatteo C, Bain JR, Galea V, et al. Botulinum toxin as an adjunct to motor learning therapy and surgery for obstetrical brachial plexus injury. Developmental Medicine and Child Neurology. 2006;48(4):245-252.

**Elbow:**
- **Steindler flexorplasty**: Transfer of forearm flexor-pronator mass more proximal on humerus to augment elbow flexion
- **Free functioning muscle transfer (FFMT)**: Gracilis free flap for elbow flexion when no local donors available; primarily used in total palsy

**Forearm/Wrist/Hand:**
- **Pronator teres rerouting**: Rerouting pronator teres to become supinator (for supination deficit)
- **Wrist/hand tendon transfers**: For persistent deficits
- **Osteotomies**: Humeral rotation osteotomy for fixed internal rotation deformity (done when skeletal growth sufficient, typically >4-5 years)

### 6.4 Shoulder Dysplasia Management

- **Posterior glenohumeral subluxation/dislocation**: Develops due to muscle imbalance (dominant internal rotation) acting on growing immature glenoid
- **Glenoid retroversion**: Progressive posterior glenoid wear/deformation; can be classified using MRI (Waters classification)
- **Prevention**: Early recognition and treatment of internal rotation contracture; aggressive PT, botulinum toxin, timely surgical releases/transfers
- **Surgical management**: Anterior release + tendon transfer (mild dysplasia); closed/open reduction + tendon transfer (moderate subluxation); glenoid anteversion osteotomy (severe retroversion); humeral osteotomy (severe fixed internal rotation)
- **Serial imaging**: MRI or ultrasound to monitor joint development, especially in first 2-3 years
