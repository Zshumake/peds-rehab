## 6.1 Epidemiology

`[BOARD]`

Traumatic brain injury remains the foremost cause of death and acquired disability among children and adolescents in developed nations. In the United States, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates approximately 475,000 TBI-related emergency department visits per year in children younger than 14 years, resulting in roughly 35,000 hospitalizations and 2,200 deaths annually. The overall pediatric TBI mortality rate is approximately 2 to 5 per 100,000 children, with mortality from severe TBI approaching 15 to 25 percent (Kochanek et al., 2019).

### Age Distribution and Mechanism

The epidemiologic profile of pediatric TBI follows a bimodal pattern, with incidence peaks in infants and toddlers (birth to 4 years) and again in adolescence (15 to 19 years). Males sustain TBI at 1.5 to 2 times the rate of females across all age groups.

| Age Group | Primary Mechanisms |
|---|---|
| Infants (<1 year) | Falls; abusive head trauma (significant cause of severe TBI) |
| Toddlers/Preschool (1-4 years) | Falls (most common mechanism overall) |
| School-age (5-14 years) | Falls, sports/recreation, bicycle injuries, motor vehicle collisions |
| Adolescents (15-19 years) | Motor vehicle collisions (driver/passenger), sports, assaults |

Sport-related concussion accounts for an estimated 1.1 to 1.9 million injuries annually in individuals younger than 18 years. The severity distribution of pediatric TBI is approximately 80 percent mild (concussion), 10 percent moderate, and 10 percent severe.

> **Clinical Pearl:** While infants and toddlers sustain TBI most frequently from falls, clinicians must maintain a high index of suspicion for abusive head trauma in infants presenting with intracranial injury, particularly when the reported mechanism is inconsistent with the severity of findings.

## 6.2 The Glasgow Coma Scale and Pediatric Modifications

`[BOARD]`

### Standard GCS (Children >=2 Years and Adults)

The Glasgow Coma Scale quantifies consciousness across three domains: eye opening, verbal response, and motor response, yielding a composite score from 3 (worst) to 15 (best).

| Component | Response | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye Opening** | Spontaneous | 4 |
| | To voice | 3 |
| | To pain | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Verbal** | Oriented | 5 |
| | Confused | 4 |
| | Inappropriate words | 3 |
| | Incomprehensible sounds | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Motor** | Obeys commands | 6 |
| | Localizes pain | 5 |
| | Withdraws (flexion) | 4 |
| | Abnormal flexion (decorticate) | 3 |
| | Extension (decerebrate) | 2 |
| | None | 1 |

### Pediatric GCS for Preverbal Children (<2 Years)

Because infants and young toddlers cannot produce oriented speech, the verbal and motor subscales are modified to reflect developmentally appropriate responses.

| Component | Response | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye Opening** | Spontaneous | 4 |
| | To voice | 3 |
| | To pain | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Verbal (Modified)** | Coos, babbles, age-appropriate vocalizations | 5 |
| | Irritable, crying, but consolable | 4 |
| | Cries to pain, moaning | 3 |
| | Inconsolable, agitated | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Motor (Modified)** | Spontaneous purposeful movement | 6 |
| | Withdraws to touch | 5 |
| | Withdraws to pain | 4 |
| | Abnormal flexion | 3 |
| | Extension | 2 |
| | None | 1 |

### TBI Severity Classification

| Severity | GCS Score | LOC Duration | PTA Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild (concussion) | 13-15 | <30 minutes | <24 hours |
| Moderate | 9-12 | 30 min to 24 hours | 1-7 days |
| Severe | 3-8 | >24 hours | >7 days |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Post-traumatic amnesia (PTA) duration is among the strongest single predictors of long-term outcome after TBI. A PTA lasting longer than 7 days indicates very severe injury and portends significantly worse neurocognitive recovery.

## 6.3 PECARN Clinical Decision Rules

`[BOARD]`

### Background and Validation

The Pediatric Emergency Care Applied Research Network (PECARN) decision rules represent the largest and most rigorously validated clinical tool for determining which children with head trauma require computed tomography imaging. The landmark derivation study by Kuppermann and colleagues (2009) enrolled 42,412 children younger than 18 years across 25 emergency departments. The rules identify children at very low risk for clinically important TBI (ciTBI), defined as TBI resulting in death, neurosurgical intervention, intubation for more than 24 hours, or hospitalization for two or more nights with abnormal CT findings.

External validation studies have confirmed sensitivity approaching 100 percent for ciTBI in both age groups, with negative predictive values of 100 percent in the low-risk group. Specificity ranges from 33 to 70 percent depending on the study population.

### Algorithm for Children Younger Than 2 Years

**High-risk criteria (CT recommended):**
- GCS 14 or below
- Altered mental status (agitation, somnolence, repetitive questioning, slow response)
- Palpable skull fracture

If any criterion is present, the risk of ciTBI is approximately 4.4 percent and CT is recommended.

**Intermediate-risk criteria (CT versus observation):**
- Non-frontal scalp hematoma (occipital, parietal, or temporal location)
- Loss of consciousness lasting 5 seconds or more
- Severe mechanism of injury (motor vehicle collision with ejection, rollover, or death of another occupant; pedestrian or cyclist without helmet struck by vehicle; fall from more than 3 feet; head struck by high-impact object)
- Not acting normally per parental assessment

If one or more intermediate criteria are present without high-risk findings, the ciTBI risk is approximately 0.9 percent and clinical judgment dictates whether to image or observe.

If no high-risk or intermediate-risk criteria are met, the ciTBI risk is less than 0.02 percent and CT is not recommended.

### Algorithm for Children 2 Years and Older

**High-risk criteria (CT recommended):**
- GCS 14 or below
- Altered mental status
- Signs of basilar skull fracture (hemotympanum, raccoon eyes, Battle sign, CSF otorrhea or rhinorrhea)

If any criterion is present, the ciTBI risk is approximately 4.3 percent and CT is recommended.

**Intermediate-risk criteria (CT versus observation):**
- Any loss of consciousness
- History of vomiting
- Severe mechanism (same as above, except fall threshold is more than 5 feet for children >=2 years)
- Severe headache

If intermediate criteria are present without high-risk findings, observation for 4 to 6 hours is an acceptable alternative to immediate CT.

If no criteria are met, the ciTBI risk is less than 0.05 percent and CT is not recommended.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PECARN fall height thresholds differ by age: greater than 3 feet for children younger than 2 years, and greater than 5 feet for those aged 2 and older. Additionally, scalp hematoma location matters only in the younger age group, where non-frontal hematomas carry higher risk, while basilar skull fracture signs are used exclusively in the older group.

## 6.4 Severity Classification: Beyond GCS

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

While GCS provides the foundation for TBI severity grading, a comprehensive assessment integrates multiple clinical parameters. The Ranchos Los Amigos Levels of Cognitive Functioning Scale (Levels I through X) is used to track recovery trajectory in moderate and severe TBI across cognitive domains from no response (Level I) through purposeful and appropriate functioning (Level X). Although developed for adults, it is applicable to older children and adolescents.

Additional severity markers include the duration and character of PTA, the presence of neuroimaging abnormalities (intracranial hemorrhage, cortical contusion, diffuse axonal injury), and the need for neurosurgical intervention. In children, age-appropriate functional outcome measures such as the WeeFIM (Functional Independence Measure for Children) and the Pediatric Glasgow Outcome Scale-Extended (GOS-E Peds) provide more granular assessments of recovery.

## 6.5 Primary Versus Secondary Brain Injury

`[BOARD]`

### Primary Injury

Primary brain injury occurs at the moment of impact and represents the direct mechanical disruption of brain tissue. The principal mechanisms include:

- **Contact forces:** Skull deformation, fracture, and focal injuries (epidural hematoma, subdural hematoma, cerebral contusion)
- **Inertial (acceleration-deceleration) forces:** Diffuse axonal injury (DAI) from shearing of white matter tracts, particularly at gray-white matter junctions, the corpus callosum, and the brainstem
- **Blast injury:** Relevant in unique pediatric contexts (proximity to fireworks, rare military exposure)

Primary injury is, by definition, not amenable to treatment after it has occurred. All acute management is therefore directed at preventing or mitigating secondary injury.

### Secondary Injury

Secondary injury encompasses the cascade of cellular, biochemical, and physiological events that follow the primary insult and propagate further neural damage over hours to days. Key mechanisms include:

| Mechanism | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| Cerebral edema and elevated ICP | Cytotoxic and vasogenic edema; herniation risk |
| Hypotension and hypoxia | Among the most potent predictors of poor outcome in pediatric TBI |
| Excitotoxicity | Massive glutamate release, calcium influx, mitochondrial failure |
| Neuroinflammation | Microglial activation, cytokine release, blood-brain barrier disruption |
| Ischemia | Microvascular thrombosis, loss of autoregulation |
| Metabolic crisis | Increased glucose demand coupled with decreased supply |
| Seizures | Increase metabolic demand and ICP |
| Coagulopathy | Tissue factor release, disseminated intravascular coagulation |

The Third Edition of the Guidelines for the Management of Pediatric Severe Traumatic Brain Injury (Kochanek et al., 2019) provides evidence-based recommendations focused on minimizing secondary injury, including maintenance of cerebral perfusion pressure, avoidance of hypotension and hypoxia, ICP monitoring, hyperosmolar therapy, and temperature management.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hypotension and hypoxia are the two most modifiable secondary insults. Even a single documented episode of hypotension in the acute period following pediatric severe TBI significantly worsens outcomes. Aggressive prevention of these physiologic derangements is the cornerstone of acute TBI management.

## 6.6 Abusive Head Trauma

`[BOARD]`

### Terminology and Significance

Abusive head trauma (AHT) is the current preferred term, replacing the older designation "shaken baby syndrome" per the American Academy of Pediatrics recommendation (Christian and Block, 2009). AHT encompasses head injury resulting from inflicted trauma — including shaking, impact, or a combination — in infants and young children. It is the most common cause of traumatic death in children younger than 2 years.

### The Classic Triad

The classic constellation of findings associated with AHT consists of:

1. **Subdural hemorrhage(s):** Often bilateral, interhemispheric, or in the posterior fossa; thin-film subdural collections that may be of differing ages (indicating repeat injury)
2. **Retinal hemorrhages:** Characteristically multilayered (preretinal, intraretinal, subretinal), bilateral, extending to the periphery; hemorrhages described as "too numerous to count" are highly concerning for abuse
3. **Encephalopathy or cerebral edema:** Diffuse cerebral swelling, hypoxic-ischemic injury pattern, or diffuse axonal injury

It is important to recognize that all three elements need not be present for a diagnosis, and the triad is not pathognomonic. The differential diagnosis includes accidental trauma, coagulopathies, metabolic diseases such as glutaric aciduria type I, Menkes kinky hair syndrome, and osteogenesis imperfecta.

### Clinical Features and Evaluation

The peak age for AHT is 2 to 4 months. Presenting symptoms are frequently nonspecific — irritability, lethargy, poor feeding, vomiting, seizures, or apnea — which contributes to a well-documented rate of missed or delayed diagnoses (Jenny et al., 1999).

Physical examination may reveal bruising in pre-mobile infants (the clinical aphorism that "those who don't cruise rarely bruise"), a bulging fontanelle, and increasing head circumference. Skeletal survey findings of particular concern include metaphyseal corner fractures (bucket-handle fractures, highly specific for inflicted injury) and posterior rib fractures.

**Evaluation protocol for suspected AHT:**

1. Non-contrast CT of the head
2. MRI of the brain (within 24 to 72 hours)
3. Dilated ophthalmologic examination by an ophthalmologist
4. Full skeletal survey with repeat in 2 weeks
5. Laboratory studies: CBC, coagulation panel, hepatic transaminases, lipase, urinalysis
6. Consideration of metabolic workup when clinically appropriate

Healthcare providers are mandatory reporters of suspected child abuse in all United States jurisdictions.

### Outcomes

Mortality from AHT is approximately 15 to 25 percent. Among survivors, 60 to 80 percent experience significant long-term neurological morbidity, including cognitive impairment, motor deficits, seizure disorders, visual impairment, and behavioral disturbances. Outcomes from AHT tend to be worse than those from accidental TBI of equivalent severity, likely due to the diffuse injury pattern, associated hypoxic-ischemic component, occurrence of repeat injuries, and delays in seeking care.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The absence of a credible mechanism is the single most important red flag for AHT. When a reported mechanism (e.g., "rolled off the couch") is biomechanically inconsistent with the severity of intracranial findings, clinicians must pursue a thorough abuse evaluation.

## 6.7 Concussion: The Amsterdam 2022 Consensus

`[BOARD]`

### Definition and Pathophysiology

Concussion, synonymous with mild TBI in the sports medicine literature, is a biomechanically induced alteration in brain function. By definition, the GCS is 13 to 15, and standard structural neuroimaging is normal. Loss of consciousness, if present, is brief (less than 30 minutes), and post-traumatic amnesia resolves within 24 hours.

The underlying pathophysiology is functional rather than structural: ionic flux (potassium efflux, calcium influx), glutamate release, mitochondrial dysfunction, and a period of metabolic crisis in which glucose demand is elevated while supply is impaired. This metabolic vulnerability typically resolves within 7 to 10 days at the cellular level in adults but may persist longer in children.

### Key Changes in the Amsterdam 2022 Consensus (Sixth International Conference on Concussion in Sport)

The Sixth International Conference on Concussion in Sport, held in Amsterdam in October 2022 and published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine in June 2023 (Patricios et al., 2023), introduced several important updates to clinical practice:

1. **No same-day return to sport:** Athletes must not return to contact or collision sport on the day of a suspected concussion, regardless of symptom resolution.
2. **Symptom-limited activity replaces strict rest:** Prolonged physical and cognitive rest beyond 24 to 48 hours is not beneficial and may be harmful. Early return to light-intensity physical activity within the first 2 days is encouraged. This represents a decisive shift away from the previously advocated "cocoon" approach.
3. **Persistent post-concussion symptoms:** The term "post-concussion syndrome" has been replaced by "persistent post-concussion symptoms," defined as symptoms lasting beyond 4 weeks in children (versus 2 weeks in adults).
4. **Pediatric-specific recommendations:** Children aged 5 to 12 and adolescents aged 13 to 18 are expected to have longer recovery times (2 to 4 weeks typical) compared with adults (10 to 14 days). A more conservative approach to return-to-sport is recommended.
5. **Assessment tools:** The SCAT6 and Child-SCAT6 replace prior versions as the standardized concussion assessment instruments. Greater emphasis is placed on cervical spine assessment and vestibulo-ocular screening.
6. **Return to Learn precedes Return to Sport:** For children and adolescents, achieving academic reintegration should take priority over progressing through the return-to-sport protocol.

### Return-to-Learn Protocol

Academic reintegration follows a graduated pathway:

| Stage | Activity |
|---|---|
| 1 | Daily activities at home that do not provoke symptoms |
| 2 | Homework, reading, light cognitive work (symptom-limited) |
| 3 | Part-time school attendance (half-days, modified schedule) |
| 4 | Full school attendance with accommodations (extra time, rest breaks, reduced workload) |
| 5 | Full school activities without accommodations |

Critically, return to school does not require being fully asymptomatic; symptom-limited participation is recommended. Prolonged absence from school may itself worsen outcomes through social isolation, academic stress, and anxiety.

### Return-to-Sport Protocol (6-Step Graduated Protocol)

| Step | Activity | Objective | Minimum Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Symptom-limited daily activities | Gradual reintroduction of cognitive/physical activity | 24-48 hours |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise | Walking, swimming, stationary cycling; HR <70% max; no resistance | 24 hours |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise | Running drills, skating drills; no head impact | 24 hours |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills | Progressive complexity; may add resistance training; no body contact | 24 hours |
| 5 | Full contact practice | Requires medical clearance; restores confidence and functional assessment | 24 hours |
| 6 | Return to competition | Full game play | -- |

If symptoms recur at any step, the athlete drops back to the previous tolerated step and waits 24 hours before re-attempting progression. The minimum time from injury to return to full competition is therefore at least one week (six steps at 24 hours each), though children and adolescents frequently require longer.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most important paradigm shift in modern concussion management is the move from "rest until asymptomatic" to "early symptom-limited activity." Strict prolonged rest beyond 48 hours is not only unnecessary but may delay recovery through deconditioning, anxiety, and depression.

### Assessment Tools

| Tool | Purpose | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| SCAT6 / Child-SCAT6 | Standardized concussion assessment | Multi-domain: symptoms, cognition, balance, neurological exam |
| ImPACT | Computerized neurocognitive testing | Baseline versus post-injury comparison of memory, processing speed, reaction time |
| King-Devick Test | Saccadic eye movement screening | Rapid (2-minute) sideline tool |
| VOMS | Vestibular/ocular-motor screening | Smooth pursuit, saccades, convergence, VOR, visual motion sensitivity |
| PCSS | Post-concussion symptom scale | 22-item self-report, 0-6 severity per item |
| Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test | Graded aerobic exercise tolerance | Determines exercise threshold for recovery prescription |

## 6.8 Post-Concussion Symptoms and Active Rehabilitation

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Persistent post-concussion symptoms affect approximately 10 to 30 percent of children following concussion, with headache being the most common complaint, followed by dizziness, fatigue, cognitive difficulties, irritability, sleep disturbance, and sensitivity to light and noise.

**Risk factors for prolonged recovery include:**
- Female sex (in adolescents)
- Prior concussion history
- Premorbid mood or anxiety disorders
- Greater initial symptom burden at presentation
- Family history of psychiatric illness
- Pre-existing learning disabilities

**Active rehabilitation approaches for persistent symptoms:**
- Graded aerobic exercise (Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test protocol)
- Vestibular rehabilitation therapy
- Vision therapy for convergence and accommodation deficits
- Cervical spine physiotherapy
- Cognitive-behavioral therapy for comorbid mood and anxiety symptoms
- Academic accommodations (504 plans, Individualized Education Programs)

The CDC Guideline on Pediatric Mild TBI (Lumba-Brown et al., 2018) encompasses 19 recommendations emphasizing clinical decision rule-guided imaging, validated symptom assessment, early return to activity, and targeted referral for symptoms persisting beyond 4 weeks.

## 6.9 Second Impact Syndrome

`[BOARD]`

Second impact syndrome describes a rare but potentially fatal condition in which a second concussion is sustained before the brain has fully recovered from a prior concussion. The hallmark is rapid, catastrophic cerebral edema resulting from dysregulated cerebrovascular autoregulation, leading to massive cerebral vasodilation, markedly increased cerebral blood volume, malignant brain swelling, and transtentorial herniation.

The typical patient is an adolescent male athlete who returns to sport while still symptomatic from a previous concussion. Following a second impact — often seemingly trivial — neurological collapse occurs within minutes, with pupillary dilation, loss of consciousness, and respiratory failure. Mortality approaches 50 percent, and nearly all survivors sustain severe permanent disability.

Although the existence of second impact syndrome as a distinct entity is debated (some experts attribute reported cases to diffuse cerebral swelling from a single impact), the concept remains the primary clinical rationale for strictly prohibiting return to play while a concussed athlete remains symptomatic (Cantu, 1998; McCrory et al., 2012).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Second impact syndrome underscores the absolute importance of completing the full graduated return-to-sport protocol before resuming contact activities. No same-day return to play is permitted, regardless of how quickly symptoms resolve on the sideline.

## 6.10 Complications of Pediatric TBI

### 6.10.1 Motor Complications

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Motor deficits following moderate-to-severe pediatric TBI span a spectrum from subtle coordination difficulties to severe spastic hemiparesis or quadriparesis. The pattern of motor impairment depends on lesion location: focal contusions and hemorrhages produce lateralized deficits, while diffuse axonal injury and hypoxic-ischemic damage yield more global involvement. Ataxia, tremor, and dystonia may emerge, particularly with posterior fossa or basal ganglia injury. Motor recovery follows a trajectory similar to that of adult TBI but may be complicated by growth-related changes in biomechanics and motor demands.

### 6.10.2 Cognitive Sequelae

`[BOARD]`

Cognitive impairment is the most common long-term consequence of moderate-to-severe pediatric TBI. Affected domains include attention, processing speed, working memory, executive function, and learning. Unlike adult TBI, where recovery often plateaus within 1 to 2 years, children face a unique trajectory shaped by ongoing brain maturation and progressively increasing environmental demands.

Key cognitive rehabilitation approaches supported by evidence include:
- Attention process training
- External memory aids (smartphone apps, checklists, organizational systems)
- Goal management and executive function training (including online programs such as the Amsterdam Memory and Attention Training)
- Family-based interventions such as the Counselor-Assisted Problem-Solving (CAPS) program (Wade et al., 2010)
- School-based supports through 504 plans and Individualized Education Programs

### 6.10.3 Neuroendocrine Dysfunction

`[BOARD]`

The hypothalamic-pituitary axis is vulnerable to TBI because of its anatomic location at the skull base and its tenuous vascular supply. An estimated 10 to 30 percent of children with moderate-to-severe TBI develop some degree of pituitary dysfunction, which may be underdiagnosed (Acerini and Tasker, 2008).

| Endocrine Disorder | Key Features |
|---|---|
| Growth hormone deficiency | Most common chronic pituitary deficit; growth deceleration months to years post-injury |
| Central precocious puberty | Accelerated pubertal onset; more common when injury precedes normal pubertal age |
| Diabetes insipidus | Posterior pituitary/hypothalamic injury; polyuria, polydipsia, hypernatremia; may be transient or permanent |
| SIADH | Hyponatremia in the acute phase; more common acutely than DI |
| Adrenal insufficiency | Central (secondary); potentially life-threatening if unrecognized |
| Central hypothyroidism | Less common; screening with TSH and free T4 |
| Hypogonadism | May manifest as delayed puberty |

**Screening recommendations:** All children with moderate-to-severe TBI should undergo endocrine assessment at baseline, 3 to 6 months, 12 months, and annually for at least 5 years post-injury. Monitoring includes growth velocity, GH stimulation testing if growth decelerates, thyroid function, morning cortisol, electrolytes with osmolality, and pubertal staging.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Growth hormone deficiency is the most common long-term endocrine deficit following pediatric TBI. Any child who was growing normally before a moderate-to-severe TBI and subsequently demonstrates growth deceleration should be evaluated with GH stimulation testing.

### 6.10.4 Post-Traumatic Epilepsy

`[BOARD]`

Post-traumatic seizures are categorized by timing relative to the injury:

- **Impact seizures:** At the moment of or immediately after impact; generally benign if isolated
- **Early post-traumatic seizures:** Within 7 days; considered provoked or acute symptomatic seizures
- **Late post-traumatic seizures (post-traumatic epilepsy):** Beyond 7 days; represent true epilepsy with increased recurrence risk

The seminal population-based study by Annegers and colleagues (1998) established the risk stratification:

| TBI Severity | PTE Risk | Standardized Incidence Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | ~1-2% | 1.5 |
| Moderate | ~1.6% | 2.9 |
| Severe | ~7.4-10% | 17.0 |

Risk remained elevated for more than 10 years following severe TBI. Risk factors for post-traumatic epilepsy include severe TBI (GCS 8 or below), penetrating injury (risk up to 50 percent), intracranial hemorrhage, depressed skull fracture (especially with dural penetration), cortical contusion, prolonged LOC or PTA, and early post-traumatic seizures.

**Seizure prophylaxis:** Phenytoin (and increasingly levetiracetam) administered during the first 7 days after severe TBI effectively reduces early post-traumatic seizures. However, no antiepileptic medication has been shown to prevent late post-traumatic epilepsy; prophylaxis beyond 7 days is therefore not recommended (Temkin et al., 1990).

### 6.10.5 Autonomic Dysfunction (Paroxysmal Sympathetic Hyperactivity)

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Previously termed "sympathetic storming" or "dysautonomia," paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH) occurs following severe TBI, particularly with diffuse axonal injury. Episodes are characterized by tachycardia, hypertension, tachypnea, diaphoresis, hyperthermia, and extensor posturing, often triggered by stimulation. PSH is more common in children and young adults than in older adults and may persist for weeks to months.

Management involves removing triggers, environmental modification, and pharmacologic approaches including gabapentin, beta-blockers (propranolol), bromocriptine, intrathecal baclofen, and benzodiazepines. Recognition is important because PSH may be mistaken for seizures, sepsis, or pain.

## 6.11 "Growing Into Deficits"

`[BOARD]`

The concept of "growing into deficits" is among the most important principles distinguishing pediatric from adult TBI. It describes the phenomenon in which children who sustain TBI at a young age may appear to have a relatively good initial recovery, only to manifest increasingly apparent cognitive and behavioral deficits as they mature and face escalating developmental demands.

**Mechanism:** The developing brain relies on the progressive maturation of neural networks, particularly in the frontal lobes, which underpin executive function, abstract reasoning, planning, and social cognition. These networks develop most rapidly during late childhood and adolescence. A child who sustains diffuse brain injury at age 3, for example, may function adequately in the structured, concrete environment of early elementary school but falter when middle school demands require independent organization, multistep planning, cognitive flexibility, and complex social navigation.

**Clinical implications:**
- Recovery from pediatric TBI cannot be assumed to be complete within the first 1 to 2 years
- Long-term follow-up extending through adolescence and into early adulthood is essential
- Neuropsychological re-evaluation should be performed at developmental transition points (school entry, transition to middle school, transition to high school)
- Academic supports may need to be intensified rather than reduced over time
- Families should receive education about the possibility of emerging deficits

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Growing into deficits" means that a child with TBI at age 4 may appear to recover well initially but demonstrate executive dysfunction, social difficulties, and academic failure at age 12 as frontal lobe demands outpace the injured brain's capacity. Longitudinal surveillance at developmental transition points is essential.

## 6.12 Pediatric Versus Adult TBI: Key Differences

`[BOARD]`

| Feature | Pediatric TBI | Adult TBI |
|---|---|---|
| **Skull biomechanics** | Thinner, more deformable skull; open fontanelles and sutures in infants; greater capacity for skull deformation without fracture | Rigid skull; fracture patterns correspond to force vectors |
| **Brain water content** | Higher brain water content; less myelin; more susceptible to diffuse injury | Higher myelin content; greater structural resistance to shearing |
| **Epidural hematoma** | Less common; middle meningeal artery is not yet embedded in bony groove; can have venous bleeding from dural sinuses | More common; typically arterial from meningeal vessel laceration |
| **Injury patterns** | More diffuse injuries; less focal contusion; more susceptible to diffuse cerebral swelling | More focal contusions and hemorrhages |
| **SCIWORA** | Common in young children (<8 years) due to ligamentous laxity and large head-to-body ratio | Rare |
| **Concussion recovery** | Typically 2-4 weeks; longer than adults | Typically 10-14 days |
| **Persistent symptoms** | Defined as >4 weeks | Defined as >2 weeks |
| **Seizures** | Higher rate of early seizures (especially <2 years); lower rate of late PTE compared with adults for equivalent severity | Lower early seizure rate; higher late PTE rate |
| **Neuroplasticity** | Greater potential for neural reorganization; does NOT guarantee better outcomes | Less neuroplasticity; recovery patterns more predictable |
| **Growing into deficits** | Unique to pediatric TBI; emerging cognitive/behavioral problems years later | Not applicable; deficits are generally manifest immediately |
| **Abusive head trauma** | Major cause of severe TBI in infants; unique pathology and medicolegal considerations | Rare |
| **Second impact syndrome** | Almost exclusively in adolescents | Extremely rare |
| **Neuroendocrine dysfunction** | May affect growth and pubertal development | Does not affect growth; gonadal/adrenal effects predominate |

### The Vulnerability Versus Plasticity Debate

The traditional assumption that children "always do better" after brain injury has been significantly nuanced by contemporary evidence. Anderson and colleagues (2005) demonstrated that while neuroplasticity confers some adaptive advantages, injuries sustained before age 2 to 3 years may actually produce worse long-term cognitive outcomes than those sustained later in childhood. This "early vulnerability" hypothesis holds that injury to the immature brain disrupts the foundation upon which subsequent development is built, whereas injury to a more mature brain leaves established skills relatively intact and allows for compensatory reorganization.

The current understanding integrates both perspectives: children have enhanced capacity for neural reorganization (plasticity), but early injuries that disrupt critical developmental windows may impose a ceiling on subsequent cognitive development (vulnerability). The net outcome depends on injury severity, age at injury, lesion location, and environmental factors including rehabilitation, educational support, and family functioning.

## 6.13 Prognosis

`[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### Predictors of Outcome

**Favorable prognostic indicators:**
- Higher initial GCS
- Shorter duration of PTA
- Absence of intracranial hemorrhage
- Absence of diffuse axonal injury
- Older age at injury (beyond infancy)
- Intact family support system
- Access to rehabilitation services
- Absence of premorbid cognitive or behavioral concerns

**Unfavorable prognostic indicators:**
- GCS 8 or below
- Prolonged PTA (>7 days)
- Bilateral or diffuse intracranial lesions
- Hypotension or hypoxia in the acute period
- Pupillary abnormalities
- Abusive head trauma mechanism
- Injury before age 2 to 3 years
- Lower socioeconomic status and limited social support

### Long-Term Outcomes

Children with moderate-to-severe TBI demonstrate persistent difficulties in cognitive, behavioral, social, academic, and emotional domains extending years and even decades after injury. Meta-analytic data (Babikian and Asarnow, 2009) confirm that deficits in intellectual function, attention, memory, and executive function are enduring following severe pediatric TBI. Social outcomes are also significantly affected, with impairments in social cognition, peer relationships, and adaptive behavior (Yeates et al., 2004).

Family-centered rehabilitation, beginning in the acute care setting and continuing through school-based services and community reintegration, is critical for optimizing long-term outcomes. The Counselor-Assisted Problem-Solving (CAPS) program developed by Wade and colleagues demonstrates that online family-based interventions can improve executive function, behavioral regulation, and family functioning in adolescents after TBI.

### Functional Outcome Measures

| Measure | Application |
|---|---|
| GOS / GOS-E Peds | Global outcome; 5 or 8 levels |
| WeeFIM / FIM | Functional independence in motor and cognitive domains |
| BESS | Balance assessment post-concussion |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Prognosis after pediatric TBI depends not only on injury factors (severity, mechanism, pathology) but equally on environmental factors including family functioning, socioeconomic resources, and access to rehabilitation. Addressing modifiable environmental factors is a critical component of optimizing outcomes.
