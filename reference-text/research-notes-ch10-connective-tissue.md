# Chapter 10: Connective Tissue & JIA — Research Notes

## GUIDELINES FOUND

### ACR/Arthritis Foundation 2019 JIA Treatment Guidelines
- **Ringold S, Angeles-Han ST, Engel-Yeger B, et al. "2019 American College of Rheumatology/Arthritis Foundation Guideline for the Treatment of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis: Therapeutic Approaches for Non-Systemic Polyarthritis, Sacroiliitis, and Enthesitis." Arthritis Care Res. 2019;71(6):717-734.**
  - Treat-to-target approach: Goal is clinically inactive disease (CID) within 6 months
  - Treatment escalation ladder:
    - First-line: NSAIDs (4-6 weeks trial) + intra-articular corticosteroid injections (triamcinolone hexacetonide preferred)
    - Second-line: Conventional DMARDs (methotrexate first-line DMARD; 10-15 mg/m2/week)
    - Third-line: Biologic DMARDs (TNF inhibitors first-line biologic)
  - For oligoarticular JIA: IA corticosteroids +/- MTX
  - For polyarticular JIA: MTX first-line DMARD; add biologic if inadequate response at 3 months
  - For systemic JIA: IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors recommended as first-line biologic (over TNF inhibitors)

- **Angeles-Han ST, Ringold S, Engel-Yeger B, et al. "2019 ACR/Arthritis Foundation Guideline for the Treatment of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis: Therapeutic Approaches for Oligoarthritis, Temporomandibular Joint Arthritis, and Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis." Arthritis Care Res. 2019;71(6):703-716.**
  - sJIA: Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist) or tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor) recommended as first-line biologic
  - MAS: High-dose pulse IV methylprednisolone, cyclosporine A, anakinra

### ACR 2019 Uveitis Screening Guidelines
- **Angeles-Han ST, Ringold S, Beukelman T, et al. "2019 ACR/Arthritis Foundation Guideline for the Screening, Monitoring, and Treatment of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis-Associated Uveitis." Arthritis Care Res. 2019;71(6):864-877.**
  - Screening frequency based on risk stratification: JIA subtype, ANA status, age at onset, disease duration
  - Highest risk: Oligoarticular JIA, ANA+, onset <7 years, disease duration <4 years → slit lamp every 3 months
  - Moderate risk: ANA+, older onset or longer duration → every 6 months
  - Low risk: RF+ polyarticular, systemic JIA → every 12 months
  - **Board Pearl**: Uveitis in JIA is typically ANTERIOR, CHRONIC, and ASYMPTOMATIC (unlike adult uveitis)

### AHA 2017 Kawasaki Disease Guidelines
- **McCrindle BW, Rowley AH, Newburger JW, et al. "Diagnosis, Treatment, and Long-Term Management of Kawasaki Disease: A Scientific Statement From the American Heart Association." Circulation. 2017;135(17):e927-e999.**
  - Diagnostic criteria (classic/complete KD): Fever >=5 days PLUS 4 of 5 clinical features:
    1. Bilateral bulbar conjunctival injection (non-exudative)
    2. Oral mucous membrane changes (strawberry tongue, cracked lips)
    3. Polymorphous exanthem (rash — NOT vesicular)
    4. Extremity changes (edema, erythema → periungual desquamation in convalescence)
    5. Cervical lymphadenopathy (>=1.5 cm, usually unilateral)
  - Incomplete KD: Fever >=5 days + 2-3 criteria with supporting lab/echo findings
  - Treatment: IVIG 2 g/kg single infusion + aspirin (high-dose 80-100 mg/kg/day until afebrile, then low-dose 3-5 mg/kg/day for 6-8 weeks or until ESR/CRP normalize)
  - Refractory KD: Second IVIG dose, IV methylprednisolone, infliximab
  - Coronary artery aneurysm: 25% if untreated; <5% with timely IVIG
  - Echo: At diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6-8 weeks after onset (minimum)

### Revised Jones Criteria 2015 (Rheumatic Fever)
- **Gewitz MH, Baltimore RS, Tani LY, et al. "Revision of the Jones Criteria for the Diagnosis of Acute Rheumatic Fever in the Era of Doppler Echocardiography." Circulation. 2015;131(20):1806-1818.**
  - Evidence of preceding GAS infection PLUS:
    - **Low-risk populations**: 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria
    - **Moderate/high-risk populations**: Same, with additional major criteria and lower thresholds
  - **Major Criteria (low-risk)**:
    1. Carditis (clinical and/or subclinical on echo)
    2. Polyarthritis (migratory)
    3. Chorea (Sydenham)
    4. Erythema marginatum
    5. Subcutaneous nodules
  - **Major Criteria (moderate/high-risk)**: Monoarthritis and polyarthralgia also accepted as major
  - **Minor Criteria**: Fever (>=38.5 C low-risk; >=38.0 high-risk), polyarthralgia (low-risk only), elevated ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval

### ACR/EULAR 2019 SLE Classification Criteria
- **Aringer M, Costenbader K, Daikh D, et al. "2019 European League Against Rheumatism/American College of Rheumatology Classification Criteria for Systemic Lupus Erythematosus." Arthritis Rheumatol. 2019;71(9):1400-1412.**
  - Entry criterion: ANA >=1:80 on HEp-2 cells
  - Then score clinical + immunologic domains; >=10 points = classify as SLE
  - Domains: Constitutional, hematologic, neuropsychiatric, mucocutaneous, serosal, musculoskeletal, renal, antiphospholipid, complement, SLE-specific antibodies

### Lyme Disease — Two-Tier Testing
- **CDC/IDSA Guidelines: Wormser GP, Dattwyler RJ, Shapiro ED, et al. "The Clinical Assessment, Treatment, and Prevention of Lyme Disease, Human Granulocytic Anaplasmosis, and Babesiosis." Clin Infect Dis. 2006;43(9):1089-1134.**
  - Two-tier testing: ELISA (screening) → If positive/equivocal → Western blot (confirmatory)
  - IgM Western blot: 2 of 3 bands (23, 39, 41 kDa) — valid only in first 30 days
  - IgG Western blot: 5 of 10 bands
  - Modified two-tier testing: Two EIAs replacing Western blot (FDA-approved 2019)
  - Lyme arthritis: Large joint mono/oligoarthritis (knee in 90%); weeks-months after tick bite

## LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### JIA (Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis)
1. **Petty RE, Southwood TR, Manners P, et al. "International League of Associations for Rheumatology classification of juvenile idiopathic arthritis: second revision, Edmonton, 2001." J Rheumatol. 2004;31(2):390-392.**
   - ILAR classification: 7 subtypes (replaced earlier JRA/JCA terminology)
   - Required: Arthritis onset <16 years, lasting >=6 weeks, other causes excluded

2. **Lovell DJ, Giannini EH, Reiff A, et al. "Etanercept in children with polyarticular juvenile rheumatoid arthritis." N Engl J Med. 2000;342(11):763-769.**
   - First RCT of a biologic (etanercept) in JIA
   - Landmark trial leading to FDA approval
   - Demonstrated significant improvement vs placebo in polyarticular JIA

3. **Ruperto N, Lovell DJ, Quartier P, et al. "Abatacept in children with juvenile idiopathic arthritis: a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled withdrawal trial." Lancet. 2008;372(9636):383-391.**
   - Abatacept (T-cell co-stimulation modulator) effective in polyarticular JIA
   - FDA approved for JIA

4. **De Benedetti F, Brunner HI, Ruperto N, et al. "Randomized trial of tocilizumab in systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis." N Engl J Med. 2012;367(25):2385-2395.**
   - TENDER trial: Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6) effective for sJIA
   - Led to FDA approval for sJIA

5. **Ruperto N, Brunner HI, Quartier P, et al. "Canakinumab in systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis." N Engl J Med. 2012;367(25):2396-2406.**
   - Canakinumab (anti-IL-1beta) effective for sJIA
   - FDA approved for sJIA

### MAS (Macrophage Activation Syndrome)
6. **Ravelli A, Minoia F, Davi S, et al. "2016 Classification Criteria for Macrophage Activation Syndrome Complicating Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis." Arthritis Rheumatol. 2016;68(3):566-576.**
   - Diagnostic criteria for MAS in sJIA:
     - Febrile patient with known/suspected sJIA with:
     - Ferritin >684 ng/mL PLUS any 2 of:
       - Platelet count <=181 x 10^9/L
       - AST >48 U/L
       - Triglycerides >156 mg/dL
       - Fibrinogen <=360 mg/dL

### Juvenile Dermatomyositis (JDM)
7. **Bohan A, Peter JB. "Polymyositis and dermatomyositis." N Engl J Med. 1975;292(7):344-347; 292(8):403-407.**
   - Bohan and Peter criteria (still widely used):
     1. Symmetric proximal muscle weakness
     2. Elevated muscle enzymes (CK, aldolase, AST, LDH)
     3. EMG: myopathic changes (short, small, polyphasic motor unit potentials; fibrillations; positive sharp waves)
     4. Muscle biopsy: inflammation, necrosis, degeneration/regeneration, perifascicular atrophy
     5. Characteristic skin rash: Heliotrope rash (violaceous eyelid discoloration), Gottron papules (over MCPs, PIPs, knees, elbows)
   - Definite DM: Rash + 3 of 4 muscle criteria; Probable: Rash + 2 of 4

8. **Rider LG, Nistala K. "The juvenile idiopathic inflammatory myopathies: pathogenesis, clinical and autoantibody phenotypes, and outcomes." J Intern Med. 2016;280(1):24-38.**
   - Myositis-specific antibodies in JDM:
     - Anti-MDA5 (anti-CADM-140): Rapidly progressive ILD, amyopathic DM, skin ulceration
     - Anti-NXP2 (anti-p140): Calcinosis, severe muscle disease
     - Anti-TIF1-gamma (anti-p155/140): Severe skin disease, photosensitivity (in adults: associated with malignancy)
     - Anti-Mi-2: Classic DM, good prognosis
     - Anti-SRP: Necrotizing myopathy, severe disease

### Kawasaki Disease
9. **Newburger JW, Takahashi M, Burns JC, et al. "The treatment of Kawasaki syndrome with intravenous gamma globulin." N Engl J Med. 1986;315(6):341-347.**
   - Landmark trial establishing IVIG efficacy
   - IVIG reduces coronary artery aneurysm rate from 25% to <5%

10. **Tremoulet AH, Jain S, Jaggi P, et al. "Infliximab for intensification of primary therapy for Kawasaki disease: a phase 3 randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial." Lancet. 2014;383(9930):1731-1738.**
    - Infliximab did not significantly improve coronary outcomes when added to primary IVIG therapy
    - However, used as second-line for refractory KD

### Septic Arthritis — Kocher Criteria
11. **Kocher MS, Zurakowski D, Kasser JR. "Differentiating between septic arthritis and transient synovitis of the hip in children: an evidence-based clinical prediction algorithm." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 1999;81(12):1662-1670.**
    - 4 Kocher criteria to distinguish septic arthritis from transient synovitis of the hip:
      1. Non-weight-bearing
      2. ESR >40 mm/hr
      3. Fever (>38.5 C)
      4. WBC >12,000/mm3
    - Predicted probability of septic arthritis:
      - 0 criteria: <0.2%
      - 1 criterion: 3%
      - 2 criteria: 40%
      - 3 criteria: 93.1%
      - 4 criteria: 99.6%

12. **Caird MS, Flynn JM, Leung YL, et al. "Factors distinguishing septic arthritis from transient synovitis of the hip in children: a prospective study." J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2006;88(6):1251-1257.**
    - Added CRP >2.0 mg/dL as 5th criterion
    - CRP had strongest independent predictive value

## CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS

### ILAR Classification (see Epidemiology table above for full details)
- **Must meet**: Arthritis, onset <16 years, >=6 weeks duration, exclusion of other causes
- **Exclusion criteria vary by subtype** (e.g., oligoarticular excludes psoriasis, HLA-B27+ male >6 years, ERA features, RF+, sJIA)

### Uveitis Risk Stratification (ACR 2019)
| Risk Category | Features | Screening Interval |
|---|---|---|
| High | Oligoarticular or RF- polyarticular, ANA+, onset <7 years, duration <4 years | Every 3 months |
| Moderate | As above but onset >=7 years OR duration 4-7 years | Every 6 months |
| Low | RF+ polyarticular, sJIA, ERA, psoriatic, duration >7 years | Every 12 months |

### JADAS (Juvenile Arthritis Disease Activity Score)
- Components: Physician global assessment (0-10), parent/patient global assessment (0-10), active joint count (0-10 or 0-27 or 0-71), ESR (normalized 0-10)
- JADAS-10, JADAS-27, JADAS-71 based on joint count used
- Clinically inactive disease thresholds: JADAS-10 <=1; JADAS-27 <=1

### Wallace Criteria for Clinically Inactive Disease (CID) in JIA
1. No active arthritis
2. No fever, rash, serositis, splenomegaly, or generalized lymphadenopathy attributable to JIA
3. No active uveitis
4. Normal ESR and/or CRP
5. Physician global assessment at best possible score
6. Morning stiffness <=15 minutes

### MAS Diagnostic Criteria (Ravelli 2016)
- See Landmark Studies: Ferritin >684 + 2 of: platelets <=181K, AST >48, triglycerides >156, fibrinogen <=360

### Bohan-Peter Criteria for Dermatomyositis (see Landmark Studies)

### Childhood Myositis Assessment Scale (CMAS)
- 14 maneuvers testing strength, endurance, function
- Score 0-52 (higher = better)
- Used for JDM disease activity monitoring

### Childhood Health Assessment Questionnaire (CHAQ)
- 8 functional domains: dressing, arising, eating, walking, hygiene, reach, grip, activities
- Score 0-3 (0 = no difficulty, 3 = unable)
- Disability index: Mean of 8 domain scores

### ACR Pediatric Response Criteria (ACR Pedi 30/50/70/90)
- Improvement of >=30/50/70/90% in 3 of 6 core set variables with no more than 1 worsening by >30%:
  1. Physician global assessment
  2. Parent/patient global assessment
  3. Functional ability (CHAQ)
  4. Number of active joints
  5. Number of joints with limited ROM
  6. ESR or CRP

## BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS — HIGH-YIELD PEARLS

### JIA General
1. **JIA replaced JRA/JCA terminology**: ILAR classification (2001/2004) is the current standard
2. **7 subtypes**: Oligoarticular (50-60%), RF- polyarticular (15-20%), RF+ polyarticular (2-7%), systemic (10-15%), ERA (5-10%), psoriatic (5-8%), undifferentiated (10-15%)
3. **Oligoarticular JIA is the most common subtype**: ~50-60% of all JIA
4. **ANA positivity** correlates with uveitis risk (NOT disease activity)
5. **Uveitis in JIA is ANTERIOR, CHRONIC, and ASYMPTOMATIC** — requires slit lamp screening; NOT red painful eye
6. **ERA uveitis is DIFFERENT**: Acute anterior uveitis that IS symptomatic (red, painful eye) — like adult AS-associated uveitis

### JIA Treatment
7. **Methotrexate**: First-line DMARD; 10-15 mg/m2/week (oral or subcutaneous)
8. **TNF inhibitors** (etanercept, adalimumab): First-line biologics for non-systemic JIA
9. **sJIA biologics**: IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab) or IL-6 inhibitors (tocilizumab) — NOT TNF inhibitors
10. **Treat-to-target**: Clinically inactive disease (CID) within 6 months
11. **Intra-articular corticosteroids**: Triamcinolone hexacetonide preferred (longer duration than triamcinolone acetonide)

### FDA-Approved Biologics for JIA (as of early 2025)
| Drug | Target | Approved For |
|---|---|---|
| Etanercept (Enbrel) | TNF | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years |
| Adalimumab (Humira) | TNF | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years |
| Abatacept (Orencia) | T-cell co-stimulation (CTLA-4 Ig) | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years (IV); >=2 years (SC) |
| Tocilizumab (Actemra) | IL-6 receptor | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years; sJIA >=2 years |
| Canakinumab (Ilaris) | IL-1 beta | sJIA >=2 years |

| Golimumab (Simponi) | TNF | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years |
| Tofacitinib (Xeljanz) | JAK1/3 | Polyarticular JIA >=2 years (first JAK inhibitor for JIA) |

### MAS (Macrophage Activation Syndrome)
12. **Life-threatening complication of sJIA**: Mortality 8-22%
13. **Triggered by**: Infections (especially EBV), medication changes, disease flares
14. **Paradoxical lab changes**: ESR may DROP (falling fibrinogen) while ferritin RISES dramatically (>10,000 common)
15. **Ferritin >684, falling platelets, rising AST, rising triglycerides, falling fibrinogen** = MAS
16. **Treatment**: Pulse IV methylprednisolone, cyclosporine A, anakinra; etoposide in refractory cases
17. **Board Pearl**: MAS is a form of secondary HLH (hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis)
18. **MAS vs sJIA flare**: In MAS, ferritin is sky-high and ESR may paradoxically drop (consumed fibrinogen); in sJIA flare, both are elevated

### Juvenile Dermatomyositis (JDM)
19. **Most common pediatric inflammatory myopathy**: Incidence 2-4/million/year
20. **Heliotrope rash**: Violaceous discoloration of upper eyelids — PATHOGNOMONIC
21. **Gottron papules**: Erythematous/violaceous papules over MCPs, PIPs, DIPs, knees, elbows — PATHOGNOMONIC
22. **Calcinosis**: 20-40% of JDM patients; dystrophic calcification in skin/subcutaneous tissue/muscle
23. **Nail fold capillaroscopy**: Dilated, tortuous, dropout capillaries; correlates with disease activity
24. **V-sign and shawl sign**: Photosensitive rash distribution
25. **Lipodystrophy**: Can occur in JDM; partial or generalized
26. **No malignancy association in pediatric DM** (unlike adult DM)
27. **Anti-MDA5**: Associated with ILD and skin ulceration; clinically amyopathic
28. **Anti-NXP2**: Associated with calcinosis and severe weakness (most common antibody in JDM)

### Pediatric SLE
29. **More severe than adult SLE**: Higher rates of nephritis (50-75% vs 25-50%), CNS involvement, hematologic disease
30. **Pediatric onset**: 15-20% of all SLE; peak onset 12-14 years; F:M 4-5:1 (less female predominant than adult)
31. **Lupus nephritis**: Most significant predictor of morbidity/mortality; Class IV (diffuse proliferative) most common and severe
32. **Anti-dsDNA**: Most specific for SLE; correlates with disease activity and nephritis
33. **Anti-Smith**: Most specific antibody for SLE (but less sensitive than anti-dsDNA)
34. **Complement (C3/C4)**: Low complement correlates with active disease, especially nephritis
35. **Drug-induced lupus**: Anti-histone antibodies; minocycline in adolescents; does NOT cause nephritis or CNS disease

### Kawasaki Disease
36. **Leading cause of acquired heart disease in children** in developed countries (replaced rheumatic fever)
37. **Coronary artery aneurysm**: 25% untreated; <5% with IVIG within 10 days of fever onset
38. **Peak age**: 6 months to 5 years; 80% <5 years
39. **IVIG 2 g/kg single dose** + aspirin = standard treatment
40. **Incomplete Kawasaki**: Consider in any child with >=5 days fever and <4 classic criteria; CRP >=3 mg/dL or ESR >=40 + 3 supplemental criteria or echo abnormality
41. **Infants <6 months**: Higher risk for coronary complications; higher rate of incomplete presentation
42. **Aspirin**: HIGH dose (80-100 mg/kg/day) in acute phase → LOW dose (3-5 mg/kg/day) in convalescent phase
43. **Thrombocytosis**: Characteristic in 2nd-3rd week; platelet count may exceed 1 million
44. **Desquamation**: Periungual desquamation of fingers/toes in convalescent phase (2-3 weeks) — NOT in acute phase
45. **Reye syndrome concern**: Aspirin use in children with concurrent varicella/influenza — discuss risk/benefit

### Rheumatic Fever
46. **Jones Criteria 2015**: Evidence of GAS infection + 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria
47. **Carditis**: Most serious manifestation; mitral valve most commonly affected (mitral regurgitation)
48. **Sydenham chorea**: Delayed manifestation (2-6 months after infection); may be only manifestation
49. **Erythema marginatum**: Evanescent, annular, non-pruritic rash on trunk/proximal extremities
50. **Subcutaneous nodules**: Painless, firm, on extensor surfaces; associated with carditis
51. **Arthritis**: Migratory polyarthritis of large joints; EXQUISITELY responsive to aspirin/NSAIDs
52. **Board Pearl**: Chorea and carditis can be latent presentations; arthritis is NEVER chronic in ARF
53. **Antibiotic prophylaxis**: Penicillin VK (250 mg BID) or benzathine penicillin G IM (q4 weeks); duration depends on carditis (10 years or until age 40 if carditis with valve disease)

### Lyme Disease
54. **Borrelia burgdorferi**: Transmitted by Ixodes tick; requires >=36-48 hours of tick attachment
55. **Erythema migrans**: >=5 cm expanding, annular, target-like rash; single clinical criterion sufficient for diagnosis (no serologic confirmation needed)
56. **Lyme arthritis**: Mono/oligoarthritis (knee in 90%); weeks to months after initial infection
57. **Two-tier testing**: ELISA → Western blot; serology NOT indicated for erythema migrans (treat empirically)
58. **Facial nerve palsy**: Most common cranial neuropathy in Lyme; may be bilateral (almost pathognomonic for Lyme)
59. **Lyme carditis**: AV block (can be complete heart block); rare
60. **Treatment**: Doxycycline (>=8 years) or amoxicillin (<8 years) for early/localized; IV ceftriaxone for neuroborreliosis/carditis with advanced AV block

### Septic Arthritis
61. **Kocher criteria**: Non-weight-bearing, ESR >40, fever >38.5C, WBC >12K; 4 criteria = 99.6% probability
62. **Most common organism**: *S. aureus* (all ages after neonatal period)
63. **Neonates**: Group B Streptococcus, *S. aureus*, gram-negatives
64. **Kingella kingae**: Most common in 6 months to 4 years (culture-negative on standard media; needs special media/PCR)
65. **Joint aspirate**: WBC >50,000 with >75% PMNs = highly suggestive; >100,000 strongly suggestive
66. **Hip septic arthritis**: SURGICAL EMERGENCY (risk of AVN from increased intracapsular pressure)
67. **Transient synovitis vs septic arthritis**: Kocher criteria + CRP help distinguish; transient synovitis is benign and self-limited

### Spondyloarthropathies (Pediatric)
68. **ERA subtype of JIA**: HLA-B27+, male predominance, onset >6 years
69. **Enthesitis**: Hallmark; Achilles tendon, plantar fascia insertions
70. **Sacroiliitis**: May progress to ankylosing spondylitis in adulthood
71. **Acute anterior uveitis**: SYMPTOMATIC (unlike chronic uveitis in oligoarticular JIA)
72. **Reactive arthritis**: Post-enteric (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia) or post-urogenital; HLA-B27 associated
