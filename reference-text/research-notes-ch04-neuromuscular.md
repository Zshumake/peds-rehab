## SECTION 1: DUCHENNE MUSCULAR DYSTROPHY (DMD)

### 1.1 Genetics & Pathophysiology

- **Inheritance**: X-linked recessive; gene locus Xp21.2
- **Gene**: Dystrophin gene — largest known human gene (~2.4 Mb, 79 exons)
- **Protein**: Dystrophin — 427 kDa rod-shaped cytoskeletal protein linking the intracellular actin cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix via the dystrophin-associated glycoprotein complex (DAGC)
- **Mutation types**: ~60-70% large deletions (most common in exons 44-55 "hot spot" region and exons 2-20), ~5-15% duplications, ~25-30% point mutations/small indels
- **Pathophysiology**: Complete absence of functional dystrophin leads to sarcolemmal instability during muscle contraction, calcium influx, activation of proteases (calpains), oxidative stress, chronic inflammation, progressive myofiber necrosis with eventual replacement by fibrotic and adipose tissue
- **Reading frame rule (Monaco et al. 1988)**: Out-of-frame mutations disrupt the translational reading frame, producing a truncated, nonfunctional, rapidly degraded dystrophin protein = DMD phenotype. In-frame mutations allow production of a shortened but partially functional dystrophin = milder BMD phenotype. Rule holds in ~90-95% of cases.
- **Incidence**: Approximately 1 in 3,500-5,000 live male births worldwide; ~30% are de novo mutations (no family history)
- **Female carriers**: Usually asymptomatic but ~2.5-10% manifest symptoms (manifesting carriers) due to skewed X-inactivation; should receive cardiac screening

### 1.2 Clinical Presentation & Natural History

- **Symptom onset**: Typically age 2-5 years
- **Early signs**: Delayed motor milestones (walking often delayed to 15-18 months), frequent falls, difficulty with stairs, toe-walking, calf pseudohypertrophy (replacement of muscle by fat and fibrotic tissue), proximal > distal weakness
- **Gower sign**: Classic compensatory maneuver — child "walks up" thighs/legs with hands to rise from floor due to proximal hip and knee extensor weakness. Present by age 3-5 in most affected boys. **High-yield board fact**.
- **Gait pattern**: Hyperlordotic posture (compensatory for hip extensor weakness), wide-based waddling gait, toe-walking
- **Cognitive**: Mean IQ ~1 SD below normal (~85); 30% have intellectual disability (IQ <70); specific deficits in verbal working memory; dystrophin isoform Dp71 expressed in brain
- **Loss of ambulation**: Typically occurs between ages 9-13 years (mean ~10-12 years without corticosteroid treatment; corticosteroids extend ambulation by 2-5 years on average)
- **Scoliosis**: Develops in >90% after loss of ambulation; progressive; surgical stabilization indicated when Cobb angle >20-25 degrees and still growing, or >40-50 degrees
- **Cardiac**: Dilated cardiomyopathy develops in virtually all patients; detectable by echocardiography/MRI by age 10-15 in most; fibrosis begins in posterolateral left ventricular wall; leading cause of death along with respiratory failure
- **Respiratory**: Progressive restrictive lung disease; FVC peaks around age 12-14 then declines ~5-8% per year; nocturnal hypoventilation precedes daytime respiratory failure; eventual need for noninvasive ventilation (NIV) then potentially invasive ventilation
- **Death**: Historically in late teens to early 20s; with current multidisciplinary care (corticosteroids, cardiac management, ventilatory support), survival into 30s and beyond increasingly common

### 1.3 Diagnosis

- **Creatine kinase (CK)**: Markedly elevated — typically 10,000-50,000 IU/L (50-100x normal); elevated from birth, even before symptom onset; peaks in early childhood then gradually declines as muscle mass is lost. **Board pearl**: CK is the most sensitive initial screening test.
- **Genetic testing**: First-line diagnostic — multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA) detects deletions/duplications (~70-80% of cases); if negative, next-generation sequencing for point mutations
- **Muscle biopsy**: Now reserved for cases where genetic testing is inconclusive; shows dystrophic changes (variation in fiber size, central nucleation, necrotic/regenerating fibers, endomysial fibrosis, fatty infiltration); immunohistochemistry shows absent dystrophin staining; Western blot confirms absent dystrophin protein
- **EMG**: Myopathic pattern (low amplitude, short duration, polyphasic motor unit potentials with early recruitment); not typically needed for diagnosis when genetic testing available
- **Newborn screening**: Not yet universally implemented but CK-based screening programs piloted in multiple countries; endorsed by expert consensus

### 1.4 Corticosteroid Treatment

- **Standard of care**: Corticosteroids are the only pharmacological treatment proven to slow disease progression; recommended to start when motor function plateaus (typically age 4-6) — **before** motor decline begins
- **Agents**: Prednisone/prednisolone 0.75 mg/kg/day (most studied dose); Deflazacort 0.9 mg/kg/day (may have fewer weight gain side effects, approved by FDA 2017)
- **Benefits**: Prolongs ambulation by 2-5 years, improves/stabilizes muscle strength, delays scoliosis, delays decline in pulmonary function, may delay onset of cardiomyopathy, reduces need for scoliosis surgery
- **Side effects**: Weight gain (Cushingoid), growth retardation (short stature), behavioral changes, cataracts (more with deflazacort), osteoporosis/vertebral compression fractures, adrenal suppression, glucose intolerance, immunosuppression
- **Dosing regimens studied**: Daily, alternating day, 10 days on/10 days off, weekend dosing (Friday-Sunday); FOR DMD trial (Finding the Optimum Regimen for Duchenne — Marden et al. 2022) compared daily prednisone vs daily deflazacort vs intermittent prednisone — daily deflazacort showed most favorable risk-benefit profile
- **Duration**: Continued indefinitely (even after loss of ambulation) for cardiac and pulmonary benefits; dose adjusted for side effects
- **Vaccination**: Live vaccines contraindicated while on immunosuppressive doses; ensure varicella immunity before starting

### 1.5 Birnkrant 2018 DMD Care Considerations (Lancet Neurology — 3-Part Series)

**This is a landmark guideline set — extremely board-relevant.**

- **Birnkrant DJ, Bushby K, Bann CM, et al. Diagnosis and management of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, part 1: diagnosis, and neuromuscular, rehabilitation, endocrine, and gastrointestinal and nutritional management. Lancet Neurology. 2018;17(3):251-267.**
  - Multidisciplinary care model essential
  - Genetic diagnosis preferred over muscle biopsy
  - Corticosteroid initiation recommended at motor plateau (before decline), typically age 4-6
  - Physical therapy: stretching program for ankles, knees, hip flexors, iliotibial bands; standing program when non-ambulatory
  - Orthotic management: Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) for night use to prevent/delay equinovarus contractures; knee-ankle-foot orthoses (KAFOs) to prolong ambulation in some settings
  - Nutritional: Monitor for obesity (corticosteroid-related) and undernutrition (later disease); dysphagia screening
  - Endocrine: Monitor growth, pubertal development, bone health (DXA scans), adrenal function
  - GI: Constipation management; gastroparesis screening; gastrostomy tube consideration when oral intake inadequate

- **Birnkrant DJ, Bushby K, Bann CM, et al. Diagnosis and management of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, part 2: respiratory, cardiac, bone health, and orthopaedic management. Lancet Neurology. 2018;17(4):347-361.**
  - Respiratory: Annual pulmonary function testing from age 5-6; FVC monitoring; lung volume recruitment (breath stacking/air stacking); assisted cough devices (mechanical insufflation-exsufflation, "CoughAssist"); nocturnal NIV when FVC <50% or signs of nocturnal hypoventilation; tracheostomy ventilation when NIV fails
  - Cardiac: Echocardiography and/or cardiac MRI at diagnosis or by age 6, then at minimum every 2 years until age 10, then annually; ACE inhibitors or ARBs recommended prophylactically by age 10 or at first sign of dysfunction; beta-blockers as adjunctive therapy; cardiac MRI superior to echo for detecting early fibrosis
  - Bone health: DXA monitoring; vitamin D supplementation; bisphosphonates for vertebral fractures; lateral spine X-rays for vertebral compression fractures
  - Orthopedic: Scoliosis monitoring; posterior spinal fusion when Cobb >20-25 degrees in growing child; FVC >30-40% preferred for surgical safety; contracture management; fracture prevention

- **Birnkrant DJ, Bushby K, Bann CM, et al. Diagnosis and management of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, part 3: primary care, emergency management, psychosocial care, and transitions of care across the lifespan. Lancet Neurology. 2018;17(5):445-455.**
  - Emergency management: Stress-dose hydrocortisone for adrenal crisis prevention; avoid succinylcholine (rhabdomyolysis/hyperkalemia risk); cautious use of volatile anesthetics; cardiac monitoring during procedures
  - Psychosocial: Screen for depression, anxiety, behavioral issues; educational support; transition planning
  - Palliative care: Advance care planning discussions; symptom management; quality of life focus

### 1.6 Emerging/Gene-Based Therapies

#### Exon-Skipping (Antisense Oligonucleotides)
- **Mechanism**: Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) bind pre-mRNA to skip specific exons during splicing, restoring the reading frame to produce a truncated but partially functional dystrophin (converting DMD to milder BMD-like phenotype)
- **Eteplirsen (Exondys 51)**: FDA approved 2016 (accelerated approval); skips exon 51; applicable to ~13% of DMD mutations; phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligomer (PMO); Mendell et al. Annals of Neurology. 2013;74(5):637-647. Controversial approval — modest dystrophin increase on biopsy (~0.28-0.93% of normal); functional benefit debated
- **Golodirsen (Vyondys 53)**: FDA approved 2019; skips exon 53; applicable to ~8% of mutations
- **Viltolarsen (Viltepso)**: FDA approved 2020; skips exon 53; showed higher dystrophin production than golodirsen in studies; Clemens PR et al. Neurology. 2020;94(15 Supplement)
- **Casimersen (Amondys 45)**: FDA approved 2021; skips exon 45; applicable to ~8% of mutations

#### Gene Therapy
- **Micro-dystrophin gene therapy**: AAV-based delivery of a truncated but functional micro-dystrophin gene; multiple trials ongoing (Sarepta SRP-9001/delandistrogene moxeparvovec [Elevidys], Pfizer PF-06939926, Solid Biosciences SGT-001)
- **Delandistrogene moxeparvovec (Elevidys)**: FDA accelerated approval June 2023 for ambulatory DMD ages 4-5; AAVrh74 vector delivering micro-dystrophin; Mendell JR et al. studied in EMBARK trial
- **Challenges**: Pre-existing AAV immunity, immune responses to vector/transgene, durability of expression, one-time dosing limitation

#### Other Approaches
- **Ataluren (Translarna)**: Promotes ribosomal read-through of premature stop codons (nonsense mutations, ~10-15% of DMD); approved in EU (2014), not FDA approved; ACT DMD trial (McDonald et al. Lancet. 2017;390:1489-1498) — did not meet primary endpoint
- **Givinostat**: Histone deacetylase inhibitor; anti-fibrotic/anti-inflammatory; FDA approved March 2024 for DMD ages 6+ (EPIDYS trial)
- **Vamorolone**: Dissociative steroid (anti-inflammatory without traditional steroid side effects); FDA approved October 2023; VISION-DMD trial showed efficacy similar to prednisone with fewer side effects (Hoffman EP et al.)

### 1.7 Scoliosis Management in DMD

- Develops in >90% of non-ambulatory patients
- Progressive neuromuscular scoliosis — typically long C-curve, collapsing spine pattern
- Monitoring: Clinical exam and radiographs at least annually after loss of ambulation
- Bracing: Generally ineffective at preventing progression in DMD (unlike idiopathic scoliosis); may be used for sitting support/comfort
- **Surgical indication**: Posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation when Cobb angle >20-25 degrees in growing patient or >40-50 degrees; earlier surgery preferred while pulmonary function adequate (FVC >30-40%); corticosteroid use has reduced/delayed need for surgery in many patients
- **Preoperative optimization**: Pulmonary function assessment (FVC, PCF, blood gas), cardiac evaluation, nutritional status, stress-dose steroids
- **Outcomes**: Improved sitting balance, quality of life; does NOT improve pulmonary function but may slow its decline

## SECTION 2: BECKER MUSCULAR DYSTROPHY (BMD)

### 2.1 Genetics & Pathophysiology

- **Same gene** as DMD — dystrophin gene at Xp21.2
- **Key difference**: Dystrophin is present but abnormal (reduced quantity and/or abnormal molecular weight) — partially functional truncated protein
- **Reading frame hypothesis (Monaco et al. 1988)**: In-frame mutations preserve the translational reading frame, allowing production of truncated but partially functional dystrophin = BMD. Out-of-frame = DMD. Holds true ~90-95% of cases. **Critical board concept.**
- **Incidence**: ~1 in 18,000-30,000 live male births (approximately 3-6x less common than DMD)

### 2.2 Clinical Features

- **Onset**: Later than DMD — typically age 5-15 years (but range is wide, from 2-30+ years)
- **Course**: Slower progression; ambulation maintained beyond age 16 (by definition — if ambulation lost before 16, classified as DMD phenotype)
- **Calf pseudohypertrophy**: Present (similar to DMD)
- **CK**: Elevated but generally lower than DMD (2,000-20,000 IU/L)
- **Cardiac**: Dilated cardiomyopathy may be disproportionately severe relative to skeletal muscle weakness; cardiac failure can be the presenting or primary complaint; can occur even in ambulatory patients. **Board pearl**: Cardiac disease can be the predominant feature and cause of death in BMD.
- **Cognition**: Generally normal or mildly affected
- **Contractures/scoliosis**: Less common and less severe than DMD
- **Lifespan**: Variable — many survive into 40s-60s; cardiac disease is leading cause of death

### 2.3 Diagnosis

- Genetic testing (same approach as DMD — MLPA then sequencing)
- Muscle biopsy: Dystrophic changes (less severe than DMD); immunohistochemistry shows reduced/patchy dystrophin staining (vs. absent in DMD); Western blot shows reduced amount and/or altered molecular weight dystrophin band

### 2.4 Management

- No specific approved therapies; management is supportive
- Cardiac monitoring critical — echocardiography at diagnosis, then regularly; ACE inhibitors/ARBs for cardiomyopathy
- Physical therapy, contracture prevention, activity modification
- Corticosteroids: Less evidence than in DMD; sometimes used but not standard practice
- Scoliosis management similar principles but less commonly needed
- Anesthesia precautions same as DMD (avoid succinylcholine)

## SECTION 3: SPINAL MUSCULAR ATROPHY (SMA)

### 3.1 Genetics & Pathophysiology

- **Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive
- **Gene**: SMN1 (survival motor neuron 1) gene on chromosome 5q13 (5q11.2-q13.3)
- **Mutation**: Homozygous deletion or mutation of SMN1 gene in ~95% of cases (most common: homozygous deletion of exon 7)
- **Pathophysiology**: Loss of SMN protein leads to degeneration of alpha motor neurons in the anterior horn of the spinal cord and brainstem motor nuclei, causing progressive denervation and muscle atrophy
- **SMN2 gene**: Paralogous "backup" gene; differs from SMN1 by a single C-to-T transition in exon 7 that causes predominant exon 7 skipping during splicing, producing ~90% truncated/unstable SMN protein (SMNΔ7) and only ~10% full-length functional SMN protein
- **Copy number correlation**: Higher SMN2 copy number correlates with milder disease phenotype (more full-length SMN protein produced)
  - 1 copy SMN2 → usually Type I (severe)
  - 2 copies → usually Type I or II
  - 3 copies → usually Type II or III
  - 4+ copies → usually Type III or IV
- **Incidence**: ~1 in 6,000-10,000 live births; carrier frequency ~1 in 40-60 in general population
- **Most common genetic cause of infant death** (Type I) — **high-yield board fact**

### 3.2 Classification (SMA Types I-IV)

#### Type I (Werdnig-Hoffmann Disease / Severe Infantile)
- **Onset**: Birth to 6 months
- **Motor milestone**: Never sit independently
- **Features**: Severe hypotonia ("floppy infant"), poor head control, weak cry, weak suck/swallow, tongue fasciculations, bell-shaped chest (intercostal weakness with diaphragm sparing = paradoxical breathing pattern), areflexia
- **SMN2 copies**: Usually 1-2
- **Natural history**: Without treatment, most die by age 2 from respiratory failure
- **Frequency**: ~60% of all SMA cases

#### Type II (Dubowitz Disease / Intermediate)
- **Onset**: 6-18 months
- **Motor milestone**: Achieves sitting but never stands/walks independently
- **Features**: Proximal weakness, fine hand tremor (polyminimyoclonus), tongue fasciculations, progressive scoliosis (nearly universal), joint contractures, respiratory compromise
- **SMN2 copies**: Usually 2-3
- **Natural history**: Survival into adulthood common (70%+ survive to age 25); respiratory failure is main cause of death
- **Frequency**: ~27% of SMA cases

#### Type III (Kugelberg-Welander Disease / Mild)
- **Onset**: After 18 months (subtype IIIa: 18 months-3 years; IIIb: >3 years)
- **Motor milestone**: Achieves independent ambulation (may lose later)
- **Features**: Proximal weakness, Gower sign may be present, waddling gait, difficulty with stairs/running, calf pseudohypertrophy possible (can mimic DMD), CK mildly elevated
- **SMN2 copies**: Usually 3-4
- **Natural history**: Normal lifespan; variable — some maintain ambulation lifelong, others become wheelchair-dependent
- **Frequency**: ~12% of SMA cases

#### Type IV (Adult Onset)
- **Onset**: >21 years (sometimes classified as >30 years)
- **Motor milestone**: Normal early development
- **Features**: Mild proximal weakness, slowly progressive
- **SMN2 copies**: Usually 4+
- **Natural history**: Normal lifespan, mild disability
- **Frequency**: ~1% of SMA cases

### 3.3 Diagnosis

- **Genetic testing**: First-line — SMN1 deletion analysis (quantitative PCR, MLPA); homozygous SMN1 deletion/mutation + clinical features = diagnostic
- **SMN2 copy number**: Important for prognosis and treatment decision-making
- **EMG/NCS**: Denervation pattern — fibrillations, positive sharp waves, large amplitude/long duration motor unit potentials (chronic reinnervation), reduced recruitment; NCS show reduced CMAP amplitudes with normal sensory responses
- **Muscle biopsy**: Group atrophy (neurogenic pattern) — large groups of small atrophic fibers interspersed with groups of hypertrophied type I fibers (type grouping from reinnervation); not needed if genetic confirmation obtained
- **CK**: Normal or mildly elevated (up to 4-10x normal in Type III)

### 3.4 Newborn Screening

- **Rationale**: Early (presymptomatic) treatment dramatically improves outcomes; treatment before symptom onset can achieve near-normal motor development
- **Method**: DNA-based testing from dried blood spots; detects homozygous SMN1 deletion
- **Implementation**: Added to US Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) in 2018; now implemented in most US states and expanding globally
- **Impact**: Enables presymptomatic treatment, fundamentally changing the natural history of the disease
- **Reference**: Glascock J et al. Treatment algorithm for infants diagnosed with spinal muscular atrophy through newborn screening. Journal of Neuromuscular Diseases. 2018;5(2):145-158.

### 3.5 Disease-Modifying Therapies

#### Nusinersen (Spinraza) — ENDEAR Trial
- **Mechanism**: Antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) that modifies SMN2 pre-mRNA splicing to promote inclusion of exon 7, increasing production of full-length functional SMN protein
- **Route**: Intrathecal injection; loading doses (days 1, 14, 28, 63) then maintenance every 4 months
- **ENDEAR Trial**: Finkel RS, Mercuri E, Darras BT, et al. Nusinersen versus sham control in infantile-onset spinal muscular atrophy. New England Journal of Medicine. 2017;377(18):1723-1732.
  - Randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled trial in SMA Type I infants (onset <7 months)
  - N=121 (80 nusinersen, 41 sham)
  - **Primary endpoint**: Motor milestone response (HINE-2 responder) — 51% nusinersen vs 0% sham (p<0.001)
  - **Survival**: Significant improvement in event-free survival (death or permanent ventilation)
  - **Result**: Trial stopped early at interim analysis due to overwhelming efficacy
  - **FDA approval**: December 2016 (first FDA-approved treatment for SMA)
- **CHERISH Trial** (Type II/III, later-onset): Mercuri E et al. Nusinersen versus sham control in later-onset spinal muscular atrophy. New England Journal of Medicine. 2018;378(7):625-635. Showed significant improvement in HFMSE score (+4.0 vs -1.9 points).
- **NURTURE Trial** (presymptomatic): De Vivo DC et al. Nusinersen initiated in infants during the presymptomatic stage of spinal muscular atrophy: interim efficacy and safety results from the Phase 2 NURTURE study. Neuromuscular Disorders. 2019;29(11):842-856. Remarkable results — majority of presymptomatic infants achieved sitting and walking milestones.

#### Onasemnogene Abeparvovec (Zolgensma) — STR1VE Trial
- **Mechanism**: AAV9-based gene replacement therapy delivering a functional copy of the SMN1 gene; one-time IV infusion
- **Route**: Single IV infusion
- **STR1VE Trial**: Mendell JR, Al-Zaidy SA, Lehman KJ, et al. Five-year extension results of the Phase 1 START trial of onasemnogene abeparvovec in spinal muscular atrophy. JAMA Neurology. 2021;78(7):834-841. Also: Day JW et al. Onasemnogene abeparvovec gene therapy for symptomatic infantile-onset spinal muscular atrophy in patients with two copies of SMN2 (STR1VE): an open-label, single-arm, multicentre, phase 3 trial. Lancet Neurology. 2021;20(4):284-293.
  - Open-label, single-arm Phase 3 trial in SMA Type I infants <6 months old with biallelic SMN1 mutation, 2 copies SMN2
  - 22 patients enrolled
  - 13/22 (59%) achieved sitting independently for ≥30 seconds
  - All survived to 14 months (vs. 25% expected natural history survival without permanent ventilation)
  - Rapid and sustained improvement in CHOP-INTEND scores
- **FDA approval**: May 2019; approved for SMA patients <2 years of age with biallelic SMN1 mutation
- **Safety concerns**: Hepatotoxicity (elevated transaminases, requires prednisolone pretreatment and monitoring), thrombotic microangiopathy (rare but serious), thrombocytopenia
- **Cost**: Initially ~$2.1 million per dose (most expensive drug at time of approval)
- **Durability**: Long-term follow-up shows sustained efficacy; AAV9 vector provides persistent transgene expression

#### Risdiplam (Evrysdi) — FIREFISH & SUNFISH Trials
- **Mechanism**: Small molecule SMN2 splicing modifier (oral); promotes exon 7 inclusion in SMN2 mRNA, increasing full-length SMN protein production systemically (including CNS — crosses blood-brain barrier)
- **Route**: Oral (liquid); daily dosing
- **FIREFISH Trial** (Type I): Darras BT, Masson R, Mazurkiewicz-Beldzinska M, et al. Risdiplam-treated infants with Type 1 spinal muscular atrophy versus historical controls. New England Journal of Medicine. 2021;385(5):427-435.
  - Open-label, two-part trial in SMA Type I infants (1-7 months at enrollment)
  - Part 2: 41 infants treated
  - Primary endpoint: 29% achieved sitting independently ≥5 seconds at Month 12 (vs. 0% in natural history)
  - 93% alive at Month 12; 85% event-free (alive without permanent ventilation)
  - Significant improvement in CHOP-INTEND scores and motor milestones
- **SUNFISH Trial** (Type II/III): Mercuri E, Baranello G, Boespflug-Tanguy O, et al. Risdiplam versus placebo in type 2 and non-ambulant type 3 spinal muscular atrophy (SUNFISH part 2): a phase 3, double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trial. Lancet Neurology. 2022;21(1):42-52.
  - Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in Type II/III SMA, ages 2-25
  - Statistically significant improvement in MFM-32 total score at Month 12 (1.36 point difference, p=0.0156)
- **FDA approval**: August 2020; approved for SMA patients ≥2 months of age
- **Advantages**: Oral administration (no intrathecal injection or gene therapy infusion), systemic distribution, no age restriction (approved from 2 months), no pre-existing immunity concerns

### 3.6 SMA Functional Assessment Scales

- **CHOP-INTEND** (Children's Hospital of Philadelphia Infant Test of Neuromuscular Disorders): 0-64 points; measures motor function in infants with SMA (primarily Type I); used in clinical trials
- **HINE-2** (Hammersmith Infant Neurological Examination — Section 2): Motor milestone assessment (head control, sitting, rolling, crawling, standing, walking)
- **HFMSE** (Hammersmith Functional Motor Scale Expanded): 0-66 points; for Type II/III; measures gross motor function
- **RULM** (Revised Upper Limb Module): For non-ambulatory patients; upper extremity function
- **MFM-32** (Motor Function Measure): 32 items across 3 dimensions; validated for neuromuscular diseases ages 6-60

## SECTION 4: CHARCOT-MARIE-TOOTH DISEASE (CMT) / HEREDITARY MOTOR AND SENSORY NEUROPATHIES

### 4.1 Overview

- **Most common inherited peripheral neuropathy** — prevalence ~1 in 2,500
- **Heterogeneous group**: >100 genes identified; classified by inheritance pattern, pathophysiology (demyelinating vs. axonal), and nerve conduction velocity
- **General features**: Distal > proximal weakness and atrophy (especially peroneal muscles), sensory loss (length-dependent), pes cavus, hammertoes, hyporeflexia/areflexia, steppage gait

### 4.2 CMT Type 1 (CMT1) — Demyelinating

- **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant
- **NCV**: Motor nerve conduction velocities uniformly slowed (<38 m/s in median nerve; often 15-30 m/s)
- **Nerve biopsy**: "Onion bulb" formations — concentric layers of Schwann cell processes around demyelinated/remyelinated axons (repeated cycles of demyelination and remyelination). **Classic board histology finding.**

#### CMT1A (Most Common Form — ~50% of all CMT)
- **Gene**: PMP22 (peripheral myelin protein 22) gene
- **Locus**: Chromosome 17p11.2
- **Mutation**: Most commonly 1.5 Mb duplication containing PMP22 gene (70-80% of CMT1A); gene dosage effect — overexpression of PMP22 disrupts myelination
- **Clinical**: Onset typically in first 2 decades; slowly progressive distal weakness (foot drop, hand weakness), sensory loss, areflexia, pes cavus, hammertoes; "inverted champagne bottle" legs (distal leg atrophy)
- **Reciprocal deletion**: Deletion of the same 17p11.2 region causes HNPP (hereditary neuropathy with liability to pressure palsies) — episodes of demyelinating neuropathy at compression sites; "tomaculous" (sausage-shaped) myelin on biopsy

#### CMT1B
- **Gene**: MPZ (myelin protein zero) gene, chromosome 1q22
- **Less common than 1A**; variable severity

### 4.3 CMT Type 2 (CMT2) — Axonal

- **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant (most subtypes)
- **NCV**: Normal or near-normal motor conduction velocities (>38 m/s) — because myelin is intact; reduced CMAP amplitudes reflect axonal loss
- **Nerve biopsy**: Axonal degeneration without onion bulbs
- **CMT2A**: Most common axonal subtype; MFN2 (mitofusin 2) gene, chromosome 1p36; often more severe, earlier onset

### 4.4 CMT Type 3 (Dejerine-Sottas Disease)

- **Severe demyelinating neuropathy**, onset in infancy
- **Autosomal dominant or recessive**
- **NCV**: Severely slowed (<10 m/s)
- **Features**: Severe hypotonia/weakness in infancy, delayed motor milestones, palpably enlarged nerves
- **Genes**: PMP22, MPZ, or EGR2 mutations (point mutations, not duplications)
- **Nerve biopsy**: Prominent onion bulbs, hypomyelination
- **Now often classified as severe CMT1 rather than separate entity**

### 4.5 CMT Type 4 — Autosomal Recessive Demyelinating

- Multiple subtypes (CMT4A through CMT4J)
- Various genes affecting Schwann cell function and myelination
- Generally more severe, earlier onset
- More common in consanguineous populations

### 4.6 CMTX (X-Linked CMT)

- **CMT X1**: Second most common CMT overall (~10-15%); GJB1 gene (connexin 32) on Xq13
- **Males more severely affected**; females may be asymptomatic or mildly affected
- **NCV**: Intermediate slowing (25-40 m/s in males) — between demyelinating and axonal ranges
- **CNS involvement**: Transient white matter lesions possible

### 4.7 CMT Management (Rehabilitation Focus)

- **No disease-modifying pharmacotherapy available** (as of 2024)
- **Physical therapy**: Stretching, strengthening (avoid eccentric overload), balance training, aerobic conditioning
- **Orthotics**: AFOs for foot drop (most commonly prescribed intervention); custom shoe inserts for pes cavus
- **Surgical**: Foot/ankle reconstruction for severe pes cavus/hammertoes; tendon transfers; osteotomies
- **Assistive devices**: As needed for progressive disability
- **Genetic counseling**: Essential given varied inheritance patterns
- **Avoid**: Vincristine (can cause devastating worsening — **board pearl**)
- **Pain management**: Neuropathic pain common; gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine

| Fact | Detail |

| Most common inherited neuropathy | CMT (~1:2,500) |
| Most common CMT subtype | CMT1A (~50% of all CMT) |
| CMT1A gene/location | PMP22, 17p11.2 (duplication) |
| CMT1A reciprocal deletion | HNPP (pressure palsies) |
| CMT1 histology | Onion bulbs (Schwann cell layering) |
| CMT1 NCV | <38 m/s (demyelinating) |
| CMT2 NCV | >38 m/s (axonal) |
| Characteristic deformity | Pes cavus, hammertoes, "inverted champagne bottle" legs |
| Drug to avoid | Vincristine (devastating neuropathy) |

## SECTION 5: FRIEDREICH ATAXIA (FRDA)

### 5.1 Genetics & Pathophysiology

- **Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive — most common hereditary ataxia
- **Gene**: FXN gene on chromosome 9q21.11, encoding frataxin protein
- **Mutation**: ~96% have homozygous GAA trinucleotide repeat expansion in intron 1 of FXN gene; normal: 5-33 repeats; premutation: 34-65; pathogenic: 66-1,700+ repeats
- **Mechanism**: GAA expansion causes heterochromatin formation (DNA triplex/sticky DNA structures) → transcriptional silencing → severely reduced frataxin protein levels (5-30% of normal)
- **Frataxin function**: Mitochondrial protein involved in iron-sulfur cluster assembly; frataxin deficiency leads to mitochondrial iron accumulation, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular energy failure
- **Pathology**: Degeneration of dorsal root ganglia (DRG) neurons, spinocerebellar tracts, corticospinal tracts, dorsal columns; cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis
- **Prevalence**: ~1 in 50,000; carrier frequency ~1 in 100 in European populations
- **Anticipation**: GAA repeat size inversely correlates with age of onset and directly correlates with disease severity; larger repeats = earlier onset, more severe disease. However, true genetic anticipation (intergenerational repeat expansion) is less prominent than in other trinucleotide repeat disorders.

### 5.2 Clinical Features

- **Onset**: Typically before age 25 (mean ~10-15 years); usually before puberty
- **Neurological**: Progressive gait and limb ataxia (cerebellar + sensory), dysarthria, loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia — due to DRG/peripheral nerve involvement), loss of proprioception/vibration (dorsal column), positive Babinski sign (corticospinal tract), nystagmus
- **Board pearl**: Areflexia + Babinski sign = mixed upper and lower motor neuron findings — classic for Friedreich ataxia
- **Cardiac**: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (most common cause of death, ~60%); concentric LV hypertrophy; arrhythmias; eventually dilated cardiomyopathy in some
- **Skeletal**: Scoliosis (>80%), pes cavus, hammertoes (overlap with CMT phenotype)
- **Endocrine**: Diabetes mellitus (~30%, due to pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction and insulin resistance)
- **Vision/hearing**: Optic atrophy, sensorineural hearing loss (in some)
- **Progression**: Most patients wheelchair-dependent within 10-15 years of onset; mean age of death ~37 years (cardiac causes predominant)

### 5.3 Diagnosis

- **Genetic testing**: GAA repeat analysis of FXN gene — homozygous expansion diagnostic
- **NCS/EMG**: Axonal sensory neuropathy (absent or reduced sensory nerve action potentials [SNAPs] with normal motor NCV) — **important board distinction**: sensory axonal, not demyelinating
- **Echocardiography**: Concentric LV hypertrophy; annual screening
- **MRI brain**: Cervical spinal cord atrophy; cerebellar atrophy (less prominent than other cerebellar ataxias)
- **Frataxin protein level**: Reduced (5-30% of normal)

### 5.4 Treatment

- **Omaveloxolone (Skyclarys)**: FDA approved February 2023 — first and only FDA-approved treatment for Friedreich ataxia
  - **Mechanism**: Nrf2 activator; enhances expression of antioxidant genes, reduces oxidative stress, improves mitochondrial function
  - **Trial**: Lynch DR, Chin MP, Deez ME, et al. Safety and efficacy of omaveloxolone in Friedreich ataxia (MOXIe): a multicentre, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 2 trial. Lancet Neurology. 2021;20(5):339-348.
    - Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase 2 trial
    - N=103 patients aged 16-40 with FRDA
    - Primary endpoint: Change in mFARS (modified Friedreich Ataxia Rating Scale) at 48 weeks
    - Omaveloxolone group: -1.55 point improvement vs. +0.85 point worsening in placebo group (difference -2.40, p=0.014)
  - **Dosing**: 150 mg oral, once daily
  - **Side effects**: Elevated transaminases, headache, nausea, fatigue, diarrhea, musculoskeletal pain
  - **Age**: Approved for ages 16+ years

- **Supportive management**: Physical therapy (balance, coordination, strengthening), occupational therapy, speech therapy (dysarthria), cardiac monitoring and management (beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors), scoliosis management, diabetes management, orthotic support for pes cavus
- **Investigational**: Gene therapy approaches, frataxin replacement strategies, other antioxidants (idebenone — studied but mixed results, not FDA approved), iron chelation (deferiprone — studied but not clearly beneficial)

## SECTION 6: CONGENITAL MYOPATHIES

### 6.1 Overview

- Genetically diverse group of muscle diseases presenting at birth or infancy with hypotonia and weakness
- Defined by characteristic structural abnormalities on muscle biopsy
- Generally static or slowly progressive (in contrast to muscular dystrophies)
- **Key distinction from dystrophies**: CK normal or mildly elevated; biopsy shows structural changes (rods, cores, etc.) rather than dystrophic changes (necrosis, regeneration, fibrosis)

### 6.2 Major Types

#### Nemaline Myopathy
- **Biopsy hallmark**: Nemaline rods (rod-shaped inclusions derived from Z-disc material, composed of alpha-actinin and actin) on Gomori trichrome stain
- **Genetics**: Multiple genes (NEB [nebulin, autosomal recessive — most common], ACTA1 [actin, autosomal dominant or recessive], TPM2, TPM3, TNNT1, CFL2, KBTBD13, KLHL40, KLHL41, LMOD3)
- **Clinical**: Spectrum from severe neonatal (respiratory failure at birth) to mild childhood onset; facial weakness (elongated face), bulbar weakness, proximal > distal limb weakness, respiratory insufficiency
- **CK**: Normal or mildly elevated

#### Central Core Disease
- **Biopsy hallmark**: Central cores — well-defined areas devoid of mitochondria and oxidative enzyme activity running longitudinally through type 1 fibers; visible on NADH/oxidative stains
- **Genetics**: RYR1 (ryanodine receptor 1) gene, chromosome 19q13; autosomal dominant (most) or recessive
- **Clinical**: Hypotonia, proximal weakness, motor delay, hip dislocation, scoliosis; often non-progressive
- **Association**: **Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility** — **critical board fact**; RYR1 mutations cause both central core disease and susceptibility to malignant hyperthermia (triggered by volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine)
- **CK**: Normal or mildly elevated

#### Centronuclear/Myotubular Myopathy
- **Biopsy hallmark**: Centrally located nuclei in many muscle fibers (normally nuclei are at periphery/subsarcolemmal)
- **Genetics**:
  - X-linked myotubular myopathy (most severe): MTM1 gene (myotubularin); severe neonatal hypotonia, respiratory failure
  - Autosomal dominant centronuclear: DNM2 (dynamin 2); milder, later onset
  - Autosomal recessive centronuclear: BIN1 (amphiphysin 2); intermediate severity
- **X-linked form**: Severe, often fatal in infancy from respiratory failure; predominantly male

#### Congenital Fiber Type Disproportion (CFTD)
- **Biopsy hallmark**: Type 1 fibers are significantly smaller than type 2 fibers (>12-25% difference in mean diameter) without other specific structural abnormalities
- **Genetics**: Multiple genes (TPM3, ACTA1, RYR1, SELENON, TPM2, MYH7)
- **Clinical**: Hypotonia, diffuse weakness, contractures, respiratory involvement variable
- **Generally favorable prognosis** with many patients improving over time

### 6.3 Board-Relevant Quick Facts — Congenital Myopathies

| Type | Biopsy Finding | Key Gene | Board Pearl |
|------|---------------|----------|-------------|
| Nemaline | Nemaline rods | NEB, ACTA1 | Rod bodies on trichrome stain |
| Central core | Central cores | RYR1 | Malignant hyperthermia risk |
| Centronuclear | Central nuclei | MTM1 (X-linked) | X-linked = severe neonatal |
| CFTD | Type 1 fiber smallness | Multiple | Often improves with age |

## SECTION 7: MYOTONIC DYSTROPHY

### 7.1 Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1 (DM1 / Steinert Disease)

#### Genetics
- **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant
- **Gene**: DMPK (DM protein kinase) gene on chromosome 19q13.3
- **Mutation**: CTG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the 3' untranslated region (3'UTR) of DMPK gene
- **Normal**: 5-34 CTG repeats
- **Premutation**: 35-49 repeats (may be unstable, can expand in offspring)
- **Affected**: 50-~4,000+ repeats
- **Pathophysiology**: Expanded CUG repeats in mRNA form toxic RNA hairpin structures that sequester RNA-binding proteins (especially MBNL1 — muscleblind-like 1), causing widespread splicing dysregulation (spliceopathy) affecting multiple organ systems

#### Anticipation
- **Genetic anticipation**: Hallmark of DM1 — CTG repeat tends to expand in successive generations (especially with maternal transmission), leading to **earlier onset and more severe disease in offspring**. **Major board concept.**
- Classic pattern: Grandparent with cataracts/mild myotonia → Parent with classic DM1 → Child with congenital DM1 (massive repeat expansion)

#### Clinical Forms (by repeat size and severity)

1. **Premutation/Asymptomatic** (35-49 repeats): No symptoms but repeat may expand in offspring
2. **Mild/Late-onset** (50-150 repeats): Cataracts, mild myotonia; normal lifespan
3. **Classic Adult DM1** (100-1,000 repeats):
   - Onset: Adolescence/adulthood (typically 10-30 years)
   - Myotonia (delayed muscle relaxation after contraction — e.g., inability to release handgrip, percussion myotonia)
   - Progressive distal > proximal weakness (unlike most myopathies which are proximal); facial weakness, temporalis/masseter atrophy ("hatchet face"), ptosis, sternocleidomastoid weakness
   - Cardiac: Conduction defects (AV block, bundle branch block), arrhythmias, sudden cardiac death; annual ECG/Holter monitoring
   - Respiratory: Diaphragm/respiratory muscle weakness, central apnea, excessive daytime somnolence
   - GI: Dysphagia, gastroparesis, constipation, gallstones
   - Endocrine: Insulin resistance/diabetes, hypogonadism (testicular atrophy in males), thyroid dysfunction
   - CNS: Cognitive decline, executive dysfunction, apathy, hypersomnia, white matter lesions on MRI
   - Cataracts: Posterior subcapsular ("Christmas tree cataracts") — often earliest sign
   - CK: Normal or mildly elevated

4. **Congenital DM1** (>1,000 repeats, often 1,500-4,000+): **Board-critical entity**
   - **Almost exclusively maternally transmitted** (massive repeats transmitted by affected mothers)
   - **Presentation at birth**: Severe hypotonia ("floppy infant"), respiratory failure (often requiring ventilatory support), feeding difficulties, arthrogryposis (joint contractures), facial diplegia, tented upper lip ("fish mouth"/"tent mouth")
   - **Neonatal mortality**: Significant (~25% in severe cases) from respiratory failure
   - **Survivors**: Intellectual disability (moderate-severe), motor delays, gradual development of myotonia and weakness typical of classic DM1 in childhood/adolescence
   - **Board pearl**: Mother may be undiagnosed — always examine the mother when an infant presents with unexplained hypotonia and contractures

#### Diagnosis
- **Genetic testing**: CTG repeat analysis of DMPK gene — definitive
- **EMG**: Myotonic discharges — waxing-and-waning amplitude and frequency ("dive bomber" sound on audio monitor). **Classic board finding.** May not be present in congenital form in infancy.
- **Slit lamp exam**: Posterior subcapsular cataracts

### 7.2 Myotonic Dystrophy Type 2 (DM2 / Proximal Myotonic Myopathy / PROMM)

- **Gene**: CNBP (ZNF9) gene on chromosome 3q21.3
- **Mutation**: CCTG tetranucleotide repeat expansion in intron 1
- **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant
- **Clinical**: Generally milder than DM1; onset adulthood (20-60 years); proximal > distal weakness (unlike DM1), myotonia, myalgia, cataracts, cardiac conduction defects
- **No congenital form** (unlike DM1)
- **No clear anticipation** (unlike DM1 — CCTG repeats are highly unstable but don't show clear parent-to-child expansion correlation with severity)
- **Less relevant to pediatric rehabilitation** but important for differential diagnosis and boards

### 7.3 Board-Relevant Quick Facts — Myotonic Dystrophy

| Fact | DM1 | DM2 |
|------|-----|-----|
| Gene | DMPK, 19q13.3 | CNBP, 3q21.3 |
| Repeat | CTG (trinucleotide) | CCTG (tetranucleotide) |
| Weakness pattern | Distal > proximal | Proximal > distal |
| Anticipation | Yes (maternal) | No |
| Congenital form | Yes (maternal transmission) | No |
| Myotonia | Yes (dive bomber EMG) | Yes (milder) |
| Cataracts | Posterior subcapsular | Posterior subcapsular |
| Cardiac | Conduction defects, sudden death | Conduction defects |
| Multisystem | Yes | Yes |
