## 1. GUIDELINES FOUND

### International Clinical Practice Guideline for Early Detection of CP (2017)
- Novak I, Morgan C, Adde L, et al. Early, Accurate Diagnosis and Early Intervention in Cerebral Palsy: Advances in Diagnosis and Treatment. JAMA Pediatr. 2017;171(9):897-907.
- **[BOARD]** Landmark guideline establishing evidence-based pathway for early CP detection BEFORE 5 months corrected age.
- Best tools combination: **General Movements Assessment (GMA) + MRI + Hammersmith Infant Neurological Examination (HINE)**
  - GMA (fidgety movements absent at 3-5 months): Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 91%
  - Neonatal MRI (term equivalent): Sensitivity 86-89%, Specificity 89%
  - HINE at 3-5 months: Sensitivity 90%, Specificity 85-90%
  - Combined approach: Sensitivity >97%, Specificity >99%
- **[BOARD]** Previous average age of CP diagnosis was 12-24 months; this guideline pushes for diagnosis by 5 months corrected age in high-risk infants.

### NICE Guidelines: Spasticity in Under 19s (2012, updated 2016)
- National Institute for Health and Care Excellence. Spasticity in under 19s: management. NICE guideline CG145. 2012 (updated 2016).
- Stepped approach to spasticity management.

### AAN/AAP Practice Parameter: Diagnostic Assessment of the Child with CP (2004)
- Ashwal S, Russman BS, Blasco PA, et al. Practice parameter: diagnostic assessment of the child with cerebral palsy. Neurology. 2004;62(6):851-863.
- MRI recommended as neuroimaging of choice (yield 89%).
- Metabolic/genetic testing if no structural cause found on MRI.

### AACPDM Botulinum Toxin Systematic Reviews
- Love SC, Novak I, Kentish M, et al. Botulinum toxin assessment, intervention and after-care for lower limb spasticity in children with cerebral palsy: international consensus statement. Eur J Neurol. 2010;17(Suppl 2):9-37.

## 2. LANDMARK TRIALS & SEMINAL STUDIES

### Novak 2017 Early Detection Guideline
- Novak I, Morgan C, Adde L, et al. Early, Accurate Diagnosis and Early Intervention in Cerebral Palsy: Advances in Diagnosis and Treatment. JAMA Pediatr. 2017;171(9):897-907.
- Systematic review establishing the diagnostic pathway.
- **[BOARD]** Key evidence synthesis:
  - **General Movements Assessment (Prechtl)**:
    - Cramped-synchronized general movements at writhing age (birth-8 weeks) = high risk
    - **Absent fidgety movements** (3-5 months corrected) = BEST SINGLE PREDICTOR of CP
    - Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 91% for absent fidgety movements
  - **MRI findings**: Abnormal in ~86-90% of children with CP
    - White matter injury (PVL): ~45% of all CP, most common in preterm
    - Grey matter injury (basal ganglia/thalamus): ~13%, typical of term asphyxia
    - Malformations: ~11%
    - Focal infarcts: ~7%
    - Normal MRI: ~10-14% (more common in mild/ataxic CP)
  - **HINE**: Standardized neurological exam scoring; global scores predict GMFCS level
    - Score <57 at 3 months: abnormal
    - Score <40 at 3 months: likely GMFCS III-V
    - Score <50 at 3 months: CP likely

### CIMT (Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy) Trials
- **Original Taub studies**: Taub E, Ramey SL, DeLuca S, Echols K. Efficacy of constraint-induced movement therapy for children with cerebral palsy with asymmetric motor impairment. Pediatrics. 2004;113(2):305-312.
  - Modified CIMT for children with hemiplegic CP.
  - 6 hours/day x 21 days of CIMT improved upper extremity function.

- **ACQUIREc Trial**: Gordon AM, Hung YC, Brandao M, et al. Bimanual training and constraint-induced movement therapy in children with hemiplegic cerebral palsy: a randomized trial. Neurorehabil Neural Repair. 2011;25(8):692-702.
  - Compared CIMT vs bimanual intensive therapy (HABIT).
  - Both CIMT and HABIT showed improvements; CIMT better for unimanual capacity, HABIT better for bimanual performance.
  - **[BOARD]** CIMT improves quality of movement of the affected hand; bimanual therapy improves coordination between hands.

- **Eliasson BabyHABIT-P**: Emerging evidence for early bimanual intervention in infants as young as 3-8 months.

### SEMLS (Single-Event Multilevel Surgery)
- Rang M. (coined concept). Single-event multilevel surgery was developed to address multiple deformities simultaneously rather than repeated "birthday surgeries."
- McGinley JL, Dobson F, Ganeshalingam R, et al. Single-event multilevel surgery for children with cerebral palsy: a systematic review. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2012;54(2):117-128.
- **[BOARD]** Optimal timing: GMFCS I-III, ages 6-12 years (after gait maturation ~age 6, before skeletal maturity).
- Purpose: Address ALL gait abnormalities in one surgical session (reduce total surgical episodes, anesthesia events, and rehabilitation periods).
- Typical procedures combined: Psoas lengthening, adductor release, hamstring lengthening, rectus femoris transfer, gastrocnemius/Achilles lengthening, femoral derotation osteotomy, tibial derotation osteotomy.

### SDR (Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy)
- Park TS, Johnston JM. Surgical techniques of selective dorsal rhizotomy for spastic cerebral palsy. Neurosurg Focus. 2006;21(2):e7.
- McLaughlin J, Bjornson K, Temkin N, et al. Selective dorsal rhizotomy: meta-analysis of three randomized controlled trials. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2002;44(1):17-25.
- **[BOARD]** SDR Candidacy Criteria:
  - Age 3-8 years (some centers up to 10-12 years)
  - Spastic DIPLEGIA (pure spasticity, no dystonia/athetosis)
  - GMFCS II-III (ambulatory or near-ambulatory)
  - Adequate underlying strength
  - No fixed contractures
  - Good selective motor control
  - Motivated patient/family
  - MRI: periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) pattern preferred
- **[BOARD]** SDR permanently reduces spasticity by selectively cutting dorsal (sensory) rootlets at L1-S1/S2. Reduces spasticity by interrupting the gamma loop reflex arc.
- Complications: Sensory changes (dysesthesias), bladder dysfunction (usually transient), weakness, spinal deformity (risk of lordosis/scoliosis).

### Therapeutic Hypothermia for HIE
- Shankaran S, Laptook AR, Ehrenkranz RA, et al. Whole-body hypothermia for neonates with hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy. N Engl J Med. 2005;353(15):1574-1584.
- Azzopardi DV, Strohm B, Edwards AD, et al. Moderate hypothermia to treat perinatal asphyxial encephalopathy. N Engl J Med. 2009;361(14):1349-1358 (TOBY trial).
- **[BOARD]** Protocol: Whole-body cooling to 33.5 degrees C for 72 hours, initiated within 6 hours of birth.
- **[BOARD]** NNT = ~6-7 (number needed to treat to prevent one death or major disability).
- Reduces death or moderate/severe disability from ~60% to ~45% (RR ~0.75).
- Criteria: Term or near-term infants (>36 weeks), evidence of moderate-severe HIE (Sarnat stage II-III), within 6 hours of birth.
- Has NOT eliminated CP -- rates of CP from HIE have decreased but not disappeared.

### CPUP (Cerebral Palsy Follow-Up Programme)
- Hagglund G, Andersson S, Duppe H, et al. Prevention of hip displacement in children with cerebral palsy: the first ten years of a population-based prevention programme. J Bone Joint Surg Br. 2005;87(1):95-101.
- Swedish population-based surveillance program initiated 1994.
- **[BOARD]** Before CPUP: Hip dislocation rate requiring surgery ~8%. After CPUP implementation: Rate dropped to nearly 0%.
- Key principle: Radiographic hip surveillance with migration percentage (MP) measurement at standardized intervals based on GMFCS level.
- **Hip surveillance schedule**:
  - GMFCS I: Hip X-ray at 2 and 6 years
  - GMFCS II: Annual hip X-ray until skeletal maturity
  - GMFCS III-V: Hip X-ray every 6 months until age 8, then annually
- **[BOARD]** Migration Percentage (Reimers Index):
  - Normal: <25% (some say <30%)
  - At risk: 30-40%
  - Subluxation: 40-60%
  - Dislocation: >90-100%
- Intervention thresholds: MP 33-40% = botulinum toxin, soft tissue surgery; MP >40-50% = bony surgery (VDRO, pelvic osteotomy).

## 3. RECENT EVIDENCE (2020-2025)

### Novak 2020 State of the Evidence for CP Interventions
- Novak I, Morgan C, Fahey M, et al. State of the Evidence Traffic Lights 2019: Systematic Review of Interventions for Preventing and Treating Children with Cerebral Palsy. Curr Neurol Neurosci Rep. 2020;20(2):3.
- Updated "traffic light" systematic review (green=effective, yellow=possibly effective, red=ineffective).
- **Green light (do it)** interventions:
  - Anticonvulsants for seizures
  - Botulinum toxin A for spasticity (upper and lower limb)
  - Bisphosphonates for low bone density
  - Casting/orthoses
  - CIMT and bimanual therapy
  - Context-focused therapy (goals in natural environments)
  - Diazepam for short-term spasticity management
  - Fitness training
  - Goal-directed training
  - Hip surveillance
  - Home-based programs
  - Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) for generalized spasticity
  - Pressure care for skin integrity
  - Selective dorsal rhizotomy
  - SEMLS
- **Red light (don't do)** interventions:
  - Craniosacral therapy
  - Hyperbaric oxygen
  - Neurodevelopmental therapy (NDT/Bobath) as sole intervention for motor outcomes
  - Sensory integration therapy (as traditionally defined)

### Early Intervention in CP (2020-2024)
- Morgan C, Novak I, Dale RC, Guzzetta A, Badawi N. Single blind randomised controlled trial of GAME (Goals-Activity-Motor Enrichment) in infants at high risk of cerebral palsy. Res Dev Disabil. 2016;55:256-267.
- Growing evidence for activity-based, goal-directed early intervention starting before 6 months corrected age.
- REACH and PLAY studies: Early enriched environments and parent-delivered intervention models.

### Magnesium Sulfate for Neuroprotection
- Crowther CA, Middleton PF, Voysey M, et al. Assessing the neuroprotective benefits for babies of antenatal magnesium sulphate: An individual participant data meta-analysis. PLoS Med. 2017;14(10):e1002398.
- **[BOARD]** Antenatal magnesium sulfate given to mothers at risk of preterm delivery (<32 weeks) reduces rate of CP by ~30-40% (RR ~0.68, 95% CI 0.54-0.87). NNT ~63 for prevention of CP.
- Now standard of care in preterm labor <32 weeks gestation.

### Genomics and CP
- MacLennan AH, Thompson SC, Gecz J. Cerebral palsy: causes, pathways, and the role of genetic variants. Am J Obstet Gynecol. 2015;213(6):779-788.
- Emerging evidence that 14-31% of CP cases may have a genetic/genomic contribution.
- Whole exome sequencing identifying de novo variants in ~10-14% of cases.
- Challenges traditional view that CP is solely an acquired brain injury.

## 4. EPIDEMIOLOGY DATA

### Prevalence
- **[BOARD]** CP prevalence: ~2.0-3.5 per 1,000 live births (most commonly cited: 2-2.5/1,000).
- Overall prevalence has remained relatively stable despite advances in neonatal care.
- Prevalence in very preterm (<32 weeks): ~60-80 per 1,000 live births.
- Prevalence in extremely preterm (<28 weeks): ~100-150 per 1,000 live births.
- Term infants: ~1-1.5 per 1,000 live births.
- **[BOARD]** CP is the MOST COMMON motor disability in childhood.

### Distribution by Type
- **[BOARD]** Spastic CP: ~80-85% of all CP
  - Spastic bilateral (diplegia): ~35%
  - Spastic unilateral (hemiplegia): ~30%
  - Spastic bilateral (quadriplegia): ~15-20%
- Dyskinetic CP: ~10-15%
  - Dystonic: ~75% of dyskinetic
  - Choreoathetoid: ~25% of dyskinetic
- Ataxic CP: ~5-6%
- Mixed: ~5-10%

### Distribution by GMFCS Level (population-based studies)
- GMFCS I: ~35%
- GMFCS II: ~20%
- GMFCS III: ~10-15%
- GMFCS IV: ~15-20%
- GMFCS V: ~15-20%
- **[BOARD]** ~60% of individuals with CP are ambulatory (GMFCS I-III).

### Risk Factors
- **[BOARD]** Prematurity is the SINGLE GREATEST risk factor for CP.
- Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) = most common neuropathology in preterm CP.
- Basal ganglia/thalamic injury = most common pattern in term asphyxia CP (often dyskinetic or mixed).
- Multiple gestation (twins/triplets) increases risk.
- Male sex (slight predominance).
- Intrauterine infection/chorioamnionitis.
- Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) grades III-IV.
- Neonatal seizures.
- **[BOARD]** Only ~10% of CP is due to birth asphyxia. Many cases are due to prenatal factors.

### Associated Conditions
- **[BOARD]** CP is NOT just a motor disorder -- it is a GROUP of PERMANENT disorders of movement and posture, causing activity limitation, attributed to non-progressive disturbances in the developing fetal or infant brain.
- Intellectual disability: ~50% (ranges from 30-65% depending on CP type)
- Epilepsy: ~35-50%
- Speech/language disorders: ~50-60%
- Visual impairment: ~20-40% (cortical visual impairment most common)
- Hearing impairment: ~10-20%
- Behavioral/emotional problems: ~25%
- Chronic pain: ~50-75% (often underrecognized)
- Hip displacement: ~35% overall; up to 90% in GMFCS V
- Scoliosis: Up to 60-70% in non-ambulatory CP
- Osteoporosis/low bone density: Very common in non-ambulatory CP
- Feeding/swallowing difficulties: ~40-90% (highest in GMFCS IV-V)
- GERD: ~50-70%
- Constipation: ~50-75%
- Drooling/sialorrhea: ~40%

## 5. CLINICAL SCALES & CLASSIFICATIONS

### GMFCS (Gross Motor Function Classification System)
- Palisano R, Rosenbaum P, Walter S, et al. Development and reliability of a system to classify gross motor function in children with cerebral palsy. Dev Med Child Neurol. 1997;39(4):214-223.
- Palisano RJ, Rosenbaum P, Bartlett D, Livingston MH. Content validity of the expanded and revised Gross Motor Function Classification System. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2008;50(10):744-750.
- **[BOARD]** GMFCS Expanded and Revised (GMFCS-E&R) has 5 age bands: <2 years, 2-4 years, 4-6 years, 6-12 years, 12-18 years.
- **[BOARD]** Distinctions between levels are based on FUNCTIONAL LIMITATIONS, NEED FOR ASSISTIVE DEVICES, and QUALITY OF MOVEMENT.

**[BOARD]** GMFCS Levels (general summary -- 6-12 year age band):

**Level I**: Walks without limitations. Limitations in more advanced gross motor skills (running, jumping, speed, balance, coordination). Can participate in sports/physical activities.

**Level II**: Walks with limitations. Difficulty with uneven surfaces, inclines, long distances, crowds. May use railing on stairs. Minimal ability to run/jump. May use wheeled mobility for long distances in community.

**Level III**: Walks using a hand-held mobility device (crutches, walker) indoors. Wheeled mobility for longer distances and outdoors. May self-propel manual wheelchair or use power mobility. Sits independently or with minimal support. Floor mobility by crawling.

**Level IV**: Self-mobility with limitations; transported or uses power mobility in most settings. Maintains sitting with support. Requires adaptive seating. Standing for transfers with physical assistance. Floor mobility by rolling or crawling limited distances.

**Level V**: Transported in a manual wheelchair in all settings. Severely limited ability to maintain antigravity head and trunk postures. All areas of motor function limited. Limited achievement of self-mobility even with power technology.

**[BOARD]** Key GMFCS Facts:
- GMFCS is DESCRIPTIVE, not prescriptive.
- GMFCS level is generally STABLE after age 2 (especially after age 5).
- Level is based on USUAL performance, not BEST performance.
- Key distinction I vs II: I walks without limitations; II has limitations (uneven terrain, stairs, running).
- Key distinction II vs III: II walks without assistive device; III needs hand-held device (walker/crutches).
- Key distinction III vs IV: III walks with device; IV primarily wheelchair, may stand for transfers.
- Key distinction IV vs V: IV has some self-mobility; V is fully dependent for mobility.

### GMFCS Motor Growth Curves
- Hanna SE, Rosenbaum PL, Bartlett DJ, et al. Stability and decline in gross motor function among children and youth with cerebral palsy aged 2 to 21 years. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2009;51(4):295-302.
- **[BOARD]** Motor growth curves show:
  - GMFCS I-II: Motor skills continue to improve through adolescence, then plateau
  - GMFCS III: Peak motor function around age 6-8, then DECLINE in adolescence
  - GMFCS IV: Peak around age 5-7, then decline
  - GMFCS V: Very limited motor development; peak around age 3-5, then decline
- **[BOARD]** GMFCS III-V: Expect DECLINE in gross motor function in adolescence (loss of walking for some GMFCS III, increasing difficulty with transfers for IV-V). Clinically critical for anticipatory guidance.

### GMFM-66 vs GMFM-88
- Russell DJ, Rosenbaum PL, Wright M, Avery LM. Gross Motor Function Measure (GMFM-66 & GMFM-88) User's Manual. 2nd ed. Mac Keith Press; 2013.
- **GMFM-88**: 88 items, 5 dimensions:
  - A: Lying and Rolling (17 items)
  - B: Sitting (20 items)
  - C: Crawling and Kneeling (14 items)
  - D: Standing (13 items)
  - E: Walking, Running, Jumping (24 items)
  - Each item scored 0-3 (0=does not initiate, 1=initiates, 2=partially completes, 3=completes)
  - Raw scores per dimension, dimension percentage scores, total percentage score
- **[BOARD]** GMFM-88 is an ORDINAL scale. Cannot compare scores directly across dimensions. All 88 items assessed.
- **GMFM-66**: 66 items (subset of GMFM-88), uses Rasch analysis to create an INTERVAL scale.
  - **[BOARD]** GMFM-66 IS an interval scale (equal intervals between scores, allowing meaningful comparison of change scores).
  - Scores can be mapped onto percentile curves by GMFCS level.
  - More responsive to change than GMFM-88.
  - Can be estimated without completing all items (Gross Motor Ability Estimator -- GMAE software).
  - Score range: 0-100 (higher = better gross motor function).
- **[BOARD]** Key distinction: GMFM-88 = ordinal, all items must be tested. GMFM-66 = interval (Rasch), more sensitive to change, can estimate from subset of items.

### MACS (Manual Ability Classification System)
- Eliasson AC, Krumlinde-Sundholm L, Rosblad B, et al. The Manual Ability Classification System (MACS) for children with cerebral palsy: scale development and evidence of validity and reliability. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2006;48(7):549-554.
- **[BOARD]** MACS classifies how children with CP USE their hands to handle objects in daily activities. Ages 4-18 years (Mini-MACS for 1-4 years).

**[BOARD]** MACS Levels:

**Level I**: Handles objects easily and successfully. Minor limitations in speed, accuracy, or dexterity of fine motor tasks.

**Level II**: Handles most objects but with somewhat reduced quality and/or speed. May avoid some tasks or use alternative strategies; no restriction on independence.

**Level III**: Handles objects with difficulty; needs help to prepare and/or modify activities. Performance is slow; limited quality and quantity.

**Level IV**: Handles a limited selection of easily managed objects in adapted situations. Requires continuous support and adapted equipment; partially achieves activities.

**Level V**: Does not handle objects. Has severe limitations in ability to perform even simple actions. Requires total assistance.

### CFCS (Communication Function Classification System)
- Hidecker MJ, Paneth N, Rosenbaum PL, et al. Developing and validating the Communication Function Classification System for individuals with cerebral palsy. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2011;53(8):704-710.
- **[BOARD]** CFCS classifies everyday communication (ALL methods -- speech, gestures, AAC, eye gaze, etc.).

**[BOARD]** CFCS Levels:

**Level I**: Effective sender AND receiver with unfamiliar AND familiar partners.

**Level II**: Effective but slower paced sender AND/OR receiver with unfamiliar AND/OR familiar partners.

**Level III**: Effective sender AND receiver with familiar partners.

**Level IV**: Inconsistent sender AND/OR receiver with familiar partners.

**Level V**: Seldom effective sender AND receiver even with familiar partners.

### EDACS (Eating and Drinking Ability Classification System)
- Sellers D, Mandy A, Pennington L, Hankins M, Morris C. Development and reliability of a system to classify the eating and drinking ability of people with cerebral palsy. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2014;56(3):245-251.
- 5 levels from I (eats and drinks safely and efficiently) to V (unable to eat or drink safely, tube feeding may be considered).

### Classification of CP by Topography
**[BOARD]**:
- **Hemiplegia/unilateral**: One side of body (arm usually more affected than leg). Most common in term infants. Often middle cerebral artery territory infarct or unilateral PVL.
- **Diplegia/bilateral (legs > arms)**: Both legs more affected than arms. Most common type in preterm infants. Most common MRI finding: periventricular leukomalacia (PVL).
- **Quadriplegia/bilateral (all four limbs)**: All four limbs severely affected; often trunk/oromotor involved. GMFCS IV-V typically. Often term infants with severe HIE.
- **Monoplegia**: Single limb (rare, often actually mild hemiplegia).
- **Triplegia**: Three limbs (rare, often actually asymmetric quadriplegia).

### Rodda Gait Classification (for Hemiplegic CP)
- Rodda J, Graham HK. Classification of gait patterns in spastic hemiplegia and spastic diplegia: a basis for a management algorithm. Eur J Neurol. 2001;8(Suppl 5):98-108.
- **[BOARD]** Rodda Classification for Spastic Hemiplegia:

| Type | Ankle | Knee | Hip | Key Feature |
|------|-------|------|-----|-------------|
| **Type I: Drop Foot** | Dorsiflexion deficit in swing (foot drop) | Normal or recurvatum | Normal | Isolated foot drop; AFO sufficient |
| **Type II: True Equinus** | Equinus throughout stance & swing | Normal or recurvatum | Normal | Gastrocnemius/soleus spasticity; AFO + possible botox/surgery |
| **Type III: Equinus + Knee** | Equinus | FLEXION in stance | Flexion | Tight hamstrings + gastroc; Multi-level |
| **Type IV: Equinus + Knee + Hip** | Equinus | Flexion | Flexion + internal rotation | Multi-level involvement; needs SEMLS |

- **[BOARD]** Rodda Classification for Spastic Diplegia:

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **True Equinus** | Bilateral equinus, knees extended or recurvatum |
| **Jump Gait** | Bilateral equinus, knee flexion, hip flexion, anterior pelvic tilt -- classic diplegic gait |
| **Apparent Equinus** | Normal ankle ROM but equinus appearance due to excessive knee/hip flexion (functionally equinus without true plantarflexion contracture) |
| **Crouch Gait** | Excessive dorsiflexion, knee flexion, hip flexion -- calcaneal gait. Often develops after overlengthened heel cords or in adolescence |

**[BOARD]** Crouch gait is often iatrogenic (from prior overlengthening of Achilles tendon or isolated hamstring lengthening). Can also develop with growth/adolescence in GMFCS III-IV. Crouch gait leads to patella alta, patellofemoral pain, and eventually patellar fracture if not addressed.

## 6. SPASTICITY MANAGEMENT

### Spasticity Management Ladder (Evidence-Based)
**[BOARD]** Progressive approach:

**Step 1: Rehabilitation/Conservative**
- Stretching, positioning, serial casting
- Orthotics (AFOs, KAFOs)
- Therapeutic exercise, strengthening
- Adaptive equipment and seating

**Step 2: Focal Spasticity -- Chemodenervation**
- **Botulinum toxin A (onabotulinumtoxinA -- Botox; abobotulinumtoxinA -- Dysport)**
  - **[BOARD]** Mechanism: Blocks presynaptic acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junction. SNARE protein cleavage (cleaves SNAP-25).
  - Onset: 24-72 hours; peak effect: 2-6 weeks; duration: 3-6 months.
  - **[BOARD]** Botox dosing (onabotulinumtoxinA):
    - Individual muscle: 1-6 U/kg per muscle
    - Total body dose: **Maximum 400 U total or 12-16 U/kg** (do not exceed)
    - Large muscles (gastroc/soleus): 3-6 U/kg per side
    - Small muscles (tibialis posterior): 1-2 U/kg
    - Upper limb (biceps, pronator teres, FCU): 1-2 U/kg per muscle
  - **[BOARD]** Dysport (abobotulinumtoxinA): Dosing approximately 2.5-3:1 ratio to Botox (i.e., 2.5-3 units Dysport ~ 1 unit Botox). NOT interchangeable.
  - Minimum injection interval: 3 months (typically q3-6 months).
  - **[BOARD]** Adverse effects: Weakness (desired), local pain, rare dysphagia (especially with neck injections), rare systemic spread (FDA black box warning in pediatrics), flu-like symptoms.
  - Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity, aminoglycoside antibiotics (potentiate effect), myasthenia gravis, infection at injection site.
  - Guidance: EMG, electrical stimulation, or ultrasound for localization.

- **Phenol/alcohol nerve blocks**
  - **[BOARD]** Phenol (5-7% aqueous): Motor point or nerve block. Onset: Minutes. Duration: 3-12 months (some up to 18 months).
  - Mechanism: Protein denaturation, axonal destruction.
  - Best for: Large muscles, mixed nerves (obturator nerve block for hip adductor spasticity).
  - **[BOARD]** Advantages over botox: Longer lasting, cheaper, immediate onset, no systemic dose ceiling.
  - **[BOARD]** Disadvantages: Painful injection, risk of dysesthesias if injected near sensory nerve (avoid mixed sensory-motor nerves for peripheral blocks; obturator nerve is predominantly motor, so preferred target). Alcohol is less predictable than phenol.
  - Classic use: Obturator nerve block for hip adductor spasticity (obturator nerve is primarily motor = lower dysesthesia risk).

**Step 3: Generalized/Regional Spasticity -- Systemic Medications**
- **Oral baclofen**: GABA-B agonist. Dose: 5-80 mg/day in divided doses. Side effects: Sedation, weakness, withdrawal seizures.
- **Diazepam**: GABA-A agonist. Side effects: Sedation, dependence, tolerance. Good for short-term use, nighttime.
- **Dantrolene sodium**: Acts peripherally on muscle (inhibits calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum). **[BOARD]** Only antispasticity medication that acts PERIPHERALLY (at muscle level). Side effects: Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs), weakness. Dose: 0.5-3 mg/kg/dose TID-QID.
- **Tizanidine**: Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist (acts centrally at spinal cord). Side effects: Sedation, dry mouth, hepatotoxicity.
- **[BOARD]** Board favorite: Dantrolene = peripheral mechanism. Baclofen = central (GABA-B). Diazepam = central (GABA-A). Tizanidine = central (alpha-2).

**Step 4: Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump**
- **[BOARD]** Mechanism: Delivers baclofen directly into intrathecal space via programmable pump (implanted subcutaneously in abdomen), catheter tip usually at T10-L1.
- **[BOARD]** Indications: Generalized spasticity (GMFCS IV-V most common), failed oral medications, dystonia.
- Advantages: 100-1000x lower dose than oral; fewer systemic side effects; adjustable.
- Typical dose: 50-1000 mcg/day (start with test dose of 25-100 mcg via lumbar puncture).
- **[BOARD]** ITB WITHDRAWAL syndrome:
  - Cause: Catheter kink/disconnect, pump failure, empty reservoir, programming error
  - Symptoms: Rebound spasticity, fever, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, pruritis, rhabdomyolysis, multi-organ failure, DEATH
  - **Mimics malignant hyperthermia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, and autonomic dysreflexia**
  - Treatment: Restore ITB delivery; oral/IV/rectal baclofen, benzodiazepines, cyproheptadine. ICU admission. DO NOT use dantrolene as first line (unlike malignant hyperthermia).
  - **[BOARD]** ITB withdrawal is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY with potential mortality.
- **[BOARD]** ITB OVERDOSE:
  - Cause: Programming error, pump malfunction, catheter migration
  - Symptoms: Drowsiness, respiratory depression, hypotonia, coma, bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia
  - Treatment: Supportive (airway management, ventilation); withdraw CSF if safe; empty/turn off pump; physostigmine (rarely used)
  - **[BOARD]** Key distinction: Withdrawal = hyper-symptoms (fever, spasticity, tachycardia); Overdose = hypo-symptoms (hypotension, hypothermia, respiratory depression, flaccidity).

**Step 5: Orthopedic Surgery**
- Single-event multilevel surgery (SEMLS)
- Tendon lengthening (percutaneous, Z-lengthening, fractional lengthening)
- Tendon transfers (rectus femoris transfer to sartorius/gracilis for stiff knee gait)
- Osteotomies (femoral derotation, tibial derotation, pelvic -- Dega, Pemberton, San Diego)
- Joint stabilization (subtalar arthrodesis, triple arthrodesis)
- Guided growth (hemiepiphysiodesis for valgus/varus correction)

**Step 6: Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR)** -- see above

## 7. MRI FINDINGS IN CP

**[BOARD]** MRI is abnormal in ~86-90% of children with CP:

| MRI Finding | Percentage | CP Type Association | Timing |
|-------------|------------|--------------------| -------|
| **White matter injury/PVL** | ~45% | Spastic diplegia | Preterm (24-34 weeks) |
| **Grey matter/basal ganglia injury** | ~13% | Dyskinetic, spastic quadriplegia | Term, acute asphyxia |
| **Cortical/subcortical lesions** | ~10% | Variable | Term |
| **Brain malformations** | ~11% | Variable (often ataxic or mixed) | 1st-2nd trimester |
| **Focal infarcts** | ~7% | Spastic hemiplegia | Term, MCA territory |
| **Normal MRI** | ~10-14% | More common in mild CP, ataxia | Any |

- **[BOARD]** PVL (periventricular leukomalacia): Most common finding in preterm CP. Bilateral white matter injury near lateral ventricles. Due to vulnerability of premyelinating oligodendrocytes (24-34 weeks). Results in spastic diplegia (corticospinal tracts from leg areas run close to ventricles).
- **[BOARD]** Term HIE pattern: Basal ganglia/thalamus injury (watershed in severe, parasagittal in moderate) -- typically results in dystonic or mixed CP.

## 8. HIP SURVEILLANCE AND MANAGEMENT

### Hip Displacement in CP
- **[BOARD]** Hip displacement risk by GMFCS level:
  - GMFCS I: ~1%
  - GMFCS II: ~15%
  - GMFCS III: ~40%
  - GMFCS IV: ~70%
  - GMFCS V: ~90%
- Hip displacement in CP is due to: Spastic/overactive hip adductors and flexors, femoral anteversion, acetabular dysplasia, lack of weight bearing.
- **[BOARD]** Natural history (without intervention): Progressive subluxation -> dislocation -> pain -> windswept deformity -> difficulty with perineal hygiene and sitting.

### Management by Migration Percentage
**[BOARD]**:
- MP <30%: Observe, therapy, positioning
- MP 30-40%: Botulinum toxin to hip adductors/flexors + bracing (hip abduction orthosis)
- MP 40-50%: Soft tissue surgery (adductor/psoas release)
- MP >50%: Bony reconstruction (femoral varus derotation osteotomy +/- pelvic osteotomy -- Dega or San Diego procedure)
- Established dislocation with pain: Salvage procedures (proximal femoral resection/interposition arthroplasty, rarely total hip arthroplasty)

## 9. ORTHOTIC MANAGEMENT

### AFO Types in CP
**[BOARD]**:
- **Solid AFO**: Fixed ankle; prevents equinus; used for GMFCS III-V or severe spasticity. Limits push-off.
- **Hinged AFO (articulated)**: Allows dorsiflexion, blocks plantarflexion. Good for GMFCS I-II with equinus tendency. Allows third rocker (push-off) in gait.
- **Posterior leaf spring (PLS) AFO**: Flexible posterior strut; assists dorsiflexion in swing phase. For mild drop foot (GMFCS I-II).
- **Ground reaction AFO (GRAFO)/Floor reaction orthosis**: Rigid, extends up anterior tibia; creates extension moment at knee. **[BOARD]** Used for CROUCH GAIT (knee flexion in stance). Contraindicated in equinus or fixed knee flexion contracture.
- **DAFO (Dynamic Ankle-Foot Orthosis)**: Thin, flexible, supramalleolar or full foot plate. Good for low tone or mild spasticity. Provides proprioceptive input.
- **SMO (Supramalleolar Orthosis)**: Below malleoli; controls hindfoot valgus/varus; no ankle motion restriction. Used in hypotonia or mild CP.
- **KAFO (Knee-Ankle-Foot Orthosis)**: For quadriceps weakness or severe crouch gait. Less commonly used in CP.

## 10. ADDITIONAL BOARD-RELEVANT FACTS (TAGGED)

**[BOARD]** CP definition (Rosenbaum 2007): "A group of permanent disorders of the development of movement and posture, causing activity limitation, that are attributed to non-progressive disturbances that occurred in the developing fetal or infant brain."

**[BOARD]** CP is non-progressive brain lesion BUT the clinical manifestations CHANGE over time (muscle contractures develop, scoliosis progresses, hip displacement occurs).

**[BOARD]** Spasticity is velocity-dependent increase in muscle tone with hyperactive stretch reflexes (Lance definition). Distinguished from rigidity (velocity-independent, lead-pipe), dystonia (sustained involuntary contractions causing twisting), and spasms.

**[BOARD]** Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS): 0, 1, 1+, 2, 3, 4. Most commonly used clinical spasticity scale. Limitations: Ordinal, poor inter-rater reliability, does not distinguish spasticity from contracture.

**[BOARD]** Tardieu Scale: Better differentiation of spasticity from contracture than MAS. Measures angle of catch at fast velocity (R1) vs. passive ROM at slow velocity (R2). Spasticity = R2 - R1 (dynamic component).

**[BOARD]** Modified Tardieu: Quality of muscle reaction rated 0-5. R1 (angle of catch at fast velocity), R2 (passive ROM at slow velocity). Dynamic component = R2 - R1. If R2-R1 is large = spasticity; if R2-R1 is small = contracture.

**[BOARD]** Commonly tested muscle groups in CP for spasticity/contracture:
- Hip: Adductors (gracilis, adductor longus), hip flexors (iliopsoas), medial hamstrings
- Knee: Hamstrings, rectus femoris (stiff knee in swing)
- Ankle: Gastrocnemius (crosses knee -- test with knee extended), soleus (does not cross knee -- test with knee flexed). If equinus present with knee EXTENDED but corrects with knee FLEXED = gastrocnemius is tight (Silfverskiold test positive).

**[BOARD]** Silfverskiold test: Ankle dorsiflexion measured with knee extended vs. flexed.
- DF improves with knee flexion = gastrocnemius tightness (2-joint muscle)
- DF does NOT improve with knee flexion = soleus tightness (or both)
- Board classic: Helps determine surgical target (gastroc recession vs. Achilles lengthening).

**[BOARD]** Phelps test (confused equinus): Assess ankle DF with knee flexed AND hip extended to rule out rectus femoris contribution.

**[BOARD]** Ely test (Duncan-Ely): Prone, flex knee rapidly -- if hip flexes off table = positive = rectus femoris spasticity/contracture. Important for identifying stiff-knee gait cause.

**[BOARD]** Thomas test: Hip flexion contracture. Supine, flex opposite hip to flatten lumbar lordosis -- if tested hip flexes off table = positive.

**[BOARD]** Growing rods in CP scoliosis: Used in young children (typically <10 years) with progressive neuromuscular scoliosis when curve >40-50 degrees but skeletally immature. Magnetically controlled growing rods (MAGEC) can be lengthened non-invasively. Definitive posterior spinal fusion typically at skeletal maturity or when curve progresses despite growing rods.

**[BOARD]** Scoliosis in CP:
- Prevalence: ~60-70% of non-ambulatory CP (GMFCS IV-V)
- Curve patterns: Typically long C-shaped thoracolumbar curves (unlike idiopathic scoliosis which is S-shaped)
- Pelvic obliquity common
- Bracing: May slow progression but does NOT prevent surgery in neuromuscular scoliosis
- Indications for fusion: >40-50 degrees progressive curve, functional decline, seating difficulty, pain
- Instrumentation: Typically posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation to pelvis (unit rod or pelvic fixation)

**[BOARD]** Botulinum toxin is NOT FDA-approved for pediatric spasticity in the US, but widely used off-label. Only FDA-approved indications in children: Cervical dystonia (adults), blepharospasm, strabismus, chronic migraine (adults). Pediatric use is evidence-based but off-label.

**[BOARD]** Stiff knee gait (rectus femoris overactivity in swing): Reduced peak knee flexion in swing phase. Evaluated with Duncan-Ely test and instrumented gait analysis (EMG showing rectus femoris activity during swing). Treatment: Rectus femoris transfer (to sartorius, gracilis, or iliotibial band).

**[BOARD]** Lever arm dysfunction: Skeletal malalignment (femoral anteversion, external tibial torsion) that reduces efficiency of muscles acting across joints. Major contributor to gait abnormalities in CP. Corrected by derotation osteotomies.

**[BOARD]** 3D instrumented gait analysis is the gold standard for surgical decision-making in ambulatory CP (GMFCS I-III) before SEMLS.
