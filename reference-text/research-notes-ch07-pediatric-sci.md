# Chapter 7: Pediatric Spinal Cord Injury & SCIWORA — Research Notes

---

## SECTION 1: EPIDEMIOLOGY OF PEDIATRIC SCI

### 1.1 Incidence & Demographics

- **Pediatric SCI** (age <16-18 years, depending on registry): Comprises approximately 3-5% of all spinal cord injuries; much less common than adult SCI
- **US incidence**: Approximately 1,200-1,400 new pediatric SCIs per year (varies by definition and age cutoff)
- **NSCISC (National Spinal Cord Injury Statistical Center)** data:
  - Pediatric cases represent ~3-5% of the NSCISC database
  - Age distribution: Bimodal — peak in ages 0-4 (often cervical, upper) and peak in adolescence 15-18 (more similar to adult patterns)
  - Sex: Male predominance — approximately 60-70% male overall; male predominance increases with age (80%+ in adolescents)
- **Mortality**: In-hospital mortality ~10-15% for pediatric SCI; higher in very young children and complete injuries
- **Reference**: Parent S, Mac-Thiong JM, Roy-Beaudry M, et al. Spinal cord injury in the pediatric population: a systematic review of the literature. Journal of Neurotrauma. 2011;28(8):1515-1524.

### 1.2 Mechanisms by Age Group

- **Children <8 years**: Motor vehicle accidents (passenger, pedestrian), falls, non-accidental trauma (child abuse — important cause in infants), sports/recreation
- **Children 8-14 years**: Motor vehicle accidents, sports injuries (diving, football, gymnastics, trampoline), bicycle accidents
- **Adolescents 15-18 years**: Motor vehicle accidents (driving), sports, violence (gunshot wounds), diving
- **Birth injuries**: Rare but can occur with difficult deliveries (forceps, breech); may cause upper cervical cord injury
- **Board pearl**: Motor vehicle accidents are the most common cause of pediatric SCI across all age groups; however, the proportion due to sports and violence increases with age

### 1.3 Injury Level Distribution by Age

**Critical age-dependent pattern — high-yield board topic:**

#### Children <8 Years — Upper Cervical Predominance
- **C1-C4 injuries** are disproportionately common in young children
- **Reasons** (anatomic factors):
  - **Large head-to-body ratio**: Head is proportionally heavier (25% of body weight at birth vs. 6% in adults), creating higher fulcrum/leverage point at upper cervical spine
  - **Ligamentous laxity**: Greater ligamentous elasticity in children; allows more spinal column displacement without fracture (predisposes to SCIWORA)
  - **Horizontal facet joint orientation**: More horizontal facets (especially C1-C3) allow greater sliding/translation movement
  - **Underdeveloped uncinate processes**: Less bony constraint on lateral flexion/translation
  - **Wedge-shaped vertebral bodies**: Anterior wedging promotes forward sliding
  - **Immature paraspinal musculature**: Less muscular support/protection
  - **Ossification centers**: Multiple ossification centers can be confused with fractures on imaging
- **Fulcrum of cervical motion**: In young children, the fulcrum of cervical motion is higher (C2-C3 level) compared to adults (C5-C6); this explains the higher rate of upper cervical injuries

#### Children ≥8 Years — Lower Cervical / Adult Pattern
- **C5-C7 injuries** become more common
- **Reasons**: As the child grows, head-to-body ratio normalizes, facets become more vertically oriented, ossification progresses, musculature develops — spinal biomechanics approach adult pattern
- **Transition point**: Approximately age 8-10 years — the cervical spine anatomy transitions to adult-like characteristics
- **Reference**: Pang D, Wilberger JE. Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormalities in children. Journal of Neurosurgery. 1982;57(1):114-129.

### 1.4 Injury Completeness

- **Higher rate of complete injuries** (ASIA A) in children compared to adults in some series, particularly in young children with upper cervical injuries
- **However**, higher rate of SCIWORA (which may have better prognosis) offsets this somewhat
- **ASIA classification**: Same International Standards for Neurological Classification of Spinal Cord Injury (ISNCSCI) used in children as adults, though assessment can be challenging in young/preverbal children

## SECTION 2: SCIWORA (SPINAL CORD INJURY WITHOUT RADIOGRAPHIC ABNORMALITY)

### 2.1 Definition & History

- **Definition**: Objective signs of myelopathy (spinal cord dysfunction) following trauma with NO evidence of fracture, dislocation, or ligamentous instability on plain radiographs AND CT scan
- **Original description**: Pang D, Wilberger JE. Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormalities in children. Journal of Neurosurgery. 1982;57(1):114-129.
  - Landmark paper describing 24 children with traumatic myelopathy but normal radiographic studies
  - Coined the term "SCIWORA"
  - Proposed that elastic deformation of the pediatric spinal column (stretching without fracture) could cause spinal cord damage
- **Prevalence in pediatric SCI**: SCIWORA accounts for approximately 6-19% of pediatric SCIs (up to 30-60% in some series of young children)
- **Much more common in children than adults**: Due to pediatric spinal column characteristics described above

### 2.2 Pang Classification of SCIWORA

**Pang subsequently refined the classification based on MRI findings.**

- **Reference**: Pang D. Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality in children, 2 decades later. Neurosurgery. 2004;55(6):1325-1342.

#### Classification Based on MRI

| Type | MRI Finding | Prognosis |
|------|------------|-----------|
| **Type I** | Major hemorrhage (large intramedullary hemorrhage/disruption) | Poor — typically complete SCI; minimal recovery expected |
| **Type II** | Minor hemorrhage (small/focal hemorrhage) | Intermediate — variable recovery |
| **Type III** | Edema only (no hemorrhage, cord edema/signal change) | Good — significant recovery expected |
| **Type IV** | Normal MRI (true SCIWORA — cord signal completely normal) | Best — generally good recovery; may have transient deficits |

**Board-relevant interpretation:**
- **Hemorrhage on MRI = worse prognosis**
- **Edema-only or normal MRI = better prognosis**
- **The MRI pattern is the strongest prognostic indicator in SCIWORA**
- **True SCIWORA (normal MRI) may represent transient cord concussion/neuropraxia** — the cord was transiently deformed but recovered structurally

### 2.3 Pathophysiology of SCIWORA

- **Elastic deformation**: The pediatric spinal column can stretch up to 2 inches (5 cm) without disruption, while the spinal cord can tolerate only ~0.25 inches (6 mm) of stretch before injury. This discrepancy allows the spinal column to deform, injure the cord, and then recoil back to normal alignment — hence, normal radiographic appearance.
- **Mechanisms**: Hyperflexion, hyperextension, longitudinal distraction, or a combination
- **Vascular injury**: Ischemia from transient compression or stretching of spinal cord vasculature may contribute
- **Ligamentous/disc injury**: While plain radiographs and CT are normal, MRI may show disc herniation, ligamentous injury, or epidural hematoma that contributed to cord compression — some authors argue these cases should be reclassified as "SCI with radiographic abnormality on MRI" (SCIWORET — SCI without radiographic evidence of trauma, referring only to plain film/CT)

### 2.4 Delayed Neurological Deficits

- **Up to 48 hours**: Neurological deficits may develop or worsen up to 48 hours after initial injury — this is a unique and critical feature of SCIWORA in children. **High-yield board fact.**
- **Mechanism**: Likely secondary injury cascade — edema progression, ischemia, reperfusion injury, delayed vascular compromise
- **Clinical implication**: Children with transient neurological symptoms after spinal trauma should be immobilized, observed for at least 48 hours, and undergo MRI even if initial symptoms resolve
- **Warning symptoms before delayed deterioration**: Some children report paresthesias, transient weakness, or numbness that resolves before frank myelopathy develops hours later
- **Reference**: Pang D, Pollack IF. Spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality in children — the SCIWORA syndrome. Journal of Trauma. 1989;29(5):654-664.

### 2.5 Evaluation of Suspected SCIWORA

1. **Plain radiographs**: Normal by definition (but should be obtained to rule out fracture/dislocation)
2. **CT spine**: Normal for bone (rules out subtle fractures); does not evaluate cord
3. **MRI spine**: **Essential** — gold standard for evaluating spinal cord parenchyma; should be obtained in ALL cases of pediatric spinal trauma with neurological symptoms, even if CT is normal
   - Findings: Cord edema (T2 hyperintensity), hemorrhage (T1/T2 signal changes), cord transection, disc herniation, epidural hematoma, ligamentous injury
   - **Normal MRI does not exclude SCIWORA** — true SCIWORA with normal MRI can occur
4. **Flexion-extension radiographs**: May be obtained AFTER acute phase (not acutely) to assess for ligamentous instability; require patient cooperation (not useful in young children or acute setting)
5. **Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs)**: May show abnormalities even when MRI is normal; can be useful in prognostication

### 2.6 Management of SCIWORA

- **Immobilization**: External cervical immobilization for 8-12 weeks recommended by some authors (Pang protocol); rigid cervical collar
- **Rationale for immobilization**: Risk of recurrent SCIWORA; children who have had one episode may be at risk for repeat injury if the ligamentous structures are stretched
- **Activity restriction**: Contact sports and high-risk activities restricted for 6-12 months after injury (varies by center and severity)
- **Recurrent SCIWORA**: Reported in children who resume activity too early; immobilization protocol designed to prevent recurrence
- **Surgical intervention**: Generally NOT indicated unless MRI shows compressive pathology (disc herniation, epidural hematoma) requiring decompression
- **Rehabilitation**: Based on neurological deficit level — same principles as other pediatric SCI

### 2.7 Prognosis

- **Overall**: Better than SCI with radiographic abnormality; but variable depending on MRI pattern
- **Complete injuries (ASIA A at presentation)**: Poor recovery even in SCIWORA
- **Incomplete injuries with edema-only on MRI**: >90% show significant neurological recovery
- **Normal MRI**: Best prognosis — most recover completely or near-completely
- **Hemorrhagic MRI lesions**: Worst prognosis among SCIWORA — similar to SCI with fracture/dislocation

## SECTION 3: ATLANTOAXIAL INSTABILITY (AAI)

### 3.1 Overview

- **Definition**: Excessive movement at the C1-C2 (atlantoaxial) joint; atlantodental interval (ADI) >4.5-5 mm on lateral flexion-extension radiographs in children (normal ADI <3 mm in adults, <4.5 mm in children)
- **Significance**: Risk of spinal cord compression at the craniocervical junction; catastrophic neurological injury or death from trivial trauma

### 3.2 Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

- **Prevalence of AAI in Down syndrome**: ~10-30% have radiographic AAI (increased ADI); only ~1-2% have symptomatic AAI (myelopathy)
- **Pathophysiology**: Generalized ligamentous laxity (affecting transverse ligament of atlas that stabilizes the dens); os odontoideum (hypoplastic/unfused dens) may coexist
- **Screening**: Special Olympics requires cervical spine radiographs (lateral flexion-extension views) for participation clearance; however, **AAP (2011)** states that lateral cervical spine radiographs have poor predictive value for identifying those at risk for symptomatic AAI; screening radiographs are no longer routinely recommended by AAP but remain required by Special Olympics
- **Reference**: American Academy of Pediatrics Committee on Genetics. Health supervision for children with Down syndrome. Pediatrics. 2011;128(2):393-406.
- **Clinical assessment**: Monitor for signs of myelopathy — new-onset neck pain, torticollis, gait deterioration, spasticity, hyperreflexia, clonus, bowel/bladder changes, changes in hand function
- **Management of symptomatic AAI**: Posterior C1-C2 fusion; activity restriction for asymptomatic AAI is debated
- **Board pearl**: Radiographic AAI is common in Down syndrome (10-30%) but symptomatic cord compression is rare (~1-2%); clinical signs of myelopathy are more important than radiographic measurements

### 3.3 Morquio Syndrome (MPS IV)

- **Mucopolysaccharidosis type IV**: Autosomal recessive lysosomal storage disorder
- **Odontoid hypoplasia**: Very common (nearly universal); dens is hypoplastic or absent, combined with ligamentous laxity
- **AAI/Craniocervical instability**: Extremely high risk; major source of morbidity and mortality
- **Cervical myelopathy**: Common; insidious onset; progressive weakness, spasticity, respiratory compromise
- **Management**: Prophylactic posterior cervical fusion often recommended before symptomatic cord compression develops; perioperative airway management is challenging due to short neck, chest wall deformity, tracheal abnormalities
- **Anesthesia risk**: High risk of cervical cord injury during intubation — fiber-optic intubation recommended; manual in-line stabilization
- **Reference**: Solanki GA, Martin KW, Gower DJ, et al. Defining the natural history of MPS IVA: the development and use of a clinical assessment algorithm. Journal of Inherited Metabolic Disease. 2013;36(2):309-318.

### 3.4 Other Conditions with AAI Risk

- **Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)**: Cervical spine involvement in ~30-60% of polyarticular JIA; atlantoaxial subluxation possible
- **Os odontoideum**: Congenital or acquired (post-fracture) unfused/hypoplastic dens; associated with C1-C2 instability
- **Klippel-Feil syndrome**: Congenital cervical vertebral fusion; instability at adjacent unfused segments
- **Skeletal dysplasias**: Various forms may have cervical spine instability (spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, diastrophic dysplasia)
- **Ehlers-Danlos syndrome**: Generalized ligamentous laxity; cervical instability possible
- **Grisel syndrome**: Non-traumatic atlantoaxial subluxation following upper respiratory infection or pharyngeal surgery (due to inflammation-related ligamentous laxity); typically in children

## SECTION 4: SCOLIOSIS IN PEDIATRIC SCI

### 4.1 Epidemiology

- **Scoliosis develops in nearly 100% of children who sustain SCI before skeletal maturity (age <10 years)** — **critical board fact**
- **Risk inversely proportional to age at injury**: Younger age = higher risk and greater severity
  - Injury before age 10: ~97-100% develop scoliosis
  - Injury at age 10-15: ~50-80%
  - Injury after skeletal maturity: ~5-10% (similar to adult SCI)
- **Type**: Paralytic neuromuscular scoliosis — typically long C-curve, collapsing pattern; different from idiopathic scoliosis
- **Contributing factors**: Muscle paralysis/imbalance, truncal asymmetry, growth asymmetry, sitting posture, loss of trunk support
- **Reference**: Dearolf WW, Betz RR, Vogel LC, et al. Scoliosis in pediatric spinal cord-injured patients. Journal of Pediatric Orthopedics. 1990;10(2):214-218.

### 4.2 Monitoring & Management

- **Monitoring**: Clinical examination and standing/sitting radiographs at least annually during growth
- **Bracing**: Limited effectiveness for neuromuscular scoliosis; may slow progression and improve sitting posture; TLSO (thoracolumbosacral orthosis); does NOT prevent need for surgery in most cases
- **Surgical indications**: Progressive curve >40-50 degrees; loss of sitting balance; respiratory compromise from thoracic deformity; skin breakdown from pelvic obliquity
- **Surgical approach**: Posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation; fusion from upper thoracic spine to pelvis typically required; longer constructs than idiopathic scoliosis
- **Special considerations**: High complication rate in SCI patients — pressure ulcers from hardware, delayed healing, osteoporosis (poor bone quality for instrumentation), respiratory compromise, perioperative autonomic instability
- **Growing rods**: May be used in very young children to allow continued spinal growth before definitive fusion; requires serial operative lengthening every 6-12 months; magnetically controlled growing rods (MAGEC) allow office-based lengthening

### 4.3 Pelvic Obliquity

- **Common with scoliosis in SCI**: Pelvic tilt associated with lumbar/thoracolumbar curves
- **Consequences**: Uneven weight distribution in wheelchair → pressure ulcers; sitting imbalance; hip subluxation/dislocation
- **Management**: Wheelchair seating modifications, surgical correction (fusion to pelvis)

## SECTION 5: NEUROGENIC BLADDER IN PEDIATRIC SCI

### 5.1 Pathophysiology

- **Depends on level of injury**:
  - **Suprasacral lesions** (above S2-S4 conus medullaris): Upper motor neuron (UMN) bladder — detrusor hyperreflexia (spastic bladder), detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD); high intravesical pressures → risk of upper tract damage (hydronephrosis, reflux, renal failure)
  - **Sacral/conus/cauda equina lesions**: Lower motor neuron (LMN) bladder — areflexic bladder; large capacity, low pressure; stress incontinence; may have denervated sphincter (decreased outlet resistance)
  - **Mixed patterns**: Common, especially with incomplete injuries

### 5.2 Evaluation

- **Urodynamic studies (UDS)**: Gold standard; assess detrusor pressure, capacity, compliance, sphincter function, presence of DSD; should be performed after spinal shock resolves and periodically thereafter
- **Renal ultrasound**: Screen for hydronephrosis, renal size, bladder wall thickening
- **Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG)**: Evaluate for vesicoureteral reflux
- **Serum creatinine/BUN**: Renal function monitoring
- **Frequency**: UDS typically performed annually or semi-annually; renal ultrasound annually; more frequently if abnormalities detected

### 5.3 Management

- **Clean intermittent catheterization (CIC)**: Mainstay of neurogenic bladder management in pediatric SCI; performed every 4-6 hours; teaches independence when developmentally appropriate (typically by age 6-8 with training); family/caregiver performs in younger children
- **Anticholinergic medications**: Oxybutynin (most commonly used in pediatrics); reduces detrusor overactivity, increases bladder capacity, reduces pressure; side effects include dry mouth, constipation, cognitive effects, heat intolerance
- **Onabotulinum toxin A (Botox) injection**: Intradetrusor injection for refractory detrusor overactivity; increasing pediatric evidence and use
- **Alpha-blockers**: May help with bladder outlet obstruction/DSD (tamsulosin, terazosin); less commonly used in young children
- **Augmentation cystoplasty**: Surgical bladder augmentation using intestinal segment (ileum most common) for refractory low-compliance/high-pressure bladder; increases capacity, reduces pressure; complications include mucus production, metabolic acidosis, bladder stones, perforation risk
- **Continent catheterizable channel (Mitrofanoff procedure)**: Appendicovesicostomy — appendix used to create catheterizable channel from bladder to umbilicus or abdomen; allows continent catheterization without urethral access; important for wheelchair users (easier access than urethral CIC)
- **Goal**: Maintain low intravesical pressures to preserve upper urinary tract function; achieve social continence
- **Board pearl**: The primary goal of neurogenic bladder management is RENAL PRESERVATION — preventing upper tract damage from high pressures

### 5.4 Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)

- **Very common** in neurogenic bladder; major source of morbidity
- **Distinguish**: Asymptomatic bacteriuria (common, generally NOT treated in CIC patients) vs. symptomatic UTI (requires treatment)
- **Symptoms of UTI in SCI**: Cloudy/malodorous urine, increased spasticity, autonomic dysreflexia, new incontinence, fever, malaise — fever may be absent in SCI patients below T6
- **Prevention**: Adequate fluid intake, regular CIC schedule, good technique, anticholinergic medications to maintain low pressures
- **Prophylactic antibiotics**: Generally NOT recommended for routine use due to resistance development; may be considered in specific situations

## SECTION 6: NEUROGENIC BOWEL IN PEDIATRIC SCI

### 6.1 Types

- **UMN bowel** (supraconal lesions): Increased rectal tone, preserved reflex activity, spastic external anal sphincter; constipation is primary problem; digital stimulation triggers reflex evacuation
- **LMN bowel** (conus/cauda equina lesions): Decreased rectal tone, areflexic, lax external sphincter; risk of incontinence from stool sitting above weak sphincter; constipation and incontinence both problematic

### 6.2 Management

- **Timed bowel program**: Regular schedule (typically every day or every other day, same time); consistency is key
- **Dietary management**: Adequate fiber, fluid intake
- **Medications**: Stool softeners (docusate), osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol/MiraLAX), stimulant laxatives (bisacodyl/senna), suppositories (bisacodyl, glycerin)
- **Digital stimulation**: For UMN bowel — stimulates rectocolic reflex; timed after meal to use gastrocolic reflex
- **Transanal irrigation (TAI)**: Peristeen or similar system — retrograde colonic irrigation; effective for both constipation and incontinence; growing evidence in pediatric SCI
- **Antegrade continence enema (ACE/Malone procedure)**: Appendicostomy creating antegrade access to cecum; allows flush enemas from cecum to rectum; very effective for achieving social continence; commonly used in pediatric SCI
- **Goal**: Predictable, regular evacuation; social continence; prevent constipation/impaction

## SECTION 7: AUTONOMIC DYSREFLEXIA (AD) IN CHILDREN

### 7.1 Definition & Pathophysiology

- **Autonomic dysreflexia**: Potentially life-threatening exaggerated sympathetic response to noxious stimuli below the level of SCI; occurs in injuries at **T6 and above** (above the splanchnic outflow)
- **Pathophysiology**: Noxious stimulus below injury level → afferent signals enter spinal cord → massive uninhibited sympathetic discharge below level of lesion (vasoconstriction, hypertension) → compensatory parasympathetic response ABOVE level of lesion (baroreceptor-mediated vagal stimulation → bradycardia, vasodilation above lesion) → BUT descending inhibitory signals cannot cross the lesion to modulate sympathetic outflow below → hypertensive crisis persists until stimulus is removed

### 7.2 Clinical Features

- **Hypertension**: Systolic BP >20-40 mmHg above baseline (note: baseline BP in children with SCI is typically LOW); in young children, "normal" BP may already be elevated for their age
- **Board pearl**: Know normal BP ranges for age — what appears "normal" on a single reading may actually be significantly elevated for a child with high-level SCI who has low baseline BP
- **Symptoms above lesion level**: Headache (often severe, pounding), facial flushing, nasal congestion, diaphoresis (sweating), blurred vision
- **Below lesion level**: Pallor, piloerection ("goose bumps"), cool skin
- **Bradycardia**: From baroreceptor-mediated vagal response (but tachycardia can also occur)
- **Complications if untreated**: Seizures, intracranial hemorrhage, retinal hemorrhage, pulmonary edema, cardiac arrhythmia, death

### 7.3 Common Triggers

- **Bladder distension**: Most common trigger (~75-85% of episodes); blocked catheter, full bladder, UTI, bladder spasm, urodynamic testing
- **Bowel distension**: Constipation, impaction, rectal distension during bowel program; second most common trigger
- **Skin**: Pressure ulcers, ingrown toenails, tight clothing/shoes, burns, skin breakdown
- **Musculoskeletal**: Fractures (may be occult in insensate limbs), heterotopic ossification
- **Other**: Menstruation, labor/delivery (adolescents), DVT, surgical procedures, temperature extremes

### 7.4 Management of Acute AD Episode

1. **Sit the patient upright** (to lower BP via orthostatic effect); dangle legs if possible
2. **Loosen all clothing, straps, abdominal binders**
3. **Identify and remove the trigger**:
   - Check bladder first: If catheterized, check for kinks/blockage; irrigate or replace catheter; if no catheter, straight catheterize (use lidocaine jelly)
   - Check bowel: Digital rectal exam (use lidocaine jelly first to prevent worsening AD); disimpact gently if needed
   - Check skin: Remove any irritants, relieve pressure
4. **Monitor BP every 2-5 minutes**
5. **If BP remains elevated after trigger identification/removal**:
   - Pharmacological treatment: Nifedipine (sublingual/oral — calcium channel blocker; rapid onset); nitropaste/nitroglycerin (topical — can be removed when BP normalizes); hydralazine; prazosin
   - Avoid beta-blockers as first-line (risk of unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction)
6. **Emergency**: If severe hypertension unresponsive to above measures — treat as hypertensive emergency; ICU admission

### 7.5 AD in Children — Special Considerations

- **Age**: Can occur in children of any age with high-level SCI; reported even in infants
- **Recognition challenges**: Young children may not be able to describe symptoms; irritability, crying, facial flushing may be the only signs
- **Lower blood pressure norms**: Normal BP values are lower in children; AD may occur at BP values that would be "normal" for adults
- **Prevention**: Regular bladder and bowel programs; avoid triggers; patient/family/school education about recognition and management; AD wallet card/emergency plan
- **Latex allergy**: Children with SCI (especially those exposed to frequent catheterization/procedures) are at increased risk for latex allergy; use latex-free products

## SECTION 8: GROWTH, PUBERTY & DEVELOPMENTAL ISSUES

### 8.1 Growth

- **Linear growth**: SCI in childhood affects growth of paralyzed limbs (reduced length, muscle mass, bone density); overall height may be affected
- **Lower extremity length discrepancy**: Paralyzed limbs may be shorter due to disuse effects on growth plates; unilateral injuries → limb length discrepancy
- **Spinal growth**: Paralytic scoliosis (see Section 4) affects trunk height and seated height
- **Nutrition**: Important for growth; may be compromised by dysphagia (in cervical injuries), depression, medication side effects; obesity also common (reduced energy expenditure)

### 8.2 Puberty

- **Puberty timing**: Generally normal or may be slightly early (central precocious puberty in some, particularly after associated TBI)
- **Males**: Testosterone production generally normal; erection function depends on level (reflexogenic erections preserved in UMN lesions above S2-S4; psychogenic erections in lower lesions); ejaculation and fertility are major long-term concerns; electroejaculation or vibrostimulation may be needed
- **Females**: Menarche typically occurs at normal age; menstrual periods resume after spinal shock (may be initially absent); fertility generally preserved; pregnancy is possible but considered high-risk (AD risk during labor, pressure ulcer risk, DVT risk, respiratory compromise in high injuries)
- **Sexual education**: Critically important and often overlooked in pediatric SCI; must address changes in sensation, function, fertility, safe practices; developmentally appropriate counseling should begin in adolescence

### 8.3 Bone Health

- **Osteoporosis**: Rapid bone mineral density loss below the level of injury; begins within weeks of injury; primarily affects sublesional skeleton (especially distal femur, proximal tibia — areas most prone to fracture)
- **Fracture risk**: Significantly increased in paralyzed limbs; may occur with minimal trauma (transfers, range of motion exercises); fractures may be painless (insensate); presenting sign may be limb swelling, erythema, warmth (mimics DVT or infection), or new-onset AD/spasticity
- **Board pearl**: In a child with SCI who presents with a warm, swollen, erythematous limb — consider fracture (in addition to DVT and infection) even with trivial/no reported trauma
- **Management**: Weight-bearing activities (standing frame, gait training if possible), calcium and vitamin D supplementation, bisphosphonates (limited pediatric SCI data); fracture management often conservative (splinting, soft immobilization — avoid rigid casts that can cause pressure ulcers)

### 8.4 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

- **Incidence in pediatric SCI**: Lower than adults (~3-10% in children vs. ~15-35% in adults)
- **Location**: Typically around large joints below the level of injury — hips most common, then knees
- **Presentation**: Swelling, warmth, decreased ROM; may mimic DVT; elevated alkaline phosphatase; triple-phase bone scan most sensitive early test
- **Management**: Physical therapy (ROM exercises — controversy about aggressive ROM); NSAIDs (indomethacin) for prevention/treatment; surgical excision if mature HO causing functional impairment (delayed until maturation, typically 12-18 months)

## SECTION 9: WHEELCHAIR PRESCRIPTION FOR GROWING CHILDREN

### 9.1 General Principles

- **Growth accommodation**: Pediatric wheelchairs must accommodate growth; adjustable components essential (seat width, depth, back height, footrest height); chairs may need to be replaced every 2-4 years during growth
- **Developmental considerations**: Wheelchair should facilitate developmental exploration, socialization, play, and education; early mobility is critical for cognitive and psychosocial development
- **Power mobility**: Can be introduced as early as 12-18 months of age for children who cannot achieve independent mobility otherwise; evidence supports early power mobility for cognitive and social development
- **Reference**: Butler C. Effects of powered mobility on self-initiated behaviors of very young children with locomotor disability. Developmental Medicine and Child Neurology. 1986;28(3):325-332.

### 9.2 Manual vs. Power Wheelchair

- **Manual wheelchair**: Appropriate when child has adequate upper extremity strength and endurance; lightweight frame (rigid or folding); proper fit critical for propulsion efficiency and injury prevention (shoulder preservation)
- **Power wheelchair**: Indicated when child cannot effectively propel manual chair; high cervical injuries; multiple limb involvement; can be drive-controlled by joystick, head array, sip-and-puff, or eye-tracking systems depending on functional level
- **Tilt-in-space**: Essential feature for pressure relief, postural management, blood pressure management (orthostatic hypotension), comfort; ability to tilt 30-45+ degrees
- **Recline**: For pressure redistribution, catheterization access, rest; combined tilt-and-recline systems

### 9.3 Seating & Positioning

- **Goals**: Pressure relief (prevent pressure ulcers), postural support (prevent/manage scoliosis and pelvic obliquity), function optimization, comfort
- **Cushion**: Pressure-distributing cushion essential; ROHO (air cell), Jay (foam/gel), hybrid designs; pressure mapping to guide selection
- **Back support**: Contoured/custom-molded for scoliosis management; thoracic lateral supports as needed
- **Headrest**: Required for cervical injuries; tilt-dependent positioning
- **Pelvic positioning**: Pelvic belt/positioning belt; anti-thrust seat; accommodate pelvic obliquity if present
- **Armrests, footrests, legrests**: Adjustable for growth; elevating legrests for edema management

### 9.4 Standing Programs

- **Standing frames/standers**: Recommended for children with SCI for bone health (weight-bearing), joint ROM, bowel/bladder function, cardiovascular conditioning, psychosocial benefits
- **Types**: Supine standers, prone standers, sit-to-stand standers, standing wheelchairs (power chairs with integrated standing feature)
- **Duration**: Typically 45-60 minutes per day recommended; may be incorporated into school day
- **Evidence**: Limited high-quality evidence for specific benefits in pediatric SCI; largely extrapolated from adult SCI and clinical experience; generally considered standard of care

## SECTION 10: ADDITIONAL PEDIATRIC SCI TOPICS

### 10.1 Spasticity Management

- **Oral medications**: Baclofen (most commonly used; GABAb agonist), tizanidine (alpha-2 agonist), diazepam (GABAa; less used in children due to sedation/tolerance), dantrolene (direct muscle relaxant; hepatotoxicity risk — liver monitoring required)
- **Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) pump**: Catheter-pump system delivering baclofen directly to intrathecal space; dramatically lower doses than oral; indicated for severe spasticity not responding to oral medications; surgical implantation; requires refills every 1-6 months; complications include catheter malfunction, infection, withdrawal syndrome (potentially fatal — fever, rebound spasticity, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, death)
- **Botulinum toxin**: Focal spasticity treatment; injection into specific muscles; temporary (3-4 months); useful for targeted spasticity reduction
- **Serial casting**: For contracture management; combined with botulinum toxin for maximal benefit
- **Surgical**: Selective dorsal rhizotomy (less commonly used in SCI than in cerebral palsy); tendon lengthening/transfers for fixed contractures

### 10.2 Thermoregulation

- **Poikilothermia**: Impaired thermoregulation in SCI (especially high-level injuries); body temperature fluctuates with environmental temperature due to loss of sympathetic control of vasomotor tone and sweating below lesion level
- **Hypothermia and hyperthermia risk**: Children are particularly vulnerable; education of families and schools critical
- **Management**: Appropriate clothing, environmental control, monitoring during temperature extremes, hydration

### 10.3 Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) / Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)

- **Risk**: Lower in prepubertal children than adults; increases with age (particularly adolescents)
- **Prophylaxis**: Controversial in pediatric SCI; generally recommended for adolescents with acute SCI (similar to adult protocols); less clear indication in young children
- **Methods**: Compression stockings, sequential compression devices, low-molecular-weight heparin (in adolescents)

### 10.4 Pressure Injuries

- **Risk factors in pediatric SCI**: Immobility, insensate skin, incontinence, poor nutrition, spasticity, bony prominences
- **Common sites**: Sacrum/coccyx, ischial tuberosities (in wheelchair users), greater trochanters, heels, occiput (in young children — larger head)
- **Prevention**: Pressure relief schedules (weight shifts every 15-30 minutes), appropriate cushion/mattress, skin inspection (twice daily), nutrition optimization, moisture management
- **Education**: Age-appropriate skin care education; children taught self-inspection (mirror for areas they cannot see); responsibility gradually transferred to child as they mature

### 10.5 Transition to Adult Care

- **Critical period**: Adolescence to young adulthood; transition should be planned and gradual
- **Key areas**: Medical management (new providers), vocational planning, independent living skills, sexual health, psychosocial support, insurance/financial planning
- **Challenges**: Pediatric SCI care model is family-centered; adult model expects patient independence; gap in services for young adults with SCI
