## [BOARD] Key Concepts

### Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

DDH encompasses a spectrum of hip joint abnormalities ranging from mild acetabular dysplasia to frank dislocation. It affects approximately 1 to 2 per 1,000 live births for frank dislocation and 10 to 20 per 1,000 for instability or dysplasia. The condition shows a strong female predominance (female-to-male ratio of 4:1 to 8:1), and the left hip is affected in roughly 60% of cases because the most common fetal position (left occiput anterior) presses the left hip against the maternal sacrum. Key risk factors include breech presentation, female sex, firstborn status, positive family history, and oligohydramnios (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2000; AAOS, 2014/2022).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Asymmetric skin folds are suggestive but not diagnostic of DDH; they are present in approximately 25% of normal infants.

**Physical Examination Maneuvers:**

| Test | Technique | What It Detects |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Barlow test** | Hip is flexed to 90 degrees; gentle adduction with posteriorly directed force | A *dislocatable* hip (the hip is currently in the joint; the maneuver pushes it out) |
| **Ortolani test** | Hip is flexed to 90 degrees; gentle abduction with anteriorly directed lift | A *reducible* hip (the hip is out; the maneuver returns it to the acetabulum with a palpable "clunk") |
| **Galeazzi sign** | Hips and knees flexed; compare knee heights bilaterally | Unilateral dislocation (the affected side shows a lower knee height) |
| **Trendelenburg sign** | Patient stands on affected leg; observe contralateral pelvis | Hip abductor weakness or instability (pelvis drops on the opposite side) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** A reliable mnemonic for examination: "Barlow is Bad" (it dislocates the hip) and "Ortolani is OK" (it reduces the hip back into the socket). The Barlow maneuver is provocative; the Ortolani maneuver is reductive. This distinction is among the most frequently tested concepts in board examinations.

**Imaging:**

Ultrasound is the preferred imaging modality before 4 to 6 months of age because the femoral head is largely cartilaginous and not visible on plain radiography. After 4 to 6 months, when the ossific nucleus appears, radiographs become useful. The AAP recommends targeted ultrasound screening for girls born breech at 6 weeks of age or whenever examination findings are equivocal (AAP, 2000).

**Graf Ultrasound Classification:**

| Type | Alpha Angle | Interpretation |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| I | Greater than 60 degrees | Normal hip |
| IIa | 50 to 59 degrees | Physiologic immaturity (acceptable if infant is younger than 3 months) |
| IIb | 50 to 59 degrees | Delayed ossification (infant older than 3 months; requires treatment) |
| IIc | 43 to 49 degrees | Critical zone (significant dysplasia) |
| III | Less than 43 degrees | Subluxated hip |
| IV | Not applicable (dislocated) | Dislocated hip |

The Graf classification was established in 1980 and remains the standard for ultrasound-based grading of infant hip morphology (Graf, 1980).

**Treatment:**

The Pavlik harness is the first-line treatment for DDH diagnosed before 6 months of age. It positions the hip in flexion of 100 to 110 degrees and abduction of less than 70 degrees, maintaining reduction while allowing active hip motion. Success rates range from 85% to 95% when initiated before 6 months. Full-time wear for 6 to 12 weeks is followed by gradual weaning (Pavlik, 1957; AAOS, 2014/2022).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Forced abduction beyond 70 degrees in the Pavlik harness increases the risk of avascular necrosis (AVN) of the femoral head. The harness is contraindicated in infants older than 6 months, in teratologic dislocations, and after a failed 3- to 4-week trial. AVN rates with proper Pavlik use are 0% to 5%.

If the Pavlik harness fails, closed reduction under general anesthesia with hip spica casting is the next step. Open reduction is reserved for hips that cannot be concentrically reduced by closed methods, typically after 12 to 18 months of age.

### Clubfoot (Talipes Equinovarus)

Clubfoot occurs in 1 to 2 per 1,000 live births, with a male-to-female ratio of 2:1. Approximately 50% of cases are bilateral. The majority (80%) are idiopathic; the remainder are associated with neuromuscular conditions such as myelomeningocele, arthrogryposis, trisomy 18, or amniotic band syndrome. A genetic basis is emerging, with PITX1 gene mutations implicated in some cases (Dobbs and Gurnett, 2009).

**The CAVE Mnemonic:**

| Letter | Deformity Component |
|--------|-------------------|
| **C** | Cavus — high medial longitudinal arch caused by forefoot pronation relative to hindfoot |
| **A** | Adductus — forefoot is deviated medially |
| **V** | Varus — hindfoot is inverted |
| **E** | Equinus — ankle is fixed in plantarflexion due to tight Achilles tendon |

**The Ponseti Method:**

The Ponseti method is the gold standard for initial management of idiopathic clubfoot, achieving correction in 90% to 95% of cases and dramatically reducing the need for extensive surgery from approximately 70% to 90% (with traditional approaches) to less than 5% (Ponseti, 1996; Morcuende et al., 2004).

The protocol consists of:
1. **Serial manipulation and casting:** Weekly long-leg casts (typically 5 to 7 casts), correcting the deformities in a specific order — cavus first (by supinating the forefoot), then adductus and varus simultaneously with each cast, and equinus last.
2. **Percutaneous Achilles tenotomy:** Performed in approximately 80% of cases to correct residual equinus after casting.
3. **Foot abduction orthosis (FAO):** A Denis Browne bar with attached shoes worn full-time for 3 months, then during sleep and naps for 4 years to prevent relapse.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Relapse rates without orthosis compliance are 20% to 40%, compared with less than 5% with good compliance. Bracing adherence is the single most important factor in preventing recurrence.

**Pirani Scoring System:**

The Pirani score quantifies clubfoot severity using 6 parameters (3 hindfoot, 3 midfoot), each scored 0 (normal), 0.5 (moderate), or 1 (severe), for a total range of 0 to 6. Hindfoot parameters include the severity of the posterior crease, emptiness of the heel, and rigidity of equinus. Midfoot parameters include curvature of the lateral border, severity of the medial crease, and coverage of the lateral head of the talus (Pirani et al., 1999).

### Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease

Legg-Calve-Perthes disease (LCPD) is an idiopathic osteonecrosis of the femoral head in children, occurring between ages 4 and 10 years with a peak at 5 to 7 years. The incidence is approximately 1 in 1,200 children, with a strong male predominance (male-to-female ratio of 4:1 to 5:1). Bilateral involvement occurs in 10% to 15% of cases but is usually asynchronous; when bilateral, clinicians should consider underlying endocrine disorders such as hypothyroidism or growth hormone deficiency.

**Waldenstrom Stages:**

| Stage | Radiographic Findings |
|-------|----------------------|
| Initial (Necrosis) | Increased density of the femoral head; widened joint space |
| Fragmentation | Mixed lytic and sclerotic areas as necrotic bone is resorbed |
| Re-ossification | New bone formation; head begins to reform |
| Remodeling | Final head shape is established by skeletal maturity |

**Herring Lateral Pillar Classification:**

This classification, based on the height of the lateral pillar of the femoral epiphysis during the fragmentation stage, is the most important prognostic tool for LCPD (Herring et al., 2004).

| Group | Lateral Pillar Height | Prognosis |
|-------|----------------------|-----------|
| A | Full height maintained | Good |
| B | Greater than 50% height maintained | Intermediate |
| B/C | Approximately 50% height | Guarded |
| C | Less than 50% height | Poor |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Age at onset is the single most important prognostic factor. Children younger than 6 years at onset generally have favorable outcomes regardless of classification. Children older than 8 years with Group B/C or C involvement have poor prognoses and typically require surgical containment procedures (femoral or pelvic osteotomy).

**Stulberg Classification (Outcome at Skeletal Maturity):**

| Class | Description | Long-Term Outcome |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| I | Normal spherical femoral head | Good |
| II | Spherical head with coxa magna, short neck, or steep acetabulum | Good |
| III | Ovoid (non-spherical) but congruent head | Fair |
| IV | Flat head with flat acetabulum | Fair |
| V | Flat head with normal acetabulum (incongruent) | Poor — early arthritis |

"Head at risk" signs on radiography include the Gage sign, calcification lateral to the epiphysis, lateral subluxation, and a horizontal physis. These findings suggest a worse prognosis (Stulberg et al., 1981).

### Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)

SCFE involves posterior and inferior displacement of the proximal femoral epiphysis relative to the metaphysis through the physis. It is the most common adolescent hip disorder, with an incidence of approximately 10.8 per 100,000. The classic patient is an overweight adolescent boy aged 10 to 16 years. Additional risk factors include African American race, hypothyroidism, growth hormone therapy, and renal osteodystrophy.

Bilateral involvement occurs in 18% to 50% of cases, often sequentially, prompting debate about prophylactic pinning of the contralateral hip, particularly in patients younger than 10 years or those with endocrine disorders.

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Antalgic gait with the affected limb externally rotated
- Referred pain to the knee or medial thigh (a common board question pitfall)
- Obligatory external rotation with hip flexion (Drehmann sign)
- Limited internal rotation and abduction

> **Clinical Pearl:** Any adolescent with knee or thigh pain should have hip radiographs to rule out SCFE. A frog-leg lateral radiograph is the most sensitive view; the Klein line (drawn along the superior femoral neck) will fail to intersect the femoral head. The classic radiographic analogy is "ice cream falling off the cone."

**Classification (Loder et al., 1993):**

| Stability | Definition | AVN Risk |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Stable | Patient is able to weight-bear (with or without crutches) | 0% to 10% |
| Unstable | Patient is unable to weight-bear | 24% to 47% |

**Treatment:**
- Stable SCFE: In situ percutaneous single screw fixation. Critically, the slip should NOT be reduced because forced reduction dramatically increases the risk of AVN.
- Unstable SCFE: Urgent surgical stabilization. This is an orthopedic emergency.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Never attempt to manipulate or reduce a stable SCFE. The treatment is gentle positioning and in situ fixation. Forced reduction can compromise the already tenuous blood supply to the femoral head.

### Apophysitis Conditions

**Osgood-Schlatter Disease:**

Osgood-Schlatter disease is a traction apophysitis at the tibial tubercle where the patellar tendon inserts. It affects children aged 10 to 15 years during rapid growth, is bilateral in 20% to 30%, and has become approximately equal in sex distribution with increasing female sports participation. The condition is self-limited and resolves with skeletal maturity.

Treatment is conservative: activity modification, ice, NSAIDs, quadriceps stretching and strengthening, and patellar tendon straps during sport. Radiographs may show fragmentation or prominence of the tibial tubercle apophysis.

**Sever Disease (Calcaneal Apophysitis):**

Sever disease is the analogous traction apophysitis of the calcaneal apophysis at the Achilles tendon insertion. It is the most common cause of heel pain in the growing child, typically affecting children aged 8 to 14. Treatment mirrors that of Osgood-Schlatter: activity modification, heel cups or cushions, calf stretching, and NSAIDs.

**Sinding-Larsen-Johansson Syndrome:**

This condition involves traction apophysitis at the inferior pole of the patella — the same mechanism as Osgood-Schlatter but at the proximal attachment of the patellar tendon. Management is identical.

> **Clinical Pearl:** All three traction apophysitis conditions share the same pathophysiology: repetitive microtrauma at a tendon-apophysis junction during rapid growth. They are self-limited and do not require surgical intervention. Board questions may ask you to compare locations: Osgood-Schlatter = tibial tubercle, Sinding-Larsen-Johansson = inferior patella, Sever = calcaneus.

### Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS)

AIS is defined as a lateral spinal curvature of 10 degrees or greater (Cobb angle) with no identifiable underlying cause, occurring in patients aged 10 to 18 years. The prevalence of curves exceeding 10 degrees is 2% to 3% of adolescents. While small curves affect males and females equally, the female-to-male ratio increases to 8:1 for curves exceeding 30 degrees.

**Cobb Angle Measurement:**

The Cobb angle is measured by drawing lines along the superior endplate of the most tilted upper vertebra and the inferior endplate of the most tilted lower vertebra, then measuring the angle between perpendicular lines drawn from these endplates.

| Cobb Angle | Management |
|------------|-----------|
| Less than 10 degrees | Normal variant; not classified as scoliosis |
| 10 to 24 degrees | Observation with serial radiographs every 6 to 12 months during growth |
| 25 to 40 degrees | Bracing (thoracolumbosacral orthosis / Boston brace) |
| 40 to 50 degrees (or greater than 50 degrees) | Surgical intervention (posterior spinal fusion) |
| Greater than 50 degrees | Will likely progress even after skeletal maturity at approximately 1 degree per year |

**Risser Sign:**

The Risser sign grades iliac apophysis ossification as a marker of skeletal maturity, from 0 (no ossification, significant growth remaining) through 5 (complete fusion, skeletal maturity reached).

| Grade | Ossification of Iliac Apophysis | Implication |
|-------|--------------------------------|-------------|
| 0 | None | Significant growth remaining |
| 1 | 0% to 25% (lateral) | Substantial growth |
| 2 | 25% to 50% | Moderate growth |
| 3 | 50% to 75% | Decreasing growth |
| 4 | 75% to 100% (not fused) | Minimal growth |
| 5 | Complete fusion | Skeletal maturity |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The highest risk for curve progression is Risser 0 to 1 with a Cobb angle of 20 degrees or more (68% progression risk). Bracing is most effective at Risser 0 to 2.

**Screening:**

The Scoliosis Research Society (SRS), AAOS, and AAP support screening with the Adams forward bend test at ages 10 to 12 for girls and 13 to 14 for boys. A scoliometer reading of 7 degrees or more warrants referral (SRS/AAOS/POSNA/AAP, 2015).

**BrAIST Trial:**

The Bracing in Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis Trial (BrAIST) is the landmark randomized controlled trial for scoliosis bracing. Published in 2013 and stopped early for efficacy, it enrolled 242 patients (combined randomized and preference cohorts) with Risser 0 to 2, Cobb angle 25 to 40 degrees, and ages 10 to 15 years. Key findings (Weinstein et al., 2013):

- Bracing success rate: 72% (combined cohort) versus 48% with observation alone
- Dose-response relationship: at least 13 hours per day of wear achieved 90% to 93% success
- The lowest quartile of wear time (0 to 6 hours/day) had outcomes worse than observation
- Braces were prescribed for at least 18 hours per day

> **Clinical Pearl:** A right thoracic curve is the most common pattern in AIS. A left thoracic curve is a red flag that should prompt MRI to rule out syringomyelia, Chiari malformation, or spinal cord tumor. Scoliosis in neuromuscular disease differs from idiopathic scoliosis: curves tend to be long, C-shaped, more rapidly progressive, and often require earlier surgical intervention.

Long-term natural history data from a 50-year follow-up study showed that untreated AIS patients had back pain and cosmetic concerns but most led productive lives. However, curves exceeding 50 degrees continued to progress at approximately 1 degree per year in adulthood (Weinstein et al., 2003).

### Congenital Muscular Torticollis (CMT)

CMT results from unilateral shortening or fibrosis of the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) muscle. The incidence is 0.3% to 2% of live births. The right SCM is preferentially affected (approximately 75% of cases). A palpable SCM mass (fibromatosis colli) is present in approximately 20% of cases and typically resolves by 4 to 6 months.

**Clinical Features:**
- The head tilts toward the affected SCM and the chin rotates away from it
- Associated DDH in 10% to 20% (all CMT patients should receive hip screening)
- Associated plagiocephaly in 80% to 90%

**Treatment:**
Conservative management with passive range of motion stretching, tummy time, and environmental repositioning is effective in 90% to 95% of cases by age 1 year. Surgical SCM release (unipolar or bipolar) is considered if the condition persists beyond 12 to 18 months despite consistent therapy.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Before diagnosing CMT, clinicians must exclude other causes of torticollis including posterior fossa tumors, Klippel-Feil syndrome, atlantoaxial rotary subluxation, and ocular torticollis (where the head tilt compensates for an extraocular muscle palsy or refractive error).

### Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI)

OI is a heritable disorder of type I collagen (COL1A1/COL1A2 gene mutations) with an overall incidence of 1 in 15,000 to 20,000 live births. The Sillence classification (1979) originally described 4 types, now expanded to more than 15 molecular subtypes (Sillence et al., 1979).

**Sillence Classification:**

| Type | Severity | Inheritance | Key Features |
|------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| I (Mild) | Mild; most common (~50%) | Autosomal dominant | Blue sclerae; mild short stature or normal height; fractures decrease after puberty; dentinogenesis imperfecta in ~50%; hearing loss (~50%, typically 2nd-3rd decade, mixed type) |
| II (Perinatal Lethal) | Most severe (~10%) | Usually new dominant mutation | Lethal in the perinatal period; beaded ribs; severe long bone deformity |
| III (Severe Deforming) | Severe (~10%) | Variable | Most severe form compatible with survival; progressive deformity; very short stature; gray/blue sclerae; dentinogenesis imperfecta |
| IV (Moderate) | Moderate (~10%) | Autosomal dominant | Normal to gray sclerae; variable severity; moderate short stature |

The molecular defect in Type I is quantitative (reduced production of normal collagen), while Types II through IV involve qualitative defects (structurally abnormal collagen). Additional characteristic findings include wormian bones (extra bones within cranial sutures) visible on skull radiographs.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Distinguishing OI from non-accidental trauma (child abuse) is a critical clinical and board skill. Both conditions present with multiple fractures, but OI is suggested by additional findings: blue sclerae, family history, wormian bones, dentinogenesis imperfecta, hearing loss, and joint hypermobility.

**Treatment:**
Bisphosphonate therapy (intravenous pamidronate or zoledronate) has become the standard of care for moderate to severe OI, as demonstrated by Glorieux et al. (1998). Cyclic pamidronate reduces fracture rates, increases bone mineral density, and decreases bone pain. Rehabilitation focuses on safe mobility, fracture prevention, aquatic therapy, and strengthening within protected limits.

### Salter-Harris Fracture Classification

The Salter-Harris classification (1963) categorizes fractures involving the physis (growth plate) of long bones in children. The SALTR mnemonic aids recall of fracture patterns.

| Type | Mnemonic | Fracture Pattern | Frequency | Prognosis |
|------|----------|-----------------|-----------|-----------|
| I | **S**lipped (Straight across) | Through the physis only; epiphysis slips | 6% | Good |
| II | **A**bove | Through the physis and metaphysis (Thurston-Holland fragment) | 75% (most common) | Good |
| III | **L**ower (beLow) | Through the physis and epiphysis (intra-articular) | 8% | Fair — requires anatomic reduction |
| IV | **T**hrough (Two) | Through metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis | 10% | Fair to poor — usually requires ORIF |
| V | **R**ammed (cRush) | Crush injury to the physis | 1% | Poor — growth arrest is common; often a retrospective diagnosis |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Type I fractures may have a completely normal radiograph. Diagnosis is clinical, based on tenderness localized to the physis. If a child has point tenderness at the growth plate after trauma, treat as a Salter-Harris I fracture even if radiographs are negative.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Thurston-Holland fragment — a triangular metaphyseal fragment — is pathognomonic for a Salter-Harris Type II fracture. Types III, IV, and V carry the highest risk of growth disturbance. A Peterson Type VI (loss of a portion of the physis from trauma, infection, or burn) was added subsequently.

### Pediatric Sports Injuries

Several overuse injuries are unique to the growing skeleton because the physis and apophysis are weaker than surrounding ligaments and tendons.

**Little League Elbow:** Medial epicondyle apophysitis from repetitive valgus stress during throwing. Young athletes present with medial elbow pain exacerbated by throwing. Treatment involves rest, modification of throwing mechanics, and adherence to USA Baseball pitch count guidelines.

**Little League Shoulder:** Stress fracture through the proximal humeral physis from repetitive overhead throwing. Radiographs may show widening of the proximal humeral physis. Treatment is rest and gradual return to sport.

**Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD):** Involves separation of a segment of articular cartilage and underlying subchondral bone. In young throwers, the capitellum (lateral elbow) is affected by valgus overload. In adolescents, the lateral femoral condyle of the knee is the most common location. Stable lesions in skeletally immature patients may heal with activity restriction, while unstable or displaced lesions require surgical fixation or removal.

### The Limping Child: Differential Diagnosis by Age

The limping child is a common board scenario. Organizing the differential by age is essential.

| Age Group | Key Diagnoses to Consider |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1 to 3 years | Toddler's fracture, septic arthritis, DDH (late presentation), JIA, leukemia |
| 4 to 10 years | Legg-Calve-Perthes disease, transient synovitis, septic arthritis, JIA, bone tumors (Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma) |
| 11 to 16 years | SCFE, Osgood-Schlatter disease, osteochondritis dissecans, stress fractures, bone tumors |
| Any age | Septic arthritis (surgical emergency), osteomyelitis, fracture, malignancy, JIA |

> **Clinical Pearl:** A toddler's fracture is a spiral or oblique fracture of the distal tibial shaft that occurs with low-energy twisting mechanisms. It may be occult on initial radiographs, and a bone scan or repeat films at 10 to 14 days may be necessary for confirmation.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Transient synovitis versus septic arthritis is a critical distinction. The Kocher criteria (discussed in Chapter 10) aid in differentiation: non-weight-bearing status, ESR greater than 40 mm/hr, fever greater than 38.5 degrees Celsius, and WBC greater than 12,000/mm3. With all 4 criteria positive, the probability of septic arthritis is 99.6%.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### The Developing Pediatric Skeleton: Unique Vulnerabilities

The pediatric musculoskeletal system differs fundamentally from that of adults. The physis (growth plate) is the weakest link in the pediatric bone-ligament complex, so forces that would cause ligamentous injury in an adult often produce physeal fractures in a child. The periosteum is thicker and more biologically active, leading to faster fracture healing but also the potential for deformity with growth. Apophyses — secondary ossification centers at tendon or ligament attachment sites — are vulnerable to traction injuries, especially during periods of rapid growth when the musculotendinous unit tightens relative to the lengthening bone.

Understanding these developmental differences is essential for interpreting pediatric imaging, selecting age-appropriate treatments, and anticipating complications such as growth arrest, angular deformity, or limb length discrepancy.

### DDH: Pathoanatomy and Expanded Management

The acetabulum at birth is relatively shallow and cartilaginous, requiring concentric contact with the femoral head for normal development. Failure of this relationship — whether from mechanical factors (breech positioning), ligamentous laxity (hormonal influences), or genetic predisposition — leads to a spectrum of pathology from mild acetabular dysplasia to irreducible dislocation.

The natural history of untreated DDH depends on severity. Mild acetabular dysplasia may remodel spontaneously, particularly when the femoral head remains concentrically located. However, a frankly dislocated hip left untreated leads to progressive acetabular deformation, difficulty with ambulation, and ultimately premature osteoarthritis.

For patients who fail Pavlik harness treatment or who present late (after 6 months), closed reduction under general anesthesia with arthrography is performed. The safe zone of Ramsey (the arc between the position of maximum abduction causing redislocation and the position of maximum adduction causing dislocation) guides the degree of abduction maintained in the subsequent hip spica cast. If the safe zone is narrow (less than 25 degrees), the reduction is considered unstable and open reduction may be required.

Open reduction typically involves addressing all obstacles to reduction: the inverted labrum, the hypertrophied ligamentum teres, the transverse acetabular ligament, and the psoas tendon. After 18 months of age, femoral shortening osteotomy may be necessary to achieve atraumatic reduction without excessive pressure on the femoral head.

### Clubfoot: Beyond the Ponseti Method

While the Ponseti method has revolutionized clubfoot treatment worldwide, certain situations warrant additional consideration. Syndromic clubfoot (associated with arthrogryposis, myelomeningocele, or chromosomal anomalies) tends to be stiffer and less responsive to serial casting. These patients may require more casts, have higher relapse rates, and are more likely to need additional surgical intervention.

The correction order in the Ponseti method follows a biomechanical rationale. The first maneuver corrects cavus by supinating the forefoot relative to the hindfoot (aligning the forefoot with the hindfoot). Subsequent casts correct adductus and varus simultaneously by abducting the foot around the lateral border of the talus. The equinus is corrected last because premature dorsiflexion with persistent varus and adductus creates a rocker-bottom deformity.

Recurrence after Ponseti treatment is almost exclusively a compliance issue with the foot abduction orthosis. The orthosis maintains the foot in 60 to 70 degrees of external rotation with 10 to 15 degrees of dorsiflexion, connected by a bar at shoulder width. Education of families regarding the critical importance of bracing compliance is a central element of clubfoot management.

### LCPD: Pathophysiology and Treatment Algorithms

The pathophysiology of LCPD involves disruption of the blood supply to the femoral head, leading to osteonecrosis followed by a biologic repair process of resorption and reossification. The final femoral head shape is determined by the degree of deformity that occurs during the fragmentation and reossification stages. Containment — keeping the femoral head centered within the acetabulum — takes advantage of the acetabulum's shape to mold the softened femoral head into a spherical form.

Containment can be achieved by non-operative methods (abduction bracing) or operative methods (femoral varus osteotomy or innominate osteotomy). Current evidence favors observation alone for children under 6 years with Group A or B involvement, as prognosis is inherently good. For children older than 8 years with Group B/C or C disease, surgical containment provides the best chance of maintaining femoral head sphericity.

### SCFE: Endocrine Considerations

While the vast majority of SCFE cases are related to obesity and mechanical overload of the physis during adolescent growth, a subset is related to endocrine conditions. Hypothyroidism, growth hormone therapy, and renal osteodystrophy weaken the physis and predispose to slippage. Bilateral SCFE, particularly in a younger child (under 10 years) or a child of normal weight, should prompt an endocrine evaluation.

The mechanism of SCFE involves failure of the hypertrophic zone of the physis under shear stress. Histologically, the physis is abnormally widened and irregular. The epiphysis displaces posteriorly and inferiorly relative to the metaphysis, which moves anteriorly and superiorly — creating the radiographic appearance of the metaphysis pushing through the physis.

### Scoliosis: Neuromuscular Versus Idiopathic

Neuromuscular scoliosis, seen in conditions such as cerebral palsy, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, and spinal muscular atrophy, differs substantially from AIS in pattern, progression, and management. Neuromuscular curves are typically long, C-shaped (involving the entire thoracolumbar spine), are more rapidly progressive, and continue to worsen even after skeletal maturity. Pelvic obliquity is common and threatens seated balance.

Bracing in neuromuscular scoliosis aims to delay surgery rather than prevent it; bracing alone rarely controls curve progression. Surgical fusion often extends to the pelvis to correct pelvic obliquity and provide a stable platform for seating. Complication rates are higher than for idiopathic scoliosis surgery (25% to 50%), with surgical site infection, blood loss, and respiratory complications being most common.

### OI: Rehabilitation Principles

Rehabilitation is central to the management of OI across all severity types. For patients with mild (Type I) OI, the focus is on maintaining activity and fitness while minimizing fracture risk. Low-impact activities such as swimming, cycling, and walking are encouraged. Strengthening exercises within pain-free ranges help improve bone density and reduce fall risk.

For patients with moderate to severe OI (Types III and IV), a more cautious approach is needed. Lightweight orthoses may support ambulation. Intramedullary rodding of long bones (using telescoping rods such as the Fassier-Duval rod) provides internal stabilization and prevents progressive bowing deformity while accommodating growth. Post-surgical rehabilitation emphasizes early mobilization and return to function.

Hydrotherapy is particularly valuable because buoyancy reduces fracture risk while allowing resistance exercise. Wheelchair sports and adaptive recreation expand participation opportunities for patients with severe forms.

### Fracture Remodeling in Children

The pediatric skeleton has a remarkable capacity for fracture remodeling, which is greatest in younger children, in fractures near the physis, and in fractures that angulate in the plane of motion of the adjacent joint. This capacity decreases with age and is minimal after age 10 to 12 years.

Remodeling allows acceptance of greater degrees of angulation in younger children, particularly for distal radius and proximal humerus fractures. However, rotational deformity does NOT remodel and must be corrected at the time of treatment. Physeal fractures that result in growth arrest may produce angular deformity (from partial physeal arrest) or limb length discrepancy (from complete physeal arrest).
